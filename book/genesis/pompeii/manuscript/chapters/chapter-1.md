# Chapter 1: The Drawer

<!-- Word count: 4,898 | Target: 5,300 (floor 4,800) | Anchor: his reading glasses, folded, the click, in a drawer she can't open -->

The bottom left drawer of my father's desk would not open, and I decided that was the thing that had killed him.

Not the heart. Not the second one, the bad one in February, the one I drove four hours through sleet to sit beside and then watched him survive out of pure stubbornness so he could die in April instead, alone, on a Tuesday, of the first one I'd already grieved. No. The drawer. The drawer had done it. I knelt on the cold floor of his study and worked the brass pull with both hands and got nothing, not a millimeter, not the courteous give of a thing that intends to open and is merely being coy, and I said, out loud, to the empty room, *you absolute bastard,* and I was not talking to the drawer.

That is how grief actually works, in case anyone has told you otherwise. It does not arrive in a black veil. It arrives as a furniture dispute.

I had come up from the city on the Thursday train with two rolls of bin bags, a box of file folders I'd bought specifically because they were the wrong color and would therefore feel like a fresh start, and a plan. The plan was: clear the study, sort the study, be a competent adult about the study. I am a competent adult about other people's things for a living. I spend my days in a basement with a magnifier and a scalpel and a tray of solvents, persuading the dirt off objects that outlived everyone who ever loved them, and I am calm doing it, I am genuinely good at it, I have hands that do not shake over a fourteen-hundred-year-old hinge. I had assumed those hands would also work in here.

They did not. They had been failing all morning in small specific ways. I had picked up his coffee mug, the ugly one, the one from the dig in Cyrene with the cartoon of a sphinx looking bored, and I had stood holding it and not been able to decide which box it went in. There was no box for it. I had bought boxes for *books* and boxes for *papers* and boxes for *to the charity shop* and boxes for *I cannot deal with this today,* and not one of them was the right size for an ugly mug, so I had put it back exactly where it was, in the ring of its own old stain, and moved on to the next thing, and the next thing was the drawer, and the drawer would not open.

I want to be precise, because precision is all I have ever been able to offer the dead. The desk was oak, mid-century, too heavy for the room, bought at an auction in 1981 with money he did not have. Four drawers down the right side, all working, all already emptied by me into folders the wrong color. One long drawer across the top, working. And on the left, beneath the kneehole, a single deep drawer, the kind built for hanging files, and it was stuck, and I knew without opening it what was in it, which is the whole problem, which is why I am telling you about a drawer when I could be telling you about a man.

His reading glasses were in there.

I knew because I had watched him put them there ten thousand times. Tortoiseshell, the cheap drugstore kind, the magnification of which he was vain enough to lie about, *one point five,* he said, when they were two and we both knew it. He read with them down his nose. He read everything. He read cereal boxes and other people's gravestones and the legends on museum cases that I had personally written, slowly, moving his lips, so he could tell me afterward which comma was wrong. And when he was done reading, when he had finished the page or the chapter or the bad news, he took them off with one hand, folded the arms in, and put them in the drawer, and they made a sound when the arms closed.

*Click.*

A small plastic *click,* the most ordinary sound a house can make. The last ordinary sound before he would tell you the thing he had been reading up to. *Click,* and then: the dig had lost its funding. *Click,* and then: your mother and I. *Click,* and then: it's nothing, the doctor's a worrier, sit down, you're hovering. He folded his glasses and the world reorganized itself and I had heard that sound so many times that it lived in my body below the level of language, and I knelt there on the floor pulling at a drawer that would not give, knowing the glasses were inside it, folded, silent, never going to make the sound again, and I could not open the drawer because if I opened the drawer I would have to be a person who held them.

So I gave up. Sunk an hour into a drawer, achieved nothing, and gave up, which is also how grief works, and which is also, if I am honest, how I have lived most of my life.

---

The rest of the study I could do. The rest of the study was not the drawer.

I worked through the afternoon with the radio on, low, a station playing songs I half-knew and could not have named, which suited me, because I did not want a song I could name. I sorted his offprints into the wrong-colored folders. I found, behind the *Journal of Roman Studies* run, a sleeve of slides I did not recognize, and I held them up one at a time to the gray window light, little squares of trapped Mediterranean noon, his thumb in the corner of two of them, a trench, a stratum, a meter-stick, a wall, a wall, a wall. He photographed walls the way other men photographed their children. I had been, as a child, occasionally jealous of walls.

I made a stack of things to keep and a stack of things to give away, and the giving-away stack stayed small and dishonest, because almost nothing in the room could go to a stranger without my having to explain it, and there was no one left to explain it to but me, and I already knew.

The books were the worst, and the books were the easiest, and I cannot account for how a thing can be both. Three walls of them, double-shelved, the second row hidden behind the first, the way he hid the books he was ashamed to love behind the books he wanted you to see him loving. Behind a serious gray run of site reports I found a paperback with a spaceship on the cover, the spine creased white in seven places, read seven times, a man who excavated the actual past for a living unwinding at night in someone's invented future. I sat on the floor and read the first page of it in his voice and could not get to the second. There were marginalia in everything. He argued with dead scholars in pencil, *no,* and *rubbish,* and once, beside a colleague's grand theory about trade routes, just the word *grief,* which I have thought about every day since and still cannot translate. I kept that one. I kept too many. The keep stack became a tower, then a second tower, and the giving-away stack held a dictionary, a duplicate atlas, and nothing else, and I understood I was not clearing the room, I was rearranging it, I was a magpie pretending to be a removal van.

There was a postcard wedged in a copy of Pliny, used as a bookmark, a photograph of the Bay of Naples the blue of a thing that has been photographed too many times to mean anything, a tourist blue, and on the back, in a hand not his, a woman's looping hand I did not know, *Wish you'd come. It's all still here. Patient as ever. — D.* No year. I have wondered since which of them was patient, the bay or the woman or the man who never went, and I will not learn now, and that is one more thing the drawer took without ever opening.

I found his datebook. He still kept a paper one, a little black appointment diary from the stationer's on the high street, the same model every January for thirty years. I flipped it open to April out of something I'd call morbidity if I were being kind to myself and nosiness if I weren't. The week he died had three entries. *Dentist, Tue 11.* He had not made it to the dentist. *Reyes, call back re: Türkiye permit.* And, on the Sunday before, in his cramped slanting capitals, underlined twice: *START LIST.*

I knew that list. Everyone who works in this field knows that list, or the shape of it. The list of the things you'll get to. The dig you'll reopen, the article you'll finally write, the language you'll learn, the wreck you'll go down to, the thing under the thing under the thing. My father's version had a name. He called it the To Chase list, capital C, and he talked about it the way some men talk about heaven, a place that is real and elsewhere and waiting and that you do not have to deserve, only reach.

I should explain *next season,* since it ran under everything in that room like a water table. When I was eleven he took me to Sicily, to a small dig he was consulting on, and parked me at the edge of a trench with a trowel and a brush and a square meter of dirt that was mine, genuinely mine, he made the site director write my name on the context sheet, and I spent six days going down through someone's two-thousand-year-old kitchen floor one millimeter at a time and found a coin, a real one, green and illegible, and the look on his face when I held it up was the look I have been trying to earn back from one man or another my entire adult life. *Next season,* he said, on the plane home, *next season I'll take you on a real one. One of mine. You and me.* And there was always a next season. The funding fell through, or the permit, or the second heart, or simply the year, the way years do, and the real one stayed in the future where the To Chase list lived, a country you could see from the plane and never land in. He meant it every time. That was the cruelty of it. He was not a man who lied. He was a man who believed in next season the way he believed in the looking, completely, and died inside the belief, which I suppose is one definition of faith and one definition of failure and I have not decided which.

He had been going to start the list. On the Sunday. He had died on the Tuesday. In between he had not started the list, he had not gone to the dentist, he had not called Reyes back about the Türkiye permit, and Reyes was me, my own surname looking up at me out of his own handwriting like a stranger's, because of course we shared it, of course I was in his datebook as a permit problem in a foreign country, of course the last thing he meant to do about me was administrative.

I put the datebook on the keep stack. Then I took it off the keep stack, because keeping it felt like keeping a wound open on purpose, and then I put it back, because giving it away felt like the wound winning, and I made that exact decision roughly four hundred times that day, each one taking everything I had.

---

I should tell you about the obituary, because it is the part I am least proud of, and the part that is most true.

He had drafted his own. Of course he had. He drafted everything, he was the kind of man who left annotations in the margins of his own grocery lists, and somewhere around the second heart attack he had apparently decided that if anyone was going to get the facts of his life wrong, it would be him. I found it in the long top drawer, in a folder marked, with what I can only call optimism, *ADMIN / MISC.* Three pages, typed, single-spaced, heavily revised in pencil. A man editing the sentence the world would read about him after he could no longer argue.

It was mostly accurate and mostly dull, the way these things are. *Born. Educated. Married, briefly. Survived by his daughter, Mara, also of the profession,* which was generous, since I am a conservator and not an archaeologist, I clean what he dug, I am the janitor of his cathedral, but he had written *also of the profession* and crossed out nothing, and I had to put the page down and breathe through my nose for a while.

And then I picked it back up, and I read the next line, and the next line was where he had written, of his own forty years of excavation, of the sites he'd opened and the seasons he'd lost and the funding he'd begged and the trenches he'd backfilled with his own hands at the end of every dig because you always leave a site the way you found it, you always put the earth back:

*He never found anything that justified the looking, and considered this the best part.*

And I laughed.

I want to be clear about the laugh. It was not a fond chuckle. It was not a single tear and a wistful smile, the camera pulling back, the music swelling. It came up out of me like something dislodged, an ugly bark of a laugh, alone in a dead man's study, and once it started it would not stop, it went on and on, it bent me over the desk with my hand flat on his self-written obituary, and somewhere in it the laugh changed, the way they do, the way the body cheats, and I could no longer have told you which one I was doing, and I did not stop doing either of them for a long time.

It wasn't even funny. That was the worst of it. *He never found anything that justified the looking, and considered this the best part.* It was the saddest sentence I have ever read and I laughed at it until I couldn't breathe, because it was him, it was him entirely, it was the man who took me to a dig in Sicily when I was eleven and promised, *next season I'll take you on a real one,* and then there was always a next season and never the real one, the man who looked for forty years and was proudest of having never found the thing, because the thing, found, would have ended the looking, and the looking was the only place he was ever fully alive.

I think I understood, bent over the desk, laughing and not-laughing, that I had inherited exactly one thing from him cleanly and without dilution, and it was not his eye and not his patience and not his luck. It was the looking. The being more at home in the search than in the having. I have spent my adult life finishing other people's excavations. I have closed out three of his, posthumously, the unfinished ones, written up the reports he never wrote, and I am very good at it, and I have never once started one of my own.

The coffee was cold and I drank all of it.

---

There was a smell coming from the kitchen, and I went to find it the way you go toward a smell in someone else's house, braced, and it was the bread.

The starter, I mean. He'd kept it on the windowsill in a tall jar with a rubber band around it to mark the rise. I'd given it to him, that was the thing, I'd made it myself in the year I baked, the year I'd decided baking was a personality, the year I read three books about hydration percentages and bought a scale that measured to the gram and lectured dinner guests about crumb structure until they stopped being dinner guests. I quit it as suddenly as I'd started, the way I quit things, and I'd offered him the starter half as a joke and he had taken it entirely seriously, had named it, had fed it every Sunday for two years, flour and water, flour and water, the dumbest and most faithful kind of love, keeping a colony of wild yeast alive on a windowsill because his daughter had handed it to him and quit.

It was dead. Of course it was dead. It had been three weeks. Nobody had fed it. A starter is not a relic, it is a culture, a living thing, it eats or it dies, and this one had a gray crust on top and a thin layer of dark liquid beneath, hooch, the smell of a thing fermenting itself into a corner, and under that, faintly, the smell of vinegar and feet, and I stood there holding the jar and thought, with the part of my brain that has never once been appropriate, *eighty percent hydration, it's too far gone, you'd have to throw away ninety percent and feed the rest twice a day for a week to even know if there's anything left under the dead.*

You'd have to feed the rest.

I unscrewed the lid. The smell got worse and then, oddly, better, the way bread smells do. I poured off the hooch into the sink. I scraped the gray crust into the bin. And at the bottom of the jar, under the dead part, there was a smear of paste that was the right color, oatmeal and cream, and when I put my nose to it past all the rot there was, underneath, very faint, the smell of something that wanted to be fed.

I stood at my dead father's sink with a jar of possibly-living, possibly-dead yeast and I did not feed it.

I want to be honest about why. It was not that I'd given up. It was that feeding it was a Sunday-after-Sunday thing, a *next week and the week after* thing, a thing you only start if you intend to keep going, and I could not, standing there, promise the jar anything. I could clean what was dead. I have always been able to clean what is dead. I did not know how to keep something alive, and I was tired of being handed the living and asked to.

I put the lid back on, loose, and put the jar in the fridge, which slows it down without deciding anything, which is the most me thing I have ever done. The fridge was nearly empty. A jar of his vile English mustard, a heel of cheese gone translucent at the cut edge, half a lemon dried to a stone, and three eggs, and I stood with the cold door open and the light on my face and did the arithmetic a person does when they are not ready to leave a kitchen, which eggs, which day, who for. Nobody. The eggs would go off in the dark of a house with no one in it, and I closed the door on them and on the jar and on the bottle of milk I did not check the date of because I knew, the way you know, that the milk had also been alive on a Tuesday and was not now.

I have thought, since, about what a culture is. The word does the same work in my profession that it does on a windowsill. A culture is the living continuation of a thing, fed forward, hand to hand, generation to generation, a colony kept alive only by the constant tending of people who will not be there to taste the last loaf. You cannot conserve a culture. You can only feed it or fail to. Everything in my basement is a culture that someone stopped feeding, and that is why it is mine to clean, and I had built a career on the second half of that sentence and run, for forty years, from the first. But I did not think any of that at the sink. At the sink I thought *eighty percent hydration* and *too far gone* and *you'd have to be there every day,* and I put the living-or-dead thing in the cold and went back to the study, because the study at least held still, because the study was full of cultures no one expected me to feed.

---

This is the part where I am supposed to tell you that something glowed. That I felt a presence, that the air changed, that on the windowsill or the threshold some sign appeared that the rest of my life was about to come apart and re-form around an impossible thing. I have read enough to know the shape of that sentence. I have written museum labels for objects that people once believed were doors, hinges between the world and the other one, and I have written *function unknown* on the cards of more of them than I can count, because that is what we write when a thing clearly mattered terribly to someone and we have lost the reason.

Nothing glowed.

There was only a study going dark, and a woman in it who was not crying so much as leaking, steadily, the way an old roof does, and the radio playing a song I didn't know, and four boxes the wrong color, and a drawer that would not open, and on the desk the obituary and the datebook with *START LIST* underlined twice in a dead man's hand.

I was three weeks from a world that no longer exists, and I spent that afternoon angry at a drawer. I did not know it. There was no reason I should have known it. That is the thing about the last ordinary days, that they are exactly as ordinary as all the others, that the floor does not creak any differently, that the man who taught you the looking does not appear in the doorway to tell you the looking was always going to find you in the end. He was in the drawer. He was folded up small in the drawer, silent, and I was not ready, and the volcano that would take everything I had not yet been given was a green and pretty mountain on a coast I had never seen, steaming gently to itself, patient as my father had taught me everything worth having is patient, and I sat in his chair and I made a decision.

I would finish something of his.

Not the room. The room was an excuse, the room was a task you give yourself so you don't have to do the actual thing, *I'll just finish the room,* I'd been telling myself since Thursday, *finish the room and then you'll feel it properly,* as if grief were a permit that came through once the paperwork cleared. The room would never be finished. The room would be in boxes the wrong color in a storage unit in five years and I would still not have felt it properly. I knew that. I am not stupid about myself, only helpless.

But the list. *START LIST.* He had meant to start the To Chase, and he had died on the Tuesday, and the list did not exist, there was nothing to finish, he had not written down a single one of the things he was going to get to, which was so exactly him that I nearly laughed again. Forty years of intending and not one line. The list was a list of things he had carried only in his head, and his head was in the ground in a churchyard outside the town where he was born, and the list had gone with it.

Except.

I went back to the long top drawer. *ADMIN / MISC.* Under the obituary, under a tax thing from 1994 he had no reason to keep, there was an envelope, padded, soft with handling, the address on it gone furry where his thumb had worried it. No stamp. Never sent, or never meant to be. And clipped to the front, on a torn corner of graph paper, in his hand but hurried, the capitals he used when an idea had hold of him and he was afraid it would get away:

*FOR THE LIST. START HERE.*

He had started the list after all. Once. Three weeks before he died, on a corner of graph paper, with one item, the first thing he meant to chase, the thing important enough to be the door into all the others.

Inside the envelope was a map.

I did not open it that night. I want you to understand that I held it, padded envelope and all, in both hands, in the dark of his study, and I knew the weight of paper and I knew the weight of vellum and this was neither, this was something old and folded many times and waxed against the wet, the weight of a thing that had been carried, and I knew, the way you know an object before you've cleaned it, that whatever it was, it was authentic.

I will tell you the things my hands told me, since my hands are the only part of me I trust in the dark. The envelope had been opened and resealed at least twice; the flap had two glue-lines, one old and one newer. There was a second clipping behind the graph paper, a corner of a different sheet, with a row of numbers on it that I took at first for a phone number and then for a grid reference and then, holding it to the last of the window light, for neither, for something I did not have a name for, ten figures and a small notation in a hand that was not my father's and not the woman's from the postcard, a third hand, a stranger's, dry and forward-leaning and certain. And the smell. Wax and old paper and, under it, faint, the smell of a cellar that has been shut a long time, cold stone and the green breath of water moving somewhere it shouldn't. I noted the smell and filed it under *storage conditions* and did not let myself notice that no cellar I had ever opened smelled like a thing that was still being kept. Not valuable. Authentic, which is different, which is the one quality I have ever trusted. He had found something. After forty years of being proudest of finding nothing, my father had found one last thing, and had clipped it to a torn corner of graph paper with one instruction, *START HERE,* and had not had the time, and had folded his glasses, and had put them in the drawer, and the drawer would not open, and the map would.

I sat in his chair with the envelope in my lap and I did not feed the bread and I did not open the drawer and I did not cry properly and I did the only thing I have ever known how to do, which is to begin the looking. Tomorrow I would find out what the map was of. I told myself it was for him. I told myself it was the thing I'd finish, the one of his I'd close out, the dutiful daughter's last clean act, and then I'd come back and feed the starter or not and open the drawer or not and put the boxes in storage and go back to my basement and my solvents and the long quiet company of the dead, who at least have the decency to stay where you leave them.

That was the lie. I had been telling it so long it had a smooth handle, worn furry where my thumb went. *The dead don't leave.* I had built a whole life on it, down in the basement with the things nobody living could lose anymore, and I believed it the way you believe a floor, without thinking, right up until I stepped on the part that wasn't there.

I turned off the lamp. The map was warm where I'd been holding it, which is impossible, paper doesn't hold heat, and I noted that and dismissed it the way I dismiss every reading that doesn't fit the instrument, and in the dark of the room I had failed all day to finish, I heard, from the desk, from the drawer that would not open, very small and very clear, a sound that could not have been there.

*Click.*
