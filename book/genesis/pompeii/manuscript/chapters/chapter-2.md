# Chapter 2: Too Accurate to Be Fake

<!-- Word count: 5,076 | Target: 5,200 (floor 4,800) | Anchor: Fenn's marginal note in a stranger's hand — "The map is honest. The door is not." -->

The shop had no name on it, only a sun-bleached card in the window that said WE BUY, as if the place existed solely to take things off the recently bereaved, which, I would come to understand, was exactly correct.

I found it the way you find anything: by being unable to stop. I had not opened the map. I want that on the record. I had carried it down on the morning train in my father's old document satchel, padded envelope and all, and I had told myself I would open it on the train, and then I had told myself I would open it in the café across from the station, and then I had drunk two bad coffees and eaten nothing and not opened it, because once it was open it was a thing I was doing, and not-opening it kept it a thing I might still decide not to do. There was a return address on the envelope, faded, never used. A street I didn't know in a town I'd been to twice. So I went there instead of opening it, which is, if you think about it, a way of opening a thing without admitting you have.

The bell was the proper kind, a real brass coil that hit a real brass arc, and it announced me into a smell I knew in my body before my brain caught up: old paper, gun oil, brass polish, dust that has been dust a long time. The conservator's perfume. I have spent my life in rooms that smell like that. I relaxed half an inch and hated that I had.

The man behind the counter did not look up.

He was sorting buttons. A whole tray of them, military by the look, and he had a jeweller's loupe screwed into one eye and a pair of brass tweezers and he was moving the buttons from one compartment into another with the unhurried contempt of a man who has decided the buttons are not worth what someone wanted for them. He was sixty, maybe, in a cardigan the color of weak tea, and he had a face that had said no for a living.

"We're closing at one," he said, to the buttons.

"It's eleven."

"I know what time it is." He moved another button. "I'm managing your expectations early."

I put the envelope on the counter. I didn't take the map out. I am not, whatever my father thought, a complete amateur; you don't open the cards before you've read the dealer. "I'm not selling," I said. "I'm asking. This came from here."

He looked up then, the loupe still in, so one eye was the ordinary tired brown of a man at the end of a long career and the other was a swimming, magnified thing, fixed on the envelope. He took the loupe out. He looked at the envelope the way I look at an object across a room before I touch it, cataloguing it from a distance, deciding what it would cost him to care.

"That's not mine," he said.

"The address is yours."

"The address is the shop's. The envelope's somebody's grief." He said it flatly, no cruelty in it, the way you'd say a thing was Tuesday. "Whose?"

"My father's."

"Recent?"

"April."

He nodded once, as if that settled a category, and pulled the envelope toward him by one corner, two fingers, the grip of a man who has handled ten thousand other people's keepsakes and learned not to hold them like they're his. "Then I'm sorry for it," he said, "and I'll tell you now I don't do sentiment refunds. People come in, six weeks after, wanting to give back what Dad bought, wanting the money to mean something. I'm not a confessional. I'm a shop."

"I told you. I'm not selling."

"Everybody says that." He turned the envelope over. Found the clipping, the graph-paper corner torn off square, my father's hurried capitals: *FOR THE LIST. START HERE.* He read it without expression. "This his hand?"

"Yes."

"Then he wrote it after he bought it. We don't annotate stock." He set the clipping aside, careful, squared to the counter's edge, and I noticed that he was careful, that the contempt was a manner and not a fact, and I filed that under *trust him slightly.* "Go on, then. You've come a long way to not sell me something. What's the question."

I undid the flap. The second glue-line gave first, the newer one, my father's reseal, and then the old one, and I drew the map out into the gray light of a shop on a Tuesday morning, and I unfolded it on the counter between us, one fold at a time, the way you unfold a thing that has been waxed against the wet and folded by a hand that meant to fold it again.

I had thought I knew what I was holding. I knew the weight. I knew the wax. I did not know the *thing.*

It was not large. A little bigger than a dinner plate opened out, on a heavy laid paper gone the brown of weak tea itself, the same color as his cardigan, which I noticed and dismissed. A coastline I half-knew. A delta. A great rectangle of a city laid out in a grid, with a long building marked along the harbor in a careful inked hand, and around the long building a wall of tiny script I could not read at arm's length, Greek, and Latin, and something that was neither. And below the city, in the margin, a column of figures, and beside the figures, in a different ink, a different hand, dry and forward-leaning, a single line.

I read the city's name. I read it twice. I am ashamed of what I felt, which was not wonder. It was the specific cold drop of a person who has spent her life around forgeries recognizing the architecture of a very good one.

"It says Alexandria," I said.

"It does."

"And that's the harbor. And that's—" I stopped my finger above the long building, did not touch it, the conservator's instinct, never touch a thing you haven't decided about. "That's meant to be the Library."

"That's the legend that came with it." He had put the loupe back in. He was looking at the map now properly, professionally, and I'll say this for him, the contempt went out of his face when he worked. "The Royal Library of Alexandria. Map to it. The lost one, the burned one, the one everybody's grandfather had a treasure map to. I've had four of those through here in thirty years and three were rubbish."

"And this one."

"This one's not rubbish." He said it like an insult. "That's the trouble with it."

I waited. He let me wait. He moved a button.

"You'll have noticed," he said, "if you know anything, which you might, you've got the hands of someone who knows something, you'll have noticed it's accurate."

"It's a forgery of a thing that doesn't exist anymore. Accurate to what?"

"To its period." He set the tweezers down. "That's the whole thing, that's the only thing. Listen. Anybody can fake a map *of* somewhere. Costs you a tea bag and a candle and an afternoon. I get them by the dozen. Treasure island, X marks the spot, edges all burnt artistic. And every one of them's wrong in the same way, which is that they're accurate to *now.* They draw the coast off a modern atlas. They put the Library where the books say it was. They're maps of what we know." He tapped the laid paper, not the inked surface, the edge, a man who also knew not to touch. "This one's wrong. The coastline's wrong. The delta's wrong. There's a spit of land here that silted up and isn't there anymore, hasn't been for fifteen hundred years, and a channel here that's been dry since before your grandfather's grandfather. It's wrong in exactly the ways the place *was,* and right in exactly the ways the place *is not.*" He looked at me, the one ordinary eye. "You don't fake that. You can't. You'd have to know what's gone. Nobody fakes a thing being *gone.* They fake it being there. This is drawn by somebody who saw a coastline that nobody alive has seen, and got it right, including the parts that disappointed him."

I have given that speech, in essence, a hundred times. I have given it to students and donors and a magistrate. *Value is what people will pay. Authenticity is what the object can't help being.* I trust the second over the first the way I trust a floor over a ladder. And here it was, coming back across a counter at me in a shop with no name, out of a man sorting buttons, and I had the unpleasant sensation of meeting my own creed wearing a stranger's cardigan.

"So it's old," I said.

"Didn't say that." He picked the loupe out again, turned it in his fingers. "Paper's good. Wax is good. But the column of numbers down the side's in fresh ink, modern, and that line of writing's modern, that forward-leaning hand. So either it's an old map somebody's been scribbling on this century, or it's a clever modern hand that knew how to draw a coast that's been gone since Justinian, and I couldn't tell you which, and I priced it like I couldn't tell you which, which is to say cheap, which is why it sold."

"To my father."

"Among others." He set the loupe down. "It's been through here twice."

The shop was very quiet. Somewhere behind a curtain a clock with a tired escapement was losing time audibly. I had the map flat under both my hands now and I had not decided to put them there.

"Twice," I said.

"Came in with an estate. Big lot, whole life, the way they come. Man up the coast, never married, no family, the council cleared the house and the contents came to me because nobody else would take the books, there were a lot of books, foreign, water-damaged, the kind nobody wants and you can't burn in front of people." He said *you can't burn in front of people* and I added a third thing to the small column of things I trusted about him. "Treasure hunter, he'd called himself, in the obituary the local paper ran, which I only read because I'd bought his sofa. *Amateur antiquarian and adventurer.* He'd vanished. That was the story. Walked out one autumn forty-odd years ago and never came back, and they waited the seven years and declared him gone, and the house sat, and then it came to me."

"What was his name?"

He thought about whether to tell me. I watched him decide it cost him nothing. "Fenn," he said. "August Fenn. Wrote it on everything. Front of every book, *Ex libris August Fenn,* like a man who expected to be a collection one day." A short, dry sound that was not quite a laugh. "And he wasn't. He was a house lot. We all are."

*August Fenn.* I will not pretend I felt the floor move. I felt the way you feel when a name you've never heard arranges itself in your mouth like it was always going to be there, which is a thing I have since learned not to trust and at the time did not know enough to distrust. I looked back down at the map, at the column of figures, ten of them, that I had taken on the first night for a phone number and then a grid reference and then nothing.

"These numbers," I said. "Whose hand?"

"His. Same as the line." He leaned over, not to read it, he'd read it, but to look at me reading it. "Coordinates of some sort. I had a fellow in who does the militaria, knows his way around a map, and he said they're not any grid he uses, not Ordnance, not the marine ones, the spacing's off. Could be made up. Half of what Fenn wrote was made up. The man believed in the lost Library the way some men believe in a horse."

I read the line beside the numbers. The forward-leaning hand. I had held it to the window two nights ago in my father's dark study and read it as *a small notation* and filed it and not let myself have it, the way I had not let myself have the smell of cold water under the wax. Now it was eleven in the morning and a clock was losing time behind a curtain and I read it properly, out loud, before I could decide not to.

"*The map is honest. The door is not.*"

The shopkeeper moved a button.

I waited for him to say something. He did not. He had heard it before, of course; he'd catalogued the lot; he'd read every flyleaf and probably this line a dozen times, and to him it was a crank's marginalia, one more thing the lost-Library men wrote, no different from *Ex libris,* no different from a horse. He found nothing in it. That was the worst of it, that is the thing I have come back to in the dark more times than I can tell you: that the sentence which would, in the end, take everything I had not yet been given was, to the only other person in the room, the verbal equivalent of a button not worth its price. He had a tray of dead men's words and this was one of them. He sorted it the way he sorted the rest. Into the smaller compartment, the one for things that wouldn't sell.

"There's notes," he said, after a while, because I had not moved.

"What?"

"With it. There's a bundle. Came with the map, his hand, the same forward-leaning hand. I kept them with it because they're no good apart, the map without the notes is just a pretty fake and the notes without the map are a madman's diary, but together they're a *story,* and a story you can sell." He said *a story you can sell* without warmth and without apology, a man telling you the trade plainly, and got down off his stool with the small grunt of a man whose knees have opinions, and went behind the curtain, and the clock got louder for a moment with the curtain open, and came back, and the clock got quieter, and he set a tied bundle of papers on the counter beside the map. String, not new. Folded sheets, foxed at the edges, and the corner of a photograph showing at one fold, gray, a man at the edge of something, and a hand in the corner of the frame that I made myself not look at yet.

He let me look at the bundle without untying it, which was its own small mercy, and I looked, and I did the thing my hands do, which is read an object before the eyes are allowed an opinion. Good rag paper, the same stock as the map, which meant the same source, which meant a man who bought his paper in quantity and meant to fill it. The string was waxed linen, the kind a sailmaker uses, knotted in a way I didn't know, snug, deliberate, tied by someone who tied things for keeps. The top sheet, where it showed through, was ruled in his own hand, his own faint pencil rules, because he had wanted lines and not had paper with lines and so had ruled his own, every sheet, I would learn, ruled by hand before he wrote a word on it, which is the most patient form of madness I have ever held, and I have held a great deal of it, it is most of what survives.

"You can read them here," he said. "You can't take them out the shop unless you're buying."

"How much."

I heard myself say it. I want to be precise, because precision is the only honest thing I brought into that shop. I had come to ask a question. I had told him twice I was not selling and I had meant a thing slightly different, I had meant *I am not here to give you my father's grief back for money,* and that was still true, and underneath it, the whole time, in the cold sober part of me that does the arithmetic, I had decided to buy it. I had decided on the train. I had decided in the study, on the floor, with my father's stuck drawer at my back and the click in the dark. *Finish something of his.* You cannot finish a thing you do not own.

He named a number.

It was not a small number. It was not a Library-of-Alexandria number either, he wasn't a fool and he wasn't a fantasist, he had priced it exactly as he'd said, like a man who couldn't tell whether it was old or only clever, which is to say he had priced the *story.* But it was more than I had, in the practical sense that I had a flat I rented and a salary that conserved other people's treasures and none of my own and an overdraft I tended the way my father had tended his starter, faithfully, hopelessly, flour and water, week after week.

"That's steep," I said, "for a clever fake."

"It's cheap," he said, "for a thing that's too accurate to be one. I told you. That's the trouble with it. I can't price what I can't dismiss." He folded his arms. "You want it. I've been doing this thirty years. I knew you wanted it when you wouldn't touch it. People who don't want a thing pick it up. People who want it keep their hands flat on the glass so it can't get away."

I looked down. My hands were flat on the map.

I took them off. Too late, obviously, and he had the grace not to smile, which I also added to the column.

"Knock something off," I said, "for the notes being incomplete."

"They're not incomplete. They're complete and they're mad. That's worth more, not less." But he tilted his head, and I understood we were doing the thing now, the only honest transaction grief permits, two people pretending an object is about money so that neither has to say what it's really about. "I'll take the photograph out of it," he said. "Keep the photograph. Sell you the map and the notes. The photograph's the best thing in the lot, it's the only picture of him, it's the only thing in here that's worth what it costs."

"No."

It came out faster and flatter than I meant. He looked at me.

"The photograph stays in," I said. "It's no good apart. You said so. The map without the notes is a pretty fake and the notes without the map are a madman's diary and the man without his face is—" I did not finish it. I did not have the end of it. I had walked into the shop with a satchel full of a dead man's intentions and I was, I realized, with my hands now in my lap where they could not betray me, about to buy a second dead man to go with the first, a stranger, a self-mythologizing crank who signed his books like he expected to be remembered and was, instead, a sofa and a house lot and a line about a door, and I wanted his face in it. I wanted to know what a man looked like who could draw a coastline that had been gone since Justinian and get the disappointing parts right.

The shopkeeper unfolded his arms.

"All right," he said. He named a second number, lower than the first, higher than the knock-off-for-the-notes I'd been angling for, the number a man lands on when he's decided he likes you slightly and dislikes that he does. "Map, notes, photograph. One lot. The way it came in, the way it goes out. I don't break sets. Bad luck, breaking sets." Then, because he was honest, and I have thought about this too: "That's not superstition. That's just true. You break a set and the parts are worth nothing and you've ruined the only thing they ever had, which was being whole." He shrugged. "Goes for most things."

"I can't quite afford that," I said.

It was the truest sentence I'd spoken since the brass bell. I had a number in my head, the real one, the overdraft, the rent, the cost of being a person who cleans treasures and owns a kettle, and the second number he'd named was over the edge of it, not by a fortune, by exactly the amount that turns a purchase into a decision.

"I know," he said, not unkindly. "You've got the face of someone counting." He pushed the map an inch toward me, and the notes, the photograph showing at the fold. "Most people, that's where they stop. They count, and the counting wins, and they go home, and they feel virtuous for a week and bereft for a year, because the thing they didn't buy is the thing the grief was hiding inside, and they'll never see it again, because I'll have sold it to somebody who didn't count." He put the loupe back in his eye, the magnified one swimming up at me, done with the conversation, done with me, returning to his buttons, leaving the decision exactly where it belonged, on my side of the glass. "I'm not telling you to buy it. I'm a shop. I'm telling you what I've watched a thousand times. The counting's not about the money. You know that. You're not stupid, you've got the hands. The counting's about whether you're allowed."

Allowed.

I sat with that, on a stool that was probably also for sale, in a shop that bought things off the recently bereaved, and the clock behind the curtain lost a little more time, and I did the thing I do, I did the sunk-cost arithmetic that has run my life, the same equation that kept me kneeling at a drawer for an hour, that kept me sorting a dead man's offprints into the wrong-colored folders, that has never once, in forty years, let me leave a thing unfinished except the one thing, which is my own life. *He started the list. One item. He clipped it to a corner of graph paper and wrote START HERE and died on the Tuesday. If I leave the shop without it, the list is unstarted forever, the looking is over, the thing he found after forty years of being proudest of finding nothing goes back into a tray of words not worth their price, and I will have let the death be for nothing.* That is how the distortion works, in case anyone has sold you the idea that grief makes you wise. It makes you a worse accountant. It tells you that spending money you don't have on a dead crank's mad map is, in fact, the responsible thing, the only honorable close-out, and it tells you in your father's voice, which is unfair, which has always been unfair.

And under all of it, where I could not see it then and can see nothing else now, was the real transaction, the one neither of us named, the one the man in the cardigan had named anyway in his shop-worn way: I was not buying a map.

I was buying out.

I had a basement to go back to, and solvents, and the long quiet company of the dead who stay where you leave them, and a rented flat, and a starter in a fridge four hours north that I had put in the cold rather than decide about, and not one single thing in my whole careful conserved life that I had ever started myself. And here on a counter was a way out of all of it dressed up as a duty, a door dressed up as a list, an escape I could call an inheritance, and the only price was a number slightly over the edge of what I had, which is, I have since learned, the price of every door worth the name.

I paid it.

I want to be honest about how I paid it, because it is the part I am least proud of and the part that is most true. I did not pay it grandly. I did not feel the rest of my life turn on its hinge. I wrote a cheque, on a chequebook with a picture of a heron on it that came free with the account, and my hand did not shake, my hand never shakes over a thing I have decided about, and the shopkeeper watched me write the slightly-too-large number with the particular sorrow of a man who makes his living this way and has never once enjoyed the moment the counting loses, and he blotted the cheque and put it in a drawer that opened, smoothly, on the first pull, no resistance at all, and I noticed that the drawer opened and hated that I noticed.

"There," he said. "It's yours now. The whole set. Mind you keep it whole." He began to tie the notes back to the map with the old string, and stopped, and looked at me over the loupe. "You'll read the notes," he said. It was not a question.

"Yes."

"They're mad," he said again, but differently this time, gentler, a man handing over a thing he'd lived next to for years and was, now that it was leaving, allowing himself to have felt something about. "He thought he found it. Fenn. Not the Library. He thought the Library was the cover story, the thing you tell the council and the local paper, the horse you let people see you riding. He thought what he found was a *door.* Wrote it down a hundred ways, numbered, the man numbered everything, even his regrets, you'll see, he's got a list of regrets in there with numbers on, and the last one's not finished." He pushed the bundle the final inch across the glass, into my hands, set, whole, unbroken. "I'll tell you the one thing, and then you can go count something else. I read the lot, when it came in, the way you do. And I didn't believe a word of it. Mad as a barrel of frogs, the man, vanished up a coast chasing a door that isn't there." He took the loupe out of his eye, finally, both eyes ordinary now, both tired, both fixed on me. "But the map's too accurate to be fake. I keep coming back to it. That's the trouble with the whole lot. Everything the man wrote is mad, and the one thing in the box you can actually test is *true.*"

The bell over the door said nothing, because nobody came in, because it was a Tuesday in a shop with no name and the world was going about its business of not knowing anything had happened.

I held the set whole in both hands, the map and the mad notes and the photograph I had not yet let myself look at, and I felt the faint thing under the wax that I had filed two nights ago under *storage conditions,* the smell of a cellar shut a long time, cold stone and the green breath of water moving somewhere it shouldn't, and the shopkeeper did not smell it, I could see he did not smell it, and I did not mention it, and I have wondered since whether the not-mentioning was the first lie I told in service of the door or only the last lie I told in service of the woman I had been, who cleaned what was dead and went home.

"Mind the step," he said, returning to his buttons, "going out. It's loose."

I minded the step. I went out under the brass bell into a gray ordinary street, with a satchel that now held a dead man's intentions and a stranger's door, and I did not feel chosen, and I did not feel doomed, and nothing glowed. I felt, if you want the truth, slightly sick about the money, the way you do, the cheque-with-a-heron clearing in some clean clearing-house while I stood on a wet pavement and counted what I had left, which was less than I'd had at eleven and more than I would ever have again, though I did not know that, there was no reason I should have known that.

I found a bench in a square with a war memorial on it and pigeons that did not care, and I sat, and I did not go home. There was a train at four and a flat at the end of it and a kettle and a job that resumed on Monday and would not notice I had spent money I didn't have on a dead crank's door, and all of that was waiting and would keep, the way the dead keep, the way I had built a life on the dead keeping. I undid the waxed string with hands that had handled fourteen-hundred-year-old hinges and did not shake, and I worked the strange snug knot loose without cutting it, because you do not cut a thing a careful man tied, you learn it, and I let the photograph fall open at last into the flat gray light, and I looked at the face of August Fenn at the edge of something, decades dead, and the cold went all the way down, because the line I would not be able to unsee, the line I have seen every day since, was not the line about the door.

It was that he was looking at the camera, and he was exactly my age.
