# Pompeii (working title) — Story Bible

> Source: author's spoken idea, captured 2026-06-21. This is the canonical seed
> for the genesis run. Author canon wins over the pipeline. Comp/tone target:
> **Outlander** — sweeping time-slip historical romance, long books / series,
> historically grounded "to a certain point" (real period detail, fictional cast).

## Logline
A modern woman who inherited her late father's (or grandfather's) love of the
ancient world buys a centuries-old map in an antique shop — a map said to lead to
a lost city / the Library of Alexandria — and following its coordinates opens a
door into the Roman past, where she falls in love, builds a family, and must
ultimately choose between them and her own survival as Vesuvius erupts over
Pompeii.

## Protagonist
- A modern woman (name TBD). Her father — possibly grandfather — was an
  archaeologist; his passion for "ancient stuff" became hers after he passed.
- Loves antique stores; that's how the inciting object finds her.
- Core engine: grief/legacy of the father, curiosity, and a longing for the past
  she studies — which she literally falls into.

## The Map (inciting object)
- Found in an antique store. Purported to be a real map to a lost city /
  the Library of Alexandria (author unsure which — TO DECIDE).
- The shopkeeper knows little: he bought it at an **estate sale** belonging to a
  **self-proclaimed treasure hunter who later disappeared.**
- The treasure hunter left **notes and coordinates** marked on the map.
- The shopkeeper bought it only because it was **accurate for its time period** —
  a detail that quietly confirms it's the real thing.
- She buys it, follows it to find the site, and instead **opens a door into the
  past.**

## The Time Door (mechanism)
- Following the map's coordinates opens a doorway through time.
- She arrives in the ancient world — first in **Greece** (Delphi).
- **The Oracle of Delphi** tells her WHERE the door back is — but NOT WHEN it will
  open. She learns the "when" later, by some other means (TO DECIDE).
- The door back is located in **Pompeii** — and this is **before** the eruption.
- There is clearly **more going on** behind the map, the treasure hunter's
  disappearance, and the door (a series-long mystery — TO DEVELOP). The vanished
  treasure hunter may have gone through too.

## The Love Story
- She meets an **ex-gladiator** who had been **accused of an affair with the
  emperor's wife** (the accusation forced him out / nearly killed him — echoes of
  injustice and exile).
- They fall in love over time. She **marries and has children** — a real family,
  built across years in the past.

## The Central Dilemma (climax)
- When she finally tries to leave, she learns her **family cannot come with her**
  to the future.
- The choice: **stay in the past with the people she loves, or leave them to save
  herself** — as **Vesuvius erupts** and Pompeii is destroyed around them.
- The eruption is the literal and emotional ticking clock of the climax.

## Tone / Scope
- **Outlander-style:** long, immersive, romantic, historically textured. Likely a
  **series**, or at minimum a very long single volume.
- Historically accurate "to a certain point" — real period detail and real
  political backdrop, fictional protagonists woven through.

## HISTORICAL RECONCILIATION (must resolve before drafting)
- **Vesuvius destroyed Pompeii in 79 AD.** Emperor at the eruption: **TITUS**
  (r. 79–81). His father **VESPASIAN** reigned 69–79.
- **Nero died 68 AD** — too early for the eruption. So the "emperor whose wife the
  gladiator was accused with" should most likely be tied to the **Flavian** era
  (Vespasian/Titus) OR the backstory is set under Nero and she arrives in the late
  60s, with the romance/family spanning ~10+ years to 79 AD.
- The **Oracle of Delphi** was real and still operating in the 1st century AD
  (though in decline). Workable.
- The **Library of Alexandria** was damaged across centuries; "the lost
  library/city" works better as legend/MacGuffin than literal standing building by
  this era — fine for a map myth.
