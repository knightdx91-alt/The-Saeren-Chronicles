THE SAEREN CHRONICLES

What Needs To Be Done — Master Roadmap

==============================================

This document is your working checklist. It covers everything that needs to happen before the Saeren Chronicles trilogy is ready for submission. Work through it in order. Do not try to do everything at once.

==============================================
BEFORE YOU WRITE ANYTHING NEW
==============================================

These are the foundational tasks. Do these first.

1. Create a character sheet document for every named character.
   For each one write down: physical description, age, core personality traits, what they want, what they are afraid of, and their relationship to Viridia. This prevents drift and inconsistency across sessions.

2. Create a magic system reference document.
   Write down every rule of the magic system as it currently stands. The Moravian language, how foci work, what both-sided magic can do that one-sided cannot, the rules of bonding, what a void core is. If it is a rule, write it down. You will thank yourself later.

3. Decide Alice's fate.
   This is the single most important decision you need to make before revising book one. Does Alice survive the school attack? If yes, how and where does she go? If no, when and how does Viridia find out? This decision affects chapters 16, 17, and 18 of book one and the entire opening of book two. You cannot revise properly without knowing the answer.

4. Decide what Viridia's parents actually died from.
   The letter from her mother implies she knew she might die. This was not an accident. What happened? Who was responsible? Does it connect to the larger political story? This mystery needs an answer even if you do not reveal it until book two or three.

5. Decide who wrote the note with the focus.
   Someone knew Viridia would be going to Hazel Academy and packed her focus with a note in an unfamiliar hand. Who was that person? Why did they know? This is a loose thread that needs to be tied.

==============================================
GENERAL WRITING ADVICE FOR THIS PROJECT
==============================================

On Description:
When a new character appears for the first time give the reader three physical details. Not a list — three things the point of view character would actually notice given her emotional state in that moment. Do the same for every new location. Three grounding details then move on.

On Chapters:
A chapter ends when one of three things happens. It answers a question the reader has been holding but immediately opens a new one. It changes something in a way that cannot be undone. It creates a specific image or feeling the reader needs to sit with. If you finish a scene and think this is where the chapter ends, ask yourself: what does the reader still want to find out? If the answer is nothing, the chapter has gone on too long.

On Drift:
At the start of every new writing session paste in your character sheets, your magic system rules, and a one paragraph summary of where the story currently stands. This is the single most effective thing you can do to maintain consistency across sessions.

On Viridia's Voice:
She is fourteen, grieving, highly intelligent, and morally serious. She does not show off. She notices things other people miss. She reaches conclusions quickly and keeps them to herself until she is sure. Her humor is dry and appears rarely. When it does appear it should feel like a gift. Do not let her become wise beyond her years — let her be exactly as young as she is, carrying exactly as much as she is carrying.

On Lor-ar:
He is three hundred years old and he finds humans both fascinating and slightly exhausting. His affection for Viridia is real but he expresses it through honesty not warmth. Reduce little one slightly in the later chapters — it loses impact through repetition. Save it for moments when it genuinely means something.

On the Magic System:
Every time you introduce a new magical ability or limitation write it down in your magic system reference document immediately. Consistency is what makes a magic system feel real. One contradiction can break a reader's trust in the entire world.
