# Chapter 1: The Box of Invisible Air

On her fourth morning in the camp Viridia learned to carry water, which was not a thing she had expected the end of the world to require of her, and which she found she was glad of, because a body carrying water cannot also lie awake counting the people it failed to save.

The well stood at the center of the lanes, a real well with a real iron handle, and the line for it formed before the light was properly up. She took her place behind a boy of perhaps ten who held two buckets bigger than he was and did not complain about it, and she watched the way the others worked the handle, three full turns and a pause to let the bucket settle, before it was her turn, so that when she reached the front no one would have a reason to look at her twice. She had been four days in the resistance camp and she had already understood its first rule, which was the same rule she had carried into Hazel and out of it again: be useful, be quiet, and give no one a reason to look at you twice.

The water came up cold and smelling of stone. She filled the boy's two buckets first because he had been ahead of her, and then her own, and carried them back along the lane to the long low house where the Hazel girls had been put, eight of them and Amber, in a room meant for half that number, and not one of them had said a word of complaint either. They had learned the same rule. They had learned it in a burning corridor.

"You don't have to do that," said a voice behind her. "We have people whose whole job is the water."

The man was broad and sunburned even this early in the year, with forearms that looked as though they had been built for the specific purpose of moving things that did not want to be moved, and Viridia placed him before he finished the sentence, because she had watched him for four days without his knowing it. Brutus. He had built half the houses in the camp with those arms, and he was the sort of man who could not pass a sagging fence without stopping to set it right, and he was standing in the lane now with a hammer hanging from one fist, on his way to set something else right, and he had stopped because she was doing work that was not hers.

"I know," she said.

"But you're doing it anyway."

"I sleep better when I'm tired."

Brutus looked at her for a moment, and she let him, keeping her face the careful face, the one that gave back nothing but a polite and slightly tired girl. Whatever he had been about to say, he decided against it. "Suit yourself," he said. "The handle sticks on the third turn. Don't force it or you'll snap the pin, and then we're all hauling from the stream, which is twenty minutes each way and downhill the wrong direction." He hefted the hammer. "There's a knack. Three turns, then you lift, you don't pull. Lifting's kinder to the pin."

"Three turns, then lift," she said. "Thank you."

He grunted, which she was learning was the camp's universal currency, a sound that could mean anything from good morning to I forgive you, and went off down the lane to wherever the morning's repairs were waiting. She stood a moment with the buckets pulling at both arms and watched him go, and filed him away under the heading she kept for people who could not stop themselves from mending things, which was a short list, and which her father had been on.

She did not let herself stand there long.

The camp in daylight was not the desperate huddle some part of her had still half expected, even after four days of evidence to the contrary. It was a village. It had lanes and a well and washing on lines and a smith's shed with the fire already going, and it had children, which was the thing she could not get used to, dark-mage children playing in the open in the one place in the world that would have them. The whole of it sat inside a box of invisible air. She could see the box if she let her mana sight settle, a faint shimmer at the edges of the camp where the great ward curved up and over like the inside of a bowl, holding the village in and the world's eyes out, and she had spent a foolish hour her second night simply looking at it, because it was the largest single piece of working magic she had ever seen that was not the source itself, and because three living people were holding it up between them without rest, and that was a thing worth understanding.

She delivered the water. Amber was awake, sitting up against the wall with her knees drawn to her chest in the grey light, and she had a little more color in her than she'd had the day before, which Viridia counted as the morning's first good thing.

"You've been carrying water," Amber said. It was not a question. "You have the face of someone who's been carrying water. Virtuous. Faintly smug about it."

"There was a line. I joined the line."

"Of course you did." Amber unfolded herself with the slow care of someone whose body was still relearning that it was allowed to move, because the drain that had nearly killed her crossing the portal had not entirely let go of her yet, and would not for a while. "You know they have people for that."

"Brutus told me."

"Brutus." Amber found something almost like her old dry note. "Built the place with his bare hands and now patrols it looking for things to fix. If he ever runs out he'll fall over dead on the spot. Some men are held up entirely by their to-do lists." She took the cup Viridia handed her, and held it, and did not drink, which was its own kind of progress, because two days ago she had not been able to hold a cup at all. "Sit down a minute. You're making me tired, and I'm the one who's supposed to be recovering."

Viridia sat. Not for long. She had learned that too, in four days: that the camp ran on small tasks and that the surest way to be left alone was to always be visibly on her way to the next one, and that Amber knew exactly what she was doing and let her do it, because Amber understood the particular use a person could make of being busy.

Through the open door the camp was waking properly now. Somewhere a baby was crying and being hushed. Closer, at a long table set out in the open beside the next house down, a woman had gathered four or five small children onto benches and was teaching them their letters from slates, and her voice carried thin and patient on the cold air, and Viridia found, as she had found every morning since the first, that she could not look away from it. The woman's name was Mirelle. Viridia had learned it without meaning to, the way she learned everything, by listening. Mirelle had a baby of her own on her hip even while she taught, a fat solemn infant who watched the lesson as though it understood every word, and she had a way of waiting, when a child got a character wrong, of simply waiting, without correcting, until the child found it themselves, and the children loved her for it in the unguarded way children love a person who does not hurry them.

The letters on the slates were not the letters Hazel had taught. They were older, rounder, and at least one of them, Viridia had seen on the first morning with a small cold jolt, was a Moravian character, the language the light schools swore was dead, being passed to six-year-olds over their breakfast as a matter of course. A whole way of being, hidden under the curve of the ward, going on. It had steadied something in her, that first morning, to see it. It steadied her again now.

"She lost a husband two winters back," Amber said, following her gaze. "A raid. He held a road so a wagon of them could get clear, and the road got held, and he didn't come back from it. She had the baby four months later." She turned the cup in her hands. "I had it off one of the third-years. They've been here longer than us, they hear things. Everyone in this camp came in off a road that took something from them on the way. It's the entry fee."

"I know the fee," Viridia said.

"I know you do." Amber's voice gentled, which from Amber was rarer and worth more than warmth from anyone else. "I only mean you don't have to learn all their names at once. You do this. You walk into a place and you take its whole inventory in three days because knowing where everything is makes you feel like you can't be ambushed by it. But you can know too much too fast. It's a lot of grief to carry, other people's, on top of your own."

Viridia did not answer that, because there was no answer to it that was not either a lie or more than she could say before noon. She had taken the camp's inventory. She knew that Brutus mended things and that Mirelle waited for children to find their own letters and that there was a baby in the next house that cried at dawn and a man at the smithy whose left leg did not bend. She knew the count of the wells and the lanes and the children. And she knew the thing under all of it, the thing Amber was circling, which was that she had also been counting something else these four days, without deciding to, the way she could not help counting: she had been counting how thin the walls of the box really were.

She got up. "I'm going to go and be useful before you talk me into feeling things," she said, and Amber laughed, a real one, short and surprised, the laugh of someone who did not give them away cheaply, and for a moment it was so exactly her mother's laugh that Viridia had to turn for the door before her face did something she had not given it permission to do.

She found work at the smithy, holding stock while the smith with the stiff leg shaped a hinge, and then at the timber-pile splitting kindling under a woman's brisk instruction, and by the time the sun had cleared the trees she had made herself part of the camp's furniture, a quiet girl always carrying something, and she had used the freedom that bought her to walk the whole circuit of the ward once more and look at it properly.

It was failing. Not today, and not tomorrow, but it was failing, and she did not think anyone else could see it, because no one else could see what she could see.

The ward was anchored at three points, three posts driven into the ground at the wide corners of the camp, and at each post a thread of power ran up and into the curve of the bowl, and she could follow each thread back along the lanes to its source, which was a living person, because a ward this large could not be hung on an object and left, it had to be carried, the way you carry a held breath, by someone who does not get to put it down. Jazen anchored one. She had felt the size of him the moment she'd entered the camp, the most powerful dark mage alive, and the post nearest his house burned bright and steady. The second belonged to a lean grey man named Abe who she had seen only at a distance, and it too held.

The third was thinning.

It belonged to a man called Hiram, and she had found him on her second day by following the thread to its root the way she could not stop herself from doing, and Hiram was tired. Not the ordinary tired of a man who has slept badly. The deep tired of a person spending themselves a little faster than they can refill, day after day after day, the way a lamp burns the last of its oil with the wick turned up because there is nothing to be done but burn it. His thread did not burn steady. It guttered, very slightly, the way a flame guttered in a draft, and recovered, and guttered again, and she had watched it do that for the space of a hundred breaths on her second day and understood that the whole sheltering curve that held this village in safety was held up, at one of its three corners, by a man who was running out.

She could mend it. The thought had come to her on the second day and she had put it away on the second day and it came back to her now, standing in the lane with a length of cut wood under her arm, and she put it away again, more firmly. She could mend it. She could lend Hiram's thread the smallest fraction of what she carried banked behind her ribs and it would burn steady for a year. She could do it without anyone seeing, in the dark, the way she did everything.

And the moment she did, three men who had between them kept this camp invisible for years would feel a fourth hand on the work, and they would know there was something in their midst that could hold a ward of this size with a fraction of itself and not feel the weight, and then she would be seen, fully, for what she was, by the only people left in the world who had taken her in.

Be careful who you let see you do it.

She shifted the wood to her other arm and went to find a use for it.

***

She had hoped, in the small part of her that still hoped things, that no one in the camp would ask her what she was. For four days no one had. And then, in the middle of the afternoon, Jazen did, in his way, which was not to ask at all.

He found her at the wood-store, or let her find him, which she suspected was the truth of it, because a man who ran a camp this size by knowing things did not stumble across people by accident. He was leaning in the doorway when she came round the stack with an armful, lean and lank as she remembered, close-cropped dark hair and a dark blue shirt and the kerchief at his throat, and his eyes had the particular redness of a man who has been doing his grieving openly and is not ashamed of it, which she had marked the first night as the thing that made him most unlike her, because he let it show, and she did not.

"They tell me you've carried water, hauled stock, stacked the woodpile twice, and reorganized the medicine shelf in the long house," he said. "In four days. Without being asked."

"There was work."

"There's always work. Most people manage to walk past most of it." He pushed off the doorframe and fell into step beside her, unhurried, and she let him, because refusing would have been its own kind of confession. "My mother used to say you could learn everything you needed to know about a person in the first week, if you watched what they did with their hands when no one had given them a job. Idle hands tell on you. Yours don't idle." He glanced at her sideways. "It's the most useful kind of person to have in a camp like this. It's also exactly the kind of person who's hiding something, in my experience, because nothing keeps the hands busy like a head that doesn't dare go quiet."

Viridia set her armful down on the stack, squared it, and said nothing, which she had learned long ago was a complete sentence if you let it stand.

Jazen let it stand. He did not push. That was the thing she had not expected of him and was still recalibrating around, the way he could set a thing on the table between them and then simply leave it there, unforced, the way she set things on tables.

"I'm not going to ask you what you are," he said, exactly as he had said it at the edge of the camp on the first morning, before the council, before any of this. "You've earned the right to tell me or not. But I'll tell you what I see, and you can do with it what you like." He stopped, and looked out over the lanes, over the children and the washing and the thin curve of the ward. "I see a girl who walked into my camp with a dead man's blood on her arms and eight children behind her, and who has spent every hour since making herself small and useful, and who looks at my wards the way a healer looks at a wound." He turned to her. "You've been watching them. The wards. I've felt you at the edges. Don't trouble denying it, I'd rather you didn't, it's the one thing in here I can feel as well as you can see. What do you make of them?"

It was a test, and they both knew it was a test, and she stood in the afternoon light and weighed the two answers she could give. She could give him the small one, the safe one, the one that gave nothing away. Or she could give him a true thing, a small one, offered as a gift, the way she had learned a true thing offered plainly could buy more trust than any amount of careful silence, and could also cost her everything, and the trick her whole life had been knowing which.

"Hiram's tired," she said.

Jazen went very still.

"The corner nearest the stream," she said, keeping her voice even, keeping it the flat report of a girl who had simply noticed a thing, because the moment she let it be more than that she would be telling him how she knew. "His anchor doesn't hold as steady as the other two. It catches, then recovers, then catches. It's been doing it worse the last two days than the first two. I don't think he's sleeping. I think he's spending more than he's getting back, and I don't think he'll tell you, because he knows there's no one to spell him."

The afternoon noise of the camp went on around them, the smith's hammer, a child's shriek of laughter, the baby in the next house, and Jazen stood and looked at her and did the thing his mother had done, the recalculating thing, the slow re-weighing of a person who has just turned out to be heavier than the scale was set for.

"No one feels that but me," he said quietly. "I anchor one of the three. I can feel the other two through the work, the way you feel the other people pulling a rope. I've felt Hiram catching for a week and I haven't slept right for worrying it, and I haven't told a soul, because you're right, there's no one to spell him, and a fear you can't fix is a fear you keep to yourself." He studied her. "And you saw it in four days. From the outside. With your eyes."

She said nothing. There was nothing safe to say. The true thing was already on the table.

"I won't ask," Jazen said again, but softer now, and there was something in it that was almost gratitude and almost grief. "But I'll tell you what my mother would have told you, because I think she kept you for something, and I think the something is exactly the sort that can look at a ward and see the man underneath it failing. Whatever you are, you're not safe here, not because we'd ever hurt you, but because the capital is going to come for this camp the way it came for the school, and when it does, the difference between the people who walk out and the people who don't is going to be the people who saw it coming, and you see it. I think you can't help seeing it." He stepped back. "Keep watching the wards. Tell me what you see. That's not a small thing to ask and it's not a small thing to offer, so I'll say thank you for it the way I learned from you. Plainly." He almost smiled. "There's a council in two days. Patrols have been close. We'll talk about the road then. Until then, carry your water, if it helps you sleep. But Viridia." He paused. "When you decide what you are. There's a place for it here. I told you that. I meant the whole of it."

He left her among the stacked timber, and went off down the lane toward his house and the bright steady burn of his anchor, and she stood a while with her hands finally idle, and let them be idle, since there was no longer any point pretending to him that her stillness meant nothing.

The light was going amber over the trees when the supply line came in.

She heard it before she understood what she was hearing, a change in the texture of the camp, a current running through the lanes, people setting down what they carried and turning toward the south edge where the ward thinned to a seam that a wagon could pass through if the right hands opened it. She went with the current, because everyone went, and because a part of her that she did not examine too closely had been waiting four days for exactly this without letting herself name what she waited for.

The wagon came through the seam slow, its wheels muffled, two tired horses and a driver grey with road-dust and three of the Hazel fourth-years walking alongside it with the loose, emptied gait of people who had not stopped moving in days. They had gone out for supplies, that much Viridia knew, salt and cloth and the small iron things a camp could not make for itself, and the wagon was loaded with sacks, and the camp surged gently around it, glad, because a supply line that came back was its own good news in a place where things that went out did not always return.

She fell in with the unloading without being asked, because hands were hands and the sacks were heavy and there were not enough people who could lift them, and because work at the tailgate put her close to the wagon without putting her in anyone's eye. She took a sack of salt from a boy who was struggling with it and swung it onto the stack the way she had learned to swing things that month, with the legs and not the back, and reached for the next, and a woman beside her said, "Bless you, the back of my knees has gone," and Viridia took the woman's load too and said nothing and kept moving, sack after sack, salt and cloth and a crate of nails that bit her palms through the slats, and the ordinary rhythm of it, lift and swing and reach, was almost enough to keep the watchful part of her quiet, the part that never quite stopped taking the world's measure even when she begged it to.

It was the smell that warned her first. Under the road-dust and the horse-sweat there was woodsmoke in the wagon's canvas, the deep sour reek of a large fire long burned out, the smell that had been in her own hair for a week after Hazel and that she would know now in the dark for the rest of her life. The wagon had been somewhere that had burned. She set down the crate of nails very carefully, so that no one would see her hands, and straightened, and that was when she understood that the fourth-years had brought more than salt.

They had brought more than salt. She saw it in their faces before the wagon stopped, the way she saw everything, by the detail that did not fit, and the detail that did not fit was that not one of the three would meet the eyes of the people crowding up to greet them. They had the look of people carrying news, and news in this camp was a thing carried carefully, because it was so rarely kind.

She did not push to the front. She held at the edge of the crowd, where she could see and not be seen, the old habit, and she watched one of the fourth-years, a tall girl named Della who Viridia knew from Hazel, draw Jazen aside near the wagon's tailgate and speak to him low and fast, and she watched Jazen's face as he listened, and that was how she learned that the news touched her before she had heard a word of it, because halfway through whatever Della was saying Jazen's eyes came up and found her across the whole width of the crowd, found her exactly, the way you look at the one person a thing is about.

The camp's gladness went on around her, the unloading, the children underfoot, but Viridia stood very still in the amber light and held Jazen's gaze across the crowd and felt the cold clear part of her come forward, the part that did not feel things until later, and she knew, before he had crossed a single step toward her, that the road that wagon had traveled had passed near Hazel, and that someone on it had heard something, and that it was a name.

It was always going to be a name.

She made her face the careful face, and her hands, at her sides, curled, and she made them uncurl, and she waited for him to reach her, because she had learned that you do not run toward the thing you cannot live without knowing, in case it is the other thing, the one you cannot live with.

Jazen came across the crowd toward her, and he was not hurrying, and he was not smiling, and his red-rimmed eyes did not look away from hers the whole way, and that, she thought, with the cold clear part, was either the kindest thing or the cruelest, and she was about to find out which.

She stood her ground and let him come, and around her the camp went on being glad about salt, and she thought, with the small steady voice that had not gone quiet since the gates of Hazel: whatever he is bringing me, I will not let them see me take it. Whatever it is. I will carry the water tomorrow either way.
