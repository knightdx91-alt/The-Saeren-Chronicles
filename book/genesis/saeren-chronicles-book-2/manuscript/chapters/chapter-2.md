# Chapter 2: The Thread Pulled Taut

Jazen took her a little apart from the wagon, to the lee of the long house where the unloading noise dropped away, and he did not waste her time with the shape of it, which she was grateful for, because she could not have borne to be approached gently. He had learned that about her already, in four days. He came at it the way she would have.

"There's a rumor," he said. "I want to give it to you as a rumor, not as more than it is, because I've watched men kill themselves on rumors dressed up as facts. Della had it third-hand off a carter on the north road, and the north road runs the long way past Hazel, so it's a road that would carry such a thing. Are you listening to me as a rumor?"

"Yes," Viridia said.

"After the school burned, the capital's soldiers didn't only kill." He watched her as he said it, not unkindly, the way you watch a person you are handing something heavy. "They took. Some of the students. Not the ones who fought. The ones who could be used. Healers, the carter said. First-generation, mostly, the ones with no family of standing to make a noise about it. They were put in wagons and taken toward the capital." He paused. "He said there was a girl among them. Heavyset, he said, which is a carter's word and not a kind one. Fair hair. He said she had a healer's hands, that she'd splinted a soldier's wrist on the road without being told to, because she couldn't help herself, and the officer had let her, because a healer who can't help herself is a useful thing to own."

Viridia did not move.

She had built her whole life around stillness, around the management of her own face, and she spent all of it now, every coin she had, holding her features in the careful arrangement of a girl receiving a rumor about a stranger, because if she let it be Alice, here, in the open, with the camp's eyes anywhere near her, she did not know what her face would do, and she had a rule about her face. So she held it. Only her breath changed, and breath could be managed, and she managed it.

"That's all of it," Jazen said. "A carter's word, third-hand, no name. It could be any fair-haired girl. There were a hundred girls at that school." He was being careful with her, she understood, offering her the doubt the way you offer a railing to someone on a height. "I wouldn't have told you at all, except that you asked me, that first morning past the last houses, for true news, alive or dead, and I promised you true news when there was any. This isn't news. It's the smell of news. But you asked, and I won't keep things from you and call it kindness. That's a thing people did to me, and I hated them for it."

"Thank you," Viridia said, and her voice came out flat, which was the closest thing to breaking she allowed herself, and Jazen heard it, because he had a sister, and he did not try to fill it.

"I've put the word out on every line we have," he said. "If she's alive anywhere our people can reach, we'll get a name to go with the hands. I can't promise you it'll be good. I promised you it'd be true, and I'll keep that one." He looked at her a moment longer. "A healer who can't help herself. Is that her?"

She thought of Alice in the medical wing, splinting and bandaging, told by the lead healer that she had calm hands, which was the highest praise that ward gave, and glowing under it for a week. She thought of Alice unable to walk past a hurt thing. She thought: yes. That is exactly her. That is so precisely her that I could weep, if I were a person who wept where anyone could see.

"It might be," she said. "It sounds like a great many people."

Jazen nodded slowly, and let her have the lie, because he understood the use of one.

***

The thing about a body holding still, she had learned, was that the stillness had to go somewhere, and so she did what she always did, which was to find something to carry. She went back to the wagon and unloaded salt until there was no salt left to unload, and then she split kindling at the woodpile until the woman who ran it told her, with rough kindness, to give the axe a rest before she took her own foot off, and then, with her hands at last empty and the light going down and nowhere left to put the stillness, she walked to the camp's southern seam, where the screen drew down low, and she stood and looked out at the darkening trees and let the thing she would not let her face do happen somewhere behind her eyes instead, privately, where it cost no one anything.

Alive. Alive was not safe. Alive was a wagon and a road and the capital at the end of it, the place that had burned the school and signed the decree, the one place in the world she could not simply walk into. Alive meant there was a person, a specific person with a tooth turned out of line and a laugh that ran on too long and a kindness she could not switch off, sitting in the dark somewhere tonight, owned. Alive was worse, in its way, than the other thing, because the other thing was finished and this was not, this was a door left open onto a drop, and she would not be able to close it or to turn away from it for as long as it stayed open.

She had wanted, more than anything, to know. She had asked for it. And now she knew a little, and the little was a cruelty all of its own, because it was exactly enough to make her unable to stop.

The trees stood dark and the seam in the ward shimmered faintly where the power thinned, and she set her hand against the cold curve of it and felt the three threads holding it up, Jazen's steady, Abe's steady, Hiram's guttering, and she thought, with the steady working voice that never rested in her: when this camp moves on the capitol, and it will, I am going too. Whatever I have to be to do it. I left her once in a burning corridor because I could not reach her and there were eight others I could. I will not leave her in the capital because I am afraid of being seen.

It was the first time she had let herself decide it. She decided it standing at the seam with her hand against the chill of the screen, and she would not take it back.

And then, far off and very faint, at the bottom of the bond that ran out of her and into the world, something turned toward her.

***

She felt him before she heard anything, the way the sun's return warms the back of your neck before you understand why you are warmer. The bond had been a warm ache for four days, a thread running out of her chest toward the south and west and ending in distance, in Raizen alive and far and coming, unable to be reached and unable to be lost. Now the thread shortened. It shortened the way a fishing line shortens when the thing on the end of it has decided to come in, and a wordless rush traveled up it toward her, recognition and relief and a joy so uncomplicated that it cut clean through everything else she was carrying, because it was a baby's joy, a young thing's joy, and it had no grief in it at all.

*Coming,* the bond said, not in words, because he was still too young and too far for words, but in the shape under words. *Coming coming coming.*

She did not run. She stood at the seam and watched the dark sky over the trees, and the camp behind her went quiet in stages as people felt the change in the wards, because two large things were approaching the camp's screen from the air and a thing that large approaching from the air had only ever meant one terror, and then someone called out, low and carrying, "It's the dragons, it's only the dragons, stand easy," and the camp let its breath out, and Viridia watched two shapes come down out of the last of the light.

The first was vast and black, blacker than the sky behind it, with the kind of dark that was not a color but an absence of one, and it came down with a slow terrible grace and folded onto the open ground at the seam and was Drake, six thousand years old and built like the end of an argument. The second was small. It was small and it was every color at once, scales catching the last light and throwing it back in greens and golds and blues and a hundred shades with no names, and it did not land so much as fall off the wind in her direction, all wings and no dignity, and hit her in the chest hard enough to stagger her, and wound a tail around her arm, and pressed a small hot prismatic head under her chin, and shook.

"You're heavier," she told him, because it was the only thing she could say that would not undo her.

*Heavier,* Raizen agreed, and it was the first word, the actual first word, arriving in her head clear and pleased with itself, because he had crossed some line of age or distance in the flying and the words had come. *Bigger. Drake says I am eating him to the bone. I caught a deer. I caught it myself. It was very fast and then it was not.* A pause, and under the brightness something steadier, something that had felt across all those miles exactly how much grief was waiting at the other end of the bond, and had flown through the night to put itself against it. *You are sad. I came as fast as the sky would let me. I am here now. You can be sad while I am here. I will be the part that is not sad.*

She put her hand flat on his prismatic back and felt the small furnace of him, the all-colored heat that was not Drake's death-cold and not Varissa's single flame but every element at once, whole, the way she was whole, and she held very still and let him be the part that was not sad, for a moment, in the dark, where no one could see her take it.

The children found him before the adults did. They came in a careful, electrified pack, the rescued Hazel girls among them now, and stopped at the respectful distance that children keep from a thing that might be wonderful or might bite, and Raizen, who had never in his short life been looked at by so many people at once, sat up straighter on her shoulder and turned his head this way and that so that the lamplight ran across his scales in every color there was, openly delighted to be admired, with no instinct at all toward hiding, which Viridia watched with something that was almost envy. A small boy asked, in a whisper that carried, whether the dragon would eat him, and Raizen, who could not yet speak to anyone but her, pushed the answer down the bond so hard she laughed before she could stop it, a short surprised sound she had not given anyone in weeks.

"He says only if you're a deer," she told the boy. "Are you a deer?"

The boy considered this with enormous seriousness and decided he was not, and the pack of them breathed out and crept a half-step closer, and for a little while, in the dark at the seam, there was something in the camp that was not grief and not fear, and Viridia let it be there, and did not try to be useful, and only watched.

***

Drake did not crowd them. He sat back on his haunches at the seam, a darkness against the dark, and waited with the patience of a thing that measured its life in the rise and fall of mountains, until Raizen had finished shaking and had climbed to her shoulder to ride there with his tail round her arm, his accustomed place, grown almost too big for it now and refusing to admit it.

"He would not settle," Drake said. His voice came out of the dark like something heard through the ground. "Three days he has flown wrong, turned the wrong way in his sleep, woken with his head pointed at you. A True Bond does not like distance in its first season. We came as soon as he could carry the weather." The great head turned, and she felt the weight of his attention settle on her, ancient and not unkind. "Varissa sends her fierceness. She would have come, but one of us stays. There are things moving on the roads that a young dragon should not meet alone, and a thing as old as us turns them aside merely by being seen."

"Things," Viridia said.

"Soldiers, mostly. Men are the worst thing on the roads this year, which is a poor showing for a world that also contains me." A sound came out of him that might have been grim humor or might have been grief; with Drake the two wore the same coat. "We passed near your burned school. I will not tell you it has stopped smoking, because it has, and a lie is a poor gift. But I will tell you a true thing, since the leader of this place trades in those and I find I have caught the habit. We saw the road north of it. We saw wagon-tracks, fresh, going toward the capital, deep, the way wagons sit when they are carrying people and not grain." The head lowered until one great eye was level with her. "If your people are asking on the lines about someone taken from that school, tell them to ask at the capital, and tell them to ask quickly, while the someone is still worth keeping. That is not a kind thing to say. It is a true one. I have buried a child. I have learned the difference between a kindness that helps and a kindness that only lets you look away."

Viridia stood with Raizen riding her shoulder and the oldest living thing she had ever met telling her, in the dark, that the rumor had wheel-ruts under it, and she felt the door that the rumor had cracked open swing wider, onto a longer drop, and she did not flinch from it, because she had decided at the seam not to flinch from it.

"Thank you," she said.

"You say that the way he does." Drake tipped his head toward the camp, toward the steady bright burning that was Jazen at the center of it. "Plainly, like it costs you. It is a good way. Most people spend the word so freely it buys nothing." He rose, slow and enormous, and the seam in the ward shivered around the mass of him. "I will roost in the trees beyond your screen tonight. A thing my size inside your hidden box would be a poor sort of hiding. Send the small one out to me at dawn; he eats better when I am there to be eaten in front of. And Viridia." The pause was the same pause Jazen used, the same one she used, the gathering before a thing set carefully down. "When you go to the capital, and the small one tells me you have decided to go, though you have told no one, you should know that the pact does not end at a school's gate. We were at the making of it. If the need is great enough, you may call, and what answers may surprise even you. Do not call lightly. But do not forget that you can."

He went up into the dark, vast and silent, and was gone over the trees, and the night closed where he had been, and Viridia stood at the seam with Raizen warm and heavy on her shoulder and the camp settling behind her and the whole shape of the next thing arranging itself in her mind with a terrible clarity.

Alice was alive, probably, in the capital, owned, while she was worth keeping. The resistance would move on the capitol within the week, because Jazen would not be stopped and the council had yielded. And she, Viridia, was going with them, into the one place in the world that wanted her dead, to pull one specific friend back out of it, and she had a tiger and a dragon and a dead language and a core like a banked furnace and the offer of a pact older than nations, and she had no idea yet whether any of it would be enough, and she found, standing there, that for the first time since the gates of Hazel she had no wish to be small.

*You are less sad,* Raizen observed, pleased with himself, settling his tail more firmly round her arm. *I told you I would help. What is the capital? It sounds like a place with a great many deer.*

"It's a place we're going to have to be very careful in," she said.

*I am never careful,* Raizen said happily. *But you are careful enough for both of us. Drake says so. He says you are the most careful person he has met in six thousand years and that it is going to get you into trouble.* A pause, and under it the steady warm thing again, the part that was not sad, holding. *I will come anyway. Wherever it is. I cannot not come. That is what the bond is. I checked.*

She walked back into the camp with the dragon riding her shoulder, and she went to find Jazen, because she had something to tell him now, and it was no question this time. It was a decision, and she meant to say it plainly, the way they both did, the way it cost her, so that he would know she meant all of it.

***

He was at his house, a low place no larger than the others, which she marked, because a man who made himself a palace and a man who made himself the same hut as everyone else were two different men, and she filed him under the second. He had a lamp going and a map weighted flat on the table with stones, and he did not seem surprised to see her in the doorway with a prismatic dragon on her shoulder. He looked at Raizen for a moment with the frank interest of a man who had grown up being told dragons were a story.

"So that's the one you were reaching for in your sleep," he said. "Amber told me there was a dragon coming. I half thought it was the drain talking." He nodded at the lamplit chair across the map. "Sit, if you mean to stay. Stand, if you mean to say a hard thing and go. I can tell which from the door, usually. With you I can't, which I'm starting to think is the whole of you."

"I'll stand," Viridia said.

"A hard thing, then."

"When you march on the capital," she said, "I'm coming with you."

She watched it land. He did not laugh, which she had been ready for, and he did not say no at once, which she had also been ready for. He set down the stone he had been turning in his fingers and looked at her across the lamp for a long moment, and what crossed his face was not refusal but something older and more tired than refusal, the look of a man being asked to spend a thing he had sworn never to spend again.

"You're fourteen," he said.

"Fifteen soon."

"That is not the improvement you think it is." He rubbed his face with one hand. "Sit down. I won't argue with someone standing in a doorway like she's ready to bolt, it makes me feel like a thing she's escaping." She sat, because the request was fair, and because Raizen had begun to slide off her shoulder toward the warmth of the lamp and she let him spill onto the table, where he curled against the weighting-stones and watched Jazen with one bright eye. "Now. You want to march on a walled capital, defended by the whole standing strength of the people who burned your school, to pull out one girl who may or may not be the girl in a carter's rumor. Have I the shape of it?"

"Yes."

"And you understand that I cannot promise you a rescue. That an army marching on a capital is not a key that fits one cell. That we will be fighting for the lives of every dark mage left alive, and that in the middle of that I cannot peel men off to go looking for one healer's helper, however much her hands are missed."

"I'm not asking you to," Viridia said. "I'll find her myself. I only need to be there."

Jazen studied her, and she let him, the careful face in place, and she watched him decide that she meant it, the whole of it, and watched the deciding cost him, because he knew, better than anyone alive, what it was to march toward a fire to pull out a sister.

"I had one," he said at last. He did not say one what. He did not have to. "Jan. Younger than me. She had a laugh you could find her by in a crowd, which is a dangerous way to be when the crowd has soldiers in it. Two years ago they came through the village we were hiding in, and I was a half-mile off setting wards, and by the time I felt it and got back there was nothing to pull out of the fire, there was only the fire." He said it without performance, in a low even voice, and she understood that he had said it many times, that he had worn the words smooth so they would stop cutting his mouth on the way out, and that they cut it anyway. "I did a thing that day I have spent every day since being afraid of. I will not tell you what. I'll only tell you that there is a kind of power that comes up in you when the person you were keeping safe is gone, and it does not care who is standing nearby, and it is the most frightening thing I have ever met, and I met it in a mirror." He looked at the dragon, at the map, at her. "I sue for peace, Viridia, not because I am gentle. I sue for peace because I have seen what I am when I stop, and I would rather lose slowly than become that twice."

The lamp ticked. Raizen had gone still against the warm stones, listening in the way young things listen to grief, without understanding it and entirely understanding it at once.

"Then you know why I'm going," Viridia said quietly. "It's the same reason you went back for Jan. Except I'm not a half-mile off this time. I'm here, and I have time to decide, and I'm deciding."

"That's exactly why I want to tell you not to." He leaned back. "Because I know the road you're standing at the top of. I went down it. There's no version of it where you come back the same as you went."

"I'm already not the same as I went."

He was quiet a while. Then he gave a short breath that was almost a laugh, the kind people give when a thing is too true to argue with. "No," he said. "I don't suppose you are." He pulled the map a little straighter under its stones, the gesture of a man putting his hands to work so his head could keep moving. "All right. I won't stop you, because I don't think I can, and because a person you stop from going after their own only goes anyway, later, alone, and worse. So you'll come, and you'll stay near me until I say otherwise, and when I tell you a thing on that road you'll do it without the careful face, because the careful face is going to get someone killed in a fight and I'd rather it weren't you. Those are my terms. Tell me you'll keep them and I'll start counting you in the number, which changes how I plan, so don't say it lightly."

"I'll keep them," she said. "On the road. After the road, I keep my own counsel."

"After the road." He almost smiled, the recalculating look surfacing again. "You drive a harder bargain than the council, and they had forty years of practice." He nodded at the door. "Go and sleep, if you can. The council sits in two days and I want you there now, at the back, watching, doing the thing you do where you read the room by the one wrong detail. I've a use for a person who can see what people are really afraid of. Bring the dragon. Let them get the fright over with in private, before it matters." He paused, and the careful pause again, the gathering. "And Viridia. My mother left papers. Routes, names, the lines we run. I've been meaning to put them in front of someone she'd have trusted, and I've decided that's you, though she never told me to, which is itself a thing I keep turning over. Come tomorrow and read them with me. There may be a faster road to your friend in them than a war. There may be other things in them too." He looked, for a moment, less like a leader and more like a son. "I haven't been able to make myself read all of it. You will. You read things that cost you. I've watched you do it."

She stood, and gathered Raizen off the warm stones, and he climbed back to her shoulder grumbling wordlessly at being moved from the heat, and at the door she stopped, because there was one true thing she could give him in trade for Jan, and she had learned that one true thing, set down plainly, was the only coin that bought anything from a man like this.

"She'd have trusted you with them," she said. "Your mother. That's why she didn't put my name in a letter. You don't put the thing you're protecting in a letter. You put it in your son." And she went out into the dark before he could answer, because some gifts are spoiled by waiting to see them open, and walked back toward the long house with her dragon warm against her neck and the council two days off and her mother's killers' city at the far end of every road she could now see.
