# Chapter 3: Alive Is Not the Same as Safe

<!-- Book Two, Ch.3 | chronological | em-dash <=4 | brisk; Alice CONFIRMED; council; moral seed -->

The council met two mornings later in the old stone hall at the center of the camp, and Viridia stood at the back against the wall and made herself small and watchful, the way Jazen had asked, and read the room before a single word had been spoken, because that was the thing she did, and the room told her almost everything she needed before anyone opened their mouth.

There were five of them at the long table, and she knew four already from the night she had arrived, when she had stood at this same wall and watched them yield to Jazen and agree to march. Coram, the old man with the graying beard, soft-voiced, who had been the one to bend. The woman in the pale blue dress with the silver-and-gold spectacles, whose hands went to the worn locket at her throat when she was frightened, which she was now. The broad sunburned man, Brutus, who had built the camp and could not say he feared losing it and so said strategy instead. The stately woman with the tight silver bun and the cold grey eyes, who did her arithmetic the way Viridia did hers, and trusted no one, and was the only one in the room who was not afraid, which was its own kind of frightening.

The fifth was new. A spare grey man who sat a little apart, with the worn-thin look of someone burning more than he could put back, and Viridia knew him at once, because she had traced his thread to its root that second morning: Abe, the second of the three who held up the camp's invisible walls. She wondered why he had been brought to a council he had not sat on before, and then she understood, with the cold working part of her, that it was because the council was about to debate a thing that would decide whether the walls came down, and a man who held one up had earned a seat at that table. Hiram, the third, was not there. Hiram was asleep, she hoped, or trying to be. Hiram could not be spared even for this.

Jazen stood at the head of the table, not sitting, which she filed, because a man who stood while others sat was either a servant or a commander, and Jazen was making sure they knew which. Raizen rode her shoulder, smaller than usual, having understood without being told that this was a room to be quiet in, and the council had got the fright of him over with at the door, exactly as Jazen had wanted, with a great deal of stilling and one stifled oath from Brutus, and now they were pretending he was not there, which suited Viridia, because it meant they had stopped seeing her too.

"We march in five days," Jazen said, without preamble. "That is no longer the question on this table. You agreed it, and I have begun it, and the supply lines are turning toward it as we speak. The question on this table is who holds the camp while we are gone, and how, and for how long, and what we do if the answer is no one." He set both hands flat on the wood. "So. Abe. Tell them what you told me."

Abe spoke without lifting his eyes from the table, in the flat voice of a tired man who has rehearsed a thing he does not want to say. "The screen wants three of us to hold it steady," he said. "It can hold on two for a while. A week, maybe two, if the two are rested, which we will not be. It cannot hold on one. If Jazen marches and takes his strength off the wards, and Hiram goes on the way he is going, then there is one of me left to do the work of three, and the camp will be visible inside a fortnight, perhaps inside a week, and there is no part of that I can fix by being brave about it."

The locket clicked, open and shut, under the woman in blue's restless fingers. "Then the camp comes apart whether we march or stay," she said. "If we march, it falls behind us. If we sit, it falls anyway, because Hiram is failing and you cannot hold it alone. Is that the whole of it? Have I understood you?"

"That is the whole of it," Abe said.

"Then there is no safe choice," said the woman in blue, and her voice did not break but her hand did the breaking for her, the clasp clicking faster. "There is only the question of whether we die hidden or die moving."

"There is one other thing it could be," said the stately woman with the silver bun, speaking for the first time, in the cold even voice of someone laying a coin on a table. "It could be that we stop pretending the camp can be saved, and spend it instead. Send the children and the old south, in small groups, by night, to the river settlements where a few more faces will not be noticed. Strip the camp of everything that cannot fight. And let the rest of us march, knowing there is nothing behind us to lose, which is, I will remind this table, the strongest position an army can be in." She folded her hands. "I do not say it to be cruel. I say it because a thing you have already decided to spend cannot be used against you. Jazen is right that hiding has become a cage. Then let us not be killed defending a cage. Let us empty it ourselves, on our own terms, and walk out the door free."

It was, Viridia thought from the wall, the coldest and clearest thing anyone had said, and also the truest, and she watched the others recoil from it precisely because it was true. Brutus had gone a deep dull red. He had built the houses. He had set the anchor-posts with his own hands. To empty the camp on purpose, to walk away from it whole rather than have it taken, was a thing his body refused before his mind could weigh it, and he opened his mouth and shut it and opened it again.

"You're talking about giving it to them," he said, low. "Everything we built. Handing it over without a fight."

"I am talking about not dying for a collection of huts," the silver-bun woman said. "The people are the camp, Brutus. Not the posts you sank. The people. Move the people and the camp is wherever they stand."

"Easy to say, for someone who built none of it."

"Easier to say," she agreed, unoffended, "which is exactly why someone who built none of it should be the one to say it. You are too close to the wood to see the people for it. That is not a fault. It is only a fact, and facts are what I am for at this table."

Viridia watched Brutus take the blow and have no answer to it, because there was no answer to it, and she filed the silver-bun woman away under the heading she kept for people who were right in a way that helped no one feel better, which was a short and lonely list, and which she suspected she was on herself.

It was Coram who broke the silence, in his soft, almost sorrowful voice, the voice of a man who had already spent too many years choosing between bad things. "We are going around the same table we went around a week past," he said. "We agreed to march then. We are only now learning the price of it, which is the camp itself, and a price learned late feels like a new decision when it is only the old one arriving." He folded his spotted hands. "I do not want to leave this place. I have grown old in it. But I have grown old in it because Jazen's wards hid me, and Jazen's wards are leaving with Jazen, and I am too tired to pretend that a thing held up by three men can stand when one walks off and one is failing. So I will say what I think, and you may all be angry at me for it, as you were the last time. The lady with the silver hair is right. We empty it ourselves. We do not wait to have it taken."

And Jazen, who had stood through the whole of it without once raising his voice, looked at Mella, whose clasp had gone still on the locket, and did not say a word about her grandchildren, and said instead, "Mella. You've moved more people along these lines than anyone alive. If we send the children and the old south in small groups by night, who do you want walking them, and how far apart, and to which settlements." It was not a question about whether. It was a question about how, handed to the one person at the table whose fear was a thing with two small faces, and Viridia watched Mella reach for it the way a drowning person reaches for a rope, because being given the safety of the children to arrange was the only thing in the room that could have steadied her, and Jazen had handed it to her without ever once saying her name in the same breath as the word afraid.

Mella began, in a voice that grew firmer as it went, to speak of routes and numbers and which families had kin in the river settlements, and Brutus leaned in despite himself, because moving people was work, and work was the one language his hands trusted, and within a few minutes the table that had been frozen between dying hidden and dying moving was bent over the practical business of getting its weakest out alive, and no one had said the word surrender, and no one would, and the thing the silver-haired woman had proposed was being done under a different name by people who could not have borne to call it by hers.

Viridia, from the wall, watched Jazen do in five minutes with her reads what she could not have done at all, which was to make frightened people walk willingly toward the only choice left, and she understood, watching him, why his mother had built a resistance around this particular son, and why she had been so afraid of what he might one day do, because a man who could move a room this gently could also, on the day grief took the gentleness away, move it anywhere at all.

***

She gave them what Jazen had brought her for, afterward, in the close back room where he took his counsel, with the door shut and Raizen draped across the cold hearth pretending to sleep.

"Mella, the one in blue, has grandchildren in the camp," Viridia said. "Two, I think. She'll vote for whatever keeps them closest to her, and call it strategy, and believe it, and you can move her by telling her which choice keeps them safest and saying nothing about her at all. Brutus can't agree to abandon the camp, not because he's wrong, but because his hands won't let his head do it, so don't ask him to choose it. Ask him to defend the people instead of the place, and give him the work of moving them, and he'll do the thing she wants while believing he refused it. Coram is tired and good and he'll follow whoever frightens him least, so don't frighten him. And the woman with the silver bun is right about everything and that is exactly why no one will follow her, so you should take her advice and never once say it was hers, or you'll lose the room."

Jazen had listened to all of it without moving, leaning against the cold edge of the mantel, and now he let out a slow breath.

"My mother could do that," he said. "Walk into a room and come out with everyone's true name in her pocket. I never could. I feel the size of a man's magic from across a field and I cannot tell you for the life of me what he's afraid of." He looked at her. "You did that from a wall, in an hour, with most of them strangers."

"They're not strangers," Viridia said. "They're afraid, and frightened people are the least strange thing there is. They all want the same four things and they've only dressed them differently." She was quiet a moment. "It's not a gift. It's only what's left when you spend your whole life watching for the thing that's going to hurt you. You learn to read a room because a room read wrong is how you get caught. That's all it is."

"My mother said almost exactly that to me once," Jazen said quietly. "Word for word, near enough. I used to think it was a sad thing to say. Now I think it might be the truest thing she ever taught, and I think she taught it to you too, somehow, without ever being in the same room as you, which is a thing I keep coming back to and cannot make sit still." He pushed off the mantel. "The papers. Tomorrow. I mean it. There's something in them about you, Viridia, or about a thing my mother was doing that has your shape on it, and I have not been able to bring myself to open it, and I think you should be the one beside me when I do." He paused. "But that's tomorrow. Today there's something else. A runner came in on the east line an hour ago, while you were reading my council for me. I wanted to tell you alone."

Viridia went very still, and the cold working part of her came forward, and she knew before he said it.

***

"A name," Jazen said. "You wanted a name to go with the hands. We have one."

He said it the way she would have, plainly, without dressing it, because he had learned in six days exactly how she needed to be handed a heavy thing, and she stood very still and made her face the careful face and let him hand it to her.

"Alice Pembrook," he said. "First-year, first-generation, healer's helper. Taken alive from the school the night it burned, with a dozen others who could be put to use, and walked to the capital, and she is being held there now, in the workhouses near the infirmary district, where they keep the captured healers and put them to mending soldiers." He watched her as he said it. "She was alive eleven days ago, which is when the word leaves off, because that is how long news takes to walk that road. Eleven days ago Alice Pembrook was alive, and whole, and working, and a woman on our line who carried bread to that workhouse looked at her with our question in her mind and came away certain. Long fair hair. Freckles. A tooth turned a little out of true when she smiles. She told a guard her name was Alice P., because, she said, there'd been three Alices in her year at home and she'd always had to be Alice P." His voice gentled, only slightly, only enough. "That's her, isn't it. That's not a carter's rumor anymore. That's her."

Viridia stood in that close room with her dragon pretending to sleep on the cold hearth and her oldest friend confirmed alive eleven days ago in the one city in the world she could not walk into, and she spent every coin she had on her face, and held it, and it held, and only her breath changed, and she managed it, because she had a rule.

"Yes," she said. The word came out flat and even and cost her the whole of herself. "That's her. She's always had to be Alice P."

And then, because Jazen had given her a true thing plainly and the only payment she had was another true thing given the same way, she said the rest of it, the part she had not said to anyone, looking at the cold hearth and not at him.

"She was on overflow duty in the medical ward when the school started to burn. I couldn't reach her. The ward was on the far side of the fire and I had eight girls behind me who I could reach, and I made the count, and I took the eight, and I left her, and I have told myself every night since that there was no other choice, and there wasn't, and it doesn't help, because the not-choosing-her was still a thing I did with my own hands." She turned the dark-blue stone at her throat once, between her fingers, and stilled it. "And now you're telling me she lived. That the corridor I left her in didn't kill her. That she walked out of it, and got put in a wagon, and is mending the soldiers of the people who burned her school, in a city I'm going to have to take apart to reach. And I find that I'm glad, and that the gladness is the most frightened I have been since the gates of Hazel, because now there's something to lose again, and I had just got used to having lost it."

"I know that feeling," Jazen said. "I have it about a city full of people I've never met. Alive is heavier than dead, Viridia. Dead you set down, eventually, however long it takes. Alive you carry every step." He did not touch her, which she was grateful for. "We march in five days. The workhouses near the infirmary district. I'll have the line map it street by street between now and then, every door, every guard-change, so that when we're inside the walls and everything is on fire and no one can tell you anything, you'll already know the way. That's the most I can give you. The rest you'll have to do yourself, the way you do everything, and I think you know that, and I think it's why you didn't ask me to do it for you."

"I didn't ask because you can't," Viridia said. "And because it's mine."

"It's yours," he agreed.

***

She walked out of the hall into the ordinary noise of the camp, the smith's hammer and the children and the baby in the next house, and she did not go and find work, which was new, because she had somewhere she needed to go inside her own head first and she had learned that you could not do that with your hands full.

She climbed instead to the low rise behind the smithy, where the ground lifted toward the trees and a person could sit on a fallen log above the rooftops and not be in anyone's way, and she sat there with Raizen heavy and warm across her knees, and let herself think the thing she had been refusing to think since her second morning in the camp, all the way to the end of it, once, so that she would know its shape.

She could end the war.

Not by reaching Alice. By the other thing. The thing she had touched at the source and carried back inside her and told no one, not Lor-ar, not Jazen, not even all the way herself. She knew how the severing had been made. She had felt the cut at the root of every human core, the deliberate fraying of mankind's dark half by a frightened council six hundred years gone, and she knew, the way you know a thing in your hands before you know it in words, that what had been cut could be mended. Not for one person. For all of them. Every core alive, made whole at once, the way hers was whole. If everyone in the world woke tomorrow with the full of their gift, light and dark together, there would be no light mages and no dark mages, only mages, and the whole architecture of the thing, the supremacy and the decree and the workhouses full of healers, would have nothing left to stand on. The war would simply stop, because the reason for it would be gone.

She let her sight settle and looked down at the camp below her, and saw not the rooftops but the cores, the small steady lights of every person in it, the way she always saw them now and mostly tried not to. Mella with her two grandchildren, three lights close together by the south lane. Brutus, a banked red glow bent over a fence he was mending even now. The children at Mirelle's long table, a scatter of bright small flames. And every one of them, every single one, was a light burning on half its wick, the dark side of each core cut and frayed and capped over the way her own had never been, so that what she was looking at, from the rise, was a whole village of people who had been made smaller than they were born to be, and did not know it, and never would, unless she told them with her hands.

She could do it. She was almost sure she could do it. And to do it she would reach into every living person in the world and change the deepest thing they were, without asking a single one of them, without their knowing, the way the frightened council had reached in six hundred years ago and changed them, without asking, in the dark. The symmetry of it sat in her like a swallowed stone. She would be saving them by doing to them precisely what had been done to them. The only difference she could name, with Raizen breathing slow and warm against her, was that she meant it kindly, and she was not at all sure, when she turned it over, that the frightened council six hundred years ago had not also meant it kindly, in their way, and had not also sat on some rise above some village telling themselves that the thing they were about to do without asking was for everyone's good.

She sat on the log above the rooftops and looked the thought full in the face, and then she set it down, the way she had set it down twice before, because she was fifteen years old in five days and it was not a thing a person should be able to weigh as calmly as she had just weighed it, and the calmness frightened her more than the thought did.

Be careful who you let see you do it.

She had always heard it as a warning about being seen. She wondered now, with the dragon a heavy comfort on her lap, whether it had also been a warning about the doing, about the things she would one day be able to do, and whether her mother, with her deep-water stillness and her too-much-in-the-head, had known exactly what she was raising, and had tried, in eight quiet words, to hand a frightened child a brake before she ever needed it.

"Not yet," she said aloud, to the trees, to the rooftops, to the thought. "There are other roads first. I'll try the other roads."

*What roads,* Raizen asked sleepily, not really awake, the word drifting up the bond without any weight on it.

"I don't know yet," she told him truthfully. "I'll know them when they close."

She did not know yet that they would close, one after another, until only this one was left. She only knew that she would try them, and that the trying was the difference, the whole difference, between her and the council that had cut the world in half out of fear, and that she meant to keep that difference for exactly as long as the world would let her keep it.

***

Amber was waiting for her at the long house when she came down off the rise, sitting on the step in the last of the light with the blanket round her shoulders, and she took one look at Viridia's face, which to anyone else would have shown nothing, and said, "They've heard something about her. Sit down."

Viridia sat. She had not meant to tell anyone but Jazen, and then she found, sitting on the step beside the one person left alive who had known Alice, that the not-telling was heavier than the telling.

"She's alive," she said. "Eleven days ago. Taken to the capital. They've got her mending soldiers in a workhouse." Her voice did the flat thing, and Amber did not try to fix it, because Amber, of all people, knew better. "She told a guard her name was Alice P. That's how we're sure. Because she'd always had to be Alice P."

Amber was quiet for a moment, turning the cold cup in her hands, the chipped black nails catching the light. "Three Alices in her year," she said. "She told me that the first week. Like it was the great tragedy of her life, having to share." She let out a breath that was not quite steady. "Alive. God. I'd made my peace with the worst of it, you know. I'd done the arithmetic and put her in the column you don't reopen. And now you're telling me she's been alive the whole time, in a workhouse, being useful, because of course she'd make herself useful, she couldn't help it if she tried." She pressed the heel of her hand briefly to one eye, openly, the way Viridia never could. "She's alive and she's in the worst place in the world and we're about to go and kick the door of it in. I don't know whether to laugh or be sick."

"Both," Viridia said. "I've been doing both since noon. Quietly."

Amber laughed, the cracked surprised laugh, and bumped her shoulder against Viridia's, which from Amber was a whole speech. "Then we'll get her," she said. "Whatever it takes. You and me and a tiger and a dragon and an army of people who hate that city more than they're afraid of it." She paused, and some of the lightness went out of her, and she looked at Viridia with the directness she kept for the things that mattered. "You're already planning something more than a rescue. I can see it on you. You've got that look you get when you've worked out a thing you don't want to have worked out. Don't tell me yet. But when you're ready to, tell me before you do it, not after. Promise me that."

Viridia did not promise. She looked at the cold cup in Amber's hands and said nothing, which was its own answer, and Amber saw it land, and her jaw tightened, and she let it go, for now, because the light was failing and they were both tired and some arguments keep.

Then Viridia went and carried water, because Hiram was failing and the children were thirsty, and because a girl with her hands full of buckets cannot also turn a frightening thought over and over, and there were still, she had found, things in the world that wanted carrying.
