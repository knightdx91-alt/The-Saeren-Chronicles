# Chapter 12: Goodbyes

The road took them all morning to become an army, because an army is not a thing you declare into being along a night road, it is a thing that assembles out of the wreckage of whatever it used to be, and what these people had used to be was a camp, and a camp is the opposite of an army in every way that matters, a camp being the practice of not being seen and an army being the practice of going to where you cannot help but be, and so the first hours after the valley were not marching at all but the slow grim sorting-out of people who had loaded carts to flee and now had to unload the same carts to fight, and Viridia walked the length of the column twice before sunrise in the unkind grey and watched the sorting happen and did not interfere with it, because the sorting was Jazen's and the part of her that did not feel had already counted what the sorting would cost and found the count bearable, which was the most you could say of any count now.

They had come up out of the valley by the cart-road and stopped at the first crossing where there was water, a brown stream under a stone bridge old enough that nobody knew who had built it, and the survivors spread along the bank in the half-light and made the small fires that a camp on the run is allowed when the thing that hunted it has already done its hunting and gone home, and Viridia stood above them on the bridge and counted how many fires there were against how many fires there should have been, and stopped counting, because she had done the same sum through the night and the answer had not improved with daylight and would not.

* * *

Raizen felt them coming before she did.

She knew it the way she knew everything that moved in him now, through the bond that had been a warm ache across unmeasurable miles for a season and was a near and constant weather in her since he came back, and what moved in him as the sun came up over the stone bridge was not fear, Raizen did not fear his parents, it was the particular bracing of a child who has been told to expect a hard thing and has decided to meet it standing, and Viridia felt that bracing and looked up from the fires and saw the two shapes come down the morning out of the west, the one pitch black and burning at the edges with a fire that gave no warmth, the other brilliant scarlet against the pale sky, and she knew before they had landed what they had come for, because Raizen knew it, and what Raizen knew she knew.

They did not land in the column. They landed across the stream, in the wet meadow beyond it, where there was room, and where the survivors could see them and be afraid of them at a distance that the fear could be borne, because Drake had been alive six thousand years and knew exactly how much dragon a column of the freshly bereaved could stand to have among them, which was none, and so he put the width of a brown stream between his grief and theirs and let them keep their distance and their fear and their morning.

Raizen went to them across the bridge, and Viridia went with him, because the bond did not give her the choice of staying and because she would not have taken the choice if it had.

* * *

Drake spoke first, because Drake always spoke first, to get the hard thing said before the softness in it could be heard.

"You're going to the wall," he said. It was not a question. He had the death in him for an affinity and the death in him could read the shape of where a thing was going the way Viridia could read a core, and he had read the shape of where the army was going from across a continent and had not liked the shape of it, and he stood in the wet meadow with the black fire moving at his edges and looked at his son and said the thing. "All of you. And the boy with you." He did not look at Viridia when he said *the boy*, which was how she knew he meant Raizen and not Jazen, because Raizen was the boy to Drake and would be the boy to Drake when Raizen had outlived every human in the meadow by four thousand years. "You're taking him to the wall, and you mean to do the thing at the source if the wall doesn't break, and a True-Bond that reaches for the source reaches with both ends of itself, and the first end is you, girl, and the second is my son, and the source does not give back what it takes hold of in the same shape it took it."

"I know that," Viridia told him.

"You don't," Drake said, but he said it without heat, which was worse than heat, the way the councilor's no-blame had been worse than blame. "You know it as a sum. You don't know it the way you'll know it after. Neither do I. I have only ever stood on the after side of it once and what I stood next to was a hole." The black fire flared once and went down. "I lost a child to the last war. You know that. I taught you the symbol because of it, the mark that ends a thing, and I taught it to you as grief and not as a weapon, and you took it like a weapon anyway, because you're young, and the young cannot help it. I am not here to teach you another symbol. There isn't one. I am here because my son is going to the source tied to the other end of you and I have stood next to the hole one time too many to send him there without standing next to him first."

* * *

He went to Raizen then, and the great black head came down to the dragon that was his son, and they touched the way dragons touch, which is brow to brow, the death-fire and the prismatic light meeting at the join and neither one going out, and Drake said something to him in the deep register under hearing that Viridia felt through the bond more than heard, and what came through the bond was not words, words were a human thing and Drake was not speaking the human thing to his son, what came through was the entire mass of six thousand years deciding to let a thing it loved walk toward a hole it could not follow it into, and Viridia stood close in beside it with her hand on Raizen's flank and held her face still and unreadable, because grief that was not hers to spend was still grief she could honor by not making it about herself.

Drake's grief was loud. That was its register. He grieved the way he had always grieved, from a height, in the deep tones, the black fire flaring and the great wings half-spread, and a creature on the bank a hundred yards off who could not see the meadow began to wail and was hushed, and Drake did not care that the column heard him, he had earned the right not to care six thousand years and one dead child ago, and he poured the grief out over his son in the open the way he poured everything, and then he drew it back in, because it was said, and a thing once said does not need saying twice, and he lifted his head off Raizen's and was the gruff thing again, the *doesn't look like much* thing, the armor of it back up over the wound of it.

"Don't let the girl spend you cheap," he said to Raizen, gruff, and it was the closest Drake would come to *I love you* in front of a human, and Raizen, who spoke now, who had spoken his first words this very season, did not waste any of them on answering, he only pressed his brow back against his father's once, the answer in the pressing, and Drake took it, and that was Drake's goodbye, said loud and ended fast, as he did everything.

* * *

Varissa's goodbye was the opposite.

She had not spoken at all yet. She had stood the whole while a little behind Drake with the scarlet of her gone dull in the morning the way a banked fire goes dull, and she had let Drake say the hard thing first because Drake always said the hard thing first and because the soft thing was hers and she did not give the soft thing away in front of the hard one, and when Drake had drawn back she came forward, low, the scarlet head down nearly to the wet grass, and she did not touch Raizen brow to brow as his father had done, she put the side of her face along the whole length of him, the way one creature lies down beside another in the dark, and she stayed there, not speaking, breathing him in, and the flame she breathed came out of her only as warmth now and not as fire, a mother breathing warmth along a child she had carried in the egg and would not carry again whatever the source gave back, and Viridia felt through the bond the thing Varissa was doing, which was memorizing him, the exact heat of him and the exact light and the exact way the prismatic ran along his spine when he breathed, laying it all down inside herself where the source could not take it because the source had no reach into a mother's keeping.

"I grieve close to the ground," Varissa said at last, and she said it to Viridia, not to her son, because to her son there were no words, and to Viridia there were, and the words were a thing handed across like a key. "His father throws his up at the sky where everyone can hear it and thinks that is the honest way, and it is honest, it is, but it is not the only honest way. I will go back to the mountain and I will lie in the place where his egg lay and I will not make a sound, and that is mine, and I am telling you it is mine so that you will know, girl, when you reach for the source with my son fastened to your other end, that there are two of us grieving him already, in two ways, and that one of them is silent, and that the silent one is the one you should be afraid of, because the silent grief is the grief that does not let go, and if you spend him at that wall and do not bring him back the shape you took him, I will not throw my grief at the sky. I will bring it to you. Close to the ground. Do you understand me."

"Yes," Viridia said.

"Good." The scarlet head lifted off her son, slow, the memorizing finished or as finished as it would get. "Then we understand each other. Bring him back. Whatever shape. Just bring him back."

* * *

They left the way dragons leave, which is all at once and upward, and the column watched them go and was glad to have them gone, and Viridia stood on the bridge after with her hand still warm where it had rested on Raizen's flank and let the meadow be as large as it had been, the loud goodbye and the silent one, and filed both of them whole, and went down off the bridge to the parts of the morning that were hers.

* * *

Amber had the rescued girls.

She had them in a knot under the lee of the bridge-wall where the wind off the water did not reach, the Hazel girls who had folded into the camp over the season and the camp-born ones who had run clear of the burning in Amber's two drilled strings, and she had them doing a thing, because Amber had learned in the season that the rescued ones did better with a thing to do than with comfort, comfort being a thing that gave the grief a room to fill and a task being a thing that did not, and the task she had them at was the count, the same count Mirelle had taught them under the table, the small ones told off by name down the bench, except that there was no bench now, there was a stone wall and a cold morning, and the names they told off had two fewer in them than the names had held a week ago, and the girls said the count the way you say a thing somebody died teaching you, carefully, getting it right, because getting it right was the last thing the teaching was for.

Viridia crouched at the edge of the knot and one of the small ones, a girl with her hair come loose from its plait, looked at her and said, "Mirelle isn't in the count."

"No," Viridia said.

"Amber says we say her at the end. After the living ones. So we don't lose where she goes."

"That's right," Viridia said. "That's exactly right. Say her at the end."

She did not say the other thing, which was that there was a name she would have liked to put at the end of the count herself if a count could hold a man it had no place for, Hiram against the post with the roof going down slow over the children's heads, and that there was no count in the column with a place for Hiram in it, because Hiram had not been in the leaving, he had been the leaving, the forty minutes of it, bought and spent, and a thing that buys a count cannot be a line in it. She kept that off her face as she had kept the morning's date. The small girl did not need a second dead name to learn this morning. She had the one, and she was holding it the right way, at the end, after the living, where it would not get lost, and that was as much as a stone wall and a cold count could do for the dead, and it was more than most of the dead got.

And the small girl turned back to the count and got to the end of the living and said *Mirelle* into the cold morning the way you lay a stone on a cairn, once, plainly, and went quiet, and the other small ones went quiet with her, and Amber, over the tops of their heads, found Viridia's eyes, and Amber's face had the armor back up over it the way the silver-bun woman's face had its arithmetic and the way Viridia's had its careful, but under Amber's armor was the question, the one Amber had asked her in the dark a fortnight ago and had not asked again and had not stopped asking, *do you have the right,* and Viridia met the look and did not answer it, because there was no reply she could give it that did not fall apart in the open air, and Amber knew that, and let the not-answering stand between them the way it had stood between them since.

* * *

Brutus was on the last cart still, and his broad hands had not come off the beam.

They had not been able to take the beam from him. The healer had tried, in the dark, because a burned man bleeding under the ribs does not need a length of timber on him making the wound worse with every jolt of the cart-road, and Brutus had held it, the broad hands still in it, the outrage still in him, and the healer had let him keep it because there is a point past which you do not take a dying man's grip off the last thing his hands made, and Brutus had ridden through the night with the timber laid across him and the burn going at the slow patient pace the healer's hands could set it to and could not stop, and he was grey now under the sunburn, greyer than yesterday, the way Hiram had got grey, and Viridia came to the side of the cart and Brutus opened his eyes and found her.

"Still here," he said. There was less of the voice than there had been at the cookhouse.

"Still here," Viridia said. "The field's still ahead of us. You haven't missed it."

That was the thing he wanted, she had understood it on the road, he was keeping his grip on the timber and keeping the wound at bay with the same broad stubbornness because he did not want to die on a dark cart-road having only seen the cookhouse burn, he wanted to get to the field, to see what the field was, the thing he had made her promise about without either of them knowing what it was, and the cold working part of her had counted whether his wanting would carry him as far as the field and had returned that it might, just, if the road did not get worse, and she did not tell him the count, she told him the part of the count that was a thing to hold.

"You haven't missed it," she said again. "Hold on to the beam."

"Then I'll keep my hands where they are," Brutus said, which was the same promise worn down to fewer words, and he closed his eyes, and the broad hands stayed in the wood, and the cart went on bearing him at the front of the column where the carts went, toward the field he had not yet seen and meant to.

* * *

Somewhere in the middle of that long morning the day turned over, and it was a day that meant a thing to no one in the column but Viridia, and to Viridia it meant a thing she did not say.

She had kept the date as she kept all things, banked, off her face, a small private arithmetic running under the larger ones, and she knew without a calendar what the morning was, because her mother had told her the morning every year of the seven years she had a mother to tell her, *this was the morning,* and Viridia had counted the mornings since on her own after there was no one to tell her, and this was the morning, and she was a year older on it than she had been yesterday, and no one in the column knew, and there was no one left living to know, and that was the whole of the marking of it, a girl walking a cart-road to a war one year further from the start of herself with the knowledge of it held inside where the cold morning could not have it.

She did not tell Lor-ar. She did not tell anyone. It was not a thing that needed telling and there was no one it would have helped to tell. She only let herself know it, the once, walking, *a year older today and no one knows it,* and then she folded the knowing in with the rest and went on, because the day did not care what else the morning was, the day had a wall at the end of it, and the wall did not keep birthdays.

* * *

Lor-ar found her at the back of the column in the afternoon, where she had dropped to walk alone for a stretch, the cat-shape of him trotting up out of the long grass at the verge and growing as he came until he paced beside her at her shoulder the full seven feet of him, sky-blue and forest-green, the bone-white horns, the lightning-blue eyes, and he did not say *little one,* because *little one* was rationed hard and this was not a *little one* moment, this was the graver thing, and he knew the difference better than she did.

"You have decided something," Lor-ar said. "You have been deciding it since the valley. You have decided it now."

She walked a while before she answered him, because the thing was true and saying it true was the only way she would say it.

"If the war can't be won at the wall," she said, "I'll mend the severing. All of it. Every living core, in a single stroke, the way I told you I could on the mountain. I won't ask them. There's no way to ask them. I'll do it because every other road is shut and the bodies are real and there's nothing else left that isn't lying down to die." She kept her voice level on it, the cold working part carrying the saying so the rest of her did not have to. "I'm saying it to you because you're the only one I can say it to. Jazen would call it the only road, and he'd be right, and his being right wouldn't make it mine to do. Amber would ask me the question. I can't carry the question and the doing both. So I'm telling you. Because you won't do either of those things."

"No," Lor-ar agreed. "I will do neither of those things." He paced beside her, the great soft tread that made no sound in the grass. "But you should not have come to me hoping I would do the third thing, which is to tell you it is all right. I will not do that one either."

"I didn't come to you hoping that."

"You did," Lor-ar said, gently, which was rarer in him than the rationed name. "Everyone who tells a thing to the one creature who will not judge them is hoping, underneath, that the not-judging will feel like the absolving. It does not. I have lived three hundred years and watched a great many people mistake my silence for a blessing and walk away lighter, and they were always wrong to, and they always found out they were wrong to, after, when the silence had not in fact carried any of the weight off them at all." He turned the lightning eyes on her, and there was the ancient amusement in them and under the amusement the thing that was not amusement at all. "I will not tell you that you have the right. I do not know whether you have the right. I am a tiger and the question is a human question and it is too large even for three hundred years. I will only tell you this, which is the most I have: that whatever you decide at the wall, you will have decided it, and you, and no one else, and not me, and that you must carry it as a thing you decided and never once let yourself believe a creature in the grass told you it was fine. Because no creature did. Do you understand me, Viridia."

He said her name, not *little one,* and the name from Lor-ar was its own kind of weight.

"Yes," she said. "I understand you."

"Then we have spoken of it," Lor-ar said, "and we will not speak of it again until you have done it or not done it, because speaking of a thing you have already decided is only a way of asking to be absolved a second time, and I have told you there is no absolving in me." And he shrank, between one stride and the next, down to the house-cat shape, and sprang up onto the rail of a passing cart and rode there the rest of the afternoon with his eyes shut, a small unremarkable cat on a refugee's cart, and did not look at her again, and the not-looking was not a withdrawal, it was the thing he had given her, the weight left whole in her hands where he had refused to take any of it.

* * *

By evening the column had become, as much as it ever would, an army.

The carts went at the front where the wounded and the children and the old ones went, and the able ones walked, and the able ones who could fight had been sorted out of the able ones who could not and given the spears and the few rifles the camp had hidden against exactly this morning and never wanted to use, and the fighters walked at the flanks the way Amber's drilled girls had walked the flanks of the children leaving the valley, and the whole of it moved east, toward the capital, on the long road that the camp had spent two years pretending it would never have to take.

Jazen came back down the column at dusk and walked beside Viridia at the rear, where she had stayed, and the ash was off him now, somebody had given him water and he had used it, but the thing the ash had been on the outside of was still in him, and he looked at the column moving ahead of them in the day's dying light, the carts and the fighters and the small fires not yet lit, and he did not do the flat voice. He had stopped doing the flat voice on the bridge. Nothing remained for the flat voice to hold off.

"I counted them," he said. "While you were with the dragons. Two in three. A little better than two in three." He let that sit. "I built it for eight years and it held them all and now there's two in three of them on a road to the one place I swore I'd never take them, and the third that isn't here is the price of my building it instead of marching when there was still a school standing to march for." He shook his head once, slow. "I'm not saying it to be sorry at you. You hate being sorried at. I'm saying it because I want one person walking next to me who knows the exact size of what we're doing, and you're the only one who counts it the way I count it, and a man shouldn't carry a number like that with nobody beside him who has the same number."

"Two in three," Viridia said. "A little better."

"A little better," Jazen agreed.

And they walked on into the dusk with the same number between them, not smaller for being shared and not larger, only carried by two sets of hands instead of one, and ahead of them the column took the bend in the road where the land began its long tilt down toward the eastern plain, and past the plain, two days off, three, was the wall, and the wall was where they had always been going, and they went toward it now in the day's last grey with the dead remembered behind them and the living counted ahead of them and the one thing Viridia had told no one but a cat folded down deep where it would keep until the wall asked for it.

They took the long road east to the capital. There was no other road left to take.
