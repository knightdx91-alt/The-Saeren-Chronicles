# Chapter 6: The Window Narrows

The reply sat on the table between them for a day and a night before anyone answered it, and in that time it changed shape three times without changing a word.

*Who else knows.*

When she first read it under the coal of her core it had been a threat, plainly, the kind of thing a frightened powerful man writes when he wants to know the size of the thing standing in his road. By morning, holding it again at the council table while Jazen turned it over in his careful hands, it had become a question instead, an honest one, the question of a man trying to learn whether he was being offered a door or a trap. By the second night she had stopped trying to decide which it was, because she had understood that it was both, that a careful man in a Conclave that wanted her dead would write exactly those three words whether he meant to save her or to sell her, and that there was no reading of his hand that would tell her apart. The only way to learn which kind of man he was, was to answer him. And the only honest answer to *who else knows* was one that gave him the camp.

She did not sleep much, the second night. She lay on the narrow cot in the bare little room and held the stone at her throat and did the cold arithmetic of it, and the arithmetic would not come out. Tell him the truth, that a girl in a hidden village had sent it, and she handed a man she had never met the one fact that could end them all. Lie, soften it, say *no one of consequence,* and she taught a careful man on the first exchange that the open circle could be made to lie, and lost the one road that ran toward something other than the wall.

She was still holding both ends of it, unjoined, when the wards broke.

* * *

It came in the grey hour, the same hour she had gone out to the rowan, when the camp lay dark and sleeping and only the three thin cores burned at the rim. She was awake. She had been awake. So she felt it before the bell.

It was not a sound. It was a change in the lattice, a pressure laid against the screen from outside, the way you feel a hand on a tent wall before it pushes through, and she was up and out of the cot with the sight already coming out of its bank, and what she saw stopped her breath for exactly one beat and then she was moving.

Lights. Coming through the wood from the east, low and orderly and bright, eleven of them, twelve, burning the hard white-gold of trained light mages with focuses out and warmed, moving in the spread careful line of soldiers who have done this before. A capital patrol. Not a search party blundering, not a courier. A patrol, working a grid, and the grid had brought them to the wood that held the camp, and they did not know the camp was there because the screen still held, but the screen held because three spent men were pouring the last of themselves into it, and the patrol had stopped, because one of them had felt something the way she had felt them, a pressure on the lattice with no source, the particular wrongness of a thing being hidden.

She was at the anchor-post before she had decided to run there. Hiram was already on his feet, which was worse than if he had not been, because she could see what it cost him to be on them. He stood against the post with both hands flat on the grey wood and his core, in the sight, was not a coal any more. It was a coal under a bellows, blown white past what it had to give, the colour a thing goes just before it goes out, and he was holding the screen taut over the whole east face of the camp by main force, by will, by the simple refusal to let go, and his face had the grey in it that she had walked past at his window the night before and told herself was only tiredness.

"They felt the seam," he said, not turning. His voice came out level and small, a man rationing even his breath. "Where I let it thin to walk it. My fault. They felt the place I let it thin."

"It's not."

"It is. Doesn't matter." Behind him the bell had started, the soft iron one Brutus had hung, two short and a pause and two short, the sound that meant *up, quiet, to the lines, no light,* and the camp was coming awake the way it had drilled to come awake, low and fast and without a single window lit. "Listen. I can hold the screen or I can hold it over fighting. Not both. If you fight under it, if there's force, real force, they'll read it through the cloth like a hand under a sheet, and then they'll know exactly where and exactly how many." He turned his head, just, and his eyes found her, and they were clear in the grey ruin of his face, clear the way a thing gets clear when there is no strength left to spend on anything but the truth. "Whatever you do out there. Do it small. Do it quiet. Don't open your core where they can feel it. I'm holding the only thing keeping us a rumour. If you light up, I've held it for nothing."

She looked at him, at the white coal of him, at both palms pressed to the grey wood. She had a furnace banked behind her ribs that could have taken all twelve of them out of the wood in the space of a breath, could have unmade their focuses, closed their throats, pulled the ground out from under them, done it the way she had straightened the picture on the wall, at no cost she could feel. She had it, banked and ready. And the price of it would not have come to her and would not have come to Hiram. It would have fallen on the camp, because a furnace opening in a hidden wood is not a thing a screen can hide, and on the rim of the world where the ledger kept its sums, and it would have told twelve trained men and everyone they reported to exactly what was hiding in this wood, which was the one thing on earth the city wanted more than the camp.

"Small," she said. "Quiet. Spears."

"Spears," he said, and something in his ruined face eased, because she had understood him, and he turned back to the post and put the last of himself into the cloth.

* * *

She went out through the thin seam he had left her, the one that had betrayed them, the one place a body could pass the screen without tearing it, and she sealed it behind her with a single line of Moravian written low and tight against the ground so it cost almost nothing and showed less, and then she was outside the camp, alone, between the village and twelve men, in the wood she had walked every edge of and filed.

The sight gave her the whole of it. The patrol had spread into the trees, working toward the pressure they'd felt, careful, focuses up, and the nearest two were forty yards out and closing, and behind her the screen held like a held breath, and the cold working part laid out exactly what her job was. Not to win. There was no winning twelve trained mages with four spears. Her job was to make this wood frightening. To turn a patrol that had felt a wrongness into a patrol that had been bloodied by something in the dark and could not say what, so that they pulled back and reported a *thing in the eastern wood,* a thing with no light and no shape, and the city sent men to burn the trees and found nothing because by then the camp would be gone. Her job was to be a rumour with teeth. To do enough harm to send them home and not one watt more, because a watt more was a furnace, and a furnace was the truth.

She drew the first spear.

They were Damascus, the four of them, the watered grey steel Dangris had made her, and she had thrown them ten thousand times against the practice butts behind the meeting house in the year since Hazel, until the throw had stopped being a thing she did and become a thing her arm knew the way a hand knows a glove. She did not open her core to throw. That was the discipline of it, the new hard discipline, the one that looked from outside like doing nothing. She let her core stay a coal. She threw with her arm and her eye and the small ordinary push any mage could lend a focus, a candle's worth, a thing a screen could swallow whole, and she put the first spear through the shoulder of the nearest man before he had seen her at all.

He went down without a cry, which she had counted on, a shoulder and not a throat because a throat was a death and a death was a thing twelve men would close ranks around and hunt for, where a wound in the dark from nowhere was a thing twelve men told each other about in lowered voices for the rest of their lives. He went down and his focus went out and his light guttered in the sight, and the man behind him spun toward the place the spear had come from and threw a bolt of hard white into the trees, into the wrong trees, because she was already three steps left of where she had thrown from, low, moving the way Lor-ar moved, like spilled water.

She drew the second spear.

* * *

It went the way the best of her things went, fast and clean and almost silent, and she kept her mind off it while it happened because dwelling on it was a watt she could not spend. She threw the second spear through the thigh of a man who had got a light up over his head, a hard little sun of a thing meant to show them what was in the dark, and he dropped the light when the spear took him and it went out in the wet mould with a hiss, and the dark came back, her dark, the dark she could see in and they could not. She crossed behind a fallen oak with the third spear in her hand and the fourth at her back and her breath managed, even, her core a coal, and she felt the patrol's discipline begin to come apart in the sight, the spread careful line bunching, the men drawing toward each other the way frightened men do, which was exactly wrong, exactly what she needed, because a man alone is hard to find in the dark and four men in a knot are a target you cannot miss.

She put the third spear into the knot. Not at a man. At the ground in the middle of them, hard, so the watered steel went a hand deep into the leaf-mould between their boots and stood there humming, a spear out of nowhere planted in the earth at the centre of them with no mage and no light to show where it had come from, and at last she heard a man make a sound. A short broken sound, fear getting out before discipline could catch it, and then their lights came up all at once, four of them, five, the whole patrol throwing hard white into the trees in every direction, lighting the wood like noon, and finding nothing, because she was flat behind the oak with the fourth spear across her chest and her face in the wet leaves and her core a coal a screen could swallow, a girl-shaped absence in a wood full of light.

This was the part she had not been able to plan. Four spears, and three of them were out there in the wood now, in men and in the ground, and she had one left, and twelve men minus two on the ground was ten men with their lights up and their focuses warm and their fear turning, any second now, the way fear turns in trained men, from a thing that scatters them into a thing that makes them deadly. She had perhaps one more throw before the dark stopped being hers. And she had a furnace behind her ribs, banked to a coal, that could have ended it in a breath.

She felt the cost of not using it then, fully, the way Lor-ar had made her say the tree's price aloud and listen to it. It was not the cost to the rim. The rim would have been glad of it. It was the cost here, in the cold and the dark, in the man on the ground with her spear in his shoulder and the man with it in his thigh, in Hiram against the post pouring out the last white of himself, in the camp behind her holding its breath. The cost of restraint was not abstract and it did not fall on the ledger. It fell on the people she was protecting by being slow. Keeping the furnace banked meant Hiram bled himself grey to hide a fight she could have ended in a breath. It meant this took longer, and longer meant more lights, more throws, more chances for one of these frightened men to put a bolt of hard white through her in the dark, and if they did, the camp lost the one thing that could mend it and the one thing that could mend the world both, in a wood, over a patrol. Restraint had a body count. She was simply choosing where the bodies fell, and choosing to risk her own, because the other choice told the truth, and the truth was the only thing she could not afford to spend.

She held the coal. She drew the fourth spear. And she made it count.

* * *

She did not throw it. She had learned, throwing ten thousand times against the butts, that the spear in the hand frightens differently than the spear in the air, and she needed fright now, the last of it, the kind that turns a bloodied patrol toward home. She rose up out of the leaves behind the knot of them, close, inside the ring of their own lights where the dark was deepest because the light blinded them to it, and she put the fourth spear through the meat of the nearest man's calf from two feet away and let him see her do it. A girl. Silver-haired, slight, green-eyed, a child by the look of her, rising up out of the dark inside their own light with a grey spear in her hand and no fear on her face at all, here and then gone, because she let go of the spear the instant it bit and was three steps back into her own dark before he had finished falling, and what he carried home, what they all carried home, was not a patrol bloodied by enemy mages. It was worse. It was a wood that had a child in it who came up out of the dark inside your own light and put steel in you and was gone, and made no fire, and spoke no word, and could not be found.

It broke them. She felt it break in the sight, the discipline going all at once, the bunched knot of them turning from the wood and the wounded turning with them, dragging the man with the spear in his thigh and the man with the spear in his shoulder, lights still up and swinging wild, falling back east toward the grid line they'd come in on, fast, faster, a patrol that had found a thing in the dark and decided, the way men decide a thing together without a word, that whatever it was, it was not worth the wood. She held the dark and watched them go until the last white light was a glimmer through the trunks and then gone, and the grey hour came back, and the wood was only a wood, wet and ordinary, four spears poorer.

Her hands were steady. Her breath was even. Her core was a coal. She had done a hard thing the hard way, with her arm and her eye and not her furnace, and it had cost her four spears and the nearness of a hard white bolt in the dark twice over, and it had cost three men wounds they would carry, and it had cost Hiram everything he had, and she felt all of that, fully, in a way the rowan had not cost her anything at all. That was the difference she was only now learning to read. The easy thing cost the world and she could not feel it. The hard thing cost her and the people she loved and she felt every grain.

She went and pulled her spears from the wet earth and from where the men had dropped them, all four, and wiped them on the leaves, and went back to the seam in the screen, and unwrote her line, and went home.

* * *

Hiram was on the ground when she came through.

He had not let go of the screen. That was the thing she saw first, in the sight, before she saw the rest of it: the screen still held, taut and whole over the east face, fed by a core that had no business still burning, and under it Hiram had slid down the anchor-post to sitting and then past sitting to lying, one hand still up and flat against the grey wood as though he could hold a wall up from the floor, and the white had gone out of his core and left a thing the colour of ash with a coal somewhere far down in it, dim, the dimmest she had seen a living core go and still be living.

Mella was with him, the pale-blue-frocked woman with the silver-and-gold spectacles, on her knees in the dirt with both hands pressed to his chest and her worn locket swinging loose where she had not had time to click it shut, and Abe, the third ward-mage, spare and grey, was easing into the place Hiram had held, taking up the screen's weight a thread at a time so it would not drop when Hiram's hand finally came off the post, his own ash-coloured core flaring as it took the strain. And Jazen was there, on his knees in the dirt too, his close-cropped head bent over the old man, his careful hands doing the only thing careful hands could do, which was hold a dying man's other hand and be there while it happened or did not.

"He held it through," Jazen said, not looking up. "Whatever you did out there. They never read a watt of it. He held it the whole time." His voice did the thing Jazen's voice did, which was let the grief into it openly, no careful face, the tears already standing in his eyes and allowed to stand. "You stubborn old fool. You could have dropped it the second they turned. Abe had it. You could have let go."

"Couldn't," Hiram said. His eyes were shut. His hand was still on the post. "Not till I felt the last light leave the wood. Don't know how to. Trick of it. You hold till it's gone or you don't hold at all." A breath, shallow. "Did she send them home?"

"She sent them home."

"Spears?"

"Spears," said Viridia, kneeling. "Four wounded. No deaths. No light. They've gone east. They'll report a thing in the wood with no fire and no shape, and the city will burn the wrong trees a week from now, and we'll be gone."

Hiram's mouth moved, and it took her a moment to understand it was a smile, a small grey one. "Good girl," he said. "Did it small. Knew you would." His hand came off the post then, finally, slid down the grey wood and into the dirt, and Abe took the last thread of the screen onto himself with a grunt, and the cloth held, and Hiram did not die. He had spent everything down to the coal at the bottom and the coal held, dim and stubborn, a man on the last of his wick who would not, even now, go all the way out. But he would not hold a ward again. She could see that in the sight, the way you can see a thing is broken without a word for what. He had poured out the part of himself that did the holding and it would not come back, and they had three ward-mages, and now they had two, and one of those two was Abe, who was already grey before tonight.

She knelt in the dirt and looked at the dim coal of him and felt the easy thing rise in her like water, the new clean knowledge of how simple it would be. She could mend him. Not the death, he was not dead, but the spent place, the poured-out part, the way she had mended the worn-down hollow in the rowan where the pattern was guttering. She could find the seam where his holding had torn and let it close and give him back the white he'd burned and it would cost her a coal's worth of a furnace and cost the rim a hair, and Hiram would stand up whole and hold the ward and no one would ever know, and the camp would have its three.

*The small doors first.* She held the easy thing where it rose and she did not reach for it, and the not-reaching was the hardest thing in the wet grey dawn, harder than the spears. Because mending him here, in front of Mella and Abe and Jazen, was opening her core in the camp, was the truth, was the one thing she had just let a man bleed himself grey rather than spend. And because it was not the wall and it was not the bodies; it was a tired old man on the last of his wick, and if she would not hold the line even here, even for him, then there was no line, and she would mend the whole world by Tuesday because each single mercy was always, always easier than the cost of refusing it.

"Get him inside," she said, and stood, before her hands could decide the matter for her. "Warm. Sweet tea, if there's sugar. He'll keep. He'll just keep, that's all." She did not say *I could.* She had let no one watch her work since the year the world ended, and she was not going to start with a thing she could not take back. "Abe, can you hold it alone till light? An hour?"

"An hour," Abe said, grey and certain. "Not the day."

"You won't have to. We're not staying the day."

* * *

The council met as the sun came up, in the meeting house, around the scarred board where the children learned their letters, and there was no part of it that was not strain.

Brutus had a fist on the table the whole time, broad and sunburned, the hand that had built the camp board by board, and he kept it there like a man holding the table down. "They'll come back," he said. "Maybe not to this wood. But they felt the screen. A patrol felt a hidden thing and got bloodied for it, and that's a report that climbs. A week. Two. They'll send a column next time, not a patrol, and they'll burn the whole eastern wood to find what put steel in their men in the dark." He looked at his fist. "We empty out. We've known it. We empty out now and we don't argue the day."

"We argued the day for a reason," said Coram, old and soft-voiced. "There are sick. There are the very old. There's a woman due to deliver inside the fortnight. You don't march a camp at a day's notice without leaving some of it in the dirt behind you."

"You leave more of it in the dirt if a column comes," said the woman with the silver bun, in her cold even arithmetic, "while we're still here debating who can walk."

And so on, around the table, the strain of it, the impossible sum that would not come out, every road costing people, and Jazen at the head of it with his mother's careful hands spread on the wood and the grief of the dawn still on him, holding it, the way he held everything, by main force and the refusal to let go, which she had watched a dying man do an hour before with a screen.

She watched Jazen and she understood him better than she had the day before. He was Hiram. He was holding a thing that was tearing him and he did not know the trick of letting go, only the trick of holding till it was gone or not holding at all, and the thing he held was not a screen, it was all of them, every person around this table and every person in the lanes, and the war was the patrol, coming on, and there was no Abe to take the weight from him a thread at a time. There was only her. And the only weight she could take from him was the one she had let a man bleed grey rather than spend in front of witnesses, the furnace, the wall, the unforgivable easy thing.

She did not offer it. She had promised. She offered, instead, the small door.

"We march for the capital's reach in three days," Jazen said at last, because someone had to say it and it was his to say. "Not the city. The hills above the river, where Lightwell's people can shelter us a while and where we're a march, not a rumour, when we move on the walls. Three days to empty out clean. Brutus, you have the order of it. Coram, the slow and the sick go first and go cared for. We do not leave people in the dirt." He flattened his hands on the table. "And we go to war. There's no road left that isn't that one. I've turned this every way there is and there's no road but the walls."

"There's one," Viridia said.

The table looked at her. She did not often speak at the council, and when she did it was a single word, *yes,* or a true thing offered small, and they had learned to go quiet when she did.

"There's one road left that isn't the walls," she said. "It's a thin one. It probably closes. But it's still open this morning, and it's open for exactly as long as it takes you to empty this camp, and not a day longer, because once you're a march in the hills above the river you're a war they have to answer, and the door I'm talking about shuts the second you become a war." She turned to Jazen. "Your mother left it. A man in the Conclave with no stomach for any of this. I sent him the true thing two nights ago and he answered the same day, up the rail-wire, which means he's frightened, which means he's real. He answered three words. *Who else knows.*" She let it sit on the table the way it had sat on hers. "I can't tell yet whether he's a door or a trap. There's only one way to learn it. Let me try to reach him. Properly. Not a letter down a courier road. Let me try to reach the moderates in the capital before the march makes it impossible. Three days. The same three days you need to empty out. I spend them on the door while you load the carts. If it's a trap, we've lost nothing we weren't going to lose. If it's a door." She stopped. She would not oversell it. "If it's a door, then somewhere behind those walls there are people who'd rather not do this, and I'd like to find out how many before we make them choose a side."

The table was quiet. Brutus's fist had not moved. The woman with the silver bun was running her cold arithmetic and Viridia could see her not finding a reason to say no in it, which from her was assent. Coram had his soft old eyes on Jazen.

And Jazen held her eyes across the board where the children learned their letters, with his mother's careful hands resting on the wood and his sister two years burned and his mother freshly dead and a dying old man warm under blankets in the next building because a girl had chosen restraint over the easy thing, and she watched him weigh the three words, *who else knows,* the menace in them and the maybe, and weigh the two days the door would take from the three he needed, and weigh, last, the thing she could see he had stopped letting himself believe, which was that the war might not, after all, have to be the only road.

"You understand," he said slowly, "that if he's a trap, what you'll be doing is walking toward the one man in the capital who's already asked, in writing, exactly where the rest of us are."

"I understand it better than you do," she said. "I'm the one who has to decide whether to answer him."

The corner of Jazen's mouth moved, not a smile, the place a smile would go on a man who had not smiled in a long time. He looked down at his mother's hands on the wood, the careful ones, the ones that had sent a girl an unsigned note and an open circle and a way to reach a frightened man, and when he looked back up the grief was still standing openly in his eyes and something else was standing beside it.

"Three days," he said. "Spend them on the door. I'll empty the camp." He flattened his hands once, the gesture that meant a thing was decided. "And Viridia. When you answer him." He stopped, and chose the words the way his mother would have, with weightless precision, every one paid for. "Don't lie to him. A careful man can forgive a hard truth. He never forgives the day he learns you'll lie."

She thought about the cot, and the stone at her throat, and the two ends of it she had been holding unjoined all night, the truth that gave him the camp and the lie that lost the road. And she understood that Jazen, who held things till they were gone, had just handed her the trick of it, the only trick, which was that there was no third end to hold, and the choosing was hers, and the window for choosing was three days wide and getting narrower every hour they sat here over the children's board while the carts stood empty in the grey.

"I won't lie to him," she said.

She did not say what she would do instead, because she had not decided, and she would not put a false thing in the air merely to close a sentence. She stood, and the council stood, and the camp that had three days to disappear began, in the cold morning light through the meeting-house door, to take itself apart.
