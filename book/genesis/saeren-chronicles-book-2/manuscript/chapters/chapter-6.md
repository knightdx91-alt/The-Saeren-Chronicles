# Chapter 6: The Window Narrows

The patrol came on the third afternoon, two days before the march, and it came from the wrong direction, which was the first thing that was wrong, and it came too quietly, which was the second.

Viridia felt them before the sentries did, because she was carrying water again, and a girl carrying water with her sight half-open across the camp could not help but notice when six new cores came over the rise to the east where no cores should be. She set the buckets down without spilling them, which was a small precise act she would remember later as the last ordinary thing, and she counted. Six. Moving in the spread loose line of men who knew their work. Not flaring, not casting, banked low, which meant they were trying not to be felt, which meant they already suspected there was something here to feel.

She did not run, because a running girl is a thing soldiers shoot at on principle. She walked, fast, to Jazen's house, and found him already coming out, his face gone to the flat hard thing it became when the leader took over from the man, and she understood that he had felt them too, the moment they topped the rise, because he held a corner of the camp's screen in his own hands and a hand on a wall feels the knock.

"Six," she said.

"Six," he agreed. "Scouts. If they were sure, there'd be sixty. They're not sure. They've a rumor and a direction and they've come to see whether the rumor has walls." He was already moving, low and quick, and she went with him, because no one had told her not to and she was not going to be told now. "Which means we have one job and one only, which is to make sure not one of them goes back east able to say what he found. If a single scout reaches the capital with the word *here*, the sixty come, and the march we've planned for two days from now happens tonight, on their terms, against our weakest, with the children still half of them in the camp." He caught a passing runner by the arm, spoke three fast words, and the runner went pale and ran. "The wards stay up. We do not flare. The moment this place lights up with magic, every man on that rise knows exactly what he's found. We take them quiet, in the trees, before they see the screen, with hands and steel and nothing that shines. Can you do that."

"Yes," Viridia said.

"You have spears. I've seen them in your room corner. I made it my business." His eyes came to hers, hard and direct. "I need you to be the girl with the spears today and not the girl who could end it with a thought, because the thought lights us up and gets us all killed. Steel only. Can you hold to that, with your friend in a workhouse and the means to flatten six men in your chest."

It was the right question, and he was right to ask it, and she felt the truth of it settle into her like a cold stone. The thing she could do would save these six minutes and cost the camp the night. The hard way, the small way, the way that cost her sweat and risk and nothing the enemy could feel, was the only way that worked.

"Steel only," she said. "Get me to the trees before they reach the screen."

***

She went out through the eastern seam with four men of the camp and her four Damascus spears and Lor-ar a low blue-green shadow at her side, shrunk from his full size to something that could move through brush without folding saplings, and Raizen she had left behind, shut in Jazen's house with strict instruction, because a prismatic dragon in afternoon light was the precise opposite of not shining, and he had not liked it, and she had not had time to be kind about it.

The trees took them in. She had walked this ground in the first days, mapping it the way she mapped everything, and she knew the lie of it, where the brush thinned and where a man would naturally step to keep his footing quiet, and she put the camp's men where the scouts would not look and took for herself the place where they would. She went still against the bole of an old oak with two spears in the ground at her feet and one in her hand and the fourth across her back, and she let her breathing go long and slow and quiet, and she waited, and she did not think about Alice, and she did not think about the thing in her chest, and she made herself, by main force, only a girl with a spear behind a tree, because that was the only thing it was safe to be.

The first scout came past her at twelve feet, and he was good, he was quiet, he moved his eyes before he moved his feet, and he never once looked up into the oak's low fork where she had not gone, because she had guessed he would expect the high hide and she had taken the low one instead, pressed to the trunk at the height of an ordinary shadow. She let him pass. She let the second pass behind him. And when the third came through the gap, a little apart from the others, the way the rear man always falls a little apart, she came off the trunk with the spear already moving, and she did the thing the Dangris had taught her, which was not to aim for the man but to aim for the place the man would be, and the Damascus point took him under the arm where the leather gapped, and she put her whole weight behind it the way Venquar had drilled into her until her shoulders ached for a week, and the scout folded down around the shaft without a sound louder than a cough.

Always watch the body, Nargash had said. She watched it. She had it on the ground and the spear out and her hand over its mouth before the cough finished, and the body told her, in the way bodies do, that it was done, and she let it down quiet into the leaf-mould and was already turning, because there were five more and the trees were not large enough to hide a fight for long.

To her left one of the camp's men took the second scout the way they had agreed, low and from behind, an arm across the throat and a knife where it needed to go, and it was ugly and silent and quick, and the camp's man came up grey-faced and shaking because he was a baker and not a soldier, a man who powered no wards and swung no spears in his ordinary life and had volunteered for the trees anyway because the alternative was waiting in the camp to learn how it had gone. Viridia caught his eye across the gap and held it for half a breath, long enough to steady him, because a shaking man makes noise, and a noise now killed them all, and the baker swallowed and stilled and nodded, and she thought, even in the middle of it, that the bravery in these woods was not hers, who had a furnace in her chest and a tiger at her side, but his, who had a bread-paddle's worth of training and a family in the camp behind him and had come out anyway. She filed him to thank later, if there was a later, and turned to find the others.

***

It went wrong in the middle, the way these things do.

The fourth scout turned at the wrong instant, some animal sense pricking, and saw the camp's man closing on the second, and his mouth opened to shout, and Viridia was twenty feet off with a spear in her hand and a clear throw and a terrible choice, because a thrown spear leaves your hand and then you have three spears and a man who has seen where you are, and she threw it anyway because a shout was the one thing that could not be allowed, and it flew true and took him in the throat and the shout died as a wet sound, but the throw had cost her the spear, and the fifth scout had seen the throw, and now the fifth scout knew exactly where she stood, and he was already bringing his hand around, and his hand was not empty, it held a focus, a short black rod, and she felt him reach for his magic to do the thing Jazen had forbidden any of them to do, to light up the woods with a working that every man for a mile would feel, a flare, a beacon, *here, here, they are here.*

And the want came up in her, vast and clean and instant, to simply reach across the twenty feet and unmake him, to do the thing she had done to a captain in a burning corridor, to spend a half-thought and have him not be, and it would be so easy, it was always so easy, and her hand had already half-risen with the gift gathering hot behind her ribs before she caught it.

She caught it. She would turn the nearness of it over for weeks, the way you keep returning to a stair you nearly fell down. She did not catch it with calm. There was no calm in it. She caught it the way you catch a dropped knife, fast and clumsy and with a small animal cry behind her teeth that she bit down on, and she slammed the gathering gift back down behind her ribs so hard that her vision whited at the edges and her nose began, warm and sudden, to bleed, and instead of the thing that would shine she did the thing that would not: she drew the third spear off her back and threw it, low and flat and human, and it was a worse throw than the first because her hands were shaking now, but it did not have to be a good throw, it only had to spoil his, and it took him in the shoulder and turned him, and his flare went up into the dirt at his feet instead of into the sky, a dull contained thump of black light that died in the leaf-mould, felt by no one past the trees, and then Lor-ar was on him, two hundred pounds of sylvan tiger out of the brush, and the fifth scout was done.

The sixth ran. The sixth was wise, the sixth had seen enough, and the sixth turned east and ran for the rise and the long road home with the word *here* in his mouth, and Viridia had no spear left in her hand, two in bodies and one spoiling a flare in the dirt, and she would not, could not, reach for the gift again, not so soon, not with her nose streaming and the white still crowding her sight, and for one heartbeat she thought he would make it.

He did not make it. Lor-ar made it not so. The great tiger went from the fifth man's ruin to a blue-green streak through the undergrowth faster than the eye could hold, and ran the sixth scout down within forty yards, and it was over, and the woods were quiet, and not one working had gone up bright enough for a watcher on the rise to feel, and the camp behind its screen had not lit up, and the rumor that had walked east with six men would not finish the walk.

Viridia stood against the oak with the blood running off her chin and her three empty hands and shook, once, hard, all over, the way the body shakes when it is allowed to at last, and then she stopped, because there was no time, and wiped her face on her sleeve, and went to count the bodies and make sure of the quiet, because Nargash had taught her that the work is not done when the enemy falls, the work is done when you have watched the body long enough to be sure, and she watched them, all six, long enough to be sure, and hated it, and did it anyway.

Then there was the other work, the work no one warns you of, which was the six of them to be carried deep and put under leaf-mould and turned earth where the sixty, when they came, would find no trace to read, because a buried scout was a missing man and a missing man was only a rumor, but a scout left lying was a map with an arrow on it. The camp's men did most of the carrying, quietly, grimly, and Viridia helped, because she would not stand clean-handed over work her spears had made, and she learned, hauling a dead man by the shoulders through the brush with the light failing, that there was a particular tiredness that came after, heavier than the fighting, the tiredness of the part of you that has to put the world back the way it was before anyone notices what you did to it. Her father had come home with a version of that tiredness some nights, from the factory, though his had cost no lives. She had not understood it then. She understood it now, and wished she did not, and filed it, and kept hauling.

***

Hiram was the cost, in the end, though he had not been within a mile of the trees.

He had felt the fifth scout's flare go up, dull and contained as it was, against the inside of the screen he was holding, and he had done the only thing a man holding a wall can do when something strikes it, which was to push back, to thicken the screen against the place the blow had landed, instinctive, the way you tense against a shove, and the pushing had cost him more than he had to spend. They found him collapsed at the north anchor-post when it was over, grey to the lips and breathing wrong, his thread guttering so low that Viridia, coming back through the seam with the blood dried brown on her face, saw the camp's whole invisible roof shudder with it, a long sick flicker that ran the entire curve of the bowl before it caught and steadied, barely.

"He's not gone," said the woman tending him, a hard-handed practical woman Viridia did not know. "But he's spent past the bottom. He'll not hold the wall again. Not for weeks, if ever. Whatever he had, that flare drew the last of it out of him." She looked up. "We're down to two now. Jazen and Abe. And Jazen marches in two days."

Viridia knelt by Hiram Vell, who had told her, against a post, that after the children were out it did not much matter, and she looked at his guttering thread with the sight she could not turn off, and the old temptation rose in her again, quieter now, exhausted, but there, always there: she could mend him. Not the world. Just this one tired man. Pour in the smallest flicker and he would hold the wall for years.

And she did not, again, because it was his and not hers, and because Jazen had been right in the trees, that the thing she could do was a beacon, and a man whose dead-spent core suddenly blazed back to a year's strength in an afternoon was a beacon of a different kind, a thing the other two ward-mages would feel at once and not understand, a stranger's grip on a wall that wanted only three pairs of hands. She put her own hand over Hiram's cold one instead, which cost her nothing and gave him nothing but the knowing, and she said his whole name again, Hiram Vell, so that the man and not the wall would hear it, and his eyes found her and he managed, barely, the ghost of the thing that wanted to be humor.

"Told you," he breathed. "After the children. Doesn't much matter."

"It matters," Viridia said. "You held it through a flare. The scouts are dead and the camp didn't light and the word didn't get east, and the reason is you pushed back on a wall when a lesser man would have let it tear. You bought us the two days. Rest now. You've earned the not-mattering, and you're not going to get it, because I'm going to keep coming back to tell you that you matter until you're too well to argue."

It was the longest thing she had said to anyone all day, and it cost her, and she let it, because some costs were the right ones to pay.

***

Jazen found her at the seam afterward, in the last light, while the camp moved with the grim new speed of people who have just learned exactly how close the edge is.

"You bled," he said. He had seen the brown on her face. "Not from a wound. You've no wound. So you bled from the inside, which means you reached for the thing and pulled it back. In the middle of a fight, with a man bringing up a flare, you chose the spear and the nosebleed over the easy thing." He was quiet a moment. "I asked you this morning whether you could hold to steel with the means to flatten six men in your chest. I didn't actually believe you could. I've never met anyone who could, including me. I flattened a town once because I couldn't." He looked at her with the recalculating look gone all the way to something like awe and something like fear, because on him awe and fear were cut from the one cloth, the way grief and humor were on Drake. "You held. It nearly killed you to hold, I can see that, your eyes still aren't right. But you held, and the camp's still hidden, and that's the whole reason we have two more days instead of a battle tonight."

"It was close," Viridia said. She would not let him make it cleaner than it was. "It was the closest it's been. I had my hand half up. If Lor-ar hadn't been there to run the last one down, I can't swear I'd have held the second time. Don't make me a thing I'm not. I'm a person who barely didn't, today. That's all. Barely didn't."

"That's all anyone who holds is," Jazen said. "A person who barely didn't, over and over, until the day they don't. I know that better than the council does." He turned to look east, where the rise stood empty now and the six were cooling in the leaf-mould. "We can't wait the two days. You know that. They sent six scouts to a rumor. When the six don't come home, the rumor becomes a certainty, and the certainty sends sixty, and they'll be combing this ground inside three days looking for the patrol that didn't return. The window I thought we had just closed. We finish moving the last of the children tomorrow night, and we march at dawn the day after, a day early, because the choice was taken out of my hands in the trees this afternoon." He looked back at her. "There's one more thing. A runner came in from the south while you were in the woods. From Thorn. From your man with the open circle." He held out a folded paper, sealed with a smear of grey wax. "He answered. I haven't read it. It's addressed, in flower-code, to the friend of the friend who taught him to leave the open circle. That's you. Whatever road that is, it's still open, for now. You'd best read it before we march, because once we're on the road, the only roads left are the ones that end at a wall."

Viridia took the folded paper with the blood still dried on her knuckles, and felt the first of the peace roads pull taut in her hand, answered, alive, against every odd, two days before a war, and she did not open it there at the seam with the light going and the camp moving fast and frightened around her. She held it, the way she held everything, until the moment was right.

***

The moment was an hour later, washed and changed, in Jazen's house, with the lamp lit and Amber sitting against the wall and Raizen wound so tightly around Viridia's neck that she could barely turn her head.

He had not forgiven her for the shutting-in. He had felt the whole skirmish down the bond, the throw and the nosebleed and the white at the edges of her sight, and had been able to do nothing about any of it from behind a door, and a young creature who exists to be the part that is not afraid does not take kindly to being locked away from the part that is. *You bled,* he had said, the instant she came through the door, accusing, *you bled and I was in a house. I am never to be left in a house again. I do not care if I shine like a second sun. I would rather we both die bright than that I sit in a house and feel you bleed through a wall.* And she had not argued, because there was no arguing with it, and because some part of her, the part that had stood alone against an oak with three empty hands, agreed with him entirely. So he rode her neck now and would not be put down, and she let him, and broke the grey wax seal with her thumb.

The hand inside was a stranger's, careful, educated, the hand of a man used to writing things that could be read by people he could not afford to trust. Jazen read the flower-code off it slowly, translating under his breath, and the three of them leaned into the lamplight, and the message, stripped of its disguise, was short, and it was not a yes, and it was not a no, and it was the most frightening thing any of them had read in a week, because it was a man cracking open.

*I did the thing you said,* the open-circle man had written. *Alone. In the dark, where no one could see my face, as you instructed, because I will admit I did it half to prove you a liar. I reached for my own cut. I found it. I have been a mage forty years and I never once reached for it because I was taught there was nothing there to reach for, and there is something there, frayed and capped and waiting, exactly as you said, and I have not slept since. If this is true of me it is true of every soul in the Conclave, in the capital, in the world, and it means the thing we have built our whole order upon, that some are born to the gift and some are born lesser, is not a law of nature but a wound someone chose to make. I do not yet believe you. I cannot yet not believe you. I will hear more, from a mouth and not a page, if you can get a mouth to me safely. But understand what you are asking. You are asking me to carry to the Conclave the news that everything we are is founded on a lie, with a war already lit and the Chancellor already calling your people corruptors, and the first man to say it aloud in that room will not leave it alive. Send me your mouth. I will listen. I promise nothing else, and I am afraid of even that.*

The lamp ticked. Nobody said anything for a moment.

"That's not a no," Amber said at last, quietly. "God help him, that's a man who reached into himself and found out his whole life was built on a cut. That's the worst no-that-isn't-a-no I've ever heard." She looked at Viridia. "He wants a mouth. A person. In the capital. Before or during a war, to walk into the one room where saying the true thing gets you killed, and say it anyway, well enough to turn at least one frightened man." She already knew the answer. Her face said she already hated it. "You're going to be the mouth."

"I'm going to be the mouth," Viridia agreed. "I was always going to be the mouth. There isn't another one. Jazen can't, they hunt him by name. You can't, you're spent and they'd take one look at your core. It has to be someone who can reach the capital unseen, who knows the whole truth in her own hands and not off a page, who can prove the cut to a man's face the way the letter proved it to him alone." She turned the broken seal over in her fingers. "It has to be a person who can shadow-walk into a guarded city, sit across from a terrified councillor, and show him his own wound without lighting up every glass of true seeing in the building. There's one of those. I'm holding a dragon and reading his letter."

"And it puts you in the capital," Amber said. "Which is where Alice is. You've noticed that. The road to the man with the open circle runs straight through the city that's holding your friend."

"I've noticed," Viridia said. "I'm trying not to let it be the reason. It's a real road on its own. But yes. I've noticed."

Jazen folded the letter along its crease and set it down. "Then it's decided, and it changes the march," he said. "We move at dawn the day after tomorrow, all of us, on the capital. But you go ahead of the army, quiet, by the short way only you can take, into the city, to the man with the open circle. If you can turn the Conclave, or one voice in it, before our two armies are close enough to bleed on each other, then maybe the war stops with a vote instead of a field. It's thin. It's one frightened councillor and a girl with a dragon against six hundred years of certainty. But it's a road, and you made me a promise once that you'd walk every road there was before the last one, and this is one, so walk it." He looked at her, and the leader gave way to the son for a moment. "And if it fails, you'll be inside the walls already, near your friend, when the army arrives and the quiet roads close. I'd be lying if I said I'd planned it that way. But I'd be lying worse if I said I was sorry it fell out so."

Viridia kept the broken seal in her hand, the second road and the first one running now down the same dark street toward the same walled city, the peace and the rescue braided into one journey she would make ahead of an army, and she did not put the letter away. She kept it, where she could feel it, the way she kept everything that might, against all sense, matter, and she had promised herself she would walk every road that did.
