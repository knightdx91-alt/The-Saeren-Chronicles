# Chapter 7: Dust to Dust

The resistance left its home at dawn, and Viridia learned that an army of the grieving does not march so much as it migrates, the way a flock lifts off water, raggedly, all at once, with a great deal of noise and no apparent leader and then, somehow, a direction.

There were perhaps three hundred who could fight, and they filed out through the wide-thrown seam in the grey light with whatever they could carry, and behind them the camp stood empty for the first morning in the years of its hiding, the lanes bare, the long table where Mirelle had taught letters folded and left, the well with its sticking handle abandoned to the woods. The last of the children had gone south two nights before. What walked east now toward the capital was the part of the camp that had decided it would rather end the thing than keep hiding from it, and Viridia walked among them with Raizen on her shoulder and Lor-ar pacing the column's flank, and she watched Brutus go by with a pack on his back and a long-hafted hammer over it, the man who had built the place walking away from it without looking back, which cost him something she could see in the set of his shoulders and which he would not have thanked her for naming.

She passed Mella near the head of the column, walking with the careful upright gait of a woman who has decided not to let anyone see how tired she is, and the locket was gone from her throat, and when Viridia's eyes went to the bare place where it had hung, Mella touched it without seeming to decide to, the old gesture finding nothing there. "Sent it south with the little ones," she said, catching the look. "There's a likeness of their mother in it. My daughter. She didn't live to walk a road like this, so I sent her on ahead, in the locket, with her own babies, where it's safe. A foolishness. But you carry what you can't carry any other way, don't you. You'd know about that." And she walked on before Viridia could answer, which was a mercy, because Viridia had no answer that would not have cost her, and she filed the bare throat and the sent-ahead likeness somewhere very deep, beside the dark-blue stone she would never send anywhere, because some things you keep against your own skin precisely because you could not survive the sending of them.

Abe walked near the rear, grey and spent, one of two ward-mages left where there had been three, no longer holding anything up because there was no longer a camp to hide, only a tired man with a long road in front of him and the particular hollowness of a person whose great work is finished and was, in the end, abandoned rather than lost. Viridia marked him too. She marked all of them. It was the only honoring she knew how to give, the noticing, and she gave it as she walked, to the baker who had taken a scout in the trees and not made a sound, to the smith favoring his bad leg, keeping pace on a crutch he had made himself, to the three hundred who had decided that walking toward the thing was better than waiting for it.

She would not march with them long. That was the plan, hammered out by lamplight after the scouts. The army would take the slow road, four days to the capital, gathering what scattered dark-mage cells would join them on the way. Viridia would go the fast road, the one only she could take, ahead of everyone, in to the man with the open circle, to try the thing that might make the slow road unnecessary. But before she went, there was a thing she needed, and there was only one place to get it, and it was not on the way to anywhere.

"You're sure they'll have you," Jazen said, walking beside her at the head of the column. "The dragons. You're sure of the welcome."

"I'm sure of Varissa," Viridia said. "She's Raizen's mother. She'd cross the world for a look at him. Drake is harder, but Drake said a thing to me the night he brought Raizen back. He said the pact reached past the gate of any one school, and that if the need ran deep enough I might call on it, and be surprised by what answered." She shifted Raizen's weight. "I think I'm allowed to call. I think I'm supposed to. And I need to ask Drake to teach me something before I walk into that city, because if the peace fails, I want to be carrying the one thing that can end a working cleanly, and he's the only one alive who can teach it."

Jazen looked at her sidelong. "The death symbol," he said. "My mother's papers mentioned it. A Moravian character that ends any magic. Closes portals. Stops spells." He frowned. "That's a strange thing to want to carry into a peace talk."

"It's not for the peace talk," Viridia said. "It's for if there's no peace, and a portal tears, or a working goes wrong, or the Chancellor does the thing the Chancellor does. It's a door I want to know how to close before I'm standing in a burning building looking for it." She did not say the rest, which was that she had watched a captain die at Hazel because she had improvised a death-symbol out of fear and instinct, linking his protection-rune to an explosion, and that she never again wanted to do that thing clumsily, by accident, in a panic. If she was going to be able to end a magic, she wanted to know exactly how, so that she would never do it except on purpose. "Drake offered. I'm going to take him up on it. I'll be a day. I'll catch the column by the second night, or meet you at the staging ground past the river."

***

She opened the portal herself, which still felt like a borrowed thing, a gift from the source she had not earned so much as been handed, and she took Raizen and stepped through it out of the marching column and into the high cold country where the dragons kept their reach.

It was a place of bare stone and wind, a long valley above the tree-line where the rock had been scorched in long black streaks and melted in places to glass, the sign, she understood, of very large things breathing fire over a very long time. Varissa came down to them before Viridia had taken three steps, brilliant scarlet against the grey stone, and she did not bother with a human shape, she came as herself, vast and flame-bright, and folded down around her son with a sound Viridia felt in her chest more than heard, a deep thrumming croon, and Raizen left Viridia's shoulder in a streak of every color and met his mother in the air and they tangled together, the great red dragon and the small prismatic one, and for a moment Viridia simply stood on the scorched stone and let them have it, the reunion she had no part in, the mother and the child, and tried not to feel the particular ache of being the orphan watching it.

*He has grown,* Varissa said at last, settling, with Raizen draped triumphant along her foreleg. *And he speaks. And he has been bled near, I can feel it on the bond he carries to you, he has been frightened for you, which is good for him and bad for my temper.* One enormous eye fixed on Viridia, fierce and warm at once. *You took my son into a war.*

"I didn't take him," Viridia said. "He wouldn't be left. I tried. He told me he would sooner we both burn together than sit shut in a house and feel me hurt through a wall."

Varissa made the chest-deep sound again, and it was, Viridia realized, something like a laugh. *Yes,* she said. *That is his father in him, and his father's father, which is me. We do not sit in houses. It is our great failing and our only virtue.* The fierceness gentled, fractionally. *You have kept him whole. I smell no lasting hurt on him. I will not pretend I do not wish him a hundred miles from any war, but I bonded his father knowing what dragons are, and I will not insult my son by wishing him smaller than he is.*

"I came for more than a welcome," Viridia said, because Varissa was owed the truth plainly, and because she had not flown to a valley of dragons to make small talk. "I'm going into the capital tomorrow night. Alone, ahead of the army, by a road only I can take. I came to ask Drake to teach me a thing. But I came to ask you something too, because you've made portals, and you've been near that city in the long wars, and I haven't, and I'd be a fool to walk in blind when there's five thousand years of knowing it sitting in this valley."

Varissa's great head came around, and the fierce eye sharpened into something more attentive, more pleased, the look of a creature unused to being asked. *Then ask. Few think to. They take what Drake offers and forget there are two of us, and that the one who is not death has spent those same five thousand years learning how the living arrange themselves behind walls.*

"The capital," Viridia said. "How is it warded. If I shadow-walk in, what will feel me arrive."

*The walls themselves, no,* Varissa said, after a moment of the deep consideration Viridia was learning to wait through. *Stone does not feel a shadow pass. But they keep watchers, in the old wars they kept watchers, mages set at the high points whose whole task is to feel for a working that should not be there, the way your ward-mages feel a hand on their screen. Shadow-walking is a working. A small one, a clean one, but a working. Arrive far from the high points, in a deep shadow, in a low place, a cellar, a drain, a back court where the dark is honest, and arrive once and do not do it twice in the same night, because a watcher who feels one strange thing doubts himself, and a watcher who feels two strange things sends men.* The eye held hers. *And do not, whatever you do, open a portal inside those walls. A portal is a shout. A shadow is a whisper. You may whisper your way in. You must walk or be carried your way out, the slow human way, or whisper out again from a different dark, but you may not shout, not once, or the whole city turns its head.*

It was exactly the kind of thing Viridia had not known to ask and would have died not knowing, and she filed every word of it, and felt the particular gratitude of being handed a true and useful thing by someone who had waited a long time to be asked for it.

"Thank you," she said. "That's worth the flight on its own."

*It is worth more than the flight, and you will find out how much in a cellar somewhere, frightened, when you remember to arrive only once.* Varissa nudged Raizen gently off her foreleg, back toward Viridia. *Go up to his father. Drake is at the top, being old and difficult, which is how he grieves when he is not being loud about it. He has been waiting for you since before you thought to come. He felt you decide it. And girl.* The fierce warmth again, banked low. *Bring my son back from this city. I have given a great deal to the world's wars already. I would keep the one thing I made that is wholly good.*

***

Drake was at the head of the valley, black against the black scorched stone, so still that she did not see him until he moved, and when he spoke his voice came up through the rock the way it always did, felt before heard.

"You have come for the symbol," he said. "I knew you would. I have known since I taught your dragon to hunt and watched you decide, at a seam in the dark, to walk into a war. A person who decides that comes, sooner or later, for the way to close a door." The great head lowered. "I will teach it to you. But first you will listen to an old thing tell you why he teaches it as grief and not as a weapon, because if you learn it as a weapon you will use it as one, and there are perhaps four beings left alive who should be trusted with it, and I am about to make you the fifth, and I would like to be sure of you first."

"I'll listen," Viridia said, and sat down on the cold stone, because you listened to a thing six thousand years old sitting down, and because she had learned that the listening was itself a kind of payment, the only coin some beings would take.

He did not begin with the symbol. He began, the way the old begin, somewhere off to the side, with a thing that turned out not to be off to the side at all.

"I had a son," Drake said. "Before Raizen. Long before. In the last war but one, when men and beasts still fought on the same side as often as not, before the cutting made the world forget that the pact had ever meant anything. His name does not matter to you and I will not spend it. He was younger than I am now by four thousand years, which made him a child, in the way our kind reckons children, headstrong and bright and certain the world was simpler than it is." The black flames at the corners of his mouth guttered low. "He died in that war. Not in fire. Not by spear. A mage on the other side had learned a half-version of the symbol I am about to teach you, learned it wrong, learned it as a weapon, and he used it on my son's bond to his rider, thinking to kill the rider through the beast. It did not kill the rider. It unmade the bond. And my son, who was a True-Bonded dragon, the way Raizen is True-Bonded to you, did not survive the unmaking, because a True Bond is not a rope you can cut and leave both ends living. It is one life in two bodies. The mage reached into that one life with a word he did not understand and tore it down the middle, and both halves died, and the mage was so pleased with what he had done that he did it twice more before someone put a spear through him, and he never once understood that he had not been killing. He had been ending. He thought a clever weapon. He had a door he could not stop himself walking through."

Viridia held very still on the bare rock, and did not say she was sorry, because *sorry* was a small coin and this was not a small grief, and Drake would have heard the smallness in it.

"I tell you this," Drake went on, "so that you will understand exactly what I am putting into your head, and exactly why I have spent the four thousand years since making sure no one could ever learn it by halves again. The symbol is the character for Death. Not killing. Death. The two are not the same, and the whole of the danger is in mistaking one for the other. A spear kills. Fire kills. Those are killings, and they are loud and they are partial and the world goes on around them. The symbol does not kill. It ends. It speaks to a magic the true word for what waits at the end of all magic, and the magic, hearing its own true name, simply stops being. A portal does not close. It was never opened. A working does not fail. It unhappens. A bond." He paused, and she felt the pause go all the way down into six thousand years and a dead son. "A bond severed with the symbol is not broken. It is unmade. The two who shared it do not grieve a thing that ended. They are left with a wrongness they cannot name, a room in themselves where a thing used to be that now never was. You have seen what it did to my house. I will not describe what it did to the rider who lived. I broke the teaching of it that day, hunted down every half-copy, burned the one scroll that held it whole. I am rebuilding it now, in your head, because the world has come to a place where the alternative is worse, and because of all the beings I have known in six thousand years, you are the one I judge least likely to mistake an ending for a weapon. Do not make me wrong, girl. I have been wrong once about a child and a symbol. I have not the heart to be wrong twice."

Viridia kept still, and let his dead son sit in the air between them for a while, unhurried, because she would not be the one to move it along. Then she asked the thing she had come to ask.

"Why me," she said. "You said four should be trusted with it. Why make me the fifth."

"Because you are the near hand of the source," Drake said simply. "Because the day is coming, I think, when you will hold every magic in the world in your two hands at once, and a thing that holds every magic must know, absolutely, how to let one go cleanly, or it will keep them all by force, which is the other name for what the capital does, only larger. You need the symbol not to kill with. You need it so that when you are the largest living thing there is, you will know how to take your hand off a magic. That is the whole of why. The ending is the proof that the holding is a choice." The black eye held hers. "Do you understand the difference, girl. Between holding a thing because you have decided to, and gripping it because you cannot make yourself stop."

"Yes," Viridia said. "I think it's the only difference there is. It's the same difference as trying the other roads. It's the same difference as Hiram's core being his and not mine." She met the ancient eye. "It's the difference between me and the council that cut the world. I keep finding it everywhere. I think it might be the only thing this whole war is actually about."

Drake was quiet for a long moment, and then he made a sound she had not heard from him before, low and rumbling, and she understood after a moment that it was approval, the grudging approval of a thing that did not give it.

"Then you are the fifth," he said. "Attend. I will teach you Death, and you will learn it in one sitting, because I will not have you carrying it half-known into a city. And when you have it, you will promise me one thing, and you will mean it the way you mean things, with your whole shut face, or I will take it back out of your head, which I can do, and which would hurt us both."

"What's the promise."

"That you will never use it on a living core," Drake said. "Not the worst soldier. Not the Chancellor himself. The symbol ends magic, and a person is more than their magic, but a person whose magic is ended is a thing I will not describe to you twice. Use it on workings. On portals. On the dead things people make. Never on the living thread inside a breathing body, however much you want to, however much it would solve. That is the line. It is the only line that matters with this. Promise me, and mean it, and I will give you the most dangerous word in the world."

Viridia thought about it, honestly, the way she thought about everything, because a promise made lightly was worse than none, and she turned it over and found she could keep it, found that she wanted to keep it, that the line Drake drew was the same line she had been drawing for herself since the rise, since Hiram against his post, since the clearing with the rowan, the line between mending and unmaking, between holding by choice and holding by force.

"I promise," she said. "I'll never use it on a living core. On workings and portals and the dead things, yes. On a person, never. I mean it the whole way down."

"Then watch," said Drake, and he lifted one black talon, and he wrote, in the air, slowly, the way a master writes for a pupil and not the way a mage writes for use, the character for the end of all things, in fire that was not fire but the cold black flame of his death-affinity.

It was not a complicated shape. That frightened her before anything else did. The largest workings she had met were thickets of stroke and intent, layered and braced, the way the capital's portal-scrolls hid their requirement under a hundred lesser marks. This was three strokes. Three strokes and a stillness at the center, a kind of held breath made visible, and she understood as she watched it burn that the simplicity was the whole of its terror, that the largest thing a magic could be asked to do, to stop being, was also the plainest, because there was nothing to build, only a true name to speak and a letting-go to mean. She took it in the way she took the Moravian, whole, where no book could burn it out, and felt it settle behind her ribs beside the furnace and the banked light, a third thing now, small and cold and patient, a door she knew how to close.

"You have it," Drake said. He did not make it a question. He could see it in her, the way Jazen read the heft of a man's magic across a field. "I felt it take. Now hear the last of the teaching, which is not a stroke but a discipline. The symbol wants to be used. All true names want to be spoken, it is in their nature, the way a held breath wants to be let go. You will feel it, in a hard moment, offer itself. You will be standing somewhere with a portal tearing or a working coming at you or a thing you love in danger, and the cold small door behind your ribs will say, very reasonably, that it could simply end this, and it will be right, and it will be the easiest thing, and that is precisely the moment you must remember my son. Easy is how the cutting got done. Easy is the council. Easy is the mage who was so pleased with his clever weapon. You will end workings and portals and the dead things, and you will do it on purpose, with your whole shut face, having weighed it. You will never do it because it offered and you were tired. Tell me you understand that distinction, because it is the only one that will keep you a person and not a door that walks."

"I understand it," Viridia said. "It's the same thing you've been teaching me all afternoon. It's the same thing as Hiram's core, and the rowan, and walking every other road first. Don't do the easy enormous thing because it's offered. Do the hard small thing on purpose, or don't do it at all." She met the black eye. "I've been learning that lesson in pieces for six weeks from a tiger and a sponsor and a dead headmistress and my own mother. You've just given me the sharpest piece. I'll keep it where I keep the others."

Drake regarded her a long while, and then he lowered his great head until the black eye was level with her, near enough that she could feel the cold coming off his death-flames, and he said, more quietly than she had heard him say anything, "Then go and be the fifth, and be a better one than the four before you, who all, in their time, used it once when they should not have, and carried it after like a stone. Even I. Especially I." He drew back. "Take my grandson into your city, since he will not be left, and bring him out, and bring yourself out, and when this is over, whatever it costs, come back up the mountain and tell an old thing how it went, because I find, against all my long practice, that I have come to want to know."

It was, she understood, as near to love as six thousand years of grief disguised as bluntness could come, and she received it the way it was given, plainly, without leaning on it, and said only, "I'll come back up the mountain," which was a promise, and which she meant, and which she did not yet know whether she would be able to keep.

She opened her portal at the foot of the valley with Varissa's warning loud in her ears, a shout and not a whisper, but here in the empty high country there was no one to hear a shout, and she took Raizen, heavy and quiet and changed somehow by the valley, by his mother, by the cold thing he had felt his father put into her, and she stepped through it toward the marching war, carrying a furnace, a banked light, an ancient language, and now a small cold door, and more frightened of herself than she had been since the clearing, and more sure.

***

She came back to the column on the second night, as she had promised, stepping out of her own portal at the staging ground past the river, where three hundred fighters had made a cold camp in a fold of hills with no fires, because fires could be seen.

Jazen was waiting at the edge of it, and he read her face, which had learned, in two days among dragons, to give away even less than usual.

"You have it," he said.

"I have it," Viridia said. "And a promise attached, which I mean to keep, so don't ask me to use it on anything that breathes." She looked out over the dark cold camp, the three hundred who had walked away from their home, the small banked cores of them in her sight, every one cut, every one burning on half its wick, every one of them about to be spent against a wall for a thing she might be able to mend with a thought if the peace failed. "The march is two days from the capital now. Tomorrow night I go ahead. Into the city. To the man with the open circle." She turned to Jazen. "If it works, you'll get word to halt before you're in bowshot of the walls, and this all stops with a vote and no one here dies. If it doesn't work, I'll be inside when you arrive, and I'll do what I can from in there, and you'll do what you can from out here, and we'll hope the two add up." She let a breath go. "There's one more thing I have to do before I go, and it's the hardest, and I've been putting it off the whole march. I have to let Amber say a thing to my face. I promised her I would. Tonight, before I leave, because I don't think I'm coming back out of that city the same as I went in, whichever way it goes, and she's owed the saying of it while I'm still the person she made the promise with."
