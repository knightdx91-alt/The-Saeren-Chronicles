# Chapter 8: The Right

<!-- Book Two, Ch.8 | chronological, DIALOGUE-HEAVY | em-dash <=4 | THE moral challenge (binding) -->

The war-camp made no fires, so the night they spent within reach of the capital was the darkest Viridia had passed since the forest after Hazel, three hundred people breathing in a fold of cold hills under a sky with too many stars, and she found Amber at the edge of it, away from the others, sitting on a flat stone with her knees drawn up and her face turned to the dark line where the hills met the sky.

There was a way the camp held its quiet now that was different from sleep. It was the quiet of people who had agreed not to be heard. No fires, so no smell of smoke, only cold earth and the iron tang of too many bodies and the green crush of bracken where men had lain down on it without blankets. Somewhere off in the dark a horse shifted its weight and a picket-line creaked and went still again. The hills swallowed everything. Viridia had walked out through the sleeping shapes of an army that did not believe it would all be alive in a week, and not one of them had stirred, and that, more than the silence, was the thing that told her how close they were.

She sat down beside Amber without being asked, because she had a promise to keep and she had learned that the only way to keep the hard ones was to walk straight at them before the courage cooled.

"I leave for the city tomorrow night," Viridia said. "Ahead of the march. To the man with the open circle. You know that. I came to keep the promise I made you on Jazen's floor. You said when the roads started closing I was to come to you before the last of them and let you put it all to me, to my face. The roads are closing. This is the last open one I can name. So say it. I'm listening, the whole way, with nothing shut."

Amber said nothing for a moment, turning something over, and Viridia let her have it, because this was Amber's to begin.

"I've been rehearsing it two days," Amber said at last. "And every version sounds like I'm trying to stop you, and I'm not, that's not what this is, I want that clear before I start. I'm not the one who grabs your arm at the last second. You'll do what you'll do, and I worked out a while ago that I can't change that and shouldn't try. This isn't me stopping you. This is me making sure that when you do it, you can't tell yourself afterward that it was simple. Because I've watched you, Vir. I've watched you make hard things look like the only thing, until even you forget there was a choice. And this one, you don't get to forget there was a choice. That's the whole of what I want. Not your hand. Your knowing."

"All right," Viridia said. "Then make me know it. Properly. Don't go gentle because I'm leaving tomorrow."

***

"Here it is, then." Amber turned on the stone to face her, and in the starlight her face was stripped of the armor, the dry jokes, all of it, down to the thing underneath that she let almost no one see. "You can mend it. The cut. Not for one person. For everyone. You told me that without telling me, in Jazen's house, when I asked and you wouldn't answer. I've had it confirmed every day since by the way you won't talk about it. You can reach into the bottom of every living soul in the world and undo the thing that was done to us six hundred years ago, and make us all whole, every core at once, and most of us would never even know it happened. That's true, isn't it. Say it out loud. I want to hear you say the true thing once before you walk into a city carrying it."

"It's true," Viridia said. "I can do it. I tested it. A tree, not a person, but the difference is only the number, and the number isn't a wall. I could remake every living core in the world. I'm almost certain I could do it tonight."

"There," Amber said softly. "That wasn't so hard. Now the rest." She pulled her knees tighter. "When I was eight, men came into my house and killed my mother and my father while I was in the next room, and by the time I got to them it was nearly over, and I did the only thing an eight-year-old with too much magic and no sense could think to do, which was reach into them and try to hold them in the world by main force. And I held them. Long enough to say goodbye. And it shattered my light core doing it, for good, which is why I wear a stone on a string to fake a thing I broke and can't fix." She said it flatly, the way Viridia said her own worst things, two people who had learned the same trick of speaking grief in a level voice so it could be got out at all. "I have thought about that night every day for ten years. And do you know the part I keep, the part that matters for you. It's not the killing. It's not even losing them. It's this. The men who came did it because their government had decided what kind of magic was allowed to exist, and my parents had the wrong kind, and so men were sent to correct that, in the night, without asking, for everyone's good. That's the sentence. For everyone's good. They believed it. The men in my house were not monsters, Vir. That's the thing nobody tells you. They were tired men doing a job they'd been told was needed for the good of the world, reaching into other people's lives to fix what those people were, without asking, because someone above them had decided it was right." She let that sit. "Now tell me how what you mean to do is different."

***

Viridia did not answer at once. She had promised to listen the whole way with nothing shut, and that meant letting the question land where it was aimed, which was the center, and not turning it with the first true-sounding thing.

"I've been asking myself that since the rise," she said at last. "The day I first let myself follow it all the way down. I'll give you what I've reached, but I want you to hear that I'm not sure of it, because the day I'm sure of it is the day you should be most afraid of me." She touched the dark-blue stone at her throat, turned it once, and stilled her hand. "Here's the difference I think I have. The men in your house reached in to make people smaller. To cut, to cap, to take away. I'd be reaching in to give back the thing that was taken. They subtracted. I'd restore. Putting back what was stolen, not stealing more."

"And if I don't want it back," Amber said. "Did you think of that. You're so sure everyone wants their other half that you've never once said the word *if*. Some people built their whole lives on being what they are. Some made peace with the half they've got. Some would be terrified to wake tomorrow with a dark half they never asked for, a whole second nature moving in them that they don't know how to hold. You'd be giving a gift, fine. But a gift forced on someone who didn't ask is just a thing done to them with a nicer name on it. My parents thought their magic was a gift. The men who came thought taking it was a mercy. Everyone in this story thinks they're the one being kind, Vir. That's not the exception. That's the rule. *Kind* is what we all call it. It has never once been the thing that mattered."

"I know," Viridia said quietly. "That's the part that keeps me up. You're right that gentle isn't the difference. You said it in Jazen's house and you're right. I'd do it kindly, and kindly doesn't count, because the council that cut us probably thought they were being kind too, saving the world from a thing they feared. I've thought that exact thought. I stood on a rise and thought, the only difference I can name is that I mean it well, and I'm not sure they didn't." She held still a moment. "So I went looking for a difference that wasn't about how I'd feel doing it. And the only one I can stand on is the cost of not doing it. Amber, I can see the cores. Every one. I've watched a whole village burning on half its wick and not knowing it. And out past the village there's a war, and in the war there's a city, and in the city there are workhouses full of healers like Alice, owned, because the world decided some people's magic makes them property. Every day I don't act, that goes on. People die in that arithmetic. Not maybe. Now, tonight, while we sit here being careful. So it isn't a clean choice between doing a wrong thing and doing nothing. It's a choice between two wrongs. The wrong of reaching into everyone without asking. And the wrong of standing here able to stop the killing and choosing my own clean hands over their lives. There's no version where no one's harmed. I only get to pick which harm."

Amber was a long time saying anything to that. She had pulled a stalk of dead grass and was working it through her fingers, splitting it down the seam, and she watched her own hands do it the way a person does when the hands are the only part of them left that will hold still.

***

"Then here's the road you keep not naming," she said. "The one you're going into the city for. You find your man with the open circle, and somehow he turns the Conclave, and they vote, and they tell the world the truth, and people choose it. One at a time. In the light. Knowing. The ones who want their other half reach for it and take it back, and the ones who're frightened of it leave it be, and nobody reaches into anybody. That's the road, isn't it. That's why you're trying so hard for a thing you keep saying won't work. Because the asked-for version is the only one you actually believe is clean, and you know it, and you'd rather have it than the thing you can do with a thought."

"Yes," Viridia said. "That's exactly why. The peace road isn't really about the war. It's about asking. If the Conclave says it out loud and people choose for themselves, then no one's reached into anyone, and the thing gets done the only way it could ever be done right. That's the road I want. That's the road I'd give nearly anything for." She was quiet. "But you've already named what's wrong with it, two days ago, on the floor. It's slow. It's a council and a vote and then years, a generation, of people choosing one at a time while the war goes on and the workhouses stay full and the people dying keep dying. The clean road is real and it's right and it takes longer than the people in the workhouses have. So I can have it clean, or I can have it in time to save them, and I can't have both, and that's the trap under the trap, Amber. Not two wrongs. Clean-and-slow against fast-and-unforgivable, with bodies on the clock either way."

"And if the clean road's even a little possible," Amber pressed, "even a slim chance the Conclave turns, don't you owe it more than two days. Don't you owe it everything before you reach for the fast one."

"I owe it everything I can give it before the war makes the choice for me," Viridia said. "Which is why I'm going into the city instead of standing on a hill and mending the world tonight, which I could do, which would stop the march before a single person on either side bled. I could end it before morning, Amber. I'm choosing not to. I'm choosing the slow clean road first, with a war this close, because the trying is the only difference I've got. But I won't lie to you and say I'll wait forever. If the clean road closes and the loud road opens and our people are dying on a field in front of me, I won't stand there pure while they die so I can keep my hands clean of the thing that would save them. I think that would be its own kind of monstrous. The monster who watches, so he can feel innocent."

"There's the other shape of it," Amber said softly. "Now you've found the story where not-acting is the evil. You've got both stories now, Vir. Acting is the lesser harm, and not-acting is the monster who watches. You've armed yourself with the reasons for both directions, which means whichever you choose, you've got the speech ready. That's not a criticism. That's just what it looks like to be the person who has to decide. You end up holding all the speeches. The Chancellor holds all his too."

"Then what do I do," Viridia said, and now her voice was not level, not stripped, but had something raw moving under it, the thing she let almost no one hear. "If every road has a story that justifies it, and the stories are the same shape as the monster's stories, and I'll never know which one was true, then what am I supposed to actually do, Amber, standing on that field. Tell me. You're the only one who'll say the hard thing to my face. Say this one. What do I do."

Amber looked at her a long while, and something in her face broke open, the love under the armor, the thing she protected harder than her own life.

"You do the thing you can live with," she said. "That's all there is, at the top, where there's no one left to tell you. Not the thing that's provably right, because you've just shown me there's no proof up there. The thing you can carry. You're the one who has to wake every morning for the rest of a very long life as the person who did it. So you don't ask which is right. You ask which version of you you can stand to be at the far end of it. The girl who reached in and saved them and never knew if she had the right. Or the girl who kept her hands clean and watched. Choose the one you can live inside, Vir. And then live inside it, all the way, no flinching, because the one thing you're not allowed, the one thing that would actually make you the Chancellor, is to do the enormous thing and then spend your life pretending you didn't, or that someone made you. Own it the whole way down. That's the difference. Not clean hands. Owned hands."

Amber was silent a while after that, and when she spoke again some of the edge had gone out of her voice, replaced by something heavier and more tired.

"That's good," she said. "That's actually good. The two-wrongs thing. I've not heard you put it that well." She rubbed her face. "And it's a trap, and I think you half know it's a trap, which is why you keep saying you're not sure. Because *I'm only choosing the lesser harm* is exactly what every person who ever did a great terrible thing said to themselves on the way to doing it. The Chancellor thinks he's choosing the lesser harm. He'd say it just like you. *I could let the dark mages spread and rot the children, or I could act now and save more than I cost.* The lesser-harm argument doesn't tell you you're right, Vir. It only tells you you've found the story that lets you do the enormous thing. Everybody finds it. That's what it's for."

"I know that too," Viridia said, and her voice did the flat thing, the closest she came to breaking. "I know the lesser-harm story is the one everyone tells. I know it's the exact shape of the Chancellor's own reasoning. I've held that up against my own and tried to find where they part and some days I can't, and those are the worst days." She looked at Amber directly. "So tell me what you'd have me do instead. Honestly. Because I keep waiting for you to give me the other answer, the one where I don't act and it's all right, and you won't give it to me, because you can't, because there isn't one. You're not arguing that I should do nothing. You couldn't argue that with Alice in a workhouse and you wouldn't insult either of us by trying. You're arguing something else. What is it. Say the real thing."

Amber held her eyes a beat longer, and then she laughed, once, without any humor in it, a cracked sound.

"You really do read everyone," she said. "Even me. Even now." She wrapped her arms around her knees. "Fine. The real thing. I'm not arguing you should do nothing. You're right, I can't, not with Alice where she is. The real thing is smaller and worse than that." Her voice dropped. "The real thing is that I think you're probably going to have to do it. I've thought so a while now. The roads are closing, the way you said, and I don't think the man with the open circle turns a Conclave in two days, and I think you'll end up standing somewhere with a war in front of you and the means to stop it in your chest and no other road left, and you'll reach in and remake the world, and I think, God help me, that you'll probably be right to." She turned her wet eyes on Viridia, openly wet, unashamed of it. "And I am terrified for you. Not of you. For you. Because you'll do it, and it'll work, and the killing will stop, and you will never, ever know for certain that you were right. That's the cost no one's named to you, and it's the one I came to name. Not the void. Not the cut. This. You'll carry a thing you did to every living soul without asking, and you'll never find out if you were the hero or the next Chancellor, because the only difference is the story, and you'll have told yourself the good story, the way everyone does, and you'll never be able to prove to yourself it was true. You'll be fifteen, and you'll have done the largest thing anyone has ever done, and you'll have to live the rest of your life not knowing whether it was the most loving act in history or the most arrogant, with no one able to tell you, because there's no one above you anymore to tell you anything. That's what I came to say. You're about to climb up where there's nobody left who can absolve you. And I wanted you to hear, from someone on your floor, on your side, before you climbed: I see how alone that is. I see it. Whatever you choose, somebody saw how alone you were when you chose it."

Viridia held still a while, because there was nothing to say that would not be smaller than what Amber had just given her, and because her eyes were doing the thing they were not allowed to do, burning, and she pressed it back, the old discipline, and won, barely, because she had a rule, and even here, even now, even with the kindest and most terrible thing anyone had ever said to her sitting in the cold air between them, she would not let it spill where it could be seen.

"Thank you for that," she said at last, and her voice came out level and stripped, which was all she could manage and which Amber understood. "That's the truest thing anyone's said to me about it. You're right. I'll never know. I've started to understand that, in pieces, and you just put it in one whole. That I'm climbing somewhere there's no one left to tell me. And that I'll have to do the thing anyway, probably, and carry the not-knowing the rest of my life." She turned the stone again. "I think that might be what *be careful who you let see you do it* was always about. Not hiding. The carrying. My mother knew I'd end up somewhere no one could see me well enough to absolve me, and she was telling me to choose, while I still could, the few people who'd at least have watched. So that the not-knowing wouldn't also be alone." She looked at Amber. "You're one of the ones who watched. I'm choosing you to have seen it. That's the only thing I can give you back for this, and it's not enough, and it's all I have."

"It's enough," Amber said, and wiped her face, and pressed her shoulder against Viridia's in the dark, and they sat a while longer with the question standing unanswered between them, not resolved, only fully said at last, which was the most that could be done with it, and both of them knew it.

They did not talk again of the war or the well or the circle. Amber told her, after a time, in a low flat voice that took the place of crying, about the third Alice, the one their friend was always being measured against, the loud one with the good marks, and how Alice had hated the letter P that pinned her to last; and Viridia listened, and understood that this was Amber giving Alice back to her for the road, a person and not a thing in a workhouse, a girl who minded about her own name. It was a kind of provisioning. You sent someone toward a city with the thing they were going for made real enough to be worth the trip.

When the cold had got all the way into the stone under them, Amber stood, and held out a hand, and pulled Viridia up.

"Go and sleep," she said. "You can't shadow-walk into the lion's mouth on no sleep. And Vir." She had the dry note back in her voice, the armor coming up, but her eyes were still raw under it. "Read the man with the open circle the way you read me. Find the one thing about him that doesn't fit. That's the only edge you've ever had. Use it."

***

She left in the deepest hour, when the camp was as asleep as a frightened army gets.

Jazen saw her off at the edge, and so did Lor-ar, and Raizen rode her shoulder wound tight and silent, refusing again to be left, and she did not argue it, because Varissa had been right that they did not sit in houses, and because she had stopped being able to picture doing the frightening thing without the small warm weight of him against her neck. The dragon was no bigger than a cat now, folded down to nothing, but she could feel the whole of him through the bond, the prismatic core banked low, every element in him held still so as not to throw light. He had learned that much of hiding, this near the city. It cost him. She felt it cost him.

Lor-ar came to the edge of her sight and no further, a paleness in the dark with a tiger's patience, and he said only, *You go where I cannot follow you fully, little one. Down through the honest dark. I will be a thread on you the whole way. Do not let go of it.*

"I won't," she told him, and meant it as a promise to keep more than the thread.

Jazen pressed a folded scrap into her hand, the open-circle man's safe-house mark in flower-code, the baker's-sign place she was to make for. His face was a soldier's face by then, all the grief packed away behind it for the work, but his hand was not steady when it closed over hers.

"Two days," he said. "We'll be at the walls in two days. Turn him before then if you can. After that the quiet roads shut and it's the loud one, and I'd rather it never came to the loud one, but I've stopped believing in rather." He gripped her shoulder, once, hard. "Come back out. My mother spent her whole life making sure you'd be ready for something. I don't think she meant for the something to eat you. Come back out, Viridia."

"I'll try," she said, which was honest, and was all she had.

She walked down off the hills alone then, past the last picket, into the lower ground where the dark pooled thickest, and found what she needed at the foot of a clay bank where the night lay deepest and no light reached and no eye was turned. An honest dark in a low place, exactly as Varissa had drilled her. She did not open a portal, because a portal was a shout and the city would turn its head toward the shout. She gathered the shadow instead, the dark magic that asked no light to work, and she folded it around herself and Raizen and stepped into it the way Varissa had taught, and it took her the way it took her, fast and cold and soundless, across the two days of waiting road in a single held breath, down into a drain-mouth in a back court under the capital where the dark was honest and no watcher's eye was turned, and she arrived once, only once, as she had been told, because to arrive twice was to be seen.

She stood in the reeking dark under the city that wanted her dead and held Alice prisoner and had cut the whole world in half six hundred years ago out of fear, with a dragon banked tight on her shoulder and Amber's question sitting unanswered in her chest, and she did not flinch, because she had sworn to herself that she would walk every road that might matter to its end before the last, and this was the last one open, and she had come at last to its door.
