# Chapter 8: Dust to Dust

She slept the way Drake had told her to, which was to say badly and on purpose, lying still in the dark of the children's hall with her eyes shut and her breathing slow and her mind refusing to settle, because a person cannot order sleep the way she ordered her face. But she rested. She lay flat and let the day go out of her arms, and her arms forgot the four men for a few hours, mostly, and when the grey came up at the window she was as ready as a girl could be to go and learn the thing she had hoped, for many months, never to need.

She did not eat first. She found she could not. She drank water from the bucket by the door and stood a while at the threshold where the doorway's long dark stretched out across the boards, and felt the bond before she felt anything else, the warm line under her ribs gone from far to near in the night, so near now that she knew if she went out past the rim she would find him within an hour's flight, but she would not go out past the rim this morning. This morning she went up.

She did not wake Jazen. He had said to bring the rabbit or not, as she chose, and Drake had said the same, and she had decided in the dark that this was a thing she would rather do without a witness, because the witness was a kindness she could not afford to want. A person who learns the worst stroke on a long road should learn it the way you take a wound, alone, so that no one has to watch your face do whatever it was going to do. She left him sleeping on the bench beside the wounded man, his mother's papers slid off his knees onto the floor, and she stepped into the seam in the dark of the doorway, and she went.

* * *

The black rock took her as it had the first time, with a flood of wind and a flood of sky and the thin biting chill of a place where the air has given up most of what makes it kind. She came out of the dark standing, the way the lost ones arrive, and she held still a moment to let her stomach forgive her and her ears stop ringing, and then she looked above her, because the old one was always there before her.

He was. He lay along the high stone with his enormous head down on a forearm the size of a cottage, and the coal of his single open eye was banked low this morning, the way a fire is banked that has burned all night and means to burn all day. Raizen was not on the height. She felt him below and to the south, riding the wind, deliberately given the morning's air to himself, and she understood that this had been arranged between them, father and son, that the son would not be present for this, and the arrangement sat oddly in her, that a creature six thousand winters deep had thought of her feelings and moved his child off the mountain to spare them both.

"You came by yourself," Drake said. "Good. The rabbit would have wept, and you would have spent the lesson watching him instead of taking it." The banked eye opened the rest of the way. "And you slept. Not well. Well enough. Better than I did at your age before a thing of this size, but I was a fool at your age, and I had centuries to stop being one, and you will not be given centuries. So. Sit."

She sat on the cold stone where she had sat the day before. The wind came off the rock face and cut straight through her, and she did not let it bend her, and her mother's blue stone lay cool at her throat under her shirt.

"You will want me to start with the look of it," Drake said. "The mark. How a hand draws it. I will not, yet. A girl who learns the look of a thing first will think the look is the thing, and the look is the least of it. I will start where the lesson starts, which is with my daughter, and you will sit and you will let an ancient creature grieve out loud at you, because that is the fee, and I told you yesterday it was the only one I would charge."

"You told me the fear was the fee."

"The fear is what you pay yourself. This is what you pay me." The banked eye held on her. "Listen."

* * *

He told her about the day.

He told it slow, from the height, the way he told everything, and he did not perform it and he did not soften it, and Viridia held herself motionless and let it come down the slope at her like the scree.

"I have told you she flew at a thing she should have flown around," Drake said. "I have not told you what the thing was. It was a working. A great one, of the kind the severing-hands made in those years, when they were still learning what they had bought with the cutting. They had opened a door. Not a portal as your kind makes them, a small seam you step through; a great standing door, torn into a place that is not a place, and they had set things to come out of it, and the things that came out of it could not be killed, because a thing that is not alive cannot be made dead. You understand me. You have stood at the far edge of all your seeing. You know there is an absence past the warmth. They had cut a hole in the world and they were spending what came through it like coin, and my daughter, who was loud and brave and a fool, flew at the door to shut it, because she thought a door could be shut by something strong enough flying hard enough at it." He paused. "It cannot. A door of that kind does not care how strong you are. It is not a wall. It is an absence wearing the shape of an opening, and you cannot push an absence closed. She learned that in the last half-instant of being alive, and then she was not alive, and then she was not anything, because what came through that door did to her what the door did to everything, which is make it into nothing. There was no body. That is the part I do not say to my son. There was a place where she had been, and the place was wrong, the air over it bent the way air bends over a forge, and there was nothing in it to carry home. I made a flame in air that would not hold one and I burned three days on this mountain and there was nothing beneath me to burn for. That is the truth of it. I grieved an absence. I have been grieving an absence ever since."

The wind dropped, the way it had the day before, the whole mountain seeming to wait around the old grief.

"And then," Drake said, "I shut the door."

Viridia held her silence. She had learned by now that the only true answer to a grief this old was to give it the room to finish.

"I did not fly at it. I had watched my daughter fly at it. I am a fool, child, but I am not a fool twice over the same grave." The banked eye returned from down the years and found her. "I used the symbol. The oldest one. The one your people buried furthest down. And the door that could not be pushed shut, that did not care how strong a thing flew at it, that had eaten my daughter and left me nothing to bury, simply was not, anymore. One stroke. One breath. And the thing that had taken everything from me gave nothing back and asked nothing of me and was gone, clean, the way a candle is gone when you pinch the wick, and I knelt on the stone where my daughter had been and I understood that the worst thing that had ever happened to me had just been undone by something so easy it frightened me more than the door had." His head settled lower against the stone. "That is the lesson. Before the look of it. I want you to understand, before I teach your hand to make it, that you will hate how easy it is. You will close the worst thing you ever face, and it will cost you nothing you can feel, and you will kneel in the place where the thing was and you will wish it had been harder. Because a working that ends the world's worst making and takes not one drop from you is a working that has taken the drop somewhere you cannot watch it go, and you, of all the souls I have met in all my years, already know what it is to pay where you cannot see the coin leave your hand."

"Yes," she told him, and the word came out level and stripped, the way it always did at the edge of too much.

"Then you are ready. Stand. Find a clear span of stone. You will make it once, and you will not fill it, and then I will tell you why making it and filling it are two separate mercies, and we will both be glad you did not learn the second one tired."

* * *

He taught her the look of it.

It was not, she thought, what a person would expect of the oldest and most-dreaded mark in the world. She had braced for something vast, a thing of a hundred strokes, a sigil so dense with intent that the making of it would take an hour and a steady hand. It was not that. It was very nearly nothing. It was a ring, but not joined; an unjoined ring, like the sign she had been writing all week, the one she signed her letters with, and the sameness of it went through her like ice-water, because the sign of the man who might bring peace and the sign that ended all magic were the same broken ring, and the only difference was a single line drawn inward, toward the center, beginning at the gap in the ring and running in and stopping before it reached the middle. An unjoined ring, with a line that pointed at the hollow it left.

"It is a door that will not close," Drake said, watching her draw it in the grit with a splinter of dark stone. "That is what it says. Not *closed.* Not *ended.* It says, here is a thing that will not close, and then it says, and yet, and the *and yet* is the line you run inward, and the line inward is the whole of it. You are not closing the door. You are telling the door that it was never truly opened. You are telling the magic that it was never truly made. That is why it ends any working and shuts any portal, because it does not fight the thing. It unsays it. It reaches back to the breath before the thing was made, and it stands in that breath, and it says *no,* once, and then the thing simply is not, because the breath that made it has been spent on unmaking it instead." The eye watched her hand. "Again. Your inward line trembles. It must not tremble. A trembling inward line is a door half-unsaid, and a door half-unsaid is worse than one left wide. Again."

She made it again. And again. Her hand did not tremble the fourth time, or the fifth, and somewhere in the seventh she felt the thing she had been dreading, which was that the mark wanted to be made. It had a rightness to it the way a true sentence had a rightness, the way a child's word had sat right on a slate. It fit her hand. It fit the part of her that did the sums, the cold working part, which looked at the open ring with its line turned inward and understood it at once and without effort, and approved, and that was the worst of all. Her hand and her cold working part both agreed that the mark was *good,* and neither of them was the part of her that was supposed to be afraid.

"You feel it," Drake said. It was not a question. "It is easy in your hand. It is easy in your head. It is the easiest thing you have ever learned, and you have learned to step through shadow and you have learned to bring a dead tree back, and this is lighter than either, and that is the whole of why I made you sit through my daughter first. So that when the easiness came you would have the weight of her to set against it. Make it once more, full now, the intent behind it, but on nothing, on a thing that is already nothing, and feel what it costs you, and then do not make it again until the day you have no other door."

She drew it on the air. She put the intent behind it, the *no, once,* the reaching back to the breath before a making, and she aimed it at nothing at all, a hand's width of empty wind above the stone, and the mark took her intent and used it and handed it back, and the wind in that hand's width simply was not, for an instant, a small clean absence the exact shape of the line she had drawn, and then the air rushed in and filled it and there was nothing to show she had done a thing, and it had taken nothing from her. Not a draught of the fire in her chest. Not a swallow. Nothing she could feel. She knelt on the height with the mark fading off her fingers, and she understood the old one completely, and she was afraid in just the way he had promised, which was not afraid of the mark but afraid of how little it had asked, and she set her free hand flat against the cold to steady what the easiness had done to her stomach.

"There," Drake said, low. "Now you have it. Now you carry it. I will not ask you to swear anything over it, because a soul that needs to swear over a thing this final is a soul that should not hold it, and you have not sworn, which is how I know you can. Go home, child. You have learned to end things. Go and try every door that is not this one, the way the loud one with the bitten nails counselled you, and pray to whatever you pray to that you never run the inward line at anything that breathes." The eye banked low again, the lesson done. "And eat something. You came up here on water and a poor night, and a girl who carries the unmaking should not also carry an empty stomach. It makes the easiness easier, and that is the one way we do not want it to slide."

* * *

She came home to a camp that had stopped.

She knew it before her feet finished arriving, the way you know a house is wrong from the doorway, by the quality of the quiet. The camp was never silent; it had the baby Amber complained of and the small ones at their slates and the ring of Brutus at his endless mending, and it had none of those now. It had a knot of people at the meeting-house door, and Jazen at the heart of the knot with a paper in his hand, and his face. Jazen, who let his grief be the weather of him, who never kept a thing off his face the way she kept everything off hers, had a face that was doing a thing she had not seen it do, which was nothing. It had gone flat. It had gone to the hard certain thing without the grief over it, and the grief not being there was worse than the grief, because it meant whatever the paper said had taken his grief and burned it for fuel.

"It came back," he said, when he saw her. He did not ask where she had been. He held the paper out, and his hand was steady, and the steadiness was the alarm. "The wire. Your careful man. Word came back."

She took it. She read it standing in the wind with the knot of people watching her not move.

It was not from the careful man. That was the first wrongness, and the cold working part marked it before she had read three lines. It was a broadsheet. A printed thing, the capital's own type, the kind that goes up on station walls and gets read aloud in market squares to people who cannot read it for themselves, and across the top in letters meant to be seen from across a road it said A WARNING TO EVERY MOTHER. And beneath that, in the smaller type that did the real work, it said the dark mages were not dead. That they had never been dead. That they hid in the wild places and reached out now, in these very weeks, up the honest wire that carried a nation's letters, to the good and faithful folk of the light, with poisoned words. That they had a girl. A child, it said, used as a lure, sent to whisper to careful men in the night that the old severed gift could be *given back,* freely, to any who would have it, forced on no one, and here the sheet quoted her, quoted the true words she had set down in a dead woman's hand, her offer of a room and an hour and proof laid in honest eyes, and the sheet set those words in the capital's type and called them what the capital needed them to be, which was a plot to corrupt the children of the light, to open their cores to a dark that would not let go once it was inside, to make every faithful family into a thing the dark could use. *They will come for your children with kind words,* the sheet said. *They came for ours with kind words once. We did not forget. Watch the wire. Watch the strangers. Watch the quiet man at the next desk who reads his letters too long.*

She read it twice. Her hand stayed steady. Drake had taught her, an hour ago, to fear easy things, and there was nothing easy here, so she was not afraid; she was something colder than afraid, and she let the cold do its sums.

"He didn't sell us," she said. The words came out level and stripped. "Read it. He never answered at all. They have my first words, the careful overture, *forced on no one,* the exact line. That's the first letter. Not the second. We only sent the second yesterday, three words of his own question answered." She turned the sheet over, found nothing on the back, turned it again. "They have the first and not the second, which means they took the first off the wire. They had a tap on the line he used, or on his desk, and they lifted the overture the day it landed and they sat on it and they printed it, and the silence we read as a quiet man counting his rope, that wasn't him counting. That was the capital deciding how to spend it." The cold working part finished the sum and she said its end without wanting to. "The question that came back to me. *Who else knows.* That might have been him. Or it might have been them, fishing, asking in his voice to learn the size of the council. And I gave it. I told the truth of the number. I handed them the rope."

* * *

The knot of people had gone very quiet, and Jazen took the broadsheet back out of her hands, not unkindly, the way you take a sharp thing from someone who has cut herself on it and not yet felt it.

"You don't know that," he said. "The second message went up yesterday, the way you told me to send it. If they have a tap, they have that now as well. But you don't know they held it when you answered. You don't know the question was theirs and not his. You're running the cruellest possible sum and calling it the only one."

"It's the sum that keeps people breathing." She heard how she sounded and could not make herself sound otherwise. "If it was him, then a quiet man is sitting in a grey office having watched his own overture turned into a poster that tells the whole capital to hunt for the man at the next desk who reads his letters too long. He will never lean toward another latch as long as he lives. That door is shut whether he was loyal or not, because they didn't need to turn him; they only needed to make leaning fatal, and they have. And if it was them, if the question was a hook in his voice, then I told the capital the size of the council with my own pen, and they will know there are fewer than the fingers of two hands who could name him, and they will go hunting those few." She made herself say the last of it plainly, because she had sworn once that she would not be a person who looked away from the thing she had done. "Either road, the way through the moderates is closed. Either road, I closed it, by writing the truest letter I knew how to write, because the truth made a better poster than a lie would have. They couldn't have done this with a lie. They needed my real words to make it land. The very thing meant to win the quiet man, that I told him the honest number and made it small for no one, is the thing that turned deadly in their type. I was right that a man held by a lie lets go the instant the lie does. I just never once thought about who else might be holding the same wire."

Jazen looked at the broadsheet a long moment, the capital's letters, her own truth inside them.

"My mother had this happen," he said, at last. "Twice that I know of. You put out the honest hand, and the honest hand is the thing they sever and hold up, because honest hands are the only ones that work on a poster. She told me once it was the foulest feeling in the work, worse than losing people, because losing people is at least a thing the enemy did. This they make you do to yourself. They take your best self and they print it." He folded the sheet, careful, and did not put it in the fire, because a thing of this kind you keep, to learn from, the way you keep a scar. "It is not your fault, Viridia. It is the work. The work eats good intentions and pastes them on walls."

"It is my fault," she said, not as penance, as arithmetic. "It is the work and it is my fault. Both can stand. I sent it. I will learn from it. But I will not make it smaller than it is, because the next time I put out an honest hand I need to remember that the honest hand is exactly the part they will sever, and a person who tells herself *it is only the work* is a person who reaches out the same hand the same way and loses the next one too." She looked at the folded sheet in his fist, her truest words inside the capital's type. "How many doors does that leave."

"One that I know of. Perhaps." Jazen's flat face did not move. "There is a councilor. A real one, not a quiet man at a desk, a moderate with a seat at the table, who has spoken against the war twice in chamber and lived through it. My mother kept a thread to him. It is a thin thread and an old one, and after this morning it is the only one we have left, and a councilor reading that poster over his breakfast may decide the safest thing is to shout against the dark louder than anyone, the way frightened men do. But it is a door. It is the last one." He slid the sheet inside his shirt. "Rest. Eat. We weigh it tonight. You learned your other thing on the mountain, the ending thing, do not pretend I can't read it on you, and now you have watched a door close that you tried to open with nothing but the truth, and I want you to sit with both before you let either point you anywhere, because I have watched people decide the only door left was the one that ends things, on a morning shaped exactly like this one, and I do not want it to be you."

He went in to tell the council the road through the moderates was a poster now.

* * *

She did not rest. She went to find food, because the old one had told her to and because there was nothing else useful to do with her hands, and she was at the edge of the lanes with a heel of bread she was not eating when Amber found her.

Amber had been climbing back all week, the armor settling back over the hurt by slow degrees, and there was none of the climbing in her now. She came down the lane fast, the bitten black nails of one hand wrapped white around a copy of the broadsheet, the other hand at the necklace at her throat, the Lightwell-linked thing that kept her core alight, gripping it the way Mella gripped her locket, the tell of a frightened person who did not know she had one. She stopped close enough that the wind could not get between them, and her eyes, which had cried themselves dry in the woods and not wept since, were doing a thing that was not weeping and was worse.

"I ran your line," Amber said. Her voice was very level, the way Viridia's went level, and the levelness was the alarm. "Did you know that. Did it cross your mind even once, while you were writing your true and honest letter, that the *line,* the courier, the wire, the hands that carried it, was *me.* I asked Jazen for work. He gave me the message line, because I'm fast and I'm quiet and I look like a girl nobody watches. I carried your overture to the relay. I put it on the wire with my own two hands. And this morning I got to read it again." She held the poster up between them, shaking, not with fear. "On *this.* Word for word. The thing I carried, in their type, telling every mother in the capital to watch for a girl who whispers about giving back the gift. I *am* the girl on the poster, Viridia. I carried her. I made her real."

"Amber."

"No. You don't get the flat voice with me. I know the flat voice. I invented half of it." She did not lower the poster. "I told you in the worst of it, that night by the fire when you came in, that whatever you were planning, I was in. Whatever it was. And I meant it, and I still mean it, and that is the trouble, because I am starting to understand I said *whatever it is* to a person who has never told me what it is. You went up a mountain this morning. You came back changed. You're standing there eating bread you aren't eating with the careful face pulled on so tight I can see the seams, and there's a thing you learned up there, I can feel it on you the way I could always feel things on you, and a peace road just died, and I watched your face when you read this and you weren't surprised. You were sad. You weren't surprised. Like a girl crossing items off a list." Her voice cracked, finally, the one small human failure, and she let it, which from her was the loudest alarm there was. "What is at the bottom of the list, Viridia. What is the thing you keep trying all the little doors *before.* Because I have been carrying your messages and telling you I'm in, and I am starting to think I never asked the only question that counts, which is, in to *what.* What are you really going to do."

The wind came up off the lanes and went through them both. Viridia stood with the bread in her hand and the unjoined mark and its inward stroke still fresh in her fingers and the dead door folded inside Jazen's shirt, and she looked at the only person who had ever made her say the true thing aloud, and she understood she was not going to get out of this one wearing the flat voice, and that the question Amber had just put to her was not a thing you answered standing in a lane with bread going stale in your fist. It was the kind that took the rest of a night, or the rest of a war, and Amber was not going to let her fold it away, because Amber had carried the girl on the poster up the wire herself, and a person who has made your secret real has bought the right to know what it was.

"Sit down," Viridia said. "Not here."

"I don't want to sit down. I want an answer."

"I know," Viridia said. "That's why we have to sit down."
