# Chapter 5: The Furnace and the Match

<!-- Book Two, Ch.5 | essayistic->scene | em-dash <=4 | brisk; the power TESTED; cost named; void-absence stirs (abstract) -->

There is a difference between knowing you can do a thing and having done it, and Viridia had spent three days letting the first stand in for the second, because the second frightened her, and on the morning before the march she decided she could no longer afford the comfort of not knowing exactly what she was.

So she went out past the wards, beyond the thinning camp, with Lor-ar walking ahead at his full and terrible size and Raizen riding her shoulder, into the deep autumn woods where there was no one to see, because the one thing she had never let herself do, in six weeks of being the only whole person she had met, was to find out the true measure of what she carried. She had banked it. She had hidden it. She had spent her whole strength learning to seem small. She had never once stood somewhere safe and let it open all the way, because she had been afraid of two things, that it would be less than she feared, and that it would be more, and she did not know which frightened her worse.

She found a clearing where a stream cut a bank, a quiet place, the kind her father would have liked, and she set her feet and let her sight come fully open, and looked at the world the way she had taught herself never to look at it for long.

The woods were full of cores. She had known that, the way you know a fact, but knowing it and standing inside it were different rooms. Every living thing burned. The trees were slow green embers, banked and patient, older than the camp and indifferent to it. The stream ran bright with the small quick sparks of fish. A fox somewhere to the north was a clean hot coal moving low through the brush. Birds were scatterings of light in the branches. And under all of it, beneath the soil, the land itself had a glow, faint and enormous and even, the way a hearthstone holds heat hours after the fire is out, and she understood, standing in it, that this was the source's spillage, the warmth of the great well of all magic seeping up through the floor of the world into everything that lived on it, and that she was connected to it, that the thread that ran out of her ran down into that, and that if she chose she could feel the whole world's worth of it at once and it would not burn her, because it was hers, because she was, in some way she was only beginning to hold, its near end.

"All right," she said aloud, to Lor-ar, to Raizen, to herself. "I'm going to open it. Stand back from me. If I do something I shouldn't, say so."

*I am three centuries old,* Lor-ar said, settling onto his haunches at the edge of the clearing, unbothered. *I have stood near worse than a frightened girl with a large core. Open it. I will tell you if the trees begin to die, which is the usual sign that a person has opened too far.*

It was not entirely a joke, and she knew it was not, and she opened it anyway.

***

She let the banked thing behind her ribs come up.

She had spent six weeks holding it to a flicker, a candle-flame she kept small by main force, and now she stopped holding, the way you stop pressing on a door you have been leaning against, and it came up, and up, and kept coming, and she understood within three breaths that she had badly underestimated what she had been carrying. It was not a candle. It had never been a candle. The candle had been the part she let herself feel. The rest of it rose now through her like a tide coming into a harbor at speed, filling channels she had not known were dry, and the clearing brightened around her in her sight until the slow green embers of the trees looked like guttering matchheads beside her, until the warm even glow of the land itself was the one thing in the woods on her scale, and she stood in the middle of it, a girl-shaped furnace in a clearing full of candles, and was, for one bright vertiginous moment, completely without fear, because there was nothing in the visible world large enough to be afraid of.

Then the fox, the clean hot coal to the north, turned and bolted, and the birds went up out of the branches in a single startled sheet, and the fish scattered to the deep bank, and Viridia understood that every living thing in reach of her had just felt a predator wake up, and she banked herself down hard, fast, the way she had practiced, and the woods settled, slowly, suspicious, and Lor-ar said, mildly, *That. That is the thing you will need to learn to do without the woods noticing. You shine, little one. Banked, you are a hidden lamp under a cloth. Open, you are a sunrise, and everything with eyes turns toward a sunrise, including the things you would least like to have looking.*

"I felt them all," Viridia said. Her voice came out unsteady, and she let it, because there was no one here who would use it against her. "Every core in the woods. The fox. The fish. The trees. The land underneath. I felt where each one was cut. Lor-ar, they're all cut. Every animal, every tree, every living thing, the dark half capped and frayed, the same as the people, the same as me before I was whole. The whole world is burning on half its wick and it doesn't know, because it has never once felt what the other half would be."

*And you could mend it,* Lor-ar said. It was not a question. He had been watching her think for six weeks. *That is what this morning is truly about. Not how large you are. Whether the largeness is enough to do the thing you have not told the boy or the dragon-children or me, though I have known it since the night you stood at the screen's edge and decided not to flinch.*

"Yes," Viridia said. "That's what this morning is about."

***

She did the test then, the real one, the one she had come out to do, and it was very small, and it told her everything.

She chose a single thing. Not a person. She would not practice the thing she feared most on a person, not even to learn it, because the not-doing-it-to-people-without-leave was the one line she had drawn and she did not intend to start the morning by stepping over it. She chose a sapling at the edge of the clearing, a young rowan no taller than she was, its core a thin green thread, capped and frayed at the dark end like everything else, and she knelt by it and put her two hands around its slim trunk, and she reached, very carefully, with the smallest fraction of what she now knew she held, for the cut at the root of its small wild magic.

She found it at once. That was the first thing it taught her: that she did not have to search. The cut was the same cut, in the rowan as in the fox as in Hiram as in the council six hundred years gone, one wound made once and inherited down every living line since, and her hands knew it the way her hands knew the shape of her own face in the dark. What it taught her next was that mending was not difficult. She had braced for difficulty. There was none. She drew the frayed dark end of the rowan's thread up out of its capping, the way you draw a splinter you have finally got a grip on, and she joined it back to the whole, and it took, and held, and the sapling's core, which had been a thin green thread, ran suddenly bright and double, light and dark together, whole, the way hers was whole, and the little tree did not know anything had happened to it, and went on being a rowan, taller in its light by no inch at all, and entirely changed at the root.

It had taken her perhaps the length of three breaths. It had cost her perhaps as much as lifting a cup. And the last of it, the lesson she had come out to learn and had dreaded learning, was that there was no difference in kind between the rowan and the world. The rowan had been one cut. The world was the same cut, repeated past counting, and the joining was the same joining, and she had just proved on a sapling that she knew how, and that it was easy, and that the only thing standing between this morning and a world made whole was the number, and the number, she understood with the cold clear part of her, was not a wall. It was only a number. She was the near end of the source itself. She did not have to mend each core by hand. She had only to reach through the one connection she already was, down into the deep steady glow beneath the soil of the world, and let the joining run outward along every thread at once, the way a single struck match does not light each wick in a row by walking to it but sets a whole spilled field alight at the speed of the thing catching.

She could do it. Not someday. Now. This morning, kneeling by a stream, she could have remade the magic of every living thing on the face of the world before the fox finished running, and no one would have known why, and most of them would never know it had happened, and the few who understood enough to ask would have only her word for what she had done in a clearing with her hands around a tree.

She took her hands off the rowan and sat back on her heels and was, for a while, very quiet.

***

"It frightens you," Lor-ar observed. "Good. The day it stops frightening you is the day you should be locked somewhere with no living thing in reach."

"It's so easy," Viridia said. "That's the part I can't hold. I thought it would be hard. I thought, if it's hard, the hardness will tell me when to stop, the way pain tells you. But it's easy, Lor-ar. It's easier than carrying water. I could do the whole world the way I did the tree and the only thing it would cost me is the deciding, and I've watched the deciding cost the council that cut us nothing at all, so I don't trust the deciding to stop me either." She looked at her own hands. "There has to be a cost. Things this large have a cost. If I can't find it, it doesn't mean there isn't one. It means I haven't found it yet, and doing a thing whose cost you haven't found is exactly how the world got cut in the first place."

And because she meant to find the cost before she ever did the thing, she did the one more test she had been most afraid of, which was to reach, not toward the trees, not toward the warm glow of the land, but out, past the edge of the world's lattice, toward the place she had felt once at the source and never since: the absence. The rim. The thing the source itself flinched from, that she had touched the edge of in the deep place under the well and had not had a word for, and still did not.

She reached toward it the way you put a hand out into a dark room to find the wall, carefully, expecting cold.

It was not cold. Cold was a thing. This was the absence of the thing, a place in her perception where the living glow of the world simply stopped, where there were no cores, no embers, no spillage, no light and no dark, only a smooth and patient nothing that did not burn because there was nothing there to burn, and as she brushed the edge of it with the furthest reach of her attention she felt it do something she had not expected and would think about for a long time after.

It noticed her.

Not as a person notices. Not as the fox had noticed, turning to run. It was nothing so warm as fear. It was the way a low place notices water, the way an empty thing is aware of a full one across a distance with no feeling in the awareness at all, a pure and bottomless leaning-toward, and she understood, brushing it, that the absence was not indifferent to her, that it had been leaning toward the source since before there were people to cut, and that she, the living mouth of the source, the fullest thing in the world, was exactly the sort of fullness an emptiness that large would lean toward hardest, and that if she ever made the world more full than it already was, more whole, more bright, the leaning would only grow, the way water leans harder toward a deeper drop.

She pulled her attention back so fast that Raizen startled awake on her shoulder, and the clearing was only a clearing again, the stream, the rowan, the suspicious settling birds, and she knelt by the bank with her heart going and understood that she had found the cost, or the edge of it, the first true price-tag on the unforgivable thing, and that it was not a cost to her at all.

*You touched the cold place,* Raizen said. He had come fully awake now, and the brightness had gone out of him, the easy chatter, and what was left was older than his weeks, older than him, the racial memory that ran in dragons under the young surface like a deep current under a bright stream. *Drake has a word for it that I am not allowed to say until I am grown. He says the oldest of us remember when it was nearer. He says it is not a monster and not a hunger and not anything you can fight, because there is nothing there to fight, which is the whole trouble with it.* The small prismatic head pressed under her jaw, and the warmth came back into him by an effort she could feel, the deliberate choosing to be the part that was not afraid. *I do not like that you can reach it. Drake cannot reach it. Varissa cannot. Only the source can feel it, and you are the source's near end, so you can feel it too, and I felt it lean when you brushed it, all the way down the bond, and I did not like it. Do not touch it again to test it. Some things you learn by touching. That one you have learned enough.*

"I've learned enough," Viridia agreed, and put her hand to his small back, and felt the all-colored furnace of him steady itself against her on purpose, a young creature spending warmth it did not have to spare to be the thing she needed, and she thought: he is the only whole thing in the world besides me, the only other core that burns on both its halves, and the absence did not lean toward him the way it leaned toward me, because he is one and I am the door to all of it. The thought sat in her, cold and clear. To make the world whole would be to make the whole world as bright as the absence had only ever felt in her alone. She would be turning up every wick in the whole of the world at once, in a house she now knew had something patient and bottomless leaning against the far wall.

It was a cost to the world. Mend every core, make the world whole and bright and full, and the emptiness at the rim would lean toward all that new fullness the way it had never had cause to lean before. She did not know what the leaning would do. She did not know if it would do anything in her lifetime, or in a hundred lifetimes, or whether it was a danger at all or only a feeling she had borrowed from a place too strange to read. But she knew now that the act was not free, that nothing she did to the whole world would be free, and that *we don't know what it costs* was the precise sentence the frightened council could have said about the cutting, six hundred years ago, before they did it anyway.

***

"There's a road I'm going to try first," she told Lor-ar, on the walk back, with Raizen drowsing again and the camp's wards rising faintly ahead through the trees. "A man on the Conclave who might listen. Jazen found him in his mother's papers. If a light mage with a voice in their council can be made to hear that the severing was a choice and not a law of the world, then maybe it doesn't have to be me reaching into everyone in the dark. Maybe it can be people choosing it, in the light, for themselves, one council vote at a time, slow and partial and theirs." She heard how thin it sounded even as she said it, one circle, one maybe, against a war four days off. "It probably won't work. But I'm going to try it, and the next one after it, and the one after that, before I do the thing in the clearing on a scale I can't take back. Because the trying is the only thing that makes me different from the council, and I felt the absence lean toward me today, and I would rather be a girl who tried every other road than the kind of person an emptiness that large is glad to see coming."

*That,* said Lor-ar, *is the wisest thing you have said since I have known you, and you are about to spend the next weeks discovering how little wisdom helps.* He paced beside her, vast and blue-green and patient. *The roads will close, little one. I have lived through three wars and I have never once seen the reasonable road stay open under the weight of frightened men. You will try them because you must, and they will close because they always do, and then you will stand at the last one with the absence leaning at your back and a friend in a workhouse and a war on a field, and you will have to choose with no clean choice left, which is the only kind of choosing that has ever been asked of anyone who could change anything.* He turned one lightning-blue eye on her. *Try the roads. I would think less of you if you did not. But do not let the trying fool you into thinking the hard choice can be escaped. It can only be earned, so that when you make it, you make it as the girl who tried everything, and not as the council who tried nothing. The choice will be the same choice. You will only be a different person making it.*

They came back through the seam into the camp as the light was going, and the camp was smaller than it had been that morning, another group walked south in the night to come, and Viridia went and found Jazen, and told him she was ready to send the first true thing they had to the man with the open circle beside his name.

***

They drafted it that night in Jazen's house, the three of them, because Amber had come in uninvited and sat herself on the floor by the cold hearth with the blanket pulled close round her and said she wasn't leaving, and Viridia had not argued, because Amber had a way of knowing when she was being kept out of a thing and a longer way of making you pay for it later.

The trouble, Viridia found, was that the truth was the one thing she could not simply write down. She could write that the severing was deliberate, and the man would think it propaganda. She could write that his magic was half of what it should be, and he would think it a threat. The papers had marked him as a maybe, a man who would hear it if it came from the right mouth, but a letter had no mouth, and the right thing said on a page by a stranger was only ink. So they argued it line by line, what to claim and what to hold back, how much truth a frightened man could swallow before it stopped sounding like truth and started sounding like the thing his Chancellor had warned him the dark mages would say.

"You have to give him a way to check it," Viridia said at last. "A thing he can prove to himself, alone, without trusting us. That's the only truth that survives the journey. Not believe us. Go and look." She thought of the rowan, whole now at the root and knowing nothing of it. "Tell him this. Any mage who has ever been near the well of magic, the source, the place they send the top-marked students once and tell them never to speak of, can feel, if they reach for it honestly, that their own core is capped. That the dark half is there, frayed, waiting, not absent. Tell him to reach for his own cut and find it, in private, where his Chancellor cannot see his face. He will not believe our letter. He might believe his own hand."

Jazen wrote it in his mother's flower-code, to be read plain by the one man and meaningless to any other, and it was good, and it was thin, and they all three knew it was thin.

It was Amber who said the thing, when the letter was sealed and the runner sent for and the lamp burning low.

"And when he reaches for his own cut and finds it," she said, from the floor, not lightly, "and writes back, you're right, we're all of us only half what we were born to be, what do you tell him then? That you can fix it? That you, one fifteen-year-old, can reach into him and every soul he has ever met and mend the lot of them, whether they vote for it or not?" She had not raised her voice. That was the worst of it. "Because that's the part I keep waiting for you to say out loud, Vir, and you keep not saying it, and I've decided I'm going to keep sitting on people's floors until you do."

The lamp ticked. Jazen looked between them and had the sense to say nothing, because he could feel, the way he always felt the strength of a stranger from clear across a field, that this was a thing between the two of them and older than him.

"I'm trying the other roads first," Viridia said. It was not an answer, and Amber's face said she knew it was not.

"I heard you. The other roads." Amber pulled the blanket tighter. "I'm not against you. I want that on the record before I'm against you, because I think there is coming a day I'll have to be, and I'd like you to remember I started out on your floor on your side. But I watched a government decide what people's magic was allowed to be, Vir. I shattered my own rather than let them have the last say in mine. And I can't yet tell the difference, when I lie awake, between what they did and what you are working yourself up to do, except that you would be gentler about it, and I am not sure gentle is the part that matters." She looked up, and her eyes were wet, openly, the way Viridia's never were. "Try the other roads. Try all of them. And when they close, because they are going to close, come and find me before you do the last thing, and let me say all this to you again, properly, so that whatever you choose, you chose it with someone telling you the cost to your face. That is all I want. Not to stop you. To make sure you cannot pretend no one warned you."

"I won't pretend that," Viridia said quietly. "I'll come and find you. I promise that one."

"Good," said Amber, and wiped her eyes without shame, and got up off the floor, and the thing between them folded itself away again, not resolved, only set down, the way they both set things down, to be lifted later when there was strength for it.

The runner went out through the seam in the dark with the sealed letter, south and west toward the place the papers called Thorn, and Viridia waited at the screen's edge and watched the small core of him dwindle into the trees and out of her seeing, carrying one circle, one maybe, the first of the roads. She made herself the promise again, the one she had made on the rise and in the clearing and now here, that she would walk every other road to its end before the last one. And she felt, somewhere far past the trees, at the rim of everything, the patient bottomless leaning of the absence, and she did not flinch from that either, because there were four days left, and a road untried was a road that closed for nothing.
