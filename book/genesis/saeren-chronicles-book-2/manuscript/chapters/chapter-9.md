# Chapter 9: The Almost

The city told three lies about itself before she had walked a hundred yards, and she catalogued all three, because cataloguing was what her mind did when the rest of her wanted to run.

The first lie was the bread. It was not yet light, the hour when the bakeries woke the streets ahead of the people, and the smell came down the wet alley out of nowhere, warm and yeasted and ordinary, the smell of a place where children were about to be sent to school with something in their hands. She breathed it in before she could stop herself, and her body, which had walked two days of road in a single cold held breath, believed for half a second that it had arrived somewhere safe. Then she came out of the alley mouth and saw the bakery's iron shutter chained and a notice nailed across it, RECLAIMED BY ORDER OF THE CONCLAVE, the ink not a week old, and understood that the bread she smelled was being baked under new ownership, and that the man who had built the smell was gone wherever the order had sent him. A city could keep making bread after it had eaten its bakers. That was the first lie. It smelled like a place that fed people.

The second lie was the cleanness. The capital's lower streets had been washed in the night, sluiced down with river water against the summer stink, and the cobbles shone under the gas lamps, black and slick and innocent, as if nothing that happened on them in daylight could leave a mark that water did not lift. She walked on the clean stones in her dead woman's boots and kept to the shadow side, where the lamp bars did not reach, and read the second lie for what it was. A floor scrubbed that hard is a floor that has something to scrub.

The third lie was the cores.

She had banked her sight low, the way she banked Raizen, a flicker and no more, because to open it wide here was to light a fire on a hill, and the glasses up in the towers would feel the heat of it. But even banked, even with her inner eye half-lidded, she could not not see them. They came up out of the waking houses on either side of her as she passed, behind shutters, under blankets, two and three and four to a room, the small steady lamps of sleeping people, a whole city's worth, more cores than she had ever held in her sight at once. From a distance, on the hills, she had thought of the capital as the enemy, a single dark shape with the Chancellor's face. Up close it was this. Bakers and laundresses and children, lamp after lamp after lamp, stacked floor on floor up the hill toward the white towers, each one breathing, each one dreaming, every one of them alight on half a wick and not knowing there was a second half to burn, because the men in the towers had decided six hundred years ago that a city of whole people was more than a city of frightened men could be trusted to rule. That was not a lie the city told. That was the truth under all three lies, and it was the worst thing she had ever seen, and she had seen a school burn.

She put all of it away in the place she kept for things she was not allowed to feel yet, and walked on toward the baker's-sign door, with Raizen a tight banked warmth at her neck and Amber's question riding in her chest like a stone she had swallowed and could not bring back up.

The flower-code mark Jazen had drawn for her took her north and east, off the river streets and up into the close brick lanes where the infirmary district began, and that was the first thing that went wrong, though it did not feel like a wrong thing yet. It felt like luck.

Because the lane she was meant to follow ran along the back wall of a long low building with a high blind face and a single barred door, and her banked sight, passing it, snagged.

She had learned the texture of cut cores by now, the flat even burn of them, every lamp in the city the same brightness as every other, because that was the point of the cutting, to make everyone the same small size. But the cores behind this wall were not the same as the street's. They were dimmer. Worn down past the half-wick to something thinner, a guttering, the light of people spent and spent and not let to recover, the way a lamp goes blue and small when the oil is nearly out and no one has come to fill it. A dozen of them, two dozen, low to the ground, packed close. And threaded through the dim worn lamps, hard and bright by contrast, three cores that belonged to armed men, standing the way men stand when the people they are watching cannot run.

A workhouse. The healers' workhouse, near the infirmary district, the one Amber had named on the cold hillside, the one she had carried south in her chest the whole march. Owned hands setting bone all day on a guttering core and sleeping chained to the bench.

She stopped walking, which she should not have done, and made herself start again, slower, a tired girl on an early errand, and as she passed the barred door she let her ordinary eyes do the work her sight was not allowed to. She read the wall the way she read a person, for the one detail that did not fit, and she found it: not the bars, not the blind face, not the guard, all of which fit a prison perfectly. The thing that did not fit was a low square hatch at the base of the wall, knee-high, its iron grate rusted half off its hinge, the kind of hatch a building has for coal or for waste, the kind no one thinks to bar because no one believes a grown thing could come through it. Through the gap she could feel the dim cores nearest, the closest of them not six feet inside the wall, and one of those dim worn lamps, she could not have said how, she would never be able to say how, had a quality the others did not, a particular held-in pattern under the dimness that her whole body recognised before her mind could name it, the way a footstep on the stair is known before it is counted.

Alice was behind that wall.

She did not let her sight open. She did not let her hand go to the grate. She did not let her face do anything at all, on a public street, under the glasses, with three armed men ten feet from a girl whose core would have lit the city like a struck match if she had once let it loose. She stood for the length of one breath at the base of the wall of the place that held the person she had walked into a city that wanted her dead to be near, and she did the hardest thing she had done since she left the camp, which was nothing. She marked the hatch. She marked the count of guards and the worn blue of the cores and the way the lane ran empty behind. She set the way in aside, in the place she kept for things she would come back for, and she made herself a promise so quiet it had no words in it, only a shape, and the shape was: not now, and not never.

Then she walked on, because the meeting was the thing that might make the hatch unnecessary. If the man with the open circle turned, the workhouse opened from the top, by a vote, in the light, and Alice walked out a free person instead of a thing dragged backward through a coal hatch in the dark. That was the whole of why she had come the slow clean way. She owed the hatch the meeting first. She left Alice behind the wall, which was the cost of trying the right road, and she did not look back at the blind face of the building, because looking back was a thing a watcher would have read.

*She is close,* came the thread of Raizen against the bond, the smallest possible thought, banked like his fire. *I felt you find her.*

*I know.*

*You walked past.*

*I know,* she told him, and kept walking, and the dragon said nothing else, but she felt the whole prismatic core of him pull tight at her neck with a grief that matched her own, every element in him held still, and she was glad past saying that she had let him come.

The safe house under the baker's sign was a real bakery, which was the kind of joke Lady Lightwell would have made: hide the heart of the resistance behind a thing that smelled of bread, in a city that had started eating its bakers. The sign over the door was a painted wheat-sheaf gone brown with age, and beneath it, where a careless eye would read it as part of the carved border, was the flower-code mark, a five-petalled thing with one petal left open, the open petal that meant the same as the open circle in Lightwell's lists, the maybe, the sign of the people who had not decided they were enemies of everyone unlike themselves. She gave the knock Jazen had drilled into her, two and three and one, and waited in the grey street with her banked sight feeling for the harder lights of steel and finding none, and the door opened on a chain, and an eye looked at her, and then the chain came off and a hand drew her in fast out of the light.

The room beyond the shop was a baker's back room, flour-dusted and warm, the ovens cold at this hour, a long scrubbed table, and at the table, alone, stood the man Lady Lightwell had marked in the most guarded pages she kept and never found the right mouth to send.

He was older than she had let herself imagine. She had built him, on the march, into something with the Chancellor's straightness or Jazen's banked fire, and he was neither. He was perhaps sixty, soft through the middle, his light-mage robe of good grey wool worn shiny at the elbows and cuffs like a thing worn every day for thirty years by a man with no second one. His hair was white and combed flat with water and would not stay flat. And the one detail that did not fit, the thing her eye went to and held, was his hands: a councillor's hands should have been soft, ink-stained at most, the hands of a man who voted and signed and was carried places. His were a labourer's hands, scarred across the backs, the nails square and short and clean, the hands of a man who still did something with them when no one was looking, and she filed that away and did not yet know what it meant.

"You're the child," he said. His voice was low and careful and tired, the voice of a man who had learned long ago not to be overheard. "Lightwell's last reader. I had given up believing she would ever send anyone. She has been dead a year."

"She didn't send me," Viridia said. "She left a mark and a guess in a book, and I followed it. My name is Viridia."

"Halden Voss." He did not offer the hand, in this city you did not, but he inclined his head, an old formal courtesy out of place in a flour-dusted back room. "I sit on the Conclave. I have sat on it for nineteen years. I voted for the decree that put your people on the road I am told is two days from these walls." He let that stand, unsoftened, the way an honest man lays a debt on the table before he says anything else. "Sit, child. I have very little time and I expect you have less. Tell me the thing Lightwell thought I would hear. And tell it to me straight, because I have been lied to by experts my whole life, and I no longer have the patience to be lied to by amateurs."

She sat. Raizen stayed banked and hidden in the dark of her collar, a warmth she did not name to the councillor, because a dragon at her throat was a thing a frightened man might read wrong before he read it right. She laid her hands open on the scrubbed boards, the way she had seen Jazen do it when he wanted to be believed, and she told Halden Voss the truth.

She told it the way Amber had taught her without meaning to, with the hard part first. She did not begin with the gift. She began with the cut. She told him that the severing was not a thing that had happened to the world, the way a flood happens or a winter, but a thing that had been done to it, on purpose, by frightened men with a council and a vote, six hundred years ago, who had reached into every living core and capped it at half because a world of whole people was more than they knew how to rule. She told him she could see it. She told him she could see the seam where the cut was made, in him, in every core in the city, the place where the second half had been folded down and sealed. She told him the dark magic the Conclave taught its children to fear as corruption was not corruption at all but the missing half, the part that had been stolen, sealed off behind a wall of fear so old that the fear had become a fact and no one alive remembered it had ever been a choice.

She watched his face the whole time she said it, the way she watched everyone, for the one thing that did not fit. And what did not fit, in Halden Voss, was that nothing in his face fought her. An honest man hearing a lie sets his jaw against it; an honest man hearing a truth he has feared all his life goes still, and Voss went still, stiller and stiller as she spoke, his labourer's hands spread open on the wood beside hers, and she understood that she was not telling this man something new. She was telling him something he had already half-known and spent his whole life not letting himself know.

"You feel it," she said, when she had finished. It was not a question. "You've felt it for years. The half that isn't there. The wall in yourself you were taught not to look at. That's why she marked you. Not because you'd believe me. Because you already did, and you've been carrying it alone."

Voss was quiet a long while, his eyes on his own scarred hands.

"When I was a young man," he said at last, "I could not sleep. For a year, perhaps more. Not the ordinary not-sleeping. I would lie in the dark and feel that there was a room in the house I lived in that I had never been into, and that I could not find the door, and that something of mine was kept in that room, something I needed, and that I would die without ever once setting foot in it." He turned his hands over, looked at the scarred backs of them. "I went to a healer. He gave me a draught. It stopped the dreaming but not the thing under the dreaming. In the end I learned to live alongside it, the way you learn to live alongside a sound you cannot stop, the river under the city, the blood in your own ear. I told myself it was the common lot. That every man feels, somewhere in him, that he is less than he was meant to be, and calls it the human condition, and gets on." He looked up at her, and his tired eyes were wet, and he did not wipe them, which she marked, because it was the opposite of her own discipline and she did not have it in her to scorn a thing she could not do. "You are telling me it was not the human condition. You are telling me it was a door. And that someone put it there, and could have not."

"Yes."

"Prove it." He said it without heat, almost gently. "Not because I doubt you, child. Because I have to be able to prove it to nineteen men who will want me dead for asking the question. Show me the door. Show me my own."

And there it was, the thing she had not been certain she would be allowed to do, the proving of the cut to a man who reached for it himself. She had thought she would have to argue it, to win it inch by inch against a lifetime of fear. He had reached out and asked her for it instead. He had been waiting sixty years for someone to come into his back room and say the room he could not find was real.

"I won't open it," she said. "I'm not going to give you your second half across a baker's table in the dark, without asking, the way they took it from you without asking. That's the whole of the thing, councillor. I won't do to anyone what the council that cut us did. So I'll show you the wall, not what's behind it. I'll show you it's there, and that it was built, and you'll feel the build of it, the seam, the work of hands in it. Then you'll know it wasn't always there, because the world doesn't have seams. Only made things have seams. Will that do."

"That will do," he said, very low.

She banked her sight lower still, narrowed it to a thread, because she would touch one core in this city and one only and she would do it so small that no glint in any tower would catch the heat of it. She reached, not into Halden Voss, only up to the edge of him, to the place in his core where the half-wick burned, and she let him feel her find the seam: the line where the flame had been folded down and sealed, smooth where a natural edge would be ragged, regular where a true thing would be wild, the unmistakable evenness of a cut made by an instrument and a hand and an intent. She did not open it. She laid the lightest possible attention along the seam, the way you run a fingernail along a scar to show someone it is a scar and not a wrinkle of the skin, here, and here, the edge of it, the made-ness of it, and then she drew back, and closed her sight down to nothing, all of it, before it could be more than a flicker.

It had taken perhaps three seconds.

Halden Voss made a sound she would remember for the rest of her life, which was not a cry and not a word but the noise a man makes when a thing he has been told all his days was the shape of the world turns out to be a thing that was done to him, and could have not been done, and the grief of the not-having-to and the fury of the done-anyway arrive in him at the same instant and have nowhere to go. He pressed both scarred hands down on the wood and bowed his white head over them and breathed, and she sat across from him and let him, and did not say she was glad, and did not say she was sorry, because both were small coins and this was not small.

"Sixty years," he said into the table. "I felt it for sixty years and I let them tell me it was nothing. I voted. I signed. I sat in that chamber under that man and I voted to do to your people the very thing that was done to me, because I had been taught to call the missing half a corruption, and I was afraid of the dark in myself that was only ever my own light folded down where I couldn't reach it." He raised his head. There was something in his face now that had not been there when she came in, a straightening, the labourer's hands gone still and certain on the wood. "All right," he said. "All right. You came to ask me a thing. Ask it. Whatever it is, the answer is closer to yes than you came here hoping for. Ask it before I lose my nerve, child, because at my age the nerve does not come back twice in one morning."

And for the length of a breath in that warm flour-smelling room, with an old man weeping over the door he had finally found and a fierce certainty rising in his ruined voice, the thing she had walked every other road to reach actually, achingly, almost was. The peace road. The Conclave turned by the right mouth saying the right thing at last. A vote in the light. Alice walking out the top of the workhouse a free woman. The war halted in bowshot of the walls. Every lamp in the city offered its second half and the frightened ones left whole-as-they-were and the door opened by asking, the only way it could ever be opened right. She felt it stand up in the room, real enough to touch, close enough that her chest ached with the nearness of it, the one clean road, the one she would have given nearly anything for, swinging open in front of her at last.

"I need you to put it to the Conclave," she said, and her voice was not quite level, because hope is harder to hold a level voice through than grief. "The truth of the cut. The proof you can carry, that you just felt. I need you to ask for a vote. Not to force the gift on anyone. To tell the world it was a cut and not a curse, and let people choose, one at a time, in the light, whether they want their other half back. That's all. The asking. If the Conclave says it out loud, the war stops, because there's nothing left to fight. The army outside your walls didn't come to conquer you. It came because every other door was barred. Open one door, councillor, and there's no war."

"Two days," Halden Voss said. "You said two days."

"Two days."

He stood. He was steadier on his feet now than he had been sitting. He crossed to a shelf where a baker kept his ledgers and took down not a ledger but a thin black book hidden behind them, and from the book he drew a single folded page, and he held it a moment, and she watched his labourer's hands and understood, before he spoke, that the hands were a councillor's after all, and that what they did when no one was looking was write.

"The Conclave sits the day after tomorrow," he said. "On the eve of your war, as it happens. The High Chancellor will speak first, as he always does, on the dark menace at the gates. And then the floor opens, and any sitting member may be heard, and for nineteen years I have sat in that chamber and been heard on roads and grain prices and the height of the river, and never once on the only thing that mattered, because I could not prove it and I was afraid." He unfolded the page. It was covered, edge to edge, in a small dense labourer's hand. "I have been writing this speech for thirty years," he said. "I never knew it. I thought I was writing other things. Letters I never sent. The same argument, over and over, in different clothes, to no one. I could never finish it, because I could never prove the first line." He looked at her across the warm room. "You have just given me the first line. I can finish it now. I will stand two mornings from now, on the eve of the war, and I will say it, and I will hold up the proof, and I will ask for the vote." A breath. "It may not pass. The chamber is full of frightened men and true believers and the Chancellor's creatures. But it will be said, in the light, before all of them, for the first time in six hundred years. And once a thing is said in that chamber it cannot be unsaid. Even if they kill me for it, the words will have been in the air, and there will be men in that room who could not sleep, like me, who will hear their own door named aloud, and some of them will not be able to stop hearing it after. That is how a thing like this turns, child. Not in one vote. In one man who could not sleep, saying it out loud, so the next one cannot sleep either."

Viridia sat very still and let herself believe it, for one whole breath, all the way.

"Thank you," she said, and it came out of her not level, not stripped, but warm and uneven and young, which it had not been in front of anyone since the school burned, because for the first time since the rise she had heard a light mage say the thing she had walked into a city that wanted her dead to hear one of them say. "I came here believing this road was thin and slow and probably already closed. You've just made it the realest thing in the room. I don't have the words for what that is."

"You have done it for me," Voss said. "The realest thing. We are even." Something almost like a smile moved on his tired face. "Go now, the way you came. Be far from this city when I speak, because the moment I speak, the Chancellor will look for the mouth that gave me the first line, and you must not be a mouth he can find. Get your army its word to halt. And, child." He folded the speech back into the black book. "Whatever happens in that chamber. Whatever they do to me. You came the right way. You came asking. Remember that you came asking, however this ends, because there is a road past this one that I think you can see and I cannot, and I do not want you to walk it believing you skipped the asking. You did not skip it. You sat in my back room and asked. I was there."

She did not know, then, why his saying it made the cold come back into her chest. She would know later.

She rose, and pulled the dark around the edges of her again where it pooled in the back-room corners, and Raizen stirred banked at her throat, and she gave the old man a last look, the white hair that would not stay flat, the shiny elbows of the one robe, the scarred writing hands, and she filed Halden Voss away in the heading she kept for the small number of people who had ever surprised her into hope, and she stepped toward the deepest corner of the room to go.

And as she did, her banked sight, narrowed to almost nothing, brushed the front of the bakery on its way down toward the dark, the way a hand trails the wall of a stairwell without meaning to, and it snagged a second time.

Across the grey street, in the recessed doorway of a chandler's shop that should have been shut at this hour, stood a man.

He was nothing. That was the first wrong thing. He was a labourer in a flat cap with his hands in his pockets, a man waiting for a shop to open, the most ordinary core in the lane, half-wick and steady like all the rest, and her sight would have passed him without a flicker except for the one detail that did not fit, and the detail was this: he was not cold. The morning was cold and grey and the man had been standing in that doorway long enough that his core should have showed the small dimming a body makes when it banks itself against the chill, and it did not. His core was steady and warm and patient in a way that a man killing time before a shop opens is never patient, the particular stillness of a man who is exactly where he means to be, doing exactly what he was sent to do, for as long as it takes.

A watcher. Set on the bakery. Not steel, not a soldier, nothing a frightened resistance would have caught at the door. A patient ordinary man with warm patient eyes, watching the one shop in the lane with a wheat-sheaf sign and an open petal carved in its border.

And then he turned his head, slowly, and looked up the street toward the white towers, and lifted one hand out of his pocket, and made a small unhurried gesture, two fingers, a thing that meant nothing and meant everything, and high above the city, in the towers, she felt the answer come: a single cold true-seeing glint swung down out of the watching network on the road, swung off the army's road it had been minding, and turned, and found the lane, and steadied on the bakery with the patience of a thing that has been told exactly where to look and has all the time in the world to look there.

She had not been the only one to read Halden Voss. The Chancellor had marked him too, years since, the one man on the Conclave who could not sleep, and set a patient warm-eyed nothing in a doorway to wait for the day the right mouth finally came to the door. And the right mouth had come. She had walked the slow clean road all the way to its end, the way she had sworn she would, asking instead of taking, careful instead of loud, withholding the very thing that would have lit the watcher up like a struck match and shown her the trap before she stepped into it. She had kept her core banked and her death symbol cold and her hands clean, every coin of restraint spent exactly as she had promised, and the clean road had led her, asking and asking, straight up to a patient man in a doorway who had been waiting for her to arrive at it.

The glint held on the bakery. Behind her, through the wall, she felt Halden Voss settle at his table with his thirty-year speech and his black book and his finally-found first line, an old man who did not know that the morning he found the courage to be seen was the morning he had been seen, and that the seeing had already, silently, two fingers lifted in a cold doorway, begun to close.

She had two days. The watcher had a gesture. And somewhere up in the white towers a man in silver-framed glasses was already being told that the right mouth had come to the marked door at last, and was already, she knew with the cold clear part of her that never once stopped working, deciding what to do with the both of them before the Conclave ever sat.

She stepped into the dark, and did not let it take her yet, and looked one last time through the front wall of the bakery at the warm patient core in the doorway across the street, and understood that the almost was already over, and that the only person in the lane who knew it was her.
