# Comp Titles

*The Saeren Chronicles — Book One: The Hazel Years*

These comps are chosen for the **quiet / upmarket / character-first / grief-driven** end of
YA fantasy — not the loud commercial blockbusters. Each is recent (within ~7 years),
genre-adjacent, and successful without being an unattainable megahit. The point of a comp is
to tell the right agent "this is the *kind* of book, and the *kind* of reader," and to keep
the wrong champions away.

---

## Primary comps (lead with these)

**1. *A Far Wilder Magic* — Allison Saft (2022)**
*Why it fits:* Lush, lyrical, slow-burn YA fantasy whose engine is interiority and longing
rather than spectacle; an early-20th-century-flavored world with intimate, personal magic and
two outsiders defined by loss and not-belonging.
*How Saeren is distinct:* Saeren's central relationship is found family and a friendship, not
romance; its grief is fresh and raw (a just-orphaned protagonist) rather than ambient; and its
magic system carries a moral/political argument — a world that severed itself — at its core.

**2. *Sorcery of Thorns* — Margaret Rogerson (2019)**
*Why it fits:* An orphaned girl, an institutional setting with a hidden purpose, grief and
loss threaded through, sharp interior voice, and magic treated as something old, textual, and
alive. The same upmarket-YA reader who loves Rogerson's tone will recognize this book.
*How Saeren is distinct:* Saeren is quieter and more restrained — closer to literary than to
adventure — and its protagonist's defining trait is composure-as-armor rather than impulsive
heroism; the institution here is not just hiding a secret, it is built to *suppress* the
protagonist's existence.

---

## Supporting comps (use one if a third is wanted, or to fine-tune tone)

**3. *Spinning Silver* — Naomi Novik (2018)**
*Why it fits:* Character-rich, emotionally complex fantasy with strong female interiority,
parental loss in its bones, and a cold, beautiful, fable-like restraint. Signals "literary
fantasy reader."
*How Saeren is distinct:* Saeren is firmly YA and contemporary-school-flavored (trains,
motorcars, a prep school), with a single tight POV and a coming-of-age spine.
*(Slightly outside the 5-year window; use only if you want the literary-fantasy signal and
can frame it as a touchstone rather than a recent comp.)*

**4. Frances Hardinge — *A Skinful of Shadows* / her body of work (2017– )**
*Why it fits:* The gold standard for *literary*, grief-and-rage-and-tenderness YA/crossover
fantasy with a quiet, watchful girl at the center and prose that trusts the reader. A signal
to literary-leaning gatekeepers about ambition and register.
*How Saeren is distinct:* Saeren is more emotionally direct and more obviously series-shaped,
with a found-family warmth Hardinge usually keeps cooler.

---

## How to deploy

- In the **query**, name **two**: *A Far Wilder Magic* meets *Sorcery of Thorns* is the
  cleanest, most current, most defensible pairing.
- Keep **Hardinge** in your back pocket for literary-leaning agents whose MSWL signals
  ambition/voice over hook.
- **Do NOT** comp Bardugo's *Six of Crows*, Sarah J. Maas, *Fourth Wing*, or "the next Harry
  Potter." They are the wrong promise for this book and will set up the reader to be
  disappointed by the very things that make it good (its quiet, its patience, its withheld
  payoffs). The earlier draft package comped Bardugo/Pierce/Nix; those are superseded —
  Pierce and Nix are not recent, and all three set a louder expectation than the book delivers.
