Three days after both her parents are killed, fourteen-year-old Viridia Saeren is delivered to Hazel Preparatory, the country's most prestigious school for light mages. She arrives carrying a grief she refuses to let anyone see and a secret she doesn't yet understand: she is the only person alive born with both halves of a magic the world severed in two, long ago, out of fear. Hazel promises to nurture talent. In truth it is the quiet machinery built to erase what Viridia is — and the safest place she has ever been handed turns out to be the most dangerous place she could be.

Over a single school year, Viridia bonds an extinct three-hundred-year-old tiger and a newborn dragon, learns the dead language that lets a mage write intent directly into magic, and discovers that her warm headmistress runs a secret resistance from inside a school full of dark-mage girls hiding behind borrowed light. As she begins, against all odds, to heal — to make a true friend in Alice, to laugh again — the most powerful man in the country arrives wearing glasses built to see exactly what she is hiding. When he leaves, he signs the order that brings the war through the front door. Viridia survives the night that ends Hazel only by making a choice she will carry forever: she saves the many, and loses the one friend who ever loved her without wanting anything back — and she does not know, when the smoke clears, whether Alice is alive or dead.

THE HAZEL YEARS will appeal to readers of **Leigh Bardugo's Grisha trilogy** (a politicized magic system and a morally complex heroine), **Tamora Pierce's Tortall books** (a young woman quietly outgrowing a world that underestimates her), and **Garth Nix's Old Kingdom** (dark magic that is not inherently evil, and consequences that bite). It shares their DNA without being derivative of any of them.
