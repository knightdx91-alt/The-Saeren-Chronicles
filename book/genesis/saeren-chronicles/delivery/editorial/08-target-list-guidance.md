# Target List Guidance

*The Saeren Chronicles — Book One: Hazel Academy*
Who to query, and how to find them. This file deliberately gives you **profiles and search
methods**, not invented names — query only agents/presses you have personally verified are
open, reputable, and a genuine fit.

---

## Positioning recap (this drives the whole list)

This is a **quiet, interior, slow-burn, grief-driven, literary-leaning YA fantasy** in the
loudest, most saturated commercial lane in publishing (magic school + chosen-one). An honest
professional read concluded: **the quality is not the blocker; the positioning is.** The book
is *closest to a clean yes* with **boutique / literary-leaning agents and small/independent
presses**, and is **fully viable to self-publish**. It is a *harder* sell to a Big-5-focused
top agency looking for a loud, hook-first, standalone-payoff debut — not because it isn't good,
but because it's the wrong shape for that buyer.

So: aim for taste-matched gatekeepers, not the biggest names.

---

## Agent profile to look for

Query agents whose stated wishlist (MSWL / agency bio / interviews) signals:

- **"Upmarket" or "literary" YA / crossover fantasy** (the word *upmarket* is the strongest tell).
- **Character-first, voice-driven, quiet, interior** as explicit positives.
- **Grief, loss, mental-health interiority, found family** as themes they actively want.
- **Slow burn / atmospheric / "books that take their time."**
- Comp affinity for **Allison Saft, Margaret Rogerson, Naomi Novik, Frances Hardinge, Laini
  Taylor**, or "literary fantasy."
- A reputation for **debuts and developmental partnership**, not only auction-driven lead titles.

**MSWL traits to *de-prioritize* (likely mismatch):** "high-concept hooky YA," "romantasy /
spice," "fast-paced commercial," "the next [megahit]," "needs a killer one-line hook." Your book
will lose in that frame even though it's good.

### How to build the actual name list (verify everything)
- **Manuscript Wish List (manuscriptwishlist.com / #MSWL)** — filter for the traits above.
- **QueryTracker / Publishers Marketplace** — confirm the agent is *currently open*, has recent
  YA fantasy sales, and is at a legitimate agency (AALA member or equivalent).
- **Acknowledgments pages** of your comp titles — the agents thanked there represent your lane.
- **Recent deal announcements** for upmarket/literary YA fantasy (Publishers Marketplace, PW).
- **Interviews/podcasts** where the agent describes wanting "quiet" or "character-driven" work.
- Cross-check every agent against **Writer Beware** and **Absolute Write** before querying.

## Small / independent press profile

If going the small-press route (a strong fit here), look for presses that:

- Publish **literary or upmarket YA / crossover fantasy** with care for prose.
- Have a **distribution deal** (e.g., via a major distributor) and real bookstore presence.
- Pay **advances or fair royalties**, never charge the author (no "contributory"/vanity terms).
- Have a **visible, well-reviewed backlist** in your register, and authors who speak well of them.
- Accept **un-agented submissions** (many small presses do — widening your path).

Verify each against Writer Beware and recent author testimony. A good small press that *gets*
the quiet book is a better home than a big agent who wants to make it loud.

## Self-publishing path (genuinely viable here)

The honest read rates self-pub **ready now**, with one caveat: discoverability in YA fantasy is
trope/hook-driven, so success depends on promising the *right* thing.

- **Cover & blurb must promise grief / found family / a girl who refuses to be made small** —
  NOT spectacle. See `07-cover-brief.md` and the Amazon description in `06-metadata-keywords.md`.
- **Lean into the crossover adult literary-fantasy reader**, who is more patient with interiority
  than the propulsion-seeking teen reader.
- **Categories & keywords:** use the upmarket/grief/found-family framing in `06`, not the
  saturated "epic fantasy" terms where it will be buried and mismatched.
- **Strongly consider holding launch until Book Two is drafted/scheduled.** The book ends on an
  open wound (Alice) and deferred mysteries by design; readers forgive that far more readily when
  the next book is visibly coming. Releasing Book One alone indefinitely is the main risk.
- **Production:** finish CMYK cover, full front/back matter, and a widows/orphans proofing pass
  before print (see `../production/`).

## Querying strategy notes

- **Personalize on taste, not hook.** This book's appeal is a register; the highest-yield query
  opener is a genuine MSWL match (see `04-query-letter.md`).
- **Send in small batches** (6–8), adjust on response patterns, and treat full requests that
  pass as the real signal — the genesis read expects boutique/literary to be the warmest lane.
- **Be honest about the trilogy shape.** Sell it as "complete arc, larger mysteries continue,"
  and have answers ready for the deferred payoffs. The right agent will read that as confidence,
  not a gap.
