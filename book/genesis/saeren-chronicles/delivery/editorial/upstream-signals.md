# Upstream Signals — Packager Honest Assessment

## 1. Was the logline easy or hard to write?

**Easy.** The book's hook is unusually clean and self-evident: "the only person born whole in a world that severed its own magic, hiding inside the school built to erase what it can't categorize." The irony engine (the safest place is the most dangerous place) and the stakes (hide it or die) are load-bearing and surface immediately. **No PREMISE CLARITY ISSUE.** The premise is strong and shareable.

## 2. Does the query synopsis end at a point of genuine suspense?

**Yes, naturally.** The strongest pre-climax break is the night of the attack and the impossible choice: Viridia saves the many and may have lost Alice, and does not know if her friend is alive. That break creates real suspense (the unresolved fate of Alice is the book's open wound and the engine of Book Two) without revealing the camp/Jazen resolution. The escalation ladder (summoning → bond → resistance revealed → the source/well → the attack) is clearly articulated and gave several clean stopping points. **No STRUCTURAL SUSPENSE ISSUE.**

## Notes for the Orchestrator / Author

- The High Chancellor is never named on the page (only "the High Chancellor"). The foundation calls him "Erik Meros"; editorial copy deliberately uses only the title to stay true to the manuscript. If a name is wanted in cover copy later, settle the canonical spelling first.
- Comps are modern-classic anchors (Bardugo/Pierce/Nix). Bardugo and Nix remain current shelf-mates; Pierce is positioned as the genre-defining DNA reference. If an agent strictly enforces "nothing older than 5 years," consider adding one very recent YA fantasy comp.
- Alice's fate is intentionally unresolved — kept out of all no-spoiler copy. It is the trilogy's first hook; protect it.
