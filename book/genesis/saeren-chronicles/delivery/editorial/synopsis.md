# Synopsis — The Saeren Chronicles, Book One: The Hazel Years

> For agents and editors. **Contains the ending.**

---

## One-Page Synopsis

Three days after both her parents are killed, fourteen-year-old **VIRIDIA SAEREN** is delivered to **Hazel Preparatory**, an elite school for light mages. She is silver-blonde, watchful, and very good at one thing: hiding what she feels. No one sees her grieve. On her first day she is unofficially sponsored by a fourth-year, **AMBER SUMMERS**, and befriended by **ALICE PEMBROOK**, a warm, first-generation mage who becomes her first real friend.

Among her late mother's things, Viridia finds a spellbook with a thousand-year family tree (one name has no dates and "doesn't fit"), a letter hinting her mother's death had "circumstances," and an ancient **focus** with a note in an unfamiliar hand: *keep this with you always.* When a teacher, **MRS. ZORAN**, wakes the focus, it nearly drains her — it was made for someone with **both halves of the gift**, light and dark, in an age before the magic was severed.

Summoning a companion in the forest, Viridia's focus blazes white **and** black at once, and she bonds **LOR-AR**, a horned sylvan tiger three centuries old, thought extinct. Lor-ar teaches her **mana-sight** and a devastating truth: humans are *broken* — long ago the light mages severed humanity's dark half out of fear. Viridia was born with both, fused into one whole core larger than the tiger's. She is not lesser. She may be the only whole person alive. She cannot tell Alice, whose core she now sees is half of what it should be.

The forest's ancient **pact** answers her call again: she is taken to two dragons, **DRAKE** and **VARISSA**, and bonds their hatchling, **RAIZEN**, in a true-bond that ties their lives together. The headmistress, **LADY ISOLDE LIGHTWELL** (rose-tinted spectacles; blue shoes), turns out to be a dark mage hiding a black core behind a light-core necklace — and Hazel is a secret refuge and resistance hub. Lightwell tells Viridia about **JAZEN**, the grieving dark-mage leader holding off war so long as the capital kills no more dark mages by decree.

Viridia learns the **Moravian language** (writing intent directly into magic), trains in spear-combat with the warrior **DANGRIS**, and begins, against all odds, to heal — even to laugh again. Then the **HIGH CHANCELLOR** arrives to oversee the ranking test, wearing silver glasses of true seeing. Viridia banks her power and passes as ordinary; her judgment test goes **gold** (unheard-of) when she refuses to vote for killing dark-mage children. Earning top marks, she is led through a portal to **the source of all magic**, where she learns the severing was deliberate, feels an unreachable **thread bearing her mother's presence**, and senses a terrifying **void/absence** the source itself fears. Her core grows enormous. She lies to the Chancellor about what she saw — but he half-suspects.

The Chancellor leaves, signs the decree, and **the army attacks Hazel by night.** Lor-ar kills a soldier in her room; Viridia fights through burning corridors, destroys a captain using his own protection rune, and reaches the main hall where Lightwell duels the Chancellor. Lightwell telepathically orders her to **save who she can and find Jazen** — then reveals nothing of her fate. Forced to choose between rescuing the eight younger girls behind Amber's failing shield and crossing three burning corridors for Alice in the medical ward, Viridia **chooses the eight** and escapes by portal. She does not know if Alice is alive.

In the woods, the survivors are met by Jazen, who proves to be **Lightwell's hidden son**; he grieves the mother he can no longer receive letters from. At the resistance camp, a council debates war; Jazen resolves to **march on the capital.** Viridia asks him to put out word on every line for any news of Alice Pembrook. The book ends with Viridia at the camp's edge, facing forward — arrived with grief and a focus she couldn't read, leaving with a tiger, a dragon, an ancient language, a war, and no word of her friend. She does not know if Alice lives. She only knows she will not stop.

## Three-Page Synopsis

### Setup

The last ordinary morning of **VIRIDIA SAEREN**'s life smells of scorched bread and her mother's tea. Her father burns the toast; her mother, **BELLA** — a woman whose stillness is "the stillness of deep water" — tells her: *"You see more than you let on. Just be careful who you let see you do it."* Three days later, both parents are dead, and a guardian delivers Viridia to **Hazel Preparatory School**, an elite institution that trains the country's light mages. Viridia is fourteen, slight, silver-blonde, and grieving in total silence — she has decided no one will ever see her cry.

At the gate she is met by **AMBER SUMMERS**, a fourth-year with chipped black nails who notices Viridia's dark-blue stone and, on impulse, becomes her sponsor before they have even spoken. Inside, **LADY ISOLDE LIGHTWELL** — composed, rose-tinted spectacles, incongruous scuffed blue shoes — leads orientation. Viridia is befriended by **ALICE PEMBROOK**: warm, freckled, first-generation, certain she doesn't belong, and instantly able to see Viridia's grief. When Viridia, drowning silently, can't make herself stand, Alice simply says, *"Walk with me?"* — and she finds she can.

### Inciting Incident

Unpacking, Viridia finds her inheritance: her mother's **spellbook**, opening on a thousand-year family tree (one name has no dates and "doesn't fit the pattern"); a letter from Bella mentioning, ominously, "the circumstances of my passing"; and an ancient silver **focus** with a note in an unknown hand — *This has been passed down your mother's side… keep this with you always.* In magic class, **MRS. ZORAN** wakes the focus and nearly drains herself doing it; privately she warns Viridia it was made for someone with **both halves of the gift**, and that the light mages "killed their way" to a world of half-mages and gift-less people. *Knowledge is power* is inscribed on the focus — the old creed of the dark.

### Rising Action

Taken to the forest by Amber to summon a companion, Viridia reaches with everything she has — and her focus blazes **white and black at once.** She bonds **LOR-AR**, a horned sylvan tiger, three hundred years old, supposedly extinct, who refuses to be a charm and shrinks to house-cat size instead. Lor-ar teaches her **mana-sight** and the central truth of the series: humans are *broken* — their dark half was severed long ago out of fear. Viridia was born whole, both halves fused into one core larger than Lor-ar's own. She is not the lesser thing; she may be the only whole person she has ever met. Looking at Alice with the sight, she sees a half-core, a wound Alice will never feel — and resolves never to tell her. It is "a lonely kindness."

The ancient **pact** between humans and beasts, woken by her summoning, draws her to the dragons **DRAKE** and **VARISSA**, who are giving up their only hatchling. Viridia bonds **RAIZEN** at the moment he breaks the shell — a **true bond** that fuses their lifespans. Varissa warns that if harm comes to her son, she will burn the school to the ground. Lightwell, confronted, reveals (through Viridia's new sight) that she herself is a **dark mage** hiding a black core, and that Hazel is a refuge and the hub of a **resistance**. She tells Viridia of **JAZEN**, the most powerful dark mage alive, who keeps suing for peace — terrified of the monster he became the day soldiers burned his sister — and who will stop asking the day the capital kills another dark mage *by decree.* Walking the corridor, Viridia sees the truth: scattered girls all over Hazel wear light-core necklaces over hidden dark cores. *The war is already inside the walls.*

Over weeks, Viridia learns the **Moravian language** — writing intent directly into magic, bypassing the focus — and shares it with Alice and, ultimately, with Mrs. Zoran, who weeps over her first focus-free flower and vows to teach it to every girl. The **DANGRIS**, savage warrior creatures born light-only and content with it, train Viridia in the spear and gift her four Damascus weapons and the **Heart of the Spear**, naming her one of them. Riding Raizen, fighting, laughing, Viridia **begins to heal** — which, the narrative warns, is exactly when things get worse.

### Climax

The **HIGH CHANCELLOR** arrives to oversee the ranking test, wearing silver **glasses of true seeing.** Drilled by Lor-ar, Viridia banks her core to a first-year's flicker and shows him nothing. Her judgment test turns **gold** — perfect, unheard-of — when she refuses the historical "correct" answer and votes *not* to exterminate dark-mage children. As top student, she is led through a portal to a hidden cave: **the source of all magic.** Touching it whole, she learns the severing was a deliberate act of frightened equals throttling a rival, feels an unreachable **thread bearing her mother's presence** running down into the source, and senses, at the rim of the world's weave, a **void — an absence the source itself fears.** Her core swells to a furnace. She lies to the Chancellor about what she saw; he lets the lie stand, but his careful final question tells her he suspects.

He leaves and signs the decree. Days later, **the capital's army attacks Hazel at night.** Lor-ar kills a soldier who breaks down her door; Viridia fights through burning corridors with spear and Moravian magic, destroying a **captain by turning his own protection rune into a bomb.** In the main hall, **Lightwell** — necklace off, black core blazing — duels the Chancellor and is, perhaps, winning. She reaches Viridia telepathically: *Save as many as you can. Find Jazen. You were chosen.* Then Viridia faces the unbearable choice: the eight younger girls behind **Amber's failing shield**, or three burning, soldier-held corridors to the medical ward where **Alice** is on shift. She **chooses the eight**, opens a portal, and escapes into moonlit woods. The space where Alice should be holds nothing at all.

### Resolution

The survivors are intercepted by **Jazen**, who reveals he is **Lightwell's son** — placed in the world under another name so she could run her resistance from inside the school for fifteen years. Told of the attack, he grieves the mother whose unsigned, twice-yearly letters will never come again. At the hidden resistance camp, a frightened **council** (Coram, Brutus, two women, none of them villains) debates whether to keep hiding or to fight; Jazen — having lost the inside ally who made hiding safe — resolves to **march on the capital.** He tells Viridia that his mother was "keeping you for something," and that there is a place for her here: *"We're done with small."* Viridia asks him to put out word on every resistance line for any news of **Alice Pembrook**, alive or dead; he promises true news when it comes.

The book closes on Viridia at the camp's edge, taking an accounting. She arrived with three suitcases, a stone, and a single wish — to disappear. She leaves with a tiger, a dragon, the ancient language burned into a memory no fire can take, four Damascus spears, a furnace-sized core, the truth of the severing and the frayed thread that might one day mend it, a war she did not choose, and no word of her friend. The girl who wanted to be small burned up in the corridors of Hazel; she does not mourn her. She reinterprets her mother's warning — *be careful who you let see you do it* — not as *hide* but as *choose*: you decide who sees. She faces forward, toward the trees, the war, and the not-knowing. She does not know if Alice is alive. She only knows she will not stop.

### Thematic Resonance

*The Hazel Years* is, on its surface, a boarding-school fantasy of bonded creatures and ancient languages; underneath, it is a book about grief and about the violence of division. Its governing idea — *division is artificial and harmful; the severing of light and dark was an act of fear, and the same impulse that broke the magic breaks people* — is dramatized rather than stated, delivered always through relationship: a teacher's dead brother, a sponsor's shattered core, a friend's invisible wound. Its question — *what is the difference between being whole and being broken, and who gets to decide?* — is never answered on the page, which is the point. Book One completes a full survival arc while seeding the trilogy's larger questions (the mother-thread, the void, the unsigned note, the family-tree name) for Books Two and Three.
