## DRAG FLAGS (tension AND momentum ≤ 5, or high-summary / nothing-changes)

- **Ch.10 — strongest drag candidate (T3/M3).** This is a *montage* chapter ("got it wrong for a week," "two weeks after he hatched," "late spring by then"). Beautifully written and emotionally true, but the only irreversible plot change is "Raizen speaks." The chapter's real job is "she is healing," which is a *state*, not an *event*. Lowest combined energy in the book. **The passage most at risk of being skimmed:** the Mrs. Zoran language-conversion sequence (¶ "She got it wrong for a week, the same as Viridia had…") — lovely, but a second iteration of a beat we already saw Viridia do.
- **Ch.7 — borderline drag (T4/M3), 4% dialogue.** Essentially a single long mentor lecture (Lor-ar's thesis on brokenness) plus the mana-sight reveal. The *idea* is huge, but plot momentum is near-zero: it ends roughly where it began (she's about to go to the office, same as the start of Ch.8's recap). The carrots-are-alive beat saves it from inertia.
- **Ch.15 — drag *by design* (T4/M3).** The deliberate "calm before the storm." It earns its place as the emotional peak and the necessary breath before Ch.16, but back-to-back with Ch.14's quieter resolution it makes a **two-chapter low-energy run right before the climax.** Acceptable, but tight.
- **Ch.6 — mild (T4/M4).** Recovery + "you are broken" set-up. Functions as connective tissue between the two big summoning/hatching beats.

## RUSH FLAGS (multiple major turns with little breath, or whiplash jump)

- **Ch.16 — controlled rush (T10 from Ch.15's T4 = +6 whiplash).** This is the largest single tension jump in the book and it is *intended* (stream/accelerated chronological). It crams: home invasion, first kill, the captain set-piece, the gallery duel, the alcove choice, the portal escape, the Alice-absence. That is a lot of irreversibles in one chapter. It mostly works because the prose accelerates with it, BUT the **captain's monologue** (the "I have girls of my own / the law is the law" speech) stalls the rush mid-climax for exposition (it's where the decree is explained). The reader is sprinting and then made to stand still for a villain's backstory. See info-dump-detector.md (Ch.16, moderate).
  - *Note:* there is a visible seam in that speech — two sentences are run together without a paragraph break: *"...what your kind did at Harrow Field." "After what he saw here, he no longer had a choice..."* The closing-then-opening quotes with no break read as a copy-edit artifact.
- **Ch.8 — dense but earned (T8).** Pact + Drake + Varissa + hatching + bond + death-threat in one chapter. High information load, but the emotional throughline (a mother giving away her only child) carries it.

## OSCILLATION CHECK (good books breathe; flag flat runs of 3+)

The book breathes well in the first half: 5–6–5–6–**7**–4–4–**8** is a healthy up/down with two spikes (Ch.5, Ch.8). 

**Flat/low run flagged: Ch.10 → Ch.11 → Ch.12 reads 3 / 5 / 6 in momentum but sits emotionally "safe" for a long stretch**, and more importantly **Ch.14 → Ch.15 is a two-chapter quiet run (T7→T4, M6→M3) immediately before the climax.** The bigger structural note: after Ch.9 *names* the war as imminent, the live external tension drops to its lowest sustained stretch of the book (Ch.10–12) — the reader is told the clock is ticking, then the clock goes quiet for ~15k words. This is the pacing correlate of the "stated-but-unfelt middle" finding in analytical-peer-review.md §3.

## DIALOGUE-DENSITY NOTES

- **Lowest:** Ch.7 (4%) and Ch.16 (7%). Ch.7 is mentor monologue; Ch.16 is action. Both are intentional, but Ch.7's near-absence of dialogue compounds its drag.
- **Highest:** Ch.9 (38%), Ch.3 (36%), Ch.12 (33%), Ch.18 (32%). These are the "two-hander" / scene chapters and they read fastest — confirming the book is most propulsive when it's people talking in rooms with subtext (Lightwell's office, Mrs. Zoran's classroom).
- **Caveat:** the measure undercounts Ch.8/14/16, which carry heavy *telepathic* dialogue (italics, no quotes). Their felt dialogue density is higher than the number.

## TOP PACING FIXES

1. **Inject one external-pressure beat into the Ch.10–11 stretch** so the war-clock named in Ch.9 keeps ticking. The "dimness on the horizon" motif is already seeded — let it cause something once.
2. **Compress Ch.10's training montage** (it does its "she's healing" work in fewer beats; the doubled "got it wrong for a week" iterations are the trim target).
3. **Move the captain's exposition** (Ch.16) out of the climax sprint — drip the decree/glasses logic earlier (Ch.15, Amber already half-explains it) so the climax can stay kinetic. Also fix the run-together quote seam in that speech.
4. **Watch the pre-climax double-low (Ch.14 quiet + Ch.15 calm).** Consider giving Ch.14's back half a sharper out-beat (the Chancellor's trap-question is good — end closer to it) so the reader doesn't coast through two soft chapters into the attack.
