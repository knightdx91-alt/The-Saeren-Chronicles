## 1. PREMISE

**Structural premise:** *Three young people who love each other cure death and birth gods in a basement, then build a benevolent biotech empire to fund mankind's escape to a new world — and discover that "saving the species" requires them to spend orphaned children, paid employees, and finally their own consciences as fuel, while a father whose daughter their industry killed closes the only door they have left.*

**Logline form:** Troy must launch his chosen people through a one-way wormhole to a survivable world or watch humanity die on a poisoned Earth — but the only way to build the ship is to become the monster his idealism swore to outrun, because he believes love is a sufficient license for any cost.

**Concept Pitch Test:** PASSES. "A teenage genius gives himself and the two people he loves superpowers in a warehouse, then they build a gene-editing empire whose secret purpose is to ship modified orphans to another planet before a grieving investigator can stop them." Instantly shareable; the orphan engine is the involuntary "oh damn."

**The Irony Engine (binding):** The contradiction generates conflict by itself — *they are saving humanity by destroying their own humanity.* "A New World" / "A New World At Any Cost." The more good they do publicly (curing disease, designer cures, jobs, hope), the more cover and capital it gives the monstrous private work. Every act of love (Troy serums the people he loves; the MCs "rescue" orphans from the system Troy grew up in) is the same act as the crime. Love is the engine AND the alibi.

**Engagement Type:**
```
engagement_type:
  primary: fascination (45%)   # moral complexity + the can't-look-away dread of the children/camp + twists
  secondary: empathy (35%)     # Troy/Raven love, the three as found family, the investigator's grief
  tertiary: intellectual (20%) # the science, the empire mechanics, the ethics puzzle
```
**Tension to manage:** Fascination wants us watching monsters; Empathy needs us loving them. RULE: never let the reader off the hook by making the MCs cartoonishly cruel (kills empathy) OR by sanitizing the cost (kills fascination). The disturbance must come from people we'd have a beer with. Stage every atrocity through a character who hesitates.

## 2. THEME AS QUESTION

**Spine question:** *Is there a number of lives that justifies any price — and who gets to decide they are the ones allowed to pay it with other people's children?*

Sub-questions braided through:
- Can you save a species without betraying every person in it one at a time?
- Is rescuing a child from a broken system the same as owning them?
- When love is the reason you do monstrous things, is it still love?

The book never answers. Troy believes yes. Raven comes to believe no. Knight believes the question itself is cowardice. The reader is left holding it.

**Theme presence:** ~80%. RECEDES in Ch.2 (warm family diner) and Ch.8 (a pure Troy/Raven domestic chapter) so the cost lands harder by contrast.

## 3. CHARACTERS

### Troy Woods — POV (first person, the Ch.1 voice)
**Surface:** 17 at origin (mid-20s post-jump), 6'11", pale blond, awkward-brilliant, warm, generous, a fanboy who quotes comics. The kind of man who remembers your coffee order. CEO who still wears the same coat.
**Wound:** Grew up in the foster system; moved schools three times; kicked out of homes; only got a real family at sixteen. He was disposable. He decided no one he loves will ever be disposable again — and then built a machine that decides exactly that for other children.
**Lie:** "If I can save enough people, the cost to a few doesn't count against me." (Disposability transferred, not healed.)
**Want:** Launch the new world — get his people through the wormhole.
**Need:** To accept that he cannot be both the savior and innocent; to let one person matter more than the species (Raven).
**Arc:** Wonder → architect → justifier → the man who signs the camp's casualty reports → forced, at the wormhole, to choose between the mission and the person. (Cliffhanger leaves which he chose ambiguous per open endpoint — see proposals.)
**Voice markers:** First-person present-tense drift, run-on enthusiasm, self-deprecating asides, comic-book references, science explained with childlike joy, occasional dropped/typo-flavored cadence smoothed but kept warm. He narrates atrocity in the same eager voice he narrates wonder — that's the horror.
**Contradiction:** A man who weaponizes orphans against the orphan system, and genuinely thinks he's the good guy. Squeamish about literal blood, fine with statistical death.

CHARACTER CHAOS (Commercial-thriller profile — 3/4):
- **Irrelevant obsession:** Fountain pens. Collects them, ranks them, stabs his own leg with one in Ch.1 to test his skin. Surfaces at board meetings (uncaps and recaps one when stalling), and once at the camp.
- **Cognitive distortion:** Sunk-cost reasoning scaled to lives — "we've already gone this far, stopping now wastes everything." Visible in every escalation.
- **Unprompted memory:** The crescent scar from the warehouse coke bottle (gone in Ch.1) — the *memory* of bleeding as a kid recurs at sterile, bloodless moments (the lab; the conference; over a child's chart). Mutates each time.
- **Failed emotional management:** Cries at a vending machine when it eats his dollar, the day after a non-dramatic, off-page death at the camp. Laughs at the wrong moment in a deposition.

### Raven — POV (close third)
**Surface:** Troy's girlfriend; sharp, dry, the operational spine of the company; phasing (walks through solid matter — described as *going thin*, *unstitching*, NEVER named after any trademark). Eyes the company's human cost the way Troy won't.
**Wound:** Has spent her life unable to be fully present/solid even before the power — the power literalizes a pre-existing tendency to slip out of rooms when things get hard.
**Lie:** "If I stay close to Troy I can keep him human." (She thinks proximity is control.)
**Want:** The dream AND Troy AND a line they won't cross.
**Need:** To accept the line was crossed long ago and choose Troy or her conscience, not both.
**Arc:** Co-founder → enforcer → the first to flinch at the children → the one who tries to smuggle a kid out / sabotage / open the door. The romance's emotional truth-teller.
**Voice markers:** Clipped, ironic, ends arguments with a question. Counts things. Touches walls to remind herself she can choose to be solid.
**Contradiction:** The one with a conscience is also the most ruthless operator — she runs the orphan program because she trusts no one else to be kind enough.

CHARACTER CHAOS (3/4):
- **Irrelevant obsession:** Citrus. Peels oranges obsessively, the smell, hoards them; the lab always smells of orange when she's stressed.
- **Cognitive distortion:** All-or-nothing — a plan is perfect or worthless; no partial credit, which makes compromise (and mercy) hard.
- **Unprompted memory:** A childhood game of hide-and-seek with Knight where she hid so well no one found her and she had to come out herself, crying — recurs when she phases through walls and can't be reached.
- **Failed emotional management:** Phases involuntarily when overwhelmed (goes thin and drops through a chair) at a mundane moment — a budget meeting, not the climax.

### Knight — POV (close third)
**Surface:** Raven's brother; bigger, louder, the muscle and the morale; blood turns to bio-electricity, eyes glow electric blue. Charismatic, jokes through everything, the one the kids love.
**Wound:** Always second to his sister and to Troy; the third wheel of a two-person love story who chose to be irreplaceable by being the most committed true believer.
**Lie:** "Doubt is betrayal. If we hesitate, everyone we'd have saved dies on us." (Conviction as belonging.)
**Want:** To matter — to be the one who makes the hard calls the lovers can't.
**Need:** To learn that his loyalty has been weaponized; that being needed for the worst jobs is not being loved.
**Arc:** Comic relief → true believer → the one who runs the camp → the one who, by Book One's end, has gone furthest and can't come back — sets up the Book Two schism between him and Raven.
**Voice markers:** Profane, warm, deflects with jokes, calls Troy "boss" sarcastically, talks to the kids like equals. Goes silent and precise when he's about to do something terrible — the joke drops out (his voice-under-pressure).
**Contradiction:** Gentlest with the actual children, hardest about the numbers. Loves them and spends them.

CHARACTER CHAOS (3/4):
- **Irrelevant obsession:** Competitive about a mobile game he plays during catastrophes; rage-quits over leaderboard rankings.
- **Cognitive distortion:** Mind-reading — assumes everyone secretly agrees with the harsh call and is just too cowardly to say it.
- **Unprompted memory:** The first shock — waking in the warehouse and hurting Raven, watching her fall through the wall. Returns whenever he uses his power deliberately on someone.
- **Failed emotional management:** Laughs uncontrollably at a funeral-adjacent scene; the electricity arcs when he loses control (sparks at the wrong moment).

### The Investigator — POV (close third) — *name PROPOSED: Marcus Vance (alt: Daniel Okonkwo, Ray Calloway)*
**Surface:** Federal genetic-ethics task-force lead, methodical, decent, the reader's surrogate conscience. Told from above to leave the company alone.
**Wound:** His daughter (PROPOSED name: **Ellie Vance**, age 9) died in a clinical trial run by *another* biotech firm. The company that killed her settled and walked. He cannot punish the guilty, so he transfers the guilt to the biggest, brightest player in the field — the MCs.

**Want:** Bring the company down; specifically, get Troy.
**Need:** To grieve his actual daughter instead of hunting a proxy.
**Arc:** Distant clock → gets uncomfortably close → realizes by Book One's end he was *right by accident* (they really are monstrous, but not for the reason he believed). The horror: the wrong man, wrong logic, correct conclusion.
**Voice markers:** Bureaucratic precision cracking around the edges; talks to his daughter's voicemail (still active); files everything; understatement.
**Contradiction:** The most ethical character in the book is driven by an unethical impulse (revenge by association).

CHARACTER CHAOS (Commercial-thriller, but he's the empathy anchor so 4/4):

## 6. VOICE DEFINITION
(Full executable spec in `voice-dna.md`. Summary here.)
**Vocabulary level:** 5 (Troy: bright, accessible, comics-and-science colloquial; investigator: 6, drier; Raven: 5 clipped; Knight: 4 profane).
**Sentence rhythm:** Troy = run-on enthusiasm + present-tense drift; others = mixed/varied, tighter in close third.
**Formality:** Conversational (Troy/Knight), Measured (Raven/Investigator).
**Humor:** Frequent (Troy/Knight), Dry/subtle (Raven), near-None then breaking (Investigator).
**Emotional register:** Open (Troy), Restrained (Raven/Investigator), Open-warm-then-flat (Knight under pressure).
**Distinctive feature:** Troy narrates horror in the same delighted voice he narrates discovery — the gap IS the dread.
**Anti-patterns:** no analytical similes, no "a wave of X washed over," no metacognitive narration, em-dashes ≤4/chapter (style gate).

**VOICE UNDER PRESSURE:**
- Troy: enthusiasm hardens into *justification* — sentences get longer, more reasons stacked, the comics references vanish. That's how we know he's lying to himself.
- Raven: goes *more* clipped, asks questions she won't answer, phases involuntarily (prose drops a beat, white space).
- Knight: the jokes stop; sentences go short, military-precise. Sparks in the description.
- Investigator: bureaucratic control tightens then snaps — addresses Ellie directly mid-paragraph.

## 7. STYLISTIC DEVICE
**Device:** Brief epigraph at each act break — fragments of company materials (the public "A New World" brochure copy, an internal memo, a redacted intake form, a child's camp evaluation). **Function:** lets the institution speak in its own bloodless voice; ironic counterpoint to the human chapters. **Frequency:** act breaks only (4×), not every chapter. **Target level:** REVEAL — each document says something no character would admit aloud (e.g., the intake form's "resilience score" column tells the reader the kids are graded as stock before any character says it).

## 8. OPENING STRATEGY
**Ch.1 already exists and is locked-as-benchmark (voice-preserved).** It is a **Voice bomb + The ordinary made strange** opening — a teenager waking with a sticky note on his forehead who has just, casually, cracked the gene for godhood. KEEP. ## MULTI-POV RECOMMENDATION (author asked — RECOMMENDED, not locked)
**Use: First-person present-tense for TROY chapters (preserving Ch.1 exactly); close-third past for RAVEN, KNIGHT, and the INVESTIGATOR chapters, each with a labeled POV header.**
Justification: Troy's first-person voice is the benchmark and the book's signature — converting it loses the asset. But first-person for four heads blurs them and strains credibility (how does Troy narrate scenes he's not in?). Close-third for the others (a) preserves Troy's primacy and intimacy, (b) lets us be in rooms Troy can't enter (the investigator's grief, the camp, Raven's doubt), (c) makes the tense-shift itself a signal — when the chapter is in present-tense first person, you are *inside* the man who can't see what he's doing; when it's past-tense third, you have the distance to judge. The form encodes the theme. Headers like **[TROY]** / **[RAVEN]** / **[KNIGHT]** / **[VANCE]** keep it clean.
Troy carries ~50% of chapters; Raven ~20%; Knight ~15%; Investigator ~15%.
