The first weeks of a world with no one at the top of it went, in Wend, like this.

Spring came up the valley the slow way it had always come, not knowing the world had changed, and the almond tree at the bottom of the garden flowered the way it flowered every year, and Viridia felt the flowers register in the source-sense as she had felt them the spring before, a small green outrageous insistence down under the breastbone, the world making more of itself because making more of itself was the only thing the world had ever known how to do. The terraces went in. The mill turned. The bridge at Hallow stayed broken because the man whose business it had been to mend a bridge was gone with the order that had employed him, and so a man named Corliss who had been a mason before the war took it into his own head one morning to go and look at it, and came back and said it could be done with the village's own backs and a fortnight, and there had followed a long argument behind the mill, over the long table where Alice taught the bowl-of-water lessons, about whether the village owed Hallow a bridge, and who would feed the men who built it, and the argument had been ugly and slow and had gone on for three evenings, and at the end of it the bridge was going to be built. That was how things got decided now. Slowly, and from below, and badly, and they got decided.

She watched it from the high wall that closed the gardens at their top, where she still went most evenings to read the cold edge of the world, and the reading was easier now, the rim leaning back off the warmth the smallest degree at a time, going the slow way to its edge, and she stood up there with the whole even traffic of the world coming and going across her and watched a village three evenings deep in a fight about a bridge, and did not go down to settle it, and the not-going-down was hers.

* * *

The Dangris came to her on a grey morning at the top of the gardens, and she knew before she turned what they had come to say.

There were three of them, the way there had been three at the cavern mouth, shadowless at the seam where the cold leaned thinnest, and the cold leaned thinnest where they stood the way it had always leaned thinnest where they stood, and she felt them as she felt the Dangris, which was as a hole in the source-sense, a place where the traffic of the world did not go because the thing standing there did not belong to the traffic of the world. They were a pact older than the war. They had come, two years gone, because a girl with a spear-pendant had called and the calling had a debt in it, and they had stood-with through a burning school and a continent and a year of cold leaning on the world, and standing-with was the whole of what they did and the whole of what they were.

The one in the middle was the one whose voice she had heard at Hazel, in the dark, the voice that was not a voice so much as a meaning arriving in her where a voice would have. *The standing is done,* it said, and there was no grief in it because grief was a thing of the warmth and the Dangris were a thing of the seam, but there was something in it that the warmth would have called grief if it had been warm. *The thing we came to stand against has gone back to its edge. The thing that called us has become a thing with no need of calling.*

"I know," she said. She had felt the pact in her chest go slack the way you feel a rope go slack, sometime in the night after the rebirth, and had not let herself name it, because naming it meant this morning was coming.

*You hold the door now,* the meaning arrived. *We held the line a girl could not. The girl has become the door. There is no line for us to hold that you do not hold better, being it.* And then the thing that was not a voice did a thing she had not expected, which was to lean, the whole shadowless three of them, the smallest degree toward her, the way a thing leans that is taking the measure of another thing it respects. *You did not call us to die for you. You called us to stand. We have stood. It was a good standing.*

She reached up to her collarbone, where the Heart of the Spear had hung since a girl two years younger than she was now had been given it by a thing with no shadow in a dark she had been very frightened in, and she did not take it off, because it was not hers to give back, it was theirs to release, and they released it, the warmth of the pact in the stone going quiet under her fingers the way Lor-ar's bond had gone quiet, not broken, only open now onto something with no further use for it.

"Will I see you again," she said, and heard her own voice do the careful flat thing it did when the alternative was the burning.

The meaning did not answer that the way a person answers it. It answered it the way a thing answers that has stood at the edge of everything since before there was a word for everything. *We will come again,* it said, which was what it had said to her in the dark at Hazel when she was fourteen and had not understood that a thing that says *we will come again* is not promising a season or a year. *When there is a standing worth the standing. We will come again.* And then they were not leaning toward her, and then the cold did not lean thinnest anywhere on the wall, and then the place in the source-sense where the traffic did not go was a place where the traffic went, ordinary, full of the village and the spring, and they were gone the way the Dangris went, which was the way a thing goes that was never quite there in the first place.

She stood at the wall a while with her hand on the quiet stone of the pendant and let the burning come up behind her eyes and looked down at the gardens until it passed, because the gardens were a good place to look when your eyes burned, and because a standing that good deserved to be seen out by a dry face, and she gave it that.

* * *

Alice laughed in the second week, and Viridia was there for it, and it was the best thing that had happened to her since the cavern.

It happened over nothing. It happened at the long table behind the mill, in the late afternoon, with four of the valley folk hunched over their bowls of still water doing the slow careful work of holding both halves of the gift, the light and the dark together, the way Alice taught it, which was not the fast way and was not the free way and was theirs at the end of it in a way the fast way never could be. The miller's elder girl, the one whose mother was a name in the low place now, had got her bowl to do the thing it was supposed to do, the dark half lying down quiet alongside the light half without either one swallowing the other, and she had been so startled at herself that she had knocked the bowl, and the water had gone across the table and into the lap of the man beside her, who had come up off the bench with a yell entirely out of proportion to a lapful of water, and the girl had clapped both hands over her mouth, mortified, and Alice had looked at the man standing there dripping and outraged and the girl horrified behind her hands, and Alice had laughed.

It came up out of her the way the crooked tooth came up, the one the change in the cell could never quite reach, a thing from before, a coal banked so low for so long that Viridia had stopped expecting the coal under it to catch. But it caught. Alice put her head back and laughed, the whole of it, the wide warm laugh with the one tooth turned out of line in it, the laugh that had come back to her slowly across the year a piece at a time and had been, until this afternoon, only pieces. And the man, who was wet and had been about to be angry, looked at the healer laughing and could not hold the anger against it, and the girl took her hands down from her mouth, and the long table behind the mill, where they had spent the spring learning to carry a thing that frightened them, laughed.

Viridia stood at the edge of it with the village and the spring coming and going across her and watched her oldest friend laugh in the late light, warm all the way down now, the warmth got down to the bottom of her at last, and the burning came up behind her eyes for the second time in two weeks, except this one was not grief, and she had not braced for it, the way she had not braced for the warmth in the cavern mouth, and she looked down at the wet table until it passed, and did not let it count, because she had settled it with herself years since that a burning was a burning whichever direction it came from, and laughed instead, out loud, with the rest of them, which was a thing she was allowed, which was a thing no rule of her mother's had ever forbidden.

Alice caught her eye across the table, still laughing, and there was a whole conversation in it that neither of them said, because Alice did not know, and would not be told, that her core had been remade by the hand of the girl across the table from her, and Viridia carried that unaskedness the way she carried everything, in the low place, unspoken. But Alice knew this much, which was enough: that the warmth was back, the whole of it, and that she was a healer again and not only a survivor, and that the girl who had taught her Moravian over a desk a lifetime ago was standing across a wet table watching her laugh and was glad of it past saying. That much passed between them. It was enough. It was, Viridia thought, more than she had ever let herself hope to be standing in the middle of.

* * *

The men came up from the city in the third week, and that was the morning she could have ruled the world and did not.

There were five of them, and they had come the slow way, riding the line to its broken end and walking the remainder, and they had the look of men who had argued the whole journey about who would speak and had not settled it. They were not soldiers and they were not the worst kind, the kind who wanted a stronger hand. They were the middling kind, the frightened reasonable kind, men who had run things under the old order in small careful ways and now stood in a world with the top gone out of it and the food in the carts spoiling and no one to say where the carts went, and they had heard, the way a thing like that is always heard, that the girl who remade the world was alive in a valley a day and a half down the slow southern line, and they had come to find her, and they had found her at the long table behind the mill on a spring morning with flour on her sleeve from helping the miller, which was not how any of them had pictured her.

She knew what they had come for before the eldest of them got through the throat-clearing of it. She had felt it walking toward her down the road for the length of an hour, five frightened cores climbing the valley with a thing they wanted on them like a smell.

The eldest got it out at last. They wanted her to come to the city. Not to rule it, he said quickly, seeing something in her face, though he did not know what he was seeing, because she had put nothing in her face. Only to settle it. To say how it should be. The factions would not stop fighting because no one among them had the standing to make the others stop, and she had the standing, she had the only standing left in the world, she had made the world, the whole bewildered changed world, with her own hand, and a world that one hand had made would hold still to be ordered by that same hand, and would they not all be saved a great deal of blood if the hand that made them would only, please, say how things should go. He was not a wicked man. He was a tired man asking the most natural thing in the world. He was asking her to be the top of it, because there had always been a top of it, in all of their lives there had always been someone at the top of it, and the absence of a top was a thing none of them knew how to stand inside, and here, ready-made, blessed past argument, was a girl who could be one.

And she could have. That was the thing she stood inside, that morning, with the flour on her sleeve, that none of the five men in front of her could see. She could feel every living core there is. The whole even traffic of the world came and went across her, every birth and every death of it, and she was the door it all leaned on, and if she had wished to she could have leaned the world the way the world leaned on her. She had not a single doubt that it would work. She could go to the city and the factions would stop fighting, because she would say stop, and they would stop, the way a thing made holds still to be ordered by the hand that made it, and the carts would go where she said the carts went, and the bridges would be mended where she said mend them, and there would be no more deaths with no wound because she would say there would not be, and she would do all of it, she knew, she had no illusion about this, for good reasons, out of the cold working part of her that did the arithmetic, because the arithmetic would come out that more lived if she ruled than if she did not, and the arithmetic would be right.

It was the most monstrous thing anyone had ever offered her, and it was offered by a kind tired man with his hat in his hands, and that was exactly the danger, that was the whole of the danger, because no rule of her mother's had ever warned her about the monstrous thing that arrives kind, with a hat in its hands, asking please, dressed up as the obvious good. She had crossed a continent learning that the free good thing is how you lose yourself, and here it was, the freest and the most good and the most certainly herself-ending of all of them, and she could feel the cold working part of her reaching for it, *more would live,* and she stood at the long table and did not let her hand close on it.

"No," she said.

The eldest blinked. He had been braced for a great many answers and that flat small one was not among them.

"You don't understand," he said. "We're not asking you to take a crown. Only to —"

"I understand exactly what you're asking," she said, and heard her own voice do the careful flat thing, the certainty laid over the part of her that wanted, very much, the easy good. "You're asking me to decide for everyone. You're asking me to be the hand that says how it goes, because I'm the hand that made it. And I could. That's the part you should be more afraid of than you are. I could go to your city and stop your war by saying stop, and I'd be right, and more people would live, and in a year there would be a top of the world again and it would be me, and I would have stopped fighting being one before I was finished being one, because no one ever does. The last people who held a hand over everyone's chest and decided what was good for them, I spent a year cleaning up after. I will not be the next ones. Not even to do good. Especially not to do good." She put the flour-dusted hand flat on the long table, where Alice taught the slow way that was theirs at the end of it. "You'll have to decide it yourselves. All of you, in the mud, frightened, badly, the way it's being decided in this valley about a bridge. It's slower than a hand from on high and it's uglier and people will die in the deciding who wouldn't die under me. I know that. I did the arithmetic. And the answer is still no, because a world that gets its deciding handed down from the one who made it is not a world, it's a thing that one hand owns, and I did not spend everything I spent to own you. I made a world so there'd be a world. What you do with it is yours."

She left the rest unsaid, the true bottom of it, which was: *and because the day I say yes to that is the day I stop being myself and become the thing I fought, and the one thing that keeps me from being it is that I keep saying no, every time, especially when it would be good.* She kept that part because it was hers, and because saying it aloud would have made it sound like a virtue, and it was not a virtue, it was a daily refusal of a thing she wanted, and a thing you have to refuse daily is not a virtue, it is a discipline, and a discipline is not for saying out loud to frightened men with their hats in their hands.

They went away down the valley in the afternoon, the five of them, with nothing she had given them except the one thing they had not come for, which was the news that no one was coming to save them and they were going to have to save themselves, and she watched them go from the high wall with the world coming and going across her, and felt the cold working part of her go on quietly insisting, for an hour after they were out of sight, that *more would have lived,* and did not act on it, and the not-acting was the thing, the whole thing, the only thing, that kept the hand on the wall hers.

Amber came up beside her at the wall while the men were still a dark knot going down the road. She had heard the whole of it from the mill doorway.

"You wanted to," Amber said. It was not a question and it was not an accusation. It was Amber doing the thing Amber did, which was naming the true thing at the start and not at noon.

"Yes," Viridia said.

"For good reasons."

"The best ones."

Amber watched the men go a while, the black-nailed hand loose on the wall. "That's how I tell," she said finally, which made no sense until she said the rest of it. "You asked me once how you tell the difference, from the inside, between you and them. The ones who hold a hand over everyone for their own good. I never had a good answer. I've got one now." She nodded down at the road. "Them, they'd have said yes. They came up here hoping you'd say yes. And the difference between you and the thing you're scared of being isn't that you don't want it. You want it. You wanted it just now, on those reasons, I watched you want it. The difference is you said no anyway. That's the whole difference. It's not in the wanting. It's in the no." She pushed off the wall. "You keep saying the no. I'll keep watching to make sure you do. That's the arrangement. That was always the arrangement."

* * *

This is who stayed, when the spring had got its feet under it, and who went.

Drake had gone north in the first days, to the high passes, to be the old one on the rock while a range full of dragons remembered how to be themselves, and she felt him up there, small and far on the kinship that was only friendship and was enough, and once, on a clear evening, she felt him do the thing dragons do that is half flight and half grief and entirely loud, off a high rock, alone, and knew he was all right in the way Drake was ever all right, which was loudly and at a height and on his own terms. The Dangris had gone back to the seam at the edge of everything, the standing done, *we will come again* hanging in the quiet of the pendant on her collarbone. Lor-ar was a quiet open place in the forest of the world, released a year since, not called, not needed, *little one* still hanging in him the way it hung in her.

And Amber stayed. Amber, who had survived the east road on purpose and come north with the party and appointed herself the keeper of the one thing she trusted no one else to keep, stayed in Wend with her fierce ordinary contentment and her questions that grew teeth, and took it as her standing work to watch a girl with enough power to own the world keep saying no to owning it, which was, Viridia thought, the truest pact anyone had made with her, truer than the spear, because no one had given Amber a pendant for it, she had simply decided.

And Alice stayed, warm all the way down, a healer again, teaching the slow way over bowls of still water at the long table behind the mill, with the laugh back in her and the crooked tooth showing when she forgot to be careful, which was more and more often now, which was the whole measure of her mending, that she forgot more and more often to be careful.

And Raizen stayed, and Raizen was the one who was not going anywhere, the doorpost, the second warmth, the far end of the bond, learning to be a person one ordinary infuriating thing at a time. He had got the hang of his feet. He had not got the hang of buttons, the small ones, and swore at them in a language that had no business being used on a shirt. He had got the hang of an apple, though, finally, eating it the whole way down past the middle he had studied for a year and not understood, the unripe place between the flower and the rot, the human part. It was not as complicated as he had made it, he said around a mouthful of it. You simply ate the middle along with the rest and let it be part of the thing. He looked at her when he said it in a way that meant he was not talking about an apple, and she let him have that, because he was right, and because he was learning. The prismatic thing he could not stop being still ran faint off his fingers when he forgot to bank it, in the evenings, when he was tired, a thread of every colour there is, the only place left in him the dragon showed.

* * *

She went up to the high wall on the last evening of the third week, when the men from the city were three days gone and the bridge at Hallow was a day from being a bridge again, and stood with the whole breathing changed unfixed world coming and going across her, and took, for once, not the close reading but the long one.

She had spent a year reading the rim by inches, the tear here, the leaning there, the cold by the degree it gave back. She let her sight, half-come-back, guttering at the edge of its reach, go out as far as it could go, and held the door at the width that let the world keep what it made, and looked, not at the next thing or the worn place or the made cut, but at the whole shape of it, the way you stand back from a thing you have been close to for so long you have forgotten it has a shape.

And the shape was this. The world was not fixed. It would not be fixed. There would be deaths with no wound for a while yet, in the river district and elsewhere, where the dark half came easy to a frightened hand and the slow way had not reached. There would be men checking other men's hands at bridges, the sorting growing back in new clothes, because a thing six hundred years deep does not go out of a people in a spring. There would be hunger where the carts sat, and quarrels that turned to worse than quarrels, and somewhere far off in the let-go country to the west, she could feel it faintly even now, a place where the cold had not finished leaning back, a tired clever sort of trouble going on being trouble in its own low way, the kind that does not announce itself, the kind that picks up a piece of chalk over a half-read warning because it cannot help itself, and she left that one alone too, as she had left the bridge alone and the city alone, because she was the door and not the world's hand and the world had to be allowed to be the world. But the cold was leaning back off it, the smallest degree at a time, going the slow way to its edge. The traffic across her was more births than deaths in the springtime, the lung of the world running full for the first time in six hundred years and full of a clamour of beginnings. The men in the mud were learning, slowly, badly, from below, to grow their deciding out of their own middle. It would take longer than an ordinary life, and hers was not ordinary, hers was the very long way off, and so she would get to see how it came out, all of it, the whole long ungoverned quarrelsome unfixed coming-out of it, which no one in six hundred years had been given to see, and which was either the heaviest gift or the heaviest cost and was almost certainly both.

It was a Tuesday, or near enough. A child was born somewhere far west and she carried it across the sill of her, glad. An old man went out gently somewhere east and she carried that too, and held the door at the width, and stood on the wall with the spring coming up the valley and her oldest friend laughing somewhere below by the mill and the doorpost rooted warm on the far end of the bond and the long view opening out in front of her, mile on mile of it, year on year of it, more than she could see the end of, which was the point, which was exactly the point, because a world you can see the end of is a world somebody owns, and she had refused, would go on refusing, every evening on the wall for the whole of the very long way off, to be the one who owned it.

She looked out at the long view and let it open, and did not look away.
