Raizen learned to count the way a child learns it, which is to say by getting it wrong first, and he got the years wrong the way he had got the buttons wrong, with a flat disbelieving fury, sitting on the garden wall in the last of an evening with his bare feet hanging and the apple core going brown in his hand.

He had asked her how long.

He had not asked it the way the men from the city asked their questions, with a thing they wanted hidden inside the words. He asked it the way he asked everything now, the new flat honest way of a person who had spent a year of evenings finding out that human talk had no edges he could read by the bond, that a person could say a small ordinary sentence and mean the whole of the world by it and you could only know which by their face. He had been looking at the almond tree at the bottom of the garden, the flowers gone now and the leaves come, and he had said, how long does that have, the tree, and she had told him, a hundred years if no one cuts it, more, and he had been quiet a while turning that over, and then he had said, and the man who put it in. Corliss's grandfather, she said, because the miller had told her once whose hand had set the tree there in a spring before the war, a man named Edrick who had been a boy when he dug the hole. He has fifty years on it now, Raizen said, slow, working it. Sixty. And then it goes on without him. And she had said yes, that was the shape of it, that was the shape of nearly everything, a thing planted and a hand gone and the thing going on, and Raizen had nodded as if he had got hold of a true thing, and then he had turned and looked at her with the dark ordinary eyes that still surprised her every time for not being lightning, and asked the other question, the real one under it.

How long do we have.

She had known the question was coming for a year. She had felt it the way she felt a death coming up the valley, a thing already on the road, already inevitable, only not yet arrived. And she found, now that it had arrived, that she had no clean number to hand him, because the number had never once been written down, because the True-Bond was older than the keeping of records and the few who had carried it had not written down the year they got to that no one else did.

"I don't know," she said. "Long."

"Long like the tree."

"Longer."

He took that in. The prismatic thing ran faint off the apple core in his hand, the way it ran off his fingers in the evenings when he forgot to bank it, a thread of every color there is, there and not there. "Longer than Corliss," he said. He was building it now, one post at a time, the way he built everything, the apple and the buttons and the feet, a thing taken apart down to its pieces and set back up so he could trust it would hold his weight. "Longer than the miller's girl. The one with the bowl."

"Yes."

"She'll get old."

"Yes."

"And we won't."

"Slow," Viridia said. "We'll get old slow. The slow way. But yes. We won't, the way they will."

He was quiet so long she thought he had gone down into himself the way he did, the long way down to wherever a creature shaped like a dragon for an age and a man for a year went when it was working. Then he said, in the flat new voice with nothing hidden in it, "How many of them. Will we watch go." And there it was, the thing he had been counting toward, the sum at the bottom of the column, and he had not been counting years at all, he had been counting people, the way you only learn to do when you have just got hands and a face and a span you can feel the end of and have begun, for the first time, to love things that have an end nearer than yours.

She gave it to him plain. That was the one thing the bond had taught her she could not do to him, gentle a true thing into something smaller, because he would feel her doing it down the bond and trust her the less for it, and his trust was the only floor either of them had to stand on for the very long way off.

"All of them," she said. "All of these. Everyone we love who isn't on the far end of this." She touched her own breastbone, where the bond sat warm, where it had sat warm since a school burned. "Amber. Alice. Corliss and the girl with the bowl and the miller and every soul in this valley and every soul we haven't met yet and will. We'll outlast the whole of it. We'll bury them. We'll bury the people who bury them. And then we'll go on."

* * *

She had known the shape of it for a year. She had kept herself from standing all the way inside the shape until now, because there had always been a nearer thing, a tear at the rim or a cold place leaning or a war or a cavern, and a person who is the throttle of the world becomes very good at the nearer thing as a way of not standing inside the far one. But the war was over. The rim leaned back off the warmth by the smallest degree, taking the slow road to its edge with no help wanted from her. The nearer things had run out, the way they do, and what was left when they ran out was the far thing, standing there where it had been standing all along, patient as the rim, waiting for her to have run out of nearer things to look at instead of it.

She felt every birth and every death there is. That was the cost she had paid in the cavern and would go on paying, the door held at its width under her palm forever, the whole even traffic of the world coming and going across her with no eyelid she could shut against it. She had learned to carry that. What she had kept herself from carrying, until an evening on the wall with a man counting people on his fingers, was that she would feel them across a span granted to no one before her, that the deaths she carried would not be only the world's strangers but her own, one at a time, down a length of years so long the front of it was lost in the haze the way the back of the valley was lost in the haze, and that she would carry each of those across the sill of her the way she carried the strangers, glad and grieving in the same breath, and then go on.

Amber would go. That was the one she made herself look at, standing on the wall the next evening with the village coming and going across her, because Amber was the nearest and the hardest and a person who has learned anything has learned to look at the hardest one first and not the easiest. Amber, who had survived the east road on purpose. Amber, with the black under her nails and the questions that grew teeth and the fierce ordinary contentment she had built out of nothing in a valley at the end of a war. Amber's hair would go grey. Viridia could see it already if she made herself, the grey coming up at the temples in some year not yet arrived, the black nails on a hand gone knotted and slow, the questions getting fewer because the breath to ask them got shorter, and then a winter, some winter a long way down the road but not so long as all that, a winter when the door of the world carried Amber across the sill of her the way it carried the old man east, gently, and Viridia would be standing somewhere feeling her oldest watcher go out under her own hand without a wound, and would have to hold the door at its width while she did it, because the door does not stop for the people you love, the door is the reason the people you love got to be alive at all.

She looked down at the gardens and held them until her eyes stopped burning. There was no one on the wall to see her do it. She gave it the dry face anyway, out of the old discipline, and because a grief that big deserved better than to be spent in front of the empty evening, deserved to be saved, banked low, carried down the long way off and let out a little at a time, the way you carry water across a desert, because she was going to need it for a great many years.

It was a Tuesday or near enough. She had stopped being sure of the days.

* * *

Raizen found her on the wall because Raizen could always find her, the bond a lit window in whatever dark house either of them stood in, and he came up the steps in the new careful way he had with his feet and stood beside her not touching, which was a thing he had learned, that a person sometimes wants you near and not on them, and he had learned it the hard way, the way he learned everything, by getting it wrong and feeling the wrongness come back down the bond.

"You looked at it," he said. Not a question. He had felt her looking. The bond did not let either of them hide the big things anymore, only the small ones, the small ones a person is allowed to keep.

"Amber," she said.

He nodded. He had looked at it too, she could feel that he had, the same evening, the count he had been building post by post, and he had got further into it than she had, because he was newer to people and so loved them with less skin grown over the loving, the way a new thing loves, raw. "I did the whole column," he said. "After. Last night. I did Amber and then I did Alice and then I did the girl with the bowl and I did Corliss and I did the miller and I kept doing it and it didn't stop." He turned the dark eyes on her. "It doesn't have a bottom. The column. I kept waiting for it to come out to a number and it doesn't come out. It just goes."

"No," she said. "It doesn't have a bottom."

"How do you carry a thing with no bottom."

And this was the thing, she thought, this was the whole thing, that he had asked her the question that no one else alive could ask her, because no one else alive had the same span at the far end of the same bond, because his was the only other life as long as hers, and that meant his was the only shoulder in the whole turning world that her weight would not crush, the only one she could lean the true size of it into and not break the thing she leaned on. Amber had stood under the spent weight of her on a rise after the cavern and had not flinched, but Amber would age and Amber would go, and you cannot lean the weight of a span on a thing inside the span. You can only lean it on the other end of itself.

"You don't carry it," she said. "Not all at once. That's the trick I haven't got the hang of yet and I'll have the rest of forever to practice it. You carry the nearest one. You love Amber while Amber's here to be loved, the whole of it, like there's no column under her, and when the door takes her you carry that one, just that one, all the way across, and you don't carry the next one until the next one comes. The column's only unbearable if you stand at the top of it and look down the whole drop. You don't look down the drop. You look at the stair you're on." She heard herself, and almost laughed at herself, because she had spent two years learning to do exactly the opposite of this, to look down the whole drop of the world and read the cold at the bottom of it, and here she was telling a year-old man to do the human thing instead, the small near thing, the stair you're on. "I don't always manage it," she said, more honestly. "I looked down the whole drop tonight. That's why my eyes did the thing."

"I felt them do the thing." He said it carefully, the way he said the true small things, because he knew by now that her eyes doing the thing was a place no one else was let, that the front of her face was a wall the whole world saw and the bond was the one door in it, and that she had let him be the door, once, in a dark room at the middle of the war when she had broken the one time she would ever let anyone watch her break, and had let no one since and would let no one again, except, in the small evening way, him.

"Yes," she said.

"You don't have to do the dry face with me." He said it like he was setting it down between them, a thing he had worked out and wanted to give her. "On the wall. With everyone else. I understand it now, the dry face, I worked it out, it's the same as me banking the colors so I don't frighten the miller. It's a thing you do so the world can stand to be near you. But I'm the far end. The colors don't frighten me and the wet face doesn't either. You can put it down, with me. You don't have to carry that one across too."

And she found, standing on the wall with the village coming and going across her and the man who had been a dragon offering her the one thing no one else in her life had ever had it in them to offer, which was a place to set the weight down without it costing her the thing she was, that her eyes did the thing again, and that this time she did not look down at the gardens. She let them burn and looked at him. She did not weep, because she did not weep, because that door had been opened the one sanctioned time and would not open the whole way again, not even here; the guardrail held even against the kindness of it. But she let the burning stand in her eyes where he could see it, and did not turn the dry wall onto him, and that, between the two of them, with the very long way off in front of them and only each other at the far end of it, was the whole of what weeping was for, and was enough.

"All right," she said, when she could. "With you. Just with you."

"That's the arrangement," he said, which was Amber's word, which he had taken off Amber the way he took words off everyone now, magpie, building his human speech out of the scraps of the people he loved, and the borrowing of it made her almost laugh again past the burning, because Amber would have words about him stealing her word, and Amber would not get to have those words for very many more years, and both of those things were true at once and would go on being true at once for the rest of the very long way off, the grief and the almost-laughing in the same breath, which was, she was beginning to understand, simply what it was going to be like, forever, to be the two of them.

* * *

She thought about whether she was still herself on the wall in the evenings, the way you worry a loose tooth, because it was the question under all the other questions and she had never once been able to leave it alone.

She had become a thing with power over everything. She did not flinch from saying it to herself in the plain words, on the wall, where no one could hear her say it; flinching from the plain words was the first way you lost yourself, by dressing the size of what you were in language small enough to live with. She was the throttle of the world. She could feel every living core there is and she was the door they all leaned on and if she leaned back the world would lean the way she leaned, she had no doubt of it, she had tested the edges of it without meaning to and the edges had not held against her and never would. The men from the city had seen one small face of it and had come up the valley wanting her to be the top of the world, and they had been right that she could, and they had been wrong, the way the kind tired monstrous offer is always wrong, that she should. A girl from a desk in a school had become the nearest thing the world had to its own held breath. There was no honest small word for that. She made herself use the true big ones, on the wall, alone, so they would not rot in her by going unsaid.

And the question that lived under the big words was the one Amber had handed her on the wall the week before, the one Amber had carried two years before she found the answer to it: how do you tell, from the inside, between you and the thing you fought. The High Chancellor had held a hand over everyone's chest and decided what was good for them and had been, she had no doubt, certain of his own good heart the whole way down, because no one is the villain of the thing they see from the inside, no one wakes a tyrant, you become one a kind reasonable yes at a time, each yes more obviously good than the last. She had a hand that could hold over every chest in the world. She had the cold working part of her that did the arithmetic and the arithmetic came out, every time, that more would live if she only said yes. She was, in the plain words, exactly the shape of the thing she had crossed a continent and burned a year of her life to stop, with only one difference between her and it, and the difference was not in her power, which was greater than his had ever been, and was not in her wanting, because she wanted it, she had stood at the long table with the flour on her sleeve and wanted to fix the whole frightened bleeding world with a word and had felt the cold working part of her reach for it.

The difference was the no.

That was the whole answer, the one Amber had found and handed her down on the wall, and the thing about it that she turned over now in the evenings was how thin it was, how unheroic, how it was not a quality she had but a thing she did, over and over, not once at a wall to frightened men but every single evening, alone, with no one there to be brave in front of, when the cold working part of her went on insisting in the quiet that more would live, and she heard it, and she did not act on it. There was a tear in the rim tonight, west of the let-go country, and she could have leaned the door and closed it for the village that sat under it and saved, the arithmetic said, eleven lives across the coming month, and she let it lean back its own slow degree instead and let the eleven take their chance with the slow way, because a world whose tears she closed by leaning was a world she leaned, and a world she leaned was a world she owned, and she had not spent everything she spent to own it. She let the eleven go to their chance. She would feel some of them die. She would carry them across the sill of her, eleven strangers she could have saved, and the carrying of them was the price of the no, and the no was the price of staying herself, and she paid it, that evening, the way she would pay it every evening for the whole of the very long way off, quietly, with no one watching, with the cold working part of her going on insisting in the dark that she was wrong.

She was still herself. She knew it the way she knew the births from the deaths in the traffic across her, not by an argument but by a fact she could feel: she was still the girl who had said no to the easy good, because she was, at this exact evening, saying no to the easy good, and a person who is still saying the no is still the person who first said it. The day she stopped saying it she would stop being that person, and would become the other one, the kind reasonable one with a hand over every chest, and no moment would ever stand out that she could point to and say it happened here, only a slow accumulation of yeses each more obviously good than the last. So she did not let there be a first yes. That was all. That was the whole of staying herself, across a span granted to no one before her: a refusal, daily, of a thing she wanted, for no reward, witnessed by no one, world without end. It was not a virtue. A thing you have to do again every evening forever is not a virtue, it is a discipline, and she had decided, on the wall, in the plain words, that she could live inside a discipline. She had been living inside one since she was eleven and a desk in a school. The discipline had only got larger. She had not.

The world was borrowed. She turned that one over last, every evening, because it was the one that let her sleep. She had made the world, with her own hand, in a cavern, and a thing you make is the most owned thing there is, and the whole danger lived in that, in the made thing holding still to be ordered by the hand that made it. So she made herself think of it the other way, the true way, the way that kept the hand on the wall hers: she had not made the world to own it, she had made it so there would be a world, and a thing you make so that it can be itself is not a thing you own, it is a thing you have lent your hand to and then taken your hand off. The world was borrowed. She held the door so the world could go on being the world, and what the world did inside the door it held was the world's, not hers, the bridge and the quarrel and the bad slow deciding from below and the eleven who would take their chance, all of it borrowed back to its own keeping, the loan being precisely the not-owning, the not-leaning, the no. She would hold the door for the whole of the very long way off and never once close her hand on what came through it. That was the lease. That was the whole lease, and she had read it, every evening, and signed it again, and meant it.

* * *

The last of the light went off the valley the way it went off every evening, up the far slope and then off the top of it and then gone, and the chill rose out of the valley the way it rose every night, and the first stars came out over the let-go country to the west where a tired clever sort of trouble went on being trouble in its own low way and she left it alone, as she left the bridge alone and the city alone, because she was the door and not the world's hand. Down by the mill a light was lit and went on burning, and that was Alice, working late over the bowls of still water with someone who could not yet hold both halves of the gift, teaching the slow way that was theirs at the end of it, and the warmth of Alice was a steady thing in the traffic across her, warm all the way down now, the crooked tooth and the laugh and the whole of her mended and mortal and good for, the column said, some forty more years if the slow way and her own stubborn heart held, which was a long time and was nothing, both, at once.

Amber came out of the mill doorway and stood a moment in the lit square of it, the black-nailed hand up against the frame, looking up the dark slope toward the wall the way Amber did, checking, the keeper of the one thing she trusted no one else to keep, and Viridia raised a hand off the cold stone of the wall so Amber would see her there and not have to climb to know it, and down in the lit doorway Amber raised one back, brief, the arrangement, and went in. She would do that for the rest of her life. She would do it with grey hair and a slow hand and fewer questions, until the winter she did not come to the doorway, and Viridia would stand on the wall that evening and raise a hand at an empty lit square and carry her across the sill, just that one, all the way, and not look down the rest of the drop. But not yet. Not for a long time yet. Tonight Amber went into the warm and the doorway stayed lit and the keeper was alive in it, and that, the column with no bottom notwithstanding, was the whole of what there was to have, and it was, Viridia thought, enough, was more than enough, was nearly everything.

Raizen came up the steps behind her, careful with his feet, and stood at the far end of the wall, not on her, near, the way she wanted him, and held his peace, because the bond carried the thing without a word spent on it, and they stood the two of them at the two ends of the wall with the whole breathing changed unfixed borrowed world coming and going across her and the very long way off opening out in front of them both, mile on mile of it, year on year, more than either of them could see the end of, which was the point, which was exactly the point. A child was born somewhere far west and she carried it across the sill, glad. The almond tree that Edrick's hand had set in a spring before the war stirred its new leaves in the chill rising off the slope, fifty years gone and the tree going on, the way nearly everything went on, a hand gone and the thing it made still standing.

It felt, very nearly, like an ending. The war was over and the world was borrowed and held and the people she loved were lit and warm below her and the one who could carry the span was at the far end of the wall, and there is a kind of evening, after the long thing is done, that has the still gold weight of a last page to it, that asks nothing further of you but to stand in it and let it be over.

She stood in it. She let it be very nearly over. And under the gold stillness of it, faint, far off, in no direction she could name, the cold working part of her went on quietly insisting that the world was not, in fact, finished with her yet; and she set the thought aside, the way she set aside the tear in the rim and the eleven and the trouble to the west, and held the door at its width, and watched the stars come out over the long view, and did not look away.
