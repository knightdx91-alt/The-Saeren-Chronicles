## Reader Reports
**The Devourer:** Engaged by the confrontation's stakes once the men arrive; the slow-news front half is where they'd risk skimming. The hook is moral, not "what happens next," so propulsion is intellectual. Would keep going on the strength of the trilogy's momentum.

**The Critic:** This is their chapter. Would underline "a man going on shaving in a house that is burning," "correct and monstrous were not opposites," and the river-district-leaning-wider final image. Only quibble: the political exposition explains itself to the last drop; a critic might want one fact left for the reader to assemble.

**The Hostile:** Challenges — "Is this a chapter or an essay with two characters in it?" The front third is near-pure exposition. Also: Vale's question ("why is once allowed and twice forbidden") is the strongest in the chapter and Viridia explicitly cannot answer it; the hostile reader may read the non-answer as the author dodging. (Defense: the non-answer IS the point — "I have to find it every day instead of building it once." Earned.)

**The Casual Reader:** Likes Viridia, likes Amber, feels the dread. Would buy the trilogy on Book One/Two; would not have bought on this chapter cold (no propulsive event). Vibes: somber, smart, a little heavy. Stays for the character, not the plot engine. 7/10 — above the worry line but not a page-one grab.

**The Devoted Reader (active — literary/series):** Would adopt this hard. This is the chapter that names the trilogy's central moral machine and ties it to Book One's "decided for everyone without asking" and Book Two's rebirth-without-consent. Re-read reward: the "ground remembered the shape" line and the tear leaning "a fraction wider toward the warmth she had become" plant the binding pressure-differential reveal. Reddit-post material.
