## ANAPHORA RULING (the assigned question — 2 beats, held to 2 per the prior caution)
Two anaphora beats were placed, per the Ch.5 caution that Ch.6-7 must NOT stack a third per chapter. The two:
1. **"That was the thing. That was the part the count would not let her not-know"** → into the long single sentence cataloguing Sela (line 25), and its echo "It was so close her sight should have had it... should have read the cloth..." (line 19).
2. **"It would keep coming back. It would keep coming back across more of the year..." → "Not this year. Not in time for Sela. Not... in time for the next one"** (line 113), and the closing **"She would go to the source"** refrain (lines 123, 137 — bookending the resolve).

**Verdict: both read as INTENTIONAL VOICE, not tic, and the count was correctly held to 2.** Reasoning:
1. **They do different work.** Beat 1 is the *count refusing to look away* — the repetition enacts the cold working part forcing the near, faced death onto her (the form is the compulsion). Beat 2 / the "She would go to the source" refrain is *a decision hardening* — the repetition is the sound of a resolve being set, and the bookend placement (it opens the resolve section and closes the chapter) is structural, not cadence-filler.
2. **They are spaced far apart** (the mill scene vs the night wall) and are not the same construction.
3. **They are consistent with the established Book One/Two/Ch.1-5 register** (the "cold working part," the long ramifying sentence collapsing to a bare clause: "She had gone north." / "She stood up." / "Viridia let go of the hand.").
**Caution carried forward:** the device is now a recurring tool across the act (Ch.4, Ch.5, Ch.6). Two per chapter is the ceiling; **Ch.7 must not add a third per-chapter beat**, and the architect should consider a chapter or two in Act Two that withholds anaphora entirely so the tool stays voice and never tips to fingerprint across the book.
