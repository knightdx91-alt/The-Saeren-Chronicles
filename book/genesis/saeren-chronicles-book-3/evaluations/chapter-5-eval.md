## ACT-WATCH RESOLUTION (the assigned question)
Ch.4 restored event-momentum (Casual 7→8) and ended on a counting-problem hook. Ch.5 was tasked to go interior/dialogue-heavy AGAIN — a structural risk flagged in the Ch.4 watch — and to earn that with a revelation strong enough to carry a low-event chapter. **It earns it, and then some.** The chapter has exactly one external event (Vale's departure, a half-page) and is otherwise two long interiors (the wall with Amber; the wall at night), but it delivers the trilogy's central binding gut-punch — *the Horrors are pulled toward the source, the source is her, she is the reason the tears open* — as a slow-detonating discovery rather than an announcement. The revelation is structurally load-bearing for the whole book and it lands here with full force. **Casual holds at 8/10** (does not climb — there is no on-page incident — but does not slip, because the night-wall sequence reads as a thriller-of-the-mind: she is uncovering something, and the uncovering accelerates). The dialogue-heavy structure is justified: this is the chapter where the book's moral engine becomes a personal terror, and that conversion is interior by nature.

**One structural note for the orchestrator (not a chapter defect):** outline.md assigns the "pull points at her / toward the source" crystallization to **Ch.7 (END OF ACT ONE)**, held-not-confirmed at Ch.6's close. Ch.5 has pulled the *full confirmation* forward ("The pull came from her" / "I made this"). This is a strong choice on its own terms — the discovery is more powerful undelayed — but it means **Ch.6 and Ch.7 must be re-briefed**: Ch.7 can no longer be the reveal; it should become the *act of going to the source to measure/confirm exactly how full and how wide* (which Ch.5's final beat already sets up: "She would have to go and stand at the centre of the pull and feel it pulling"). Flag this to the architect so Act One's back third doesn't re-reveal a thing the reader already has. Not a reason to revise Ch.5.

## ANAPHORA RULING (the assigned question)
Two anaphora beats were flagged for judgment: the "no wall / no line / no rule" passage (lines 79-83) and the "She knew / She had known" cadence (lines 158-159, "She knew where she had to go. She had known, really, since the trail turned west").

**Verdict: both read as INTENTIONAL VOICE, not tic.** Reasoning:
1. **They are doing different work.** The "no wall" anaphora is an *argument under construction* — she is stripping the question to its true piece, and the repetition enacts the removing of false comforts one at a time ("The wall was not a thing she had. The wall was a thing she did"). The "She knew / She had known" beat is a *recognition of foreknowledge* — the repetition is the precise meaning (she is admitting she already knew before she let herself know). The form serves the content in both; neither is decorative cadence-filler.
2. **They are spaced far apart** (a wall-scene apart) and are not the same construction, so they don't read as a single repeated mannerism within the reading experience.
3. **They are consistent with the established Book One/Two/Ch.1-4 register** — the "cold working part," "say the true thing at noon," the long ramifying sentence that collapses to a bare clause. This is the series voice, allowlisted and earned.
**Caution for the act, not this chapter:** anaphora is now a recurring tool (Ch.4's "you will do, and do, and do"; Ch.4's "Especially when it costs nothing"). It is still under the tic threshold, but the architect should ensure Ch.6-7 do NOT add a third anaphora beat per chapter, or the device will tip from voice to fingerprint. Track it; do not cut it here.
