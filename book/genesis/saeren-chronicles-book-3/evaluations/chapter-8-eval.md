## Character — Marick (the assigned question): real, sympathetic-and-culpable, NOT a flat villain?
**YES, decisively — this is the chapter's highest achievement.** Marick embodies the trilogy's "control through magic" temptation as a *person*, not a thesis-prop:
- **Sympathetic:** the physical portrait is pitiable, not menacing (narrow chest, balding-and-hiding-it, forgotten spectacles, the stoop of "a whole life bent over books held too close to a lamp"). His wound is specific and earned — "forty years being cleverer than his gift would let him show," a candle in a world that ruled candles. The relief on Fen's-door face that "bad news arriving was at least bad news arriving."
- **Culpable:** he is given the chapter's most damning line and owns it without flinching — "I said it didn't matter... I picked up the chalk." He read a warning he could only half-make-out and overrode it. He is the proximate spark and the chapter never lets him off it.
- **The temptation embodied:** crucially, his reasoning is *correct about the world* — "the men who broke the world were not the men I would take my caution from" — which is Viridia's own reasoning. The chapter's nerve is that it makes the reader agree with the villain. "He was not wrong. That was the terrible thing." That is the "control through magic" temptation rendered as seduction-by-justice, not cackling evil.
- **The final menace is the right kind:** not violence but RECOGNITION — the clever man arriving at the dangerous question ("What are you listening to?"), and the new light at the back of his eyes "worse than the hand-checkers at the ford." He becomes dangerous by being perceptive, which is exactly the Book Two Ch.20 / final-image seed. Marick is a full antagonist with a soul and a point. No flatness.

## Character — Fen (the assigned question): planted light, not telegraphed?
**YES.** Fen is established with restraint appropriate to a seed that pays off in Ch.16 and Ch.22:
- Three small independent gestures give him interiority without spotlighting him as Important: the shame at checking a hand ("the most hopeful thing she had seen in three days"), the animal weighing that "came down, fractionally, on the side of the strangers," and the small involuntary sound when the warning is spoken aloud.
- The narrative tags the planting with a forward-shadow that is suggestive, not explicit: "which she would, much later, on a worse day, be glad past saying that she had seen." This flags Fen as load-bearing to the re-reader WITHOUT naming what he becomes (the one who runs). It is a promise, not a spoiler.
- He is NOT over-described, NOT given a monologue, NOT made conspicuously central. The "girl/not a girl" intro and the ink "from writing left to right" make him memorable in one stroke. Light touch confirmed. The final-image binding ("Fen runs") is set up cleanly here.

## The gut-punch structure (the assigned question): dread escalation, not anticlimax?
**It lands as intended — dread, not anticlimax — and the craft choices that make it work are deliberate:**
1. **The hope is BUILT before it is broken**, so the reversal has something to break. The whole road west is spent letting her (and the reader) want the clean win: "a cause and the cause would have a face and the face would have a hand and the hand could be stopped." The chapter earns the let-down by funding the hope first.
2. **The close is rendered as a real, satisfying competence beat** — she draws the mark, it costs nothing, the worst door in the world becomes a wall — so the reader gets the dopamine of resolution *first*. The anticlimax risk would be if the close were anticlimactic; instead the close is a WIN, and then the win is revealed to be irrelevant.
3. **The non-change is given its own beat, set apart** ("And the pull did not slow." as a standalone line; "the rim leaned in exactly as hard the instant after as the instant before"), and then *measured* ("made the cold working part read it twice, three times") so the reader feels her refusing to believe it. The hydraulics metaphor (symptom vs pressure) makes the non-change comprehensible rather than arbitrary — it reads as a horrifying *logic*, not a cheat.
4. **The escalation is correctly placed AFTER the resolution** — Marick's lit question and the unspoken "the tear is me" turn the floor-drop into forward dread (the real cause is unfixable by the only tool she has). This is the opposite of anticlimax: the chapter spends its climax-energy on the realization, not the action. Dread escalation confirmed.
