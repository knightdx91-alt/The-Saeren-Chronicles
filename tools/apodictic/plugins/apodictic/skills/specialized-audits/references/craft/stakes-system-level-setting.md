# Stakes System Audit: Level-Setting Research Brief
**Three-Model Synthesis (Opus46 + Codex53 + Gemini) | APODICTIC Development Editor v4.15**

---

## PURPOSE

The Stakes System Audit diagnoses a specific failure mode: the manuscript signals risk but does not structurally convert that risk into escalating consequence and reader pressure over time. This differs from Decision Pressure Audit (which tests choice plausibility under stakes), Rhythm Audit (which tracks pacing mechanics), and Character Architecture Audit (which tests agency coherence).

The stakes audit targets the temporal architecture of consequence—how risk manifests, compounds, and forces commitment pressure across the narrative trajectory. Failure here produces manuscripts where signals of danger persist without delivering escalating cost; where stakes language grows but stakes load remains static; where climaxes are labeled high-stakes without structural backup.

The structural isomorphism across companion audits: each diagnoses when a system *signals* its operational presence without *manifesting* its load. Force architecture signals motion but fails to compound momentum. Decision pressure signals choice plausibility but fails to compound commitment cost. Stakes architecture signals risk but fails to compound consequence.

**Anchor line: Escalation without payment is suspense vocabulary without suspense architecture.**

---

## QUESTION 1: THEORETICAL GROUNDING

### What Makes Stakes Work as Narrative Architecture?

Stakes function through six interlocking mechanisms:

**1. Narrative Desire and Drive**

Stakes are the structure that makes reader desire legible and motivating. Desire without stakes is curiosity; stakes without desire is abstract threat. Stakes bridge desire to cost: the reader wants the outcome, and stakes make that outcome costly. Narrative desire requires two properties: (a) the outcome must be specific enough to track, and (b) the cost of failing to achieve it must be specific enough to feel.

The diagnostic problem: manuscripts often mistake hope for stakes. Hope is "wouldn't it be nice if character achieved X." Stakes is "character will suffer specified consequence if character fails to achieve X." Hope generates curiosity. Stakes generates pressure.

**2. Risk Perception and Appraisal Under Uncertainty**

Stakes operate within cognitive limits of uncertainty management. Readers cannot feel pressure about threats they cannot model. The brain requires three properties to appraise risk:

- *Comprehensible threat type* (concrete enough to simulate mentally)
- *Plausible probability distribution* (reader can assess likelihood without precise numbers)
- *Specifiable consequence domain* (what failure looks like, in what dimension)

Threats that are abstract, invisible, or mechanically vague do not register as stakes. They register as thematic weight. Thematic weight can enhance stakes; it cannot replace them.

Risk appraisal follows a two-factor model adapted from cognitive psychology: *perceived severity* (how bad is the outcome?) and *perceived vulnerability* (how likely is it to happen to this character?). Both must be active for stakes to register as pressure. A threat that is severe but implausible (the sun will explode) or plausible but trivial (character might stub their toe) fails to create narrative stakes. Manuscripts that maximize severity language without establishing vulnerability produce the sensation of rhetorical alarm without structural pressure.

The diagnostic problem: manuscripts often use abstract threat language ("everything is at stake," "the world might end") without modeling how readers should mentally simulate the threat. Consequence must be specific to be felt as pressure.

**3. Walk-Away Vulnerability**

A critical test for personal coupling: can the protagonist plausibly walk away from the stakes? If yes, stakes depend entirely on external motivation (plot needs character there). If no, the character's specific position—their values, commitments, relationships, entanglements—makes departure impossible or prohibitively costly. This is the coupling mechanism that converts external threat into personal pressure.

Walk-away vulnerability operates as a gradient, not a binary. Early narrative: character could theoretically walk away but has reasons not to. Mid-narrative: walking away would cost something specific. Late narrative: walking away is impossible without abandoning something the character cannot abandon. Escalation is partly measured by the escalating impossibility of exit.

The diagnostic problem: manuscripts often create characters who stay in danger because the plot requires it. The walk-away test reveals whether the character's continued engagement is architecturally motivated or plot-mandated.

**4. Motivation and Commitment Pressure**

Stakes create commitment pressure by making continued character action appear rational under uncertainty. If stakes are low, character choices appear over-committed. If stakes are invisible until climax, character choices appear arbitrary. Escalating stakes should make readers understand why character continues investing in the pursuit when costs mount.

Commitment pressure operates on a three-point spectrum:

- *Insufficient stakes*: reader believes character should abandon the pursuit
- *Sufficient stakes*: reader accepts character's continued investment
- *Escalating stakes*: reader feels pressure mounting, sees character's position tightening, anticipates forced choices

The diagnostic problem: manuscripts often hold stakes flat (sufficient but non-escalating), creating a reading experience where the character could plausibly exit the narrative but doesn't. This produces the sensation of "nothing's really at stake," even when stakes exist.

**5. Consequence Design in Storytelling Craft**

Consequence has structural properties: *type, timing, irreversibility, and manifestation*.

Type distinguishes: personal (character's own safety, relationships, identity); relational (character's responsibility for others); external (world-state consequences); moral (ethical self-concept, complicity); existential (stakes to identity, meaning, possibility). Most strong narratives layer multiple consequence types.

Timing distinguishes: immediate (next scene), proximate (next act), delayed (climax or beyond). Immediate consequences create urgency. Delayed consequences create dread. Untimed consequences (hanging threats without manifestation window) create abstract weight that may or may not register as pressure.

Irreversibility distinguishes: reversible (character can recover, restart, undo); quasi-reversible (character can recover but at cost); irreversible (failure closes possibilities permanently). Irreversible consequences increase pressure exponentially.

Manifestation distinguishes: abstract (consequence is thematic or intellectual), concrete (consequence is physical or material), psychological (consequence is internal to character). Readers feel abstract consequences least; they feel concrete and psychological consequences more acutely.

The diagnostic problem: manuscripts often confuse consequence *statement* with consequence *manifestation*. A character can declare "I'll lose everything" and readers still not feel that consequence because they haven't seen it take cost. The gap between declared stakes and manifested cost is a specific, diagnosable failure.

**6. Scene-Level vs. Act-Level Escalation Models**

Stakes escalate through two complementary systems:

*Scene-level escalation* increases the stakes of the immediate conflict: character risks more in this scene than the previous one. Does the character face higher stakes in the confrontation? This is the tightest, most immediate escalation metric.

*Act-level escalation* increases the overall narrative pressure: the character's options narrow, the threat grows, previous choices close off retreats. This is the broader structural escalation metric.

Strong stakes narratives do both. Scene-level escalation without act-level escalation produces the sensation of spinning wheels (each scene is tense but the overall situation doesn't compound). Act-level escalation without scene-level escalation produces the sensation of slow accumulation without immediate pressure.

The diagnostic problem: manuscripts often construct one without the other, creating either constant urgency that feels repetitive, or slow mounting pressure that feels static scene-to-scene.

### Diagnostic Takeaway

A testable property that distinguishes real stakes escalation from rhetorical "high stakes" claims: **Can the reader specify what the character concretely loses at each major story beat, in order, with increasing cost?**

If the answer is no—if stakes remain abstract, or multiply without compounding, or delay manifestation until climax—then stakes signals are rhetorical rather than structural. The manuscript is broadcasting high-stakes tone without building high-stakes architecture.

Secondary test from risk appraisal framework: **Does the manuscript establish both severity and vulnerability for its primary threat, or does it maximize severity language while leaving vulnerability unmodeled?**

## QUESTION 2: FAILURE TAXONOMY

### Where Stakes Collapse

Stakes fail through specific structural patterns. The following catalog distinguishes individual failure modes from their underlying mechanisms:

**1. Abstract Signaling Without Manifestation** — Character or narrator repeatedly tells reader stakes are high, but the manuscript never shows stakes taking cost. Consequence is stated as philosophy, not revealed as friction. Reader encounters language like "everything was at stake" attached to scenes where nothing actually costs the character. The sensation: told stakes, not felt stakes.

**2. The Plateau** — Stakes escalate in early-to-middle narrative, then hold flat through final act. Character moves toward climax without escalating cost. This is the most common failure: manuscripts build to a summit and then level off. Reader feels maximum pressure at midpoint, not at climax.

**3. Regression Under Pressure** — Stakes escalate to a peak, then reader witnesses character finding workarounds, luck, or resource infusions that materially lower stakes. A character is cornered, then unexpectedly receives aid. Stakes language stays high; actual pressure lowers. This breaks reader trust in stakes architecture.

**4. Single-Axis Dependency** — Stakes depend entirely on one threat type. When that single axis is threatened, stakes feel real. When narrative focuses elsewhere, stakes feel suspended. Reader cannot track compound pressure when stakes remain monolithic.

**5. The Escalation Mirage** — Language of stakes grows more intense, but actual consequence profile stays constant. "The stakes have never been higher" appears at climax, but the character's actual position and options are structurally identical to act two. Intensity without escalation.

**6. Peak Prematurity** — Stakes reach their highest structural load before the climax. The most intense consequences, the narrowest options, the most irreversible pressure all arrive at midpoint or late-middle narrative. Everything after is denouement dressed as climax. Reader feels the story peaked early regardless of how much action follows.

**7. Visibility Collapse** — Threat is specified early, reader understands what's at stake, then the manuscript stops manifesting the threat. The antagonist disappears. The time limit is introduced but never referenced. The consequence is not shown deteriorating. Stakes become invisible.

**8. Manifestation Delay Until Climax** — Reader doesn't understand what character stands to lose until the climactic scene. Stakes are present structurally but invisible to reader until final act. This produces the sensation of arbitrary stakes insertion rather than escalating pressure.

**9. Consequence Cascade Without Escalation Geometry** — Multiple consequences hit at once in climactic sequence, but they don't compound. Three problems surface simultaneously, but reader doesn't feel they're causally linked or that earlier choices locked character into this position. Feels like complication, not consequence.

**10. The Shock Carousel** — Stakes manifest as serial revelation rather than escalation. Character learns "oh no, there's also THIS threat" repeatedly across final act. Each revelation is treated as equivalent surprise. Feels like threat multiplication rather than consequence deepening.

**11. The Substitution Flaw** — Protagonist is interchangeable with any other character. The specific character's values, history, relationships, and vulnerabilities do not determine the stakes. Any protagonist would face identical pressures identically. Stakes are plot-driven, not character-driven.

**12. False Choice Under Stakes** — Character appears to face high-stakes choice, but actually one option is clearly preferable or narratively foreclosed. Reader doesn't feel the choice pressure because the architecture is illusory.

**13. Climax Stakes Mismatch** — The stakes declared or manifested in climax are structurally smaller than the stakes reader felt in middle narrative. Reader expected escalation; climax delivers deflation.

**14. Consequence Without Cost** — Character faces a stated consequence, but the manifestation has minimal impact. Announced loss is revealed to be minor. Promised pain is brief or easily absorbed.

**15. Escalation in Language But Not Mechanism** — Scene descriptions, character inner monologue, and narrator language grow more intense and urgent, but the actual plot mechanics stay constant.

**16. The Cyclical Bind** — Character faces the same fundamental stakes across multiple acts without meaningful escalation. The stakes have returned, not escalated.

**17. Options Multiplication Instead of Narrowing** — Escalating stakes should narrow character options. Instead, manuscript introduces new possible solutions, allies, or escape routes as stakes rise. Reader doesn't feel trapped.

**18. Postponed Irreversibility** — Consequences are all quasi-reversible through most of narrative, then suddenly irrevocable in climax. Reader hasn't internalized irreversibility, so final irreversible stakes feel tonally inconsistent.

**19. The Authority Vacuum** — Threat is specified but threat-credibility is unclear. Manuscript fails to establish the antagonist or system can actually deliver the threatened consequence.

**20. Consequence Isolation** — Character faces personal stakes but narrative has decoupled character from all relational/external stakes. Character's failure has no ripple consequences. Stakes feel contained and smaller.

**21. The Sufficiency Trap** — Stakes are sufficient to motivate character action but insufficient to create reader pressure. Character is clearly motivated. Reader doesn't feel why the stakes matter to them.

**22. Escalation Announcement Without Escalation Evidence** — Character or narrator announces escalation without the narrative architecture confirming it. Reader hears the claim, doesn't see the evidence.

**23. Cost Amnesia** — Character experiences consequences in early narrative, reader witnesses cost, but later narrative ignores the cost and treats character as unaffected. Consequence is paid in one scene and forgotten by the next major unit.

**24. Consequence Reversibility Inconsistency** — Early consequences are permanent, later consequences are reversible. Reader loses coherent model of what consequences cost.

**25. The Walk-Away Problem** — Character could plausibly exit the stakes situation at any point. No architectural mechanism binds them. Reader wonders why the character is still engaged, and the only answer is "because the plot requires it."

### Structural-Isomorphism Anchor

**Escalation without payment is suspense vocabulary without suspense architecture.**

## QUESTION 3: POSITIVE CASES

### What Integrated Stakes Architecture Looks Like

Strong stakes architecture appears across genres through specific, extractable techniques:

**THRILLER/SUSPENSE**

*Technique: Layered Consequence Timeline* — Character faces immediate threat (this scene), proximate threat (next act), and deferred threat (climax). These are distinct consequence types on distinct timelines. Escalation is built by tightening the proximate threat while the immediate threat still presses. Example: Character must avoid detection today, must solve mystery before antagonist acts, must preserve personal relationships through the deception. Each layer is visible and trackable. Escalation is felt as the character's capacity to manage all three timelines simultaneously shrinks.

*Technique: Irreversibility Ratchet* — Early-act stakes are quasi-reversible. Mid-act stakes introduce irreversibility. Final act stakes are explicitly irreversible. Escalation is felt as the character's error margin shrinks because consequences are no longer recoverable.

*Technique: Tightening Immediacy* — Early scenes: antagonist threat is temporally distant. Mid-scenes: timeline compresses. Late scenes: immediacy approaches real-time. Reader feels escalation as the window to prevent disaster shrinks from abstract to concrete time pressure.

**MYSTERY/INVESTIGATION**

*Technique: Consequence Domain Expansion* — Character begins investigating with narrow consequence scope. As investigation progresses, consequences expand: solving the puzzle reveals broader implications. Each expansion makes stakes larger because they ripple into more consequence domains.

*Technique: Investigation-Consequence Binding* — Each investigative revelation tightens character's position. Finding clue A means character is now implicated. Finding clue B means character must maintain deception. Finding clue C means character must choose between revealing truth and protecting someone. The investigation itself manufactures consequence.

*Technique: Delayed-Revelation Consequence Architecture* — Reader understands stakes exist but doesn't know their full shape until final acts. When truth emerges, reader experiences both the investigation resolution and character's stakes resolution simultaneously. Requires tight control: reader must feel stakes are present even while hidden.

**ROMANCE**

*Technique: Personal Coupling + Relational Consequence* — Character's emotional stakes are bound to relational stakes. Escalation occurs when the two converge: character cannot have the relationship without vulnerability, cannot have vulnerability without risking the relationship. The bind tightens across narrative.

*Technique: Irreversible Authenticity* — Early romance: character can maintain deception about feelings. Mid-romance: deception becomes costlier. Late romance: deception is explicitly irreversible. Escalation is felt as authenticity becomes impossible without consequence.

*Technique: Relational Consequence Ratchet* — Stakes compound because character's choices ripple into others' stories. Each emotional beat has relational consequence that escalates.

**HORROR**

*Technique: Escalation Through Embodiment* — Threat begins external, escalates to proximate, escalates to internal. Escalation is felt as threat moves from outsideable to inescapable.

*Technique: Cost Manifestation in Accumulated Damage* — Horror sustains stakes by showing consequence accumulating. Character is scratched, then bleeding, then infected, then transforming. Escalation is felt through manifestation.

*Technique: Violation of Boundary System* — Horror stakes escalate by progressively violating character's understood boundaries of safety. Physical space, body, mind, identity. Escalation is felt as violation deepens through increasingly intimate boundaries.

**LITERARY/UPMARKET**

*Technique: Identity Stakes as Irreversibility* — Consequence is cast as threat to character's identity or coherence. Character begins with a self-model; escalating stakes force revision. The irreversibility is identity-based: character cannot unknow what they've learned about themselves.

*Technique: Relational Consequence as Central Pressure* — Stakes are built through what relationships reveal. Character loses relationship not through antagonistic action but through accumulated understanding. The bind tightens through intimate revelation.

*Technique: Thematic Stakes Binding* — Stakes operate through thematic coherence. Character acts in accordance with one value system early; escalation forces conflict between that system and something else. Resolution requires irreversible choice.

**SFF/EPIC/ADVENTURE**

*Technique: World-State + Personal Stakes Binding* — Character's personal goal is bound to world-state consequence. Neither stake alone is sufficient; escalation comes from their binding. Character cannot pursue personal goal without affecting world.

*Technique: Escalation Through Resource Depletion* — Character begins with resources. Escalation is built by systematically depleting them. As character approaches climax, they face the same threats with reduced capacity.

*Technique: Magic/Technology Cost as Stakes Structure* — If magic or technology carries cost, that cost escalates stakes. Using power increases cost. Final act, power is most potent but most costly.

**COZY/LOW-VIOLENCE**

*Technique: Interpersonal Stakes as Primary* — Stakes center on position within community. External threat is secondary to internal stakes. Escalation is felt as character's community position deteriorates.

*Technique: Low-Consequence Stakes Architecture* — Consequence is not death but irrevocable social or relational loss. Escalation through accumulating interpersonal damage.

**SATIRE/COMEDY**

*Technique: Escalation Through Exposure Risk* — Character maintains facade; escalation is built as facade cracks. Climax is reached when exposure becomes inevitable.

*Technique: Stakes-Through-Incompetence* — Escalation is felt not as stakes grow but as character's awareness of their inadequacy in the face of stakes grows more visible.

### Cross-Genre Success Signals

Across all modes, integrated stakes architecture shows these properties:

1. Reader can specify consequence at each major beat.
2. Consequence types layer rather than substitute.
3. Character's position visibly tightens.
4. Cost is manifested, not merely stated.
5. Climax carries the escalation trajectory's culmination.

## QUESTION 4: MODE CALIBRATION

### How Stakes Burden Shifts by Genre/Mode

**THRILLER/SUSPENSE**

*Integration Promise*: Escalating external threat forces internal commitment. Reader feels mounting pressure as character's options narrow and cost of failure rises.

*Characteristic Blind Spot — The Treadmill Effect*: Suspense that sustains high-stakes tone without escalation geometry. Each scene maintains maximum urgency, but the overall situation never compounds. Character is always just barely escaping. Reader begins to feel urgency is artificial.

*False-Positive Risk*: Velocity of plot can feel like escalation. A fast-paced narrative where nothing is permanently lost and character maintains options is low-stakes, regardless of how urgent the prose feels.

**MYSTERY/INVESTIGATION**

*Integration Promise*: Each investigative revelation carries consequence. Discovery itself manufactures stakes.

*Characteristic Blind Spot — The Puzzle Detachment*: Investigation drives momentum while personal stakes remain abstract. Solving becomes goal, consequence becomes secondary.

*False-Positive Risk*: Complexity can feel like escalation. Multiple threads and layers of revelation read as high-stakes when stakes are actually diluted across consequence domains.

**ROMANCE**

*Integration Promise*: Character's emotional vulnerability is the central stake. Escalation is felt as character cannot avoid the intimacy they fear.

*Characteristic Blind Spot — The Emotional Plateau*: Emotional tone sustained at intensity without escalating emotional demand. Character faces the same core fear throughout.

*False-Positive Risk*: Frequency of emotional beats can feel like escalation. Many scenes of vulnerability read as high-stakes when emotional demands aren't actually escalating.

**HORROR**

*Integration Promise*: Threat escalates through increasingly intimate violation. Escalation through manifestation of damage.

*Characteristic Blind Spot — The Scare Carousel*: Rotating threat types without binding them to escalating architecture. Shock multiplies without stakes deepening.

*False-Positive Risk*: Intensity of scares can feel like escalation. Constant shocks read as high-stakes when threat remains static.

**LITERARY/UPMARKET**

*Integration Promise*: Stakes operate through theme and identity. Escalation as character recognizes contradictions between commitments.

*Characteristic Blind Spot — The Thematic Suspended Animation*: Theme is clear but character's arc doesn't escalate the stakes. Contradiction explored but character's forced position doesn't tighten.

*False-Positive Risk*: Complexity of thematic exploration can feel like escalation. Depth of exploration and depth of stakes are not the same.

**SFF/EPIC/ADVENTURE**

*Integration Promise*: Layered personal + systemic stakes. World-state + character survival bound together.

*Characteristic Blind Spot — Scope Inflation Without Consequence*: Threat grows in scope without escalating character's personal consequence. Character's role remains constant despite enlarged threat.

*False-Positive Risk*: Scale of threat can feel like escalation. World-ending stakes where character has time, resources, and allies are lower-stakes than personal threat where character is isolated.

**COZY/LOW-VIOLENCE**

*Integration Promise*: Bounded threat with high belonging/repair stakes. Community standing as primary consequence.

*Characteristic Blind Spot — The Consequence Isolation*: Mystery solved but stakes never materialize. Character maintains standing throughout.

*False-Positive Risk*: Intimacy of community relationships can feel like high stakes when actual tolerance is too high for consequences to land.

**SATIRE/COMEDY**

*Integration Promise*: Stakes preserved through tonal modulation. Exposure risk escalation.

*Characteristic Blind Spot — The Exposure Without Consequence*: Facade increasingly obvious but no relational consequence. Character is exposed but no one cares.

*False-Positive Risk*: Comedy can function as stakes obfuscation. Escalating absurdity reads as escalating stakes when it's performing the opposite.

## QUESTION 5: THE DISTINGUISH PROBLEM

### Intentional Smallness vs. Stakes Failure

The audit must distinguish five categories of cases that can appear similar on surface reading:

**1. INTENTIONALLY MODEST STAKES vs. UNDERPOWERED STAKES**

Intentionally modest stakes are architecturally sound but operate on smaller consequence scale. The manuscript executes escalation on its chosen scale: character's position tightens, options narrow, cost accumulates. Consequence is small but structurally real.

Underpowered stakes is when the manuscript fails to escalate on any scale. Character faces a problem, the problem persists with static intensity, the problem is resolved.

*Distinguish test*: Does the character's position at climax reflect having navigated escalating pressure, or does it reflect having encountered stable pressure they happened to overcome?

**2. CYCLICAL STAKES DESIGN vs. ACCIDENTAL PLATEAU**

Cyclical design is intentional: character faces recurring stakes and each cycle adds depth to the same central contradiction. Escalation is in understanding, not in consequence load.

Accidental plateau is when the manuscript loses escalation and circles around variations of the same stakes without new understanding.

*Distinguish test*: Does the recurrence provide new information about stakes, or does it just remind reader of the stakes?

**3. DELAYED-STAKES REVELATION vs. VAGUE SETUP**

Delayed revelation: reader doesn't understand character's full stakes until late narrative, but stakes are present and buildable from textual evidence.

Vague setup: stakes are never clearly established. The revelation at climax feels inserted rather than emergent.

*Distinguish test*: Can a careful reader construct the shape of delayed stakes from preceding evidence, or does the climactic revelation feel narratively arbitrary?

**4. THEMATIC/IDENTITY STAKES vs. UNSUPPORTED ABSTRACTION**

Thematic stakes are grounded in character's demonstrated values and choices. Stakes are abstract but grounded in specific character commitments reader has witnessed.

Unsupported abstraction: narrative asserts thematic stakes without having established character's clear values or commitments.

*Distinguish test*: Can the reader specify character's core values based on acts one and two?

**5. ANTI-CLIMAX AS DELIBERATE FORM vs. PAYOFF COLLAPSE**

Anti-climax as deliberate form: low stakes at climax are intentional and serve the narrative's operating question.

Payoff collapse: climax's low stakes are accidental. Manuscript built toward high stakes, then climax fails to deliver.

*Distinguish test*: Does the climax answer the narrative's central question faithfully, even if it doesn't escalate stakes?

### Classification Decision Tree

1. Does the manuscript establish a clear stakes type by end of act one?
   - No → Likely vague setup. Check if delayed revelation is supported by textual evidence.
   - Yes → Proceed to 2.

2. Does character's position tighten across act two?
   - No → Likely modest stakes or intentional plateau. Check if plateau is thematic (cyclical design) or structural (missed escalation).
   - Yes → Proceed to 3.

3. Does act three escalate stakes further?
   - No → Check if anti-climax is deliberate. Is the low-stakes climax answering the narrative's central question? If yes, deliberate form. If no, payoff collapse.
   - Yes → Proceed to 4.

4. Are the climax stakes manifested in character consequence?
   - No → Check if stakes are thematic/identity based. If yes, may be structurally sound identity stakes. If no, likely underpowered stakes.
   - Yes → Stakes architecture is sound.

### Three-Way Classification Outcomes

| Test Profile | Classification | Editorial Action |
|---|---|---|
| All tests pass; low-stakes manuscripts show intentional scale calibration | **Intentional and Successful** | Preserve architecture; note technique; validate against contract |
| Intentional strategy visible; 1-2 channels unstable | **Intentional but Unstable** | Targeted revision on unstable channels |
| Multiple failures; no stable pressure logic | **Accidental Failure** | Structural stakes recast required |

## DESIGN CONCLUSIONS FOR THE AUDIT DRAFT

The Stakes System Audit should be structured around six diagnostic channels that map to stakes architecture:

**STX (Stakes Type Breadth)**: Catalog what types of stakes the manuscript establishes. Single-type stakes are more fragile than multi-type. Strong architecture layers multiple consequence types.

**PC (Personal Coupling)**: Measure how stakes are bound to character's demonstrated values, desires, and fears. Abstract stakes without personal coupling feel rhetorical. Includes walk-away vulnerability as a gradient test.

**IM (Immediacy Gradient)**: Measure temporal escalation. Do stakes move from distant threat to immediate threat? Do time windows compress?

**EG (Escalation Geometry)**: Measure whether character's position actually tightens. Do options narrow? Do costs compound? Does consequence accumulate? Includes peak-prematurity detection.

**MP (Manifestation and Payment)**: Measure whether stakes materialize as consequence, not just assertion.

**CL (Climax Load Integrity)**: Measure whether climax stakes are proportionate to the narrative's escalation trajectory.

The audit should produce diagnostics on each channel, identify which channels are failing, catalog the specific failure modes, and specify revision scope. It must also resolve the distinguish problem: is stakes failure present, or is the manuscript executing intentionally modest stakes, deliberate cyclical design, or anti-climax as form?
