## Purpose

This document level-sets the named-pattern subtypes and source-triage moves introduced in `ai-prose-calibration.md` v2.0. It provides earned and unearned illustrations for each named pattern (fiction and nonfiction) plus the diagnostic question that distinguishes them.

These examples are *generic illustrations*, not examples drawn from any specific manuscript. The writer's own prose remains the only material the audit operates on. Examples here exist to calibrate the auditor's eye, not to substitute into a manuscript.

The diagnostic moves throughout are firewall-compliant: each example shows the pattern in action, names what makes it earned or unearned, and gives the cut rule. None invents replacement prose for an actual manuscript.

## Fiction Named Patterns

Five patterns within AIC-2 (Velvet Fog) and AIC-7 (Discourse Leak) that appear at higher resolution than the parent flag. Each can be earned or unearned; source triage decides.

### 1. Indefinite-Pronoun Gesture (AIC-2)

**Forms.** "Something" + abstract qualifier. "Some X part" + adjective. "A kind of Y" without specifying Y.

**What it does (unearned).** Outsources specificity to the reader's imagination. The prose, not the character, is failing to commit.

**What it does (earned, rare).** The character cannot name what they are feeling, and the prose registers that incapacity as part of the experience. The indefinite is diagnostic of the character's specific incapacity.

**Earned example (illustrative).**

> Something was wrong with her hand. Not pain, not numbness, not pins and needles. Something else, something that had no name in the languages she knew.

The character is searching for the word and failing. The indefinite is diagnostic of her experience. The negations narrow the failure-to-name precisely.

**Unearned example (illustrative).**

> She felt something soft and uncertain rise in her chest, some kind of feeling she couldn't quite place.

The prose, not the character, is failing to commit. "Soft and uncertain," "some kind of feeling," "couldn't quite place" all serve the same evasion. Replace with concrete imagery: name the body part, the action, the registered specific.

**Cut rule.** Replace indefinites with concrete imagery wherever possible. Keep only when the character's specific incapacity to name is the point, and even then preferably name the failure ("she had no word for it") rather than gesture at it.

### 2. Negation Hedge (AIC-7 Hedge Drift)

**Forms.** "Not X." / "Not X, exactly." / "Not X. Not Y."

**What it does (unearned).** Narrator pretends to make a careful discrimination. The next sentence does the work the negation pretended to.

**What it does (earned).** Character is actively sorting, cataloguing, or refusing the wrong word. The negation IS the cognitive act.

**Earned example (illustrative).**

> She listened. Not for footsteps; he wouldn't come. Not for the dog either; the dog had stopped barking hours ago. For the click of the furnace, the only thing that ever told her she was alone in the house in the way she had to be alone.

The character is doing structured listening. Each negation discriminates a real possibility she's tracking. The list of what-it-isn't builds the cognitive shape of waiting.

**Unearned example (illustrative).**

> The room was quiet. Not silent, exactly. Not peaceful either. Just empty in the way old rooms get when no one's been in them for a while.

Three negation moves to land on "empty in the way old rooms get when no one's been in them for a while," which says everything the prior two sentences gestured at. Narrator-pose negation. Cut the first two sentences.

**Cut rule.** Cut when the next sentence does the work the negation pretended to. Keep when the character's cognition IS the discrimination.

### 3. Disguised Correctio (AIC-7 Template Loop)

**Forms.** "Not X, but Y" embedded in narration. "Did not X but Y." Mid-sentence reversal that pretends the narrator made a discrimination but actually just postponed the commitment.

**What it does (unearned).** Same as Negation Hedge but harder to spot because it lives inside a sentence. Almost always cuts cleanly.

**What it does (earned, rare).** The contrast does work; both halves carry weight; the contrast IS the point of the sentence.

**Earned example (illustrative).**

> She didn't love him, but she was used to the shape his absence left.

The contrast does work. Both halves carry weight. The contrast is the point.

**Unearned example (illustrative).**

> He didn't walk into the room so much as drift, his footsteps barely audible on the wood.

"Didn't walk into the room so much as" is doing nothing "drift" doesn't already do. The reversal is decorative. Cut to: "He drifted into the room, his footsteps barely audible on the wood."

**Cut rule.** Almost always cut the negation. Replace with the affirmative form. If the contrast does real work (test by reading without it; if the affirmative loses meaning, keep the contrast), keep.

### 4. Pseudo-Aphorism (AIC-7 Assistant Frame)

**Forms.** "X as Y." "X is the Y of Z." "There is a kind of X in every Y."

**What it does (unearned).** Aspires to maxim register without earning the standing of a maxim. Often has a real image right after that does the actual work.

**What it does (earned, rare).** The aphorism lands as a maxim. Carries weight on its own. Does not require an image to follow because the aphorism IS the image.

**Earned example (illustrative).**

> Grief is the rent you pay for love.

Lands as a maxim. Carries weight on its own.

**Unearned example (illustrative).**

> Memory is the architecture of the self. She walked through the kitchen and the smell of coffee brought her grandmother back, the kitchen in 1987, the radio always on.

"Memory is the architecture of the self" gestures at maxim register. The next sentence does all the work. Cut the aphorism.

**Cut rule.** Cut the aphorism, keep the image. Aphorisms earn their place when they carry weight alone, not when they preface the work.

### 5. Manifesto Cadence (AIC-7 Template Loop)

**Forms.** Three or four parallel sentences building to conclusion. Anaphora, syntactic parallelism, escalating clauses.

**What it does (unearned).** Parallel structure substitutes for actual development. Each sentence does the same work in a different register.

**What it does (earned).** Each sentence escalates, restricts, or reveals. The parallelism IS the development.

**Earned example (illustrative).**

> She had not raised her voice. She had not raised her hand. She had not, in the end, even raised her eyes when he left.

Each sentence pulls back further. The parallelism dramatizes increasing restraint. The structure IS the meaning.

**Unearned example (illustrative).**

> We must commit to youth. We must invest in our communities. We must build a better future for those who will come after us.

Three sentences saying the same thing in three near-synonymous frames. Manifesto cadence as substitute for argument. Cut to one specific commitment, or rebuild with each sentence escalating.

**Cut rule.** Earned when each sentence escalates, restricts, or reveals. Unearned when each sentence is a register variant of the same content.

## Nonfiction Named Patterns

Five argument-shaped patterns within the parallel set introduced in `ai-prose-calibration.md`. Each shown with earned and unearned examples drawn from generic policy domains.

### 1. Abstraction Shielding (AIC-2 analog)

**Earned example (illustrative).**

> The pattern recurs across child welfare systems: assessments that classify rather than diagnose, plans that schedule rather than treat.

"Child welfare systems" is doing analytical work. The pattern is what binds the systems. Replacing with three specific agency names would obscure the structural point.

**Unearned example (illustrative).**

> Stakeholders agree that more must be done to support those impacted by the policy.

"Stakeholders," "more," "support," "those impacted" are all gestures. The sentence commits to nothing. Cut entirely or rewrite with names.

**Cut rule.** Replace with specific actors when the analysis depends on knowing who. Keep when the abstraction is the point of the analysis. Diagnostic question: would naming three specific actors make the sentence sharper, or would it obscure a structural critique?

### 2. False-Balance Construction (AIC-7 Commitment Evasion analog)

**Earned example (illustrative).**

> Reasonable people disagree about whether the right age threshold for intervention is 16, 18, or 21; the developmental evidence supports the higher thresholds, but the jurisdictional arguments cut both ways.

The disagreement is real. Each position has standing. The writer's commitment (the developmental evidence) is named. The hedge does not flatten asymmetry.

**Unearned example (illustrative).**

> While reasonable people may disagree, this intervention has both benefits and costs that should be carefully weighed.

Manufactures balance where evidentiary asymmetry exists. Names no actual benefits, no actual costs, no actual disagreement.

**Cut rule.** Replace with specific named disagreement when the disagreement is real. Cut when the construction is fabricating balance.

### 3. Hedge-and-Affirm (AIC-7 Hedge Drift analog)

**Earned example (illustrative).**

> While most participants in the program will not be re-incarcerated in the two years post-release, the conditions under which they were originally sentenced predict the conditions of their release more strongly than program participation does.

The hedge is true and load-bearing. The affirm names a specific finding. The structure dramatizes the analytical move.

**Unearned example (illustrative).**

> While the intervention may have certain benefits in some cases, it is also important to consider the potential negative impacts on participants and families.

Both halves are gestures. "Certain benefits in some cases" specifies nothing. "Potential negative impacts" specifies nothing.

**Cut rule.** Earned when both halves do specific work. Unearned when both halves gesture.

### 4. Recommendation Template (AIC-7 Template Loop analog)

**Earned example (illustrative).**

> The Council should reject the FY27 budget request for expanded surveillance capacity and redirect those funds to community-based alternatives the relevant agency has under-funded for the past three fiscal years.

Specific actor (Council). Specific action (reject the request, redirect funds). Specific scope (FY27, surveillance expansion). Verifiable.

**Unearned example (illustrative).**

> The jurisdiction must commit to investing in evidence-based interventions that support positive outcomes and community safety.

Five gestures in one sentence. No actor identified beyond "the jurisdiction." No action specified. No timeline. No verification possible.

**Cut rule.** Recommendations earn their place when actor, action, scope, and verifiability are present. Generic recommendations are template-driven and almost always unearned.

### 5. Authority Laundering (AIC-7 Assistant Frame analog)

**Earned example (illustrative).**

> Scott and Steinberg's developmental-stage analysis (2008) demonstrated that adolescent risk-taking reflects ongoing neurological maturation rather than character deficit; subsequent fMRI work (Casey et al. 2011) has localized the relevant prefrontal-limbic dynamics.

Names authors, dates, claims. The reader can verify. The writer commits to the framing.

**Unearned example (illustrative).**

> Research has consistently shown that early intervention benefits at-risk populations, and experts agree that punitive approaches are counterproductive.

"Research," "consistently," "early intervention," "at-risk populations," "experts," "punitive approaches" are all gestures. The sentence borrows authority without earning it.

**Cut rule.** Authority claims earn their place when they name the authority and its specific claim. Generic appeals to research or expertise are usually authority laundering.

## The Payoff Test

The most actionable diagnostic in the framework. AI-fluent prose often takes the shape:

```
AI-fluent setup → character-voice payoff
```

The setup hedges, qualifies, or aphorizes. The payoff commits, specifies, lands. The setup is doing nothing the payoff isn't doing better.

**Diagnostic question.** Does the next sentence do the work the prior sentence pretended to? If yes, cut the prior. The payoff sentence usually has the specific image, the committed verb, the registered emotion. The setup sentence usually has a hedge, a negation, an indefinite, or an aphorism.

**Operational test.** Read the passage without the proposed-cut sentence. Does the prose still work? If yes, the setup was decorative. If no, the setup was load-bearing rhythm or register-establishing work; both stay.

**When the test fails.** Sometimes the setup is doing real work: building rhythm, establishing register, doing the cognitive labor of a character actively sorting. Then both stay. The diagnostic is contextual; pattern alone does not entitle the cut.

## Voice Slip vs. Lost Callback

When a flagged sentence resists the voice-slip rewrite — the writer says "no, the character would say this; something else is wrong" — the failure mode may be lost callback rather than voice slip. The fixes differ.

### Voice Slip

The line is in nobody's voice. It's authorial generic register imported into a character's mouth. The character wouldn't say this; the line could appear in another character's POV without changing.

**Fix:** rewrite in the character's voice, or cut.

**Diagnostic test:** If the writer cannot reproduce a similar phrase in another scene with the same character drawing on the same voice register, the line is voice slip.

### Lost Callback

The line is in voice (the character would say this) but reaches for material the reader hasn't seen. The setup was cut in earlier revision and the callback now lands on no anchor. The reader registers the line as discordant because it's gesturing at a shared frame the prose hasn't established.

**Fix:** restore the setup somewhere earlier, OR cut the callback, OR replace it with a callback to material the current text supports.

**Diagnostic test:** Search the rest of the manuscript for the term, the analogy, or the conceptual setup the line is reaching for. Absence is a strong signal that the setup got cut.

### Distinguishing the Two

The diagnostic question that distinguishes voice slip from lost callback: when you ask the writer "does the character actually use this phrase elsewhere, or refer to this idea elsewhere?", the answer for voice slip is "no, this is just authorial register." The answer for lost callback is "yes, but you cannot see it because it has been cut."

Lost callback is recoverable through restoration. Voice slip requires rewriting.

When running source triage and a flagged line fails the voice test, ask the writer the diagnostic question before concluding voice slip. If the writer recognizes the line as a stranded callback, the salvage plan changes from Recast to Restore-Setup-Elsewhere.

## "Earned by Frame" — A Third Verdict

Beyond *earned* and *unearned*, some passages are *earned by frame*: the prose itself diagnoses the problematic pattern through proximate commentary, characters calling each other out, or narrator awareness. When a chapter's diagnostic register names the AI-fluent move and uses it deliberately as evidence of altered cognition or pose, the move is doing structural work that cutting would erase.

**Earned-by-frame example (illustrative).**

A character delivers a manifesto cadence speech that is unmistakably pamphlet-shaped:

> "We are not a brittle marriage. We have absorbed so many things together. We are durable. This is just a thing the durability has to absorb."

The pattern is unearned by the cadence test (each sentence is a register variant of the same content). But the surrounding prose names it:

> The sentences were so smooth. So clean. Each one laid down on the next like a row of bricks in a wall a person was building between themselves and a window they had been looking out of.

Then a second character calls it explicitly:

> "You are speaking like a pamphlet."

The manifesto cadence is *earned by frame*: the prose makes the reader see the move as a move, as evidence of the speaker's altered state. Cutting the manifesto would erase the diagnostic. The frame is the work.

**Diagnostic test for earned-by-frame.** Does the surrounding prose make the reader see the move as a move, rather than reading past it? If yes, the move is load-bearing through the frame. Don't soften.

## Triage Rules by Passage Type

### Fiction

| Passage type | Pattern usually earned | Pattern usually unearned |
|---|---|---|
| Dialogue | Yes; character voice tolerates more pattern | Only when the pattern is uniform across all characters |
| Character interior, actively sorting | Yes; the negation IS the cognitive act | When the sorting goes nowhere |
| Character interior, generic introspection | Rarely | Almost always; "Not X. Not Y." setup is AI-fluent monologue |
| Narrator-pose commentary | Rarely | Almost always; the "There was X in his Y" structures are pose |
| Genre-required language | Yes (hypnotic induction, prayer, ritual) | When the writer is signaling the register without earning it |

### Nonfiction (Argument-Shaped)

| Passage type | Pattern usually earned | Pattern usually unearned |
|---|---|---|
| Argument under construction | Yes; thinking is the point | When the working-through goes nowhere |
| Recommendation paragraphs | Rarely | Almost always; template "must commit to" formulations are pose |
| Concession passages | When the concession is real | When false-balance constructions manufacture concession |
| Evidence deployment | When evidence is named and committed to | When authority laundering substitutes for citation |
| Framing / context-setting | When the frame does specific analytical work | When abstractions replace named actors that the writer should commit to |

## Process Notes

### Diminishing Returns Are Real

First pass through a chapter cuts substantial AI-fluency. Twentieth pass is polishing what's already polished. Stop when the next cut starts replacing voice rather than removing pose.

**Practical heuristic.** After the cuts the auditor or writer is confident about, mark the next ten cuts as "considered." Sit with them for an hour or a day. Re-examine. The ones that still want cutting are real. The ones that don't are the boundary where pose ended and voice began.

### Don't Fake Voice You Can't Sustain

When cutting AI-fluent prose leaves a vacuum, sometimes a character-specific phrase fills the space better than nothing. But only when the writer has real voice to draw from. Faking voice creates a different kind of pose, sometimes worse than the AI-fluent version.

**Operational test.** If the writer (or auditor) cannot reproduce a similar phrase in another scene with the same character drawing on the same voice register without searching, the proposed fill is faked. Leave the cut empty.

**Better than faking.** Most cuts heal on their own. The surrounding prose carries the meaning. A reader reading the revised passage almost never notices an absence; they notice presence (a forced character-voice fill that doesn't sound like the character) much more readily.

### The Single Most Useful Principle

If a writer or auditor only carries one move from this layer, carry the question: whose voice is this, and is it doing real work?

Pattern-matching surface tells without that triage produces the failure mode common in external critiques: generic recommendations that flatten character voice in the name of cleaning up prose. Source triage prevents that failure by making voice attribution the precondition for every cut.

## Calibration Discipline

When auditing using these examples:

- **Don't cut every instance of a flagged pattern.** Triage is contextual.
- **Don't replace direct prose with ornate prose.** The fix is concrete, not lush.
- **Match the figure to the genre.** Nonfiction patterns belong in argument-shaped prose. Fiction patterns belong in narrative prose. Don't import.
- **Trust the writer's voice attribution.** The auditor's job is to surface candidates with reasoning. The writer (or the writer's editor) decides whether each instance is earned, unearned, or earned by frame.

The audit produces the diagnosis. The author executes the revision. Firewall holds.
