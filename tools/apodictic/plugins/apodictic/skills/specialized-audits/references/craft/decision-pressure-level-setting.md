## Purpose

This brief level-sets a specialized audit for **decision pressure architecture**.

The target failure is not "characters make mistakes."

The target failure is **unsupported choice logic**:

1. major decisions without visible pressure geometry,
2. alternatives that vanish without explanation,
3. high-cost pivots with low causal preparation,
4. emotional intensity that does not clarify choice behavior,
5. climactic decisions that feel convenient rather than inevitable.

The structural isomorphism across companion audits: each diagnoses when a system signals presence without manifesting load. Stakes signals risk without converting to consequence. Force signals kinetic energy without converting to momentum. Decision pressure signals choice without converting to plausibility.

**Anchor line: A choice without visible pressure is a twist without setup.**

This brief asks:

1. what models explain believable decisions in fiction,
2. what failure patterns produce "I don't buy it" responses,
3. what positive integration looks like across modes,
4. how burden shifts by mode,
5. how to distinguish intentional opacity from motivation failure.

## 1) Theoretical Grounding: What Makes Decisions Feel Believable?

Decision plausibility in fiction operates through seven interlocking mechanisms.

### 1.1 Bounded rationality and satisficing

Characters are not expected to optimize. Readers hold characters to a standard of satisficing under constraint: given what this person knows, feels, fears, and has time for, is this choice defensible?

The cognitive science of bounded rationality (Simon, 1955; Gigerenzer, 2007) provides the framework. Characters operate with incomplete information, limited processing time, and competing pressures. A decision reads as believable when the constraint set visible to the reader explains why the character settled for this option rather than a better one.

The audit should never test whether characters make *good* decisions. It should test whether the manuscript makes visible the constraints that produced *this* decision. Rationality is bounded; the bounds must be legible.

### 1.2 Counterfactual generation and option visibility

Readers generate counterfactuals automatically. When a character makes a major choice, readers unconsciously ask: "Why didn't they just...?" This is not a flaw in reading—it is how human cognition processes choice. Readers are theory-of-mind machines; they model character decision spaces and test choices against their own option generation (Zunshine, 2006).

A decision reads as believable when either:

1. the reader's counterfactual was considered and rejected on page with visible reasoning,
or
2. the constraint field is tight enough that the reader's counterfactual is clearly infeasible.

A decision fails when the reader's obvious counterfactual is neither addressed nor constrained out. This is the single most common source of "I don't buy it" reactions.

The audit must track alternative visibility. Not whether every alternative is discussed—that would produce tedious fiction—but whether plausible alternatives are either acknowledged or inferably eliminated.

### 1.3 Pressure geometry and multi-axis constraint

Single-axis pressure produces underdetermined choices. If the only pressure is time, the reader can imagine solutions that don't require speed. If the only pressure is danger, the reader can imagine ways to avoid the danger.

Believable major decisions typically sit at the intersection of multiple pressure axes:

1. external threat (danger, deadline, resource depletion),
2. relational consequence (what this choice costs relationships),
3. identity/moral cost (what this choice means about who the character is),
4. information asymmetry (what the character doesn't know),
5. temporal compression (shrinking window for action).

When multiple axes converge, they eliminate alternatives that would work on single axes. The reader cannot propose a counterfactual that satisfies all constraints simultaneously. This is pressure geometry: the shape of the constraint space that makes one choice (or a small set of choices) the survivable path.

The audit should assess multi-axis pressure at major decision points. Decisions under single-axis pressure are higher risk for implausibility. Climax decisions and major pivots should show convergent constraint.

### 1.4 Emotion-cognition interaction under stress

Stress and emotion systematically alter cognition. Fear narrows attention (Easterbrook, 1959). Anger increases risk tolerance (Lerner & Keltner, 2001). Shame produces avoidance. Attachment produces risk acceptance toward the attached object. Exhaustion degrades executive function.

At the neurobiological level, acute stress activates the locus coeruleus-noradrenaline system, shifting cognition from deliberative to reactive processing. Cortisol elevation under sustained stress degrades working memory and executive function. These are not personality quirks—they are cognitive architecture.

A character under fear who makes a broad, calm, strategic assessment is less believable than a character under fear who focuses on the nearest escape route. A character in the grip of anger who carefully weighs all options is less believable than one who takes the aggressive path without full consideration.

This creates a productive diagnostic asymmetry: non-optimal decisions can be *more* believable than optimal ones, if the emotional state is legible and the cognitive alteration is consistent. The manuscript that shows a character in panic and then has them make a calm tactical decision has a coherence failure. The manuscript that shows a character in panic and has them grab the nearest weapon without checking if it's loaded has an emotion-cognition match.

The audit should test emotion-cognition coherence, not decision quality. A character who makes a terrible choice under legible emotional pressure passes. A character who makes the right choice while allegedly terrified may fail.

### 1.5 Information-state integrity

Decisions must be consistent with what the character could plausibly know, believe, and infer at the moment of choice. This is the knowledge-integrity test. It has three failure modes:

1. *Knowledge cheat*: character acts on information they don't have.
2. *Ignored known risk*: character has been explicitly shown a danger and proceeds as if it doesn't exist, without emotional or strategic justification.
3. *Reader-knowledge drift*: the character's behavior tracks what the reader knows rather than what the POV character knows. This is subtle and particularly common in third-person limited and first-person POV.

The audit needs an information-state tracking artifact that maps what the character knew vs. what the decision required.

### 1.6 Tradeoff architecture and irreversibility

Decisions feel consequential when they carry visible tradeoff: what is gained, what is lost, what becomes unavailable. Decisions without tradeoff feel frictionless. Frictionless decisions don't register as real choices—they register as the author moving the character.

Irreversibility amplifies decision weight. A choice that closes doors permanently feels heavier than a reversible choice. Escalating irreversibility across narrative time creates the sensation of tightening—each decision matters more because the undo option is shrinking.

Tradeoff and irreversibility create decision debt: earlier choices that constrain later choices. The character who chose loyalty in act one is now constrained by that loyalty in act three. Decision debt is a primary mechanism for creating the sensation of inevitable climax—the character arrives at the final choice through accumulated constraint from their own prior decisions, not through arbitrary plot mechanics.

The audit should track tradeoff at each major decision and test whether decision debt compounds toward climax.

### 1.7 Pivot preparation and decision trajectory

Major directional changes in narrative—late Act I commitment, midpoint reversal, climax choice—require proportionate preparation. The preparation is not explanation; it is accumulated pressure. A pivot is believable when prior scenes have been tightening the constraint field such that *this* turn is the shape the pressure was always pushing toward.

A pivot fails when it requires the reader to accept that the character would make this turn without visible pressure pointing this direction. This is the distinction between surprise and implausibility: surprise comes from the specific choice being unexpected; implausibility comes from the choice direction being unprepared.

Decision trajectory is the arc of option-narrowing across the narrative. Early decisions: many options, low pressure. Middle decisions: fewer options, rising pressure. Late decisions: few options, maximum pressure. Climax: one terrible choice, fully constrained. When trajectory is working, the reader feels the inexorable tightening. When trajectory fails, the reader wonders why the character is suddenly cornered.

The audit should track decision trajectory across major units and test whether option space narrows with visible causal logic.

### 1.8 Diagnostic channels for audit design

The strongest synthesis for this audit uses seven channels:

1. **Alternative Visibility** (are plausible options legible),
2. **Constraint Specificity** (are options credibly narrowed),
3. **Information-State Integrity** (does choice fit known knowledge),
4. **Emotion-Cognition Coherence** (does affect plausibly shape reasoning),
5. **Reasoning Fidelity** (is on-page logic sufficient),
6. **Tradeoff Reality** (does choice incur real cost),
7. **Pivot Integrity** (are major turns prepared and paid).

### Diagnostic takeaways (Q1)

1. Evaluate choices as pressure responses, not moral correctness.
2. Require explicit option-constraint logic at major pivots.
3. Separate irrational-but-credible from incoherent-by-convenience.
4. Track tradeoff visibility as a core plausibility signal.
5. Audit climactic decisions against earlier pressure setup.
6. Test emotion-cognition coherence as a bidirectional diagnostic: both competence collapse and stress-immune performance are failure modes.
7. A testable property: can the reader reconstruct why this character chose this option at this moment, given visible constraints, available knowledge, emotional state, and alternatives that were either considered or eliminated?

## 2) Failure Modes: Where Decision Pressure Breaks

Decision-pressure failures have repeatable mechanisms.

### 2.1 Core detachment patterns

1. **Invisible Option Set** — major decision presented without visible alternatives. Reader cannot test plausibility because they can't see what else the character could have done.

2. **False Binary Manufacture** — narrative frames two choices while credible third paths exist and are not constrained out.

3. **Option Dropout** — previously established viable path disappears between scenes without causal explanation.

4. **Pressure Vacuum** — decision framed as urgent but constraint field is insufficiently concrete to force timing or selection.

5. **Constraint Handwave** — "no choice" is asserted without demonstrable narrowing mechanics.

6. **Clock Without Teeth** — deadline exists rhetorically but doesn't materially alter planning, risk tolerance, or option set.

7. **Knowledge Cheat** — decision depends on information the character could not plausibly possess.

8. **Ignored Known Risk** — character ignores clearly established high-relevance risk without motive or constraint justification.

9. **Reader-Knowledge Drift** — decision behavior follows what the reader knows rather than what the POV character could know.

10. **Emotion-Logic Split** — emotional state and decision behavior don't cohere. Character is labeled as feeling X but decides as if feeling Y.

11. **Convenient Stupidity** — character competence temporarily collapses to force plot movement. Capability drops at decision-critical moments and restores immediately after.

12. **Panic as Universal Excuse** — panic invoked to justify implausible decisions without consistent modeling of what panic does to this character's cognition. Panic appears only at convenience pivots.

13. **Stress-Immune Performance** — character under extreme stress performs with unimpaired deliberative precision. Mirror failure of Convenient Stupidity: instead of competence dropping when plot needs failure, competence remains artificially high when stress should degrade it.

14. **Reasoning Retrofit** — motivation or rationale appears after decision as patch rather than before/during as causal driver.

15. **Motive Label Without Mechanism** — text names a motive (love, duty, revenge, loyalty) but doesn't show how that motive selected this option over alternatives.

16. **No-Tradeoff Win** — major choice delivers upside without meaningful downside, foreclosed option, or deferred cost.

17. **Cost-Blind Choice** — decision ignores obvious costs that should influence the choice under established character values.

18. **Outsourced Cost** — consequences shifted to side characters or background systems while decision-maker avoids meaningful burden.

19. **Deferred Consequence Erasure** — future costs are seeded and promised but never collected. The narrative establishes that a decision will have a price, then quietly drops the debt.

20. **Opaque Pivot** — major directional turn occurs without adequate setup pressure.

21. **Midpoint Whiplash** — midpoint strategy reversal contradicts established decision logic without bridge.

22. **Climax Choice Disconnect** — final major decision conflicts with established pressure, information, or arc logic.

23. **Resolution Jump** — ending reconciliation choice resolves core conflict without paying decision debt accumulated through the narrative.

### 2.2 Where these cluster

Most frequent concentration points:

1. late Act I commitment scenes,
2. midpoint reversals,
3. post-reveal strategy scenes,
4. climax approach decisions,
5. ending reconciliation choices.

### 2.3 Structural-isomorphism anchor

Useful reframing line for authors:

**A choice without visible pressure is a twist without setup.**

Why this works:

1. shifts diagnosis from "character dumb" to architecture gap,
2. maps decision plausibility to known setup/payoff principles,
3. preserves author agency while clarifying mechanism.

### 2.4 False positives to control

1. Sparse interiority is not automatically missing motivation.
2. Deliberate ambiguity is not automatically incoherence.
3. Flawed characters are not automatically writing failures.
4. Genre-conventional irrationality still requires pressure legibility.
5. One local convenience is not automatically systemic failure.
6. Stress-degraded decisions are not automatically bad writing; they may be good psychology.

### Diagnostic takeaways (Q2)

1. Diagnose mechanism, not taste.
2. Require evidence at decision points, not summary claims.
3. Use accumulation patterns before assigning systemic severity.
4. Weight climax failures more heavily.
5. Pair with stakes data to avoid single-channel misdiagnosis.
6. Both directions of emotion-cognition mismatch (too stupid and too capable under stress) are failure modes.

## 3) Positive Cases: What Integrated Decision Pressure Looks Like

Strong decision architecture varies by mode but shares: visible option space, credible constraints, real tradeoffs, prepared pivots.

### 3.1 Thriller/Suspense

*Technique: Information-Limit Decision Shaping* — Character's knowledge state is explicitly bounded; decisions follow from what character knows and doesn't know. Reader sees the gap. Decisions are defensible within the character's information horizon even when they're wrong from the reader's wider view.

*Technique: Resource Depletion Option Narrowing* — Character begins with options because they have resources (time, allies, ammunition, money, legal standing). Escalation depletes resources systematically. Each decision is more constrained than the last. By climax, character faces the hardest choice with the least margin.

*Technique: Reversibility Ratchet* — Early decisions are quasi-reversible. Midpoint decisions introduce irreversibility. Final act decisions are explicitly irreversible. Reader feels decision weight escalating because the undo option is shrinking.

### 3.2 Mystery/Investigation

*Technique: Evidence-Governed Choice Logic* — Each investigative decision follows from available evidence. Wrong turns are legible: character had good reason to pursue this path given what they knew. Post-reveal, reader can trace how each decision made sense at the time.

*Technique: Cost-of-Pursuit Escalation* — Investigation carries mounting cost: personal danger, relational damage, professional risk. Each decision to continue investigating is a choice with increasing price.

*Technique: Reveal-Field Decision Rewrite* — Major reveals change the decision field. Information previously supporting one path now supports another. Character must reprocess their option set with new data. Pivot moments feel earned because the information that changed the field is visible.

### 3.3 Romance

*Technique: Vulnerability-Commitment Cascade* — Early choices are emotionally safe. Each subsequent choice requires more vulnerability. The cascade creates escalating decision difficulty because the character must choose between self-protection and connection.

*Technique: Self-Protection Logic to Commitment Logic Transition* — Character's decision framework shifts across the narrative. Early choices follow self-protection logic. Later choices follow commitment logic. The transition is the arc. The transition is visible in the character's changing option evaluation.

*Technique: Relational Bid Architecture* — Each relational bid (approach, disclosure, repair) is a decision with visible alternatives (withdraw, withhold, escalate). The character's choice to bid is legible against the alternative of not bidding.

### 3.4 Horror

*Technique: Degraded-Cognition Decision Making* — Characters make decisions under cognitively compromised states: fear, exhaustion, confusion, sensory overload. Decisions that are "stupid" by calm-state standards are believable when horror has established cognitive degradation. The degradation must be modeled consistently, not invoked only at convenience.

*Technique: Shrinking-Safety-Zone Option Elimination* — Horror progressively eliminates safe options. Each escape route is closed, each ally removed, each resource depleted. The character's "stupid" choice to go into the basement is believable when every other option has been visibly eliminated.

*Technique: Information Ambiguity as Constraint* — Horror creates decision difficulty through ambiguous information. Character doesn't know if the threat is real, if the ally is trustworthy, if the escape route is safe. Decisions under genuine uncertainty are inherently more forgivable than decisions under clear information.

### 3.5 Literary/Upmarket

*Technique: Value-Conflict Decision Architecture* — Major choices dramatize value conflicts rather than external dangers. Character must choose between competing goods or competing loyalties. Neither option is wrong; both are costly. Decision plausibility comes from the reader understanding both values and seeing why neither can be abandoned.

*Technique: Accumulated Micro-Decision Consequence* — Rather than single dramatic choices, literary fiction often builds through accumulated small decisions that compound. Each individual choice is defensible; the cumulative trajectory creates the crisis.

*Technique: Social and Psychological Constraint* — Options are eliminated not by external danger but by social position, psychological limitation, class, obligation, and history. The constraint field is interior and relational rather than physical.

### 3.6 SFF/Speculative

*Technique: Rule-Constrained Decision Sets* — World rules (magic systems, technology limits, political structures) create the constraint field. Decisions are believable when they follow established rules. Decisions fail when rules are bent to force the choice the plot needs.

*Technique: Capability Cost as Decision Architecture* — Using power has cost. Each decision to deploy capability incurs a price that constrains future decisions. By climax, the character has spent resources and must face the final challenge with diminished capacity.

*Technique: System-Scale Consequence Binding* — Character's decisions affect not just personal outcomes but world-state. This creates additional decision axes: personal survival vs. systemic good.

### 3.7 Cozy/Low-Violence

*Technique: Community-Standing Decision Architecture* — Decisions carry social consequence within a bounded community. Choice plausibility comes from understanding community dynamics: who would be offended, what alliances would shift, what reputation would be damaged.

*Technique: Relational Repair as Decision Cost* — The cost of decisions is measured in relational damage that must be repaired. Each choice that serves the investigation or goal damages a relationship.

### 3.8 Satire/Comedy Hybrid

*Technique: Escalating Facade Maintenance* — Character makes increasingly costly decisions to maintain a deception or facade. Each decision is locally rational but globally irrational. Reader sees both levels simultaneously.

*Technique: Absurdity Within Internal Logic* — Comic decisions follow internal logic consistently even when that logic is absurd. Consistently absurd is still consistent. Plausibility comes from consistency, not realism.

### 3.9 Cross-mode success signals

Across successful cases:

1. alternatives are visible enough to test plausibility,
2. constraints make selection understandable,
3. tradeoffs are tangible,
4. pivots are prepared and paid,
5. choice history compounds toward climax,
6. decision logic follows from character's knowledge state, emotional state, and values.

### Diagnostic takeaways (Q3)

1. Good decision writing is pressure geometry on page.
2. Surprise and plausibility can coexist through preparation.
3. Tradeoff visibility is more important than internal monologue volume.
4. Climax credibility depends on earlier option/constraint design.
5. Mode conventions alter tolerance, not causal requirements.

## 4) Mode Calibration: How Burden Shifts by Mode

Each mode has different decision-pressure promises.

### 4.1 Thriller/Suspense

1. **Promise:** choices under tightening urgency and incomplete knowledge.
2. **Blind spot:** urgency claims replacing actual constraint design.
3. **Named failure:** **Countdown Cosplay**.
4. **False positive risk:** over-demanding exhaustive tactical exposition.

### 4.2 Mystery/Investigation

1. **Promise:** decisions update with evidence.
2. **Blind spot:** intuition leaps without inferential bridge.
3. **Named failure:** **Leap Detective**.
4. **False positive risk:** penalizing deliberate red-herring uncertainty.

### 4.3 Romance

1. **Promise:** emotionally and relationally legible commitment choices.
2. **Blind spot:** attachment pivots without vulnerability setup.
3. **Named failure:** **Heart Switch Without Circuit**.
4. **Secondary failure:** **Manufactured Misunderstanding** — external miscommunication forced to substitute for genuine vulnerability-based decision pressure; the Big Misunderstanding as plot shortcut for commitment-test architecture.
5. **False positive risk:** demanding external-risk logic for primarily relational arcs.

### 4.4 Horror

1. **Promise:** fear-shifted but credible survival decisions.
2. **Blind spot:** panic used as universal excuse for incoherence.
3. **Named failure:** **Panic Patch**.
4. **False positive risk:** judging frightened decisions by calm-state rationality.

### 4.5 Literary/Upmarket

1. **Promise:** morally and psychologically precise choice architecture.
2. **Blind spot:** opacity used to mask weak causal links.
3. **Named failure:** **Elegant Motivation Fog**.
4. **False positive risk:** over-penalizing deliberate interpretive openness.

### 4.6 SFF/Speculative

1. **Promise:** choices constrained by world/system rules.
2. **Blind spot:** rule exceptions introduced to force key turns.
3. **Named failure:** **Rule Warp Decision**.
4. **False positive risk:** demanding realist logic where stylized rule logic is coherent.

### 4.7 Cozy/Comfort

1. **Promise:** socially and relationally legible low-harm choices.
2. **Blind spot:** conflict avoidance that erases consequence.
3. **Named failure:** **Soft Dodge Loop**.
4. **False positive risk:** over-demanding high-danger pressure models.

### 4.8 Satire/Comedy Hybrid

1. **Promise:** tonal play with retained decision credibility.
2. **Blind spot:** punchline-first turns with no pressure logic.
3. **Named failure:** **Gag-Driven Pivot**.
4. **False positive risk:** flattening intentional absurdity into "implausible" by default.

### Diagnostic takeaways (Q4)

1. Calibration must tighten on mode-essential burdens.
2. Every mode has a distinct convenience-disguised-as-choice trap.
3. Decision plausibility should be mode-aware but not mode-excused.
4. Tighten/loosen rules are required for fair severity calls.
5. Calibration notes should be mandatory in audit output.

## 5) Distinguish Problem: Intentional Opacity vs Motivation Failure

### 5.1 Core distinction

The audit must separate intent from effectiveness.

A choice can be intentionally opaque and still fail.
A flawed decision can be deliberately irrational and still pass.

### 5.2 Five categories of cases that look similar on surface reading

**1. DELIBERATE WITHHOLDING vs. UNSUPPORTED PIVOT**

Deliberate withholding: author intentionally conceals character's reasoning, but textual evidence supports the choice when the reader has all information. Revelation of motive feels like puzzle-completion, not retrofit.

Unsupported pivot: author hasn't built the pressure architecture. The character acts without visible constraint because the constraint doesn't exist in the text.

*Distinguish test*: Can a careful reader, given all information available by the end of the narrative, reconstruct why the character made this choice? If reconstruction requires information that isn't in the text, it's unsupported.

**2. FLAWED CHARACTER JUDGMENT vs. AUTHORIAL CONVENIENCE**

Flawed judgment: character makes a bad call for legible reasons. Their psychology, emotional state, values, or information limitations produce a defensible but wrong choice.

Authorial convenience: character makes a bad call because the plot needs a bad call here. There's no character-specific reason for this error.

*Distinguish test*: Does the specific character's specific psychology explain why *they* would make this particular error? Or would any character in this plot position make the same mistake regardless of personality?

**3. IRRATIONAL UNDER STRESS vs. INCOHERENT BY CONVENIENCE**

Irrational under stress: character's decision quality degrades in ways consistent with their emotional state and established stress response. The irrationality is modeled, not random.

Incoherent by convenience: character's competence drops specifically and only when the plot needs them to fail. No consistent stress model explains the degradation.

*Distinguish test*: Is the character's cognitive degradation consistent with their established emotional state and previous stress responses? Or does competence selectively collapse only at plot-critical moments?

**4. MORAL AMBIGUITY vs. CAUSALITY GAP**

Moral ambiguity: character faces a genuine dilemma where neither option is clearly right. The ambiguity is in the ethics, not in the decision logic.

Causality gap: character makes a choice that doesn't follow from visible pressure. The manuscript calls it "morally complex" but the complexity is actually in the reader's inability to reconstruct why the character chose this path.

*Distinguish test*: Can the reader articulate both sides of the dilemma? If the reader can see why this was a hard choice, it's moral ambiguity. If the reader can't see why the character chose this specific path over alternatives, it's a causality gap.

**5. ANTI-PSYCHOLOGICAL STYLE vs. MISSING MOTIVATION**

Anti-psychological style: some fiction deliberately minimizes interiority and motivation access. Characters act; readers infer. The style is intentional and the option/constraint/consequence architecture is externally legible.

Missing motivation: the manuscript provides no access to decision logic—not because style withholds it, but because the logic isn't there.

*Distinguish test*: Is the constraint field externally legible? Can a reader reconstruct decision logic from action, dialogue, and context even without interior access? If external evidence supports the choice, it's style. If external evidence doesn't support it either, motivation is missing.

### 5.3 Required tests

1. **Option Set Test** — Are plausible alternatives inferable?
2. **Constraint Test** — Are constraints specific enough to narrow options?
3. **Info-State Test** — Does choice fit what character could know then?
4. **Emotion-Coherence Test** — Does affect plausibly alter reasoning profile?
5. **Tradeoff Test** — Is gain/loss structure visible and binding?
6. **Pivot Preparation Test** — Does major turn have prior pressure setup?
7. **Persistence Test** — Does choice alter later risk and behavior?

### 5.4 Classification decision tree

1. Is the option set inferable from context?
   - No → Likely invisible option set or false binary. Check if deliberate withholding is supported by post-reveal evidence.
   - Yes → Proceed to 2.

2. Are constraints specific enough to narrow the option set credibly?
   - No → Likely pressure vacuum or constraint handwave. Check if minimalist style makes constraints externally legible.
   - Yes → Proceed to 3.

3. Does the decision fit the character's information state?
   - No → Knowledge cheat or reader-knowledge drift. Check if information-state gap serves deliberate dramatic irony.
   - Yes → Proceed to 4.

4. Does the emotional state cohere with decision behavior?
   - No → Emotion-logic split or convenient stupidity. Check if stress-response model is consistent.
   - Yes → Proceed to 5.

5. Is there visible tradeoff?
   - No → No-tradeoff win or cost-blind choice. Check if tradeoff is delayed rather than absent.
   - Yes → Decision architecture is sound.

### 5.5 Four-way classification outcomes

| Test Profile | Classification | Editorial Action |
|---|---|---|
| All tests pass; mode calibration coherent | **Intentional and Successful** | Preserve architecture; tune local weak spots |
| Intentional strategy visible; 1-2 channels unstable at key decisions | **Intentional but Unstable** | Targeted revision on unstable channels |
| 3-4 pass; pattern ambiguous; may indicate emerging craft | **Ambiguous/Developmental** | Flag with specific tests for author evaluation |
| Repeated failures across channels or climax integrity fail | **Accidental Failure** | Structural recast of major decision chain |

### 5.6 Common misclassifications to avoid

1. Treating all irrational choices as failures.
2. Treating all quiet motivation as absent motivation.
3. Treating all ambiguity as depth.
4. Treating one plausible scene as proof against systemic pattern.

### Diagnostic takeaways (Q5)

1. Distinguish should be test-driven, not tone-driven.
2. Intentionality is evidence, not immunity.
3. Climax pivots deserve elevated scrutiny.
4. Tradeoff visibility is the best anti-convenience check.
5. Classification should directly drive revision scope.
