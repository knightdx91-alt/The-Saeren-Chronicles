## Purpose

This brief level-sets the `argument-persuasion.md` companion module. The core audit already diagnoses whether an argument is structurally intelligible and evidentially honest. The Persuasion module asks a different question:

**given this argument, this form, and this audience, what arrangement, framing, and credibility strategy would help it land more effectively without rewriting it or turning the system into a ghostwriter?**

The companion module is therefore advisory, not generative. It is allowed to:

1. diagnose audience mismatch
2. propose structural recalibration
3. identify sequence and framing problems

It is not allowed to draft replacement prose.

---

## Method and Confidence

Primary frames:

1. Perelman and Olbrechts-Tyteca on audience adherence
2. Bitzer on rhetorical situation
3. classical arrangement and rhetorical sequencing
4. ethos / trust formation in public argument
5. contemporary compression realities in public-facing forms

Confidence notes:

1. Audience-dependence is durable and unavoidable in real-world argument.
2. Persuasion guidance must remain downstream of structural diagnosis or it will optimize weak arguments cosmetically.
3. The firewall line remains meaningful: advising on sequence and emphasis is not the same as drafting copy.

## Research Question 1: Theoretical Grounding

### Frame 1: Audience is constitutive, not optional

Perelman and related rhetorical traditions make the key point: arguments are addressed to audiences, not abstract reason in a vacuum. That does not abolish truth or burden. It means:

1. what may remain implicit for one audience must be explicit for another
2. one audience requires early credibility scaffolding while another cares more about method detail
3. identical claims can fail or succeed depending on what is foregrounded first

Operational consequence:

The module must build an audience matrix, not assume one generic reader.

### Frame 2: Rhetorical situation changes burden timing

Bitzer's exigence, audience, and constraints map cleanly onto nonfiction argument forms. A policy brief, testimony, open letter, academic article, and op-ed may all address similar subject matter while demanding different timing:

1. when to state the claim
2. when to introduce stakes
3. when to concede
4. when to define terms
5. when to recommend action

Operational consequence:

Persuasion must be treated partly as sequence management.

### Frame 3: Arrangement is not ornamental

Classical arrangement theory remains useful in stripped-down form:

1. opening earns attention and orientation
2. framing prepares interpretation
3. proofs accumulate
4. concessions manage resistance
5. closing consolidates and directs

Operational consequence:

The Persuasion module should diagnose not just *what* is said, but whether it arrives in the right order for uptake.

### Frame 4: Trust is an architectural resource

Ethos is often misread as tone or credibility branding. In practice it is structural:

1. what the manuscript concedes
2. how carefully it marks scope
3. whether it overstates before earning confidence
4. whether it signals competence in the terms the audience recognizes

Operational consequence:

Trust-building moves should be a named dimension of the module.

### Frame 5: Compression changes what counts as enough

Public argument operates under different compression constraints than academic or legal writing. This shifts:

1. how many subclaims are viable
2. how visible warrants must be
3. how long objections can be
4. how evidence is layered

Operational consequence:

The module should calibrate by form, not just by audience posture.

### Frame 6: Persuasion and manipulation are adjacent but distinguishable

The module needs a moral line. Audience adaptation becomes manipulative when it:

1. hides decisive information
2. exploits ambiguity that the argument could resolve
3. performs fairness without real concession cost
4. intensifies affect to outrun burden

Operational consequence:

The module must preserve the core audit's severity findings rather than coaching around them.

## Research Question 2: Persuasion Failure Taxonomy

### 1. Audience blur

The manuscript behaves as though it is addressing everyone and therefore addresses no one well.

### 2. Shared-knowledge mismatch

Necessary background, warrants, or definitions are left implicit for readers who do not actually share them.

### 3. Trust lag

The manuscript asks for agreement before it has established why this reader should trust the speaker, method, or framing.

### 4. Framing-value mismatch

The argument is framed around values that are not the values most likely to move the identified audience.

### 5. Thesis timing error

The main claim lands too early for skeptical readers or too late for readers who need orientation immediately.

### 6. Concession misplacement

A concession appears, but too early, too late, or too cheaply to do persuasive work.

### 7. Recommendation-before-burden

The manuscript asks for action before the reader has been brought through problem, criteria, and tradeoff logic.

### 8. Evidence foreground mismatch

The most persuasive support for this audience is present but buried; less effective support is foregrounded.

### 9. Compression rupture

The piece cuts so aggressively that uptake suffers, even if the core claim remains technically recoverable.

### 10. Register dissonance

The tone or discursive register undermines trust or clarity for the intended audience.

### 11. Friendly-audience overconfidence

The draft leans on presumed agreement and stops doing the work of persuasion.

### 12. Hostile-audience self-sabotage

The draft gives away easy attack points through avoidable sequencing or compression choices.

### Structural isomorphism

Most persuasion failures are not "bad rhetoric" in isolation. They are **mismatches between argumentative structure and the uptake conditions of the intended audience**.

## Research Question 3: Positive Cases

### Policy briefs

Strong cases:

1. define the decision problem quickly
2. surface the recommendation early but not before criteria
3. keep concessions tied to implementation trust

### Testimony

Strong cases:

1. establish witness position clearly
2. move from observation to interpretation carefully
3. use concession to signal credibility rather than surrender

### Op-eds

Strong cases:

1. give immediate orientation
2. choose one frame, not three competing ones
3. compress objections without trivializing them

### Academic arguments

Strong cases:

1. earn trust through definition and method discipline
2. foreground the exact contribution
3. avoid burying the central claim under literature review inertia

### Open letters / advocacy writing

Strong cases:

1. know whether the real audience is signatories, opponents, institutions, or bystanders
2. avoid coalition language that blurs the actual ask

### Cross-case success signals

1. the audience can tell why the argument matters *to them*
2. the sequence reduces rather than amplifies predictable resistance
3. concessions increase trust
4. support most legible to the audience is foregrounded
5. compression never destroys the burden logic

## Research Question 4: Calibration by Audience Posture

### General audience

Need:

1. explicit orientation
2. visible stakes
3. lower jargon load

Blind spot:

assuming warrant recovery that only experts can do

### Expert audience

Need:

1. precise claim contribution
2. method and provenance credibility
3. sharper qualifier discipline

Blind spot:

over-explaining basics while under-defining contested technical terms

### Hostile audience

Need:

1. early credibility
2. defensible narrow formulation
3. strategically placed costly concessions

Blind spot:

self-righteous framing before burden has been earned

### Sympathetic audience

Need:

1. discipline against flattery and overclaim
2. enough structure to remain informative

Blind spot:

assuming agreement substitutes for argument

### Mixed audience

Need:

1. layered support
2. clean sequencing
3. language that experts and non-experts can both parse

Blind spot:

trying to satisfy everyone in every paragraph

## Research Question 4b: False-Positive Risks by Form

| Form | Signal likely to misfire | Why it's not actually a failure |
|---|---|---|
| Academic article | `PS6` Thesis Timing Error — claim appears late | Disciplinary convention may require literature review and method before thesis statement. Delay is earned, not an error. |
| Testimony | `PS10` Register Dissonance — formal or stilted tone | Legal/legislative register is intentionally formal. Colloquial testimony may undermine credibility. |
| Policy brief | `PS9` Compression Rupture — severe cuts to evidence | Policy briefs compress by design. The question is whether the *burden logic* survives, not whether the evidence is comprehensive. |
| Op-ed | `PS7` Concession Misplacement — concession absent or minimal | Space constraints may make a full concession impossible. The question is whether the concession's *work* is done, not whether it's elaborated. |
| Open letter | `PS1` Audience Blur — appears to address everyone | Open letters sometimes deliberately address multiple audiences in layers. Blur is a failure only when layering collapses into undifferentiation. |
| Advocacy journalism | `PS11` Friendly-Audience Drift — preaches to the choir | Some advocacy journalism intentionally mobilizes existing supporters rather than persuading opponents. The module should diagnose the strategy, not penalize it. |

## Research Question 5: Distinguish Problem

The module must distinguish:

1. **legitimate audience adaptation**
2. **mere style preference**
3. **manipulative optimization**

### Decision tests

1. Does the proposed adjustment clarify or merely intensify?
2. Does it preserve the actual burden of proof?
3. Does it reduce predictable misunderstanding without hiding information?
4. Does it respect the form's compression limits?
5. Would the argument remain structurally auditable after the change?

### Classification outcomes

1. **Healthy recalibration** — improves uptake while preserving integrity
2. **Optional stylistic adjustment** — may help, but not structurally necessary
3. **Manipulative drift** — improves persuasion by making the argument less examinable

## Bottom Line

Persuasion belongs in the Nonfiction Argument Engine because nonfiction writers do not only need to know whether their case is structurally valid. They also need to know whether it is arranged and framed to be heard by the audience they are actually addressing. The module's job is to guide that recalibration without crossing into ghostwriting.
