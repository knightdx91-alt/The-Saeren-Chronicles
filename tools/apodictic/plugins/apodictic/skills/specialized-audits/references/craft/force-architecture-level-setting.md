# Force Architecture Level-Setting Brief
## Three-Model Synthesis (Opus46 + Codex53 + Gemini)
*Last Updated: February 20, 2026*
*Status: Final level-setting brief for specialized audit*

---

## Purpose

This brief level-sets a specialized audit for **force architecture**.

The target failure is not "too little action" or "too much violence."

The target failure is **inert force delivery**:

1. action present on the page that does not generate reader participation,
2. intensity that runs without altering stakes structure,
3. skill display that erases rather than sharpens risk,
4. injury that leaves no residue in subsequent behavior,
5. violence that resolves logistics but bypasses character and theme pressure.

This failure is structurally isomorphic with failures diagnosed in companion audits:

- **Ornamental prose** (Literary Craft): language machinery assembled, reader's cognitive participation not generated.
- **Inert dread** (Horror Craft): horror components present, dread architecture not built.
- **Informational drift** (Mystery/Thriller Architecture): information economy assembled, reader's inferential participation not generated.
- **Inert force** (this audit): action choreography present, reader not changed by it.

The shared diagnostic pattern: **craft machinery is visible; transformation is absent.**

This brief answers five questions:

1. What cognitive and narrative models explain why force scenes work on the page?
2. What failure patterns detach force from narrative meaning?
3. What full integration looks like across delivery modes?
4. How burden shifts by delivery mode?
5. How to distinguish intentional stylization from craft failure?

---

## 1) Theoretical Grounding

### 1.1 Force readability is a cognition problem

Scene Perception and Event Comprehension Theory (SPECT) establishes that readers continuously construct and update a mental "situation model" by integrating new spatiotemporal data with existing event models. This requires coordinating two operations: mapping new information into the current model and shifting to construct new models when coherence gaps appear.

During physical conflict, this cognitive mapping is placed under extreme stress. Readers must simultaneously generate predictive inferences (what will causally unfold next) and causal antecedent inferences (linking current action to prior narrative states). When prose delivers rapid kinetic sequences without first establishing a coherent spatial and temporal geometry, the reader experiences cognitive shear—overloaded processing capacity that prevents engagement with the emotional or thematic weight of the conflict.

Event-indexing models (Zwaan, Langston, Graesser 1995) specify that readers maintain a situation model tracking five dimensions: protagonist/actor, spatial location, temporal framework, causal chain, and intentional goals. Force scenes stress all five simultaneously. When a scene drops one or more channels, the model degrades—readers do not experience "exciting chaos" but processing failure.

Event segmentation theory (Zacks et al. 2007) adds that readers parse experience into discrete units at boundary points. In force scenes, the critical boundaries are:

1. tactical shift (new constraint or approach),
2. power-relation shift (who controls the encounter),
3. objective shift (what participants are trying to achieve changes),
4. cost realization (damage registers as consequential).

Continuous kinetic throughput without marked boundaries produces **kinetic blur**: motion without segmentation, generating flat affect regardless of intensity. Boundaries that are marked but static in type produce **beat monotony**: structure without progression.

### 1.2 Suspense architecture in force scenes

Sternberg's narrative interest framework maps directly to force diagnostics:

1. **Curiosity** (what is/was true?): force scenes that withhold information about what happened—who struck, what the injury means, whether the ally survived—generate backward-directed tension.
2. **Suspense** (what will happen under threat?): force scenes that establish clear stakes and uncertain outcomes generate forward-directed tension. This is the primary engine.
3. **Surprise** (what reverses expectation?): force scenes that deliver tactical or outcome reversals generate reorientation.

The critical diagnostic finding: **force scenes fail most often by overinvesting in surprise and underbuilding suspense.** A spectacle reversal without a suspense runway reads as authorial fiat. Sustained suspense architecture with modest surprise reads as earned. The audit should weight suspense-building over surprise-delivery.

### 1.3 Friction as credibility architecture

Clausewitz's concept of friction—the gap between plan and execution under stress—provides the realism bridge across all genres.

Force scenes become credible when:

1. environment complicates intention (terrain, weather, crowd, structural damage),
2. information is partial (fog of combat, deception, limited POV),
3. execution degrades under pressure (fatigue, injury, emotional state, equipment failure),
4. adaptation is required (plans fail and characters must improvise).

Force scenes become gamey when competence appears frictionless—plans execute without degradation, environments pose no independent constraints, and information is conveniently complete.

This applies across genres. A LitRPG protagonist with a system interface faces friction through resource management, cooldown timing, imperfect information about opponents. A character in a domestic violence scenario faces friction through social masking requirements, credibility asymmetry, and escape logistics. Friction is not limited to military realism.

### 1.4 The body problem: POV and force scenes

Force scenes present a distinctive POV challenge. In non-force scenes, interiority is the primary texture of close narration. In force scenes, a common failure is that **interiority vanishes entirely.** The character becomes a camera tracking external action, or an omniscient choreographer narrating movements they could not perceive from their position.

This produces the **Disembodied Combatant** problem: the text delivers force events from no particular cognitive position.

Strong force writing anchors action in embodied perception:

1. **Sensory hierarchy shifts** under stress: visual narrowing, auditory exclusion (ears seem to stop up), time distortion (tachypsychia), proprioceptive overwhelm. The physiological aftermath of massive adrenaline dumps—involuntary tremors, hyperventilation, delayed nausea—can persist long after immediate danger passes.
2. **Information filtering**: the POV character cannot perceive everything. What they miss, misinterpret, or learn late matters as much as what they register.
3. **Decision texture**: the gap between perception, assessment, and action—even compressed to fractions of seconds—provides the interiority thread that keeps the reader inside a person rather than watching a diagram.
4. **Experience calibration**: a trained fighter perceives differently from a civilian. Beginners typically suffer target fixation; experienced fighters read biomechanical telegraphing; elite combatants maintain broad peripheral awareness. The character's experience level shapes what the prose registers.
5. **Distance-difficulty scaling**: the psychological difficulty of inflicting violence scales inversely with distance. Hand-to-hand or edged-weapon combat is exponentially more traumatic for the perpetrator than ranged engagement, crossing intimate physical boundaries.

This is not a demand for stream-of-consciousness combat. It is a demand that force scenes maintain a cognitive anchor—that someone is perceiving this, and their perception shapes what the reader receives.

### 1.5 Violence must convert into meaning

Violence is not intrinsically meaningful. Theoretical convergence:

Arendt (*On Violence*) establishes the violence/power distinction: violence is instrumental, power is relational. Violence can destroy power structures but cannot create them. Force events that destroy but don't alter power relations are structurally incomplete.

Scarry (*The Body in Pain*) argues that violence resists representation—bodily harm tends to unmake language and shared meaning. The text must represent what resists representation; the quality of that attempt is the craft.

Literary theory broadly categorizes fictional violence as either authorial (symbolic harm advancing theme) or character-imposed (harm within narrative reality). Violence functions as an existential stress test, stripping societal veneers to reveal core motives, fears, and capacities. The structural failure occurs when force operates independently of these semantic layers.

For the audit, violence functions as a **conversion engine**:

1. What changed in agency? (who can act, who is constrained)
2. What changed in relationships? (trust, loyalty, fear, obligation)
3. What changed in moral position? (guilt, justification, complicity)
4. What changed in thematic pressure? (what the narrative is about)
5. What changed in the character's self-understanding?

If none changed, the event was spectacle throughput—energy spent without narrative work done.

### 1.6 Consequence persistence is load-bearing

Force events that appear consequential in-scene but leave no behavioral residue afterward are structurally equivalent to events that didn't happen.

Coercive-control research (Stark) provides a useful model: harm is a pattern system, not an isolated incident. Diagnostic significance lies in cumulative effect and downstream behavioral change.

For fiction diagnostics: **weight downstream behavioral and relational changes over in-scene intensity.** A quiet slap that alters decision-making for three chapters outperforms a spectacular battle forgotten after one paragraph.

The persistence test tracks:

1. physical cost (does injury affect capability in later scenes?),
2. psychological cost (does trauma, fear, or aftermath alter behavior?),
3. relational cost (does the event change how characters relate?),
4. resource cost (does expenditure constrain future options?),
5. moral cost (does what the character did or suffered alter their ethical position?).

### 1.7 The abstraction ladder: compression is not incoherence

Hayakawa's Ladder of Abstraction provides a critical tool for the distinguish problem. Force scenes can operate at different levels:

1. **Concrete/granular**: blow-by-blow, spatial-mechanic detail.
2. **Mid-level**: tactical summary with selective detail.
3. **Abstract/compressed**: consequence narration with minimal choreography.

All three can succeed. All three can fail. A writer may intentionally ascend the ladder, using compressed reporting to shield the reader from violence mechanics while amplifying psychological outcome—a technique of withholding that generates suspense rather than spectacle. The audit must not privilege granularity as the default standard.

The diagnostic question is not "how much detail?" but "does the chosen abstraction level maintain legibility, causality, and conversion?"

### 1.8 Diagnostic channels for audit design

Synthesizing across theoretical grounding, six channels emerge:

1. **Legibility** (LG): can readers construct a coherent situation model?
2. **Tactical Causality** (TC): do actions follow from constraints, capabilities, and environment?
3. **Competence-Risk Tension** (CR): does skill coexist with meaningful vulnerability?
4. **Cost Persistence** (CP): do costs survive the scene and alter subsequent behavior?
5. **Escalation Kind-Shift** (ES): does conflict evolve in type, not only magnitude?
6. **Meaning Conversion** (MC): do force events produce character, relationship, or theme change?

### Diagnostic takeaways (Q1)

1. Treat force-scene failure first as a comprehension architecture problem, not a style problem.
2. Require explicit event-boundary tracking: tactical shift, power shift, objective shift, cost realization.
3. Weight suspense-building over surprise-delivery.
4. Friction applies across all genres.
5. Force scenes that lose their cognitive anchor (Disembodied Combatant) fail structurally regardless of choreographic quality.
6. Violence must convert to meaning; unconverted force is spectacle throughput.
7. Persistence outweighs intensity as the primary measure of significance.
8. Abstraction level is a style choice; legibility and conversion are craft requirements.

## 2) Failure Modes

### 2.1 Core detachment patterns

1. **White-Room Collision**: Bodies move; space does not exist. No terrain, no environmental constraint, no positional logic.
2. **Teleport Bodies**: Character positions contradict prior beat geometry without textual accounting.
3. **Actor Chain Blur**: Reader loses clear actor identity under speed—pronoun-heavy chains, interchangeable references in multi-opponent sequences.
4. **Sensory Flatline**: Force events narrated through a single sensory channel without the perceptual shifts stress produces.
5. **Disembodied Combatant**: POV character's interiority vanishes during force events. Text delivers choreography from no particular cognitive position.
6. **And-Then Sequencing**: Beat list without causal hierarchy. Events occur in order but not in consequence.
7. **Ruleless Advantage**: Outcome depends on capability, resource, or option not seeded before the force event.
8. **Frictionless Execution**: Plans execute without degradation under pressure. No environmental disruption, no informational uncertainty, no adaptation cost.
9. **Godmode Drift**: Protagonist competence escalates until meaningful risk is structurally impossible across all threat categories.
10. **Threat Collapse**: Antagonist capability established in setup collapses to preserve protagonist momentum at resolution.
11. **Plot Armor Leak**: Survival probability is visibly governed by plot needs rather than scene causality. Similar secondary characters receive harsher consequences; implausible escapes recur without cost.
12. **Flawless Execution**: Characters execute complex maneuvers under extreme stress without error or degradation of fine motor skills. Narrative fails to account for physiological impact of adrenaline.
13. **Rubber-Band Injury**: Physical harm appears in-scene and disappears in subsequent behavior.
14. **Resource Theater**: Depletion narrated rhetorically but never constraining actual behavior. Characters worry about resources but always have enough.
15. **Trauma Erasure**: Psychological impact of force events produces no behavioral change. Killing treated as mechanical transaction devoid of emotional toll.
16. **Collateral Erasure**: Bystanders, environments, and secondary characters absorb damage that is never acknowledged—economic, legal, human.
17. **Relationship Reset**: Force-event relational consequences do not persist. Betrayal, rescue, or harm fails to alter trust behavior; power dynamics return to baseline.
18. **Scale-Only Escalation**: Conflict grows in magnitude without changing in type. Same strategic problem repeated at volume.
19. **Combat Monotone**: Repeated force events with identical tactical pattern, emotional register, and narrative function.
20. **Escalation Cliff**: Build-up promises climactic force event that underdelivers on accumulated tension.
21. **Pacing-Significance Inversion**: Structural weight afforded to minor skirmishes exceeds weight allocated to narrative climax. Scene length and force-event significance are mismatched.
22. **Violence Shortcut**: Physical confrontation substitutes for unresolved relational, moral, or psychological conflict. The fight replaces the harder scene.
23. **Cool-Kill Drift**: Spectacle emphasis suppresses the ethical or emotional meaning the narrative's own logic demands. Tonal mismatch between event and presentation.
24. **Aftermath Null**: Major force events produce no durable emotional, social, or thematic residue.
25. **Conversion Failure**: Scene is vivid and well-choreographed but does not change decision logic in later scenes. Apply removal test: nothing downstream changes.

### 2.2 Where these cluster

1. **Bridge scenes after major fights**: Aftermath Null, Rubber-Band Injury, Relationship Reset.
2. **Training/progression sequences**: Godmode Drift, Combat Monotone, Scale-Only Escalation.
3. **Climax runups**: And-Then Sequencing, White-Room Collision, Pacing-Significance Inversion.
4. **Aftermath chapters**: Collateral Erasure, Aftermath Null, Trauma Erasure.
5. **Multi-opponent encounters**: Teleport Bodies, Actor Chain Blur, Threat Collapse, Disembodied Combatant.
6. **Mid-book action set pieces**: Violence Shortcut, Cool-Kill Drift, Conversion Failure.

### 2.3 Structural isomorphism

**Action that does not alter agency, cost, or meaning is to force scenes what ornamental prose is to literary craft: movement without transformation.**

### 2.4 False positives to control

1. Sparse prose is not automatically illegible. Compression can maintain all six channels.
2. High competence is not automatically no-stakes. Competent characters face environmental, informational, moral, or temporal risk.
3. Quiet aftermath is not automatically consequence failure. Suppressed response can be the consequence.
4. Stylized repetition is not automatically monotony. Ritual, trance, or dissociative pattern can be the meaning.
5. Non-explicit violence can carry high force architecture load. Off-page events can convert powerfully.
6. Abstract combat is not automatically incoherent. Summary narration can maintain causality and conversion.
7. POV withdrawal is not automatically Disembodied Combatant. Dissociation, shock, or trained-response narration can *be* the cognitive anchor.

### Diagnostic takeaways (Q2)

1. Name failure patterns by mechanism, not taste.
2. Diagnose distribution across manuscript, not isolated annoyance.
3. Track persistence and conversion as primary anti-spectacle controls.
4. Use false-positive checks before assigning systemic failure.
5. The Disembodied Combatant is a force-specific failure other audits cannot catch.

## 3) Positive Cases

Strong force architecture varies by mode but shares core properties: legibility, risk, cost, kind-shift escalation, and conversion. This section extracts specific technique patterns.

### 3.1 Military / war fiction

O'Brien, Haldeman, Cook.

1. **O'Brien's truth-by-contradiction**: Force events narrated, revised, re-narrated. The instability of the account *is* the psychological consequence.
2. **Haldeman's temporal estrangement**: Combat competence becomes obsolete across deployments. Meaning generated through the gap between what the character knows and what the situation requires.
3. **Cook's chronicler distance**: Professional detachment that itself becomes the horror. What the prose doesn't react to reveals what sustained exposure has cost.

Shared: tactical pressure tied to uncertainty and logistics, unit cohesion as independent driver, psychological load carried forward, violence altering the narrator's moral language and memory reliability.

### 3.2 Systems-driven progression action

Dinniman, Wight.

1. **Dinniman's environmental comedy-as-friction**: The system generates absurd constraints that function as genuine tactical friction. Humor and lethality coexist through internally consistent system logic.
2. **Wight's advancement-cost coupling**: Each power level unlocks capability but introduces new vulnerability categories. Progression restructures the risk profile, not merely accumulates advantage.

Shared: progression readable at scene level, combat expressing character identity, challenge requiring strategy shift not stat inflation, costs surviving level-up momentum.

### 3.3 Thriller action

Child, Ludlum.

1. **Child's preparation-as-suspense**: Competence established; suspense engine is the gap between what the reader knows the character can do and the constraints on doing it. Environmental information does the work that capability uncertainty does in other modes.
2. **Ludlum's identity-through-combat**: Force events are revelatory—each fight provides character information. The body knows before the mind does.

Shared: resource depletion and clock pressure per encounter, competence balanced by information limits, reversals causally seeded, post-action consequences altering plan architecture.

### 3.4 Horror violence

King, Jones.

1. **King's domestic-space contamination**: Violence transforms the safe space into the threat. Force events deepen rather than discharge dread because they prove the environment has turned.
2. **Jones's cultural-weight violence**: Force events carry historical harm. Each violent moment is both immediate threat and ancestral echo—force architecture layered physical, psychological, cultural.

Shared: force deepening dread rather than discharging it, bodily harm tied to identity and relational rupture, aftermath producing altered reality/trust perception, escalation in implication rather than gore amplitude.

### 3.5 Crime/noir violence

Hammett, Chandler, Ellroy.

1. **Hammett's consequence-networking**: Violence metastasizes. Each force event produces more enemies, more compromised positions, more entanglement. Competence accelerates the problem.
2. **Ellroy's institutional complicity**: Violence embedded in power structures. Individual force events expose systemic corruption rather than resolving conflict.

Shared: violence embedded in corruption networks, consequence through legal/social repercussion, force exposing moral compromise rather than confirming moral clarity, reputation and fear effects persisting.

### 3.6 Domestic violence drama

Coercive-control narratives, literary/domestic thrillers.

1. **Micro-escalation architecture**: Force events are boundary points in a pattern of escalating control. Diagnostic significance in the pattern, not the peak.
2. **Credibility asymmetry**: The perpetrator's public presentation and survivor's experience create an information gap that is itself violence. The survivor's intelligence in navigating the situation is structural, not optional.

Shared: micro-escalation and coercive pattern tracking, social masking and credibility asymmetry, force linked to agency erosion, recovery as structural arc not epilogue cleanup.

### 3.7 Literary violence

McCarthy, Morrison, O'Connor.

1. **McCarthy's violence as epistemological event**: Violence is not plot device but argument about what the world is. The reader's capacity to assign meaning to violence is part of what the text interrogates.
2. **Morrison's violence as historical testimony**: Force events carry the weight of unnarratable historical harm. Fragmented, recursive structure performs the impossibility of adequate representation.
3. **O'Connor's revelatory violence**: Force events produce moments of unwilled recognition. Violence serves as the mechanism of grace or epiphany the character could not achieve through intention.

Shared: force as recognition engine, image systems that mutate after violence, sustained moral consequence, language and structure carrying aftermath residue.

### 3.8 Superhero/speculative combat

High-power speculative with consequence tracking.

1. **Power-scale clarity**: Reader must understand relative capability for force events to generate suspense. Without legible power framework, outcomes feel arbitrary.
2. **Collateral consequence architecture**: Environment and bystanders absorb real cost from high-power conflict. When cities rebuild between encounters and civilians are scenery, force events are weightless regardless of spectacle.

Shared: power-scale clarity and rule consistency, collateral tracked across arcs, moral choice surviving spectacle, escalation in ethical complexity not power magnitude.

### Diagnostic takeaways (Q3)

1. Strong force scenes are transformation points, not ornaments.
2. Technique extraction is more useful than exemplar citation.
3. Escalation succeeds when conflict changes in kind, not only scale.
4. The best force scenes convert mechanics into character revelation.
5. Aftermath quality is as important as in-scene choreography.

## 4) Mode Calibration

### 4.1 Military / war

- **Promise**: tactical coherence, operational uncertainty, unit/psychological consequence.
- **Named failure mode**: **Map-Table Heroics** (omniscient strategic clarity replaces fog-of-war experience).
- **Tighten on**: chain-of-command logic, fog/friction effects, casualty persistence, unit dynamics, tension between individual morality and orders.
- **Loosen on**: exhaustive weapon/equipment detail if strategic causality is clear.

### 4.2 Systems-driven progression action

- **Promise**: readable progression pressure, combat-as-character-expression, challenge requiring adaptation.
- **Named failure mode**: **Stat-Sheet Spectacle** (mechanical escalation without strategic or ethical shift).
- **Tighten on**: power-system consistency, kind-shift challenge design, capability constraints surviving progression, cost-to-advancement exchange rate. Antagonists must be intelligent and tactically disciplined.
- **Loosen on**: pure physiological realism if internal system logic is consistent and cost-bearing.

### 4.3 Thriller action

- **Promise**: urgency, resource strain, competence under uncertain information.
- **Named failure mode**: **Perpetual Chase Blur** (continuous motion without accumulating consequence or evolving stakes).
- **Tighten on**: clock consequence, resource accounting, environmental improvisation, causally seeded reversals.
- **Loosen on**: full tactical decomposition when momentum is contract-central; long-term psychological rumination that would stall forward drive.

### 4.4 Horror violence

- **Promise**: force events that deepen dread and violation consequence.
- **Named failure mode**: **Shock Carousel** (rotating gore moments that discharge rather than build dread).
- **Tighten on**: aftermath residue, identity cost, threat-logic progression, dread accumulation, permanence of spiritual and emotional wounds.
- **Loosen on**: strict tactical realism when symbolic/dread function is coherent; protagonist tactical failure can be structural necessity, not flaw.

### 4.5 Crime/noir violence

- **Promise**: violence under social corruption, moral compromise, and institutional complicity.
- **Named failure mode**: **Clean Hit Myth** (violence resolves cleanly without social/legal/moral fallout).
- **Tighten on**: messiness of close-in violence, legal/social repercussion, reputation dynamics, ethical consequence, institutional entanglement.
- **Loosen on**: black-and-white morality; protagonist operating outside the law is genre-normative if consequences track.

### 4.6 Domestic violence drama

- **Promise**: patterned coercion, escalation realism, durable agency effects.
- **Named failure mode**: **Incident Theater** (domestic violence as isolated dramatic incidents rather than pattern system).
- **Tighten on**: control-pattern continuity, isolation/dependency mechanics, credibility asymmetry, survivor intelligence and active agency, recovery burden as structural.
- **Loosen on**: overt physical-event frequency; high load can be psychological/coercive without physical climax. Kinetic choreography is secondary to betrayal-of-intimacy architecture.

### 4.7 Literary violence

- **Promise**: force as ethical and perceptual transformation, violence as recognition engine.
- **Named failure mode**: **Aphoristic Carnage** (violence aestheticized into philosophical set pieces without behavioral/relational residue).
- **Tighten on**: conversion to recognition, residue in language/structure, moral pressure persistence, image-system mutation post-violence, alignment with overarching thematic thesis.
- **Loosen on**: explicit tactical granularity and fast pacing; literary mode may demand extensive psychological processing and abstracted or slowed delivery.

### 4.8 Superhero/speculative combat

- **Promise**: scaled power conflict with coherent consequence architecture and moral complexity.
- **Named failure mode**: **City-Scale Weightlessness** (catastrophic collateral with no narrative, social, or moral accounting).
- **Tighten on**: collateral accounting, rule consistency at power ceiling, moral tradeoff visibility, power-scale legibility, consequence of secret-identity and public-trust burdens.
- **Loosen on**: conventional physics and human biological limits where genre contract is explicitly stylized; internal power-system consistency replaces thermodynamic realism.

### Diagnostic takeaways (Q4)

1. Delivery mode changes what "good enough" means for each channel.
2. Tighten/loosen rules prevent false positives across genres.
3. Every mode has a spectacle-success / meaning-failure trap.
4. Calibration should be explicit in audit output.

## 5) Distinguish Problem

### 5.1 Core distinction

The audit must classify intention and execution separately. A scene can be intentionally sparse and succeed. A scene can be intentionally chaotic and fail. A scene can be accidentally effective. A scene can be intentionally ambitious and partially unstable.

### 5.2 Required decision tests

1. **Reconstructability Test**: Can a careful reader reconstruct the force event's spatial and causal chain? (Anchored in Hayakawa's Ladder: intentional abstraction that maintains reconstructability passes; confusion that cannot resolve does not.)
2. **Constraint Consistency Test**: Do outcomes respect established capability, resource, and environmental constraints?
3. **Risk Reality Test**: Does competence coexist with meaningful downside? If physical risk is nullified, do environmental, informational, temporal, or moral threats remain?
4. **Persistence Test**: Do physical, psychological, and relational costs alter later behavior? Specific behavioral evidence required.
5. **Kind-Shift Test**: Does escalation introduce new strategic or ethical problems?
6. **Conversion Test**: Does force event output alter character position, relationship structure, or theme pressure? Apply removal test.
7. **Cognitive Anchor Test**: Does the force event maintain a legible POV position—someone perceiving, filtering, and responding? (Dissociation, shock, or trained-response narration can *be* the anchor.)

### 5.3 Classification outcomes

1. **Intentional and Successful**: Stylization is legible, constrained, and conversion-bearing. No remediation needed.
2. **Intentional but Unstable**: Strategy visible but 1-2 channels collapse under load. Targeted remediation on failing channels.
3. **Ambiguous/Developmental**: Pattern could reflect intentional choice or unrecognized habit. Evidence mixed. Flag with specific tests for author evaluation.
4. **Accidental Failure**: No intentional strategy detectable. Multiple test failures. Full remediation recommended.

### 5.4 Common misclassifications to avoid

1. Sparse ≠ illegible (may pass Reconstructability).
2. Competent ≠ stakes-free (may pass Risk Reality through non-physical vectors).
3. Stylized ≠ gratuitous (may pass Conversion through thematic function).
4. Quiet ≠ cost-free (suppressed response can be the persistence evidence).
5. Abstract ≠ incoherent (summary narration can maintain causality).
6. Withdrawn POV ≠ disembodied (dissociation can be the cognitive anchor).
7. Protagonist survival ≠ plot armor (may be justified by established rules/resources).

### Diagnostic takeaways (Q5)

1. Distinguish decisions are test-based, not impression-based.
2. Intentionality never exempts conversion and persistence requirements.
3. The four-class system prevents binary overcalls.
4. Failure assignment should reference at least two channels failing simultaneously.

## Design Conclusions for the Audit Draft

The production audit should include:

1. strict non-duplication boundaries with Scene Turn, Emotional Craft, Horror Craft, Mystery/Thriller Architecture, and Character Architecture,
2. force centrality profile (low/medium/high burden) as pre-assessment calibration,
3. six-channel force delivery framework (LG, TC, CR, CP, ES, MC),
4. 25 named diagnostic flags across six families (LG-5, TC-3, CR-4, CP-5, ES-4, MC-4),
5. Force Event Ledger + Consequence Continuity Ledger + Escalation Ladder Map + recommended Legibility Snapshot Grid,
6. mode calibration matrix with 8 named failure modes,
7. seven-test distinguish framework with four-class decision matrix,
8. hard severity gates tied to climax legibility, consequence persistence, and meaning conversion,
9. pattern interpretation protocol with diagnostic combinations and special caution zones,
10. explicit audit procedure (claim lock → build artifacts → rate channels → apply flags → synthesize),
11. output template for standardized reporting,
12. firewall compliance (diagnostic only; no rewriting).

Anchor line:

**Force fails when event intensity rises faster than consequence and meaning conversion.**
