## §1. Purpose

This audit evaluates whether a manuscript's stakes system functions as a coherent escalation and consequence engine.

It is not an "intensity meter."
It is not a "bigger stakes are always better" doctrine.

### Core problem

The core failure is **stakes detachment**.

A manuscript can repeatedly signal risk,
urgency,
and danger without converting those signals into:

1. visible consequence,
2. escalating pressure,
3. narrowing options,
4. meaningful climax load.

**The fundamental question: Do stakes escalate and convert into consequential pressure, or are they stated without structural force?**

**Deficit-First Diagnostic Rule:** Do not evaluate stakes by confirming that risk, urgency, or danger are signaled on the page. You must hunt for the *absence* of conversion — the *absence* of visible consequence, the *absence* of narrowing options, and the *absence* of cost that persists after the threat passes. A scene that signals peril but pays no consequence back into the narrative is not a high-stakes scene, however charged its prose. You are auditing for stakes detachment, not validating tense atmosphere.

### Non-duplication boundaries

1. **Pass 1 Reader Experience** tells you where readers disengage. This audit explains whether stakes architecture caused that disengagement.
2. **Pass 3 Rhythm and Modulation** measures pacing shape. This audit measures risk/consequence shape.
3. **Character Architecture** diagnoses who characters are and what they want. This audit diagnoses what those wants cost under pressure.
4. **Decision Pressure Audit** tests whether choices are believable. This audit tests whether the pressure field behind those choices is structurally real.
5. **Emotional Craft** checks feeling transmission. This audit checks risk-consequence transmission. A consequence can manifest (Stakes passes) and fail to transmit affect (Emotional Craft fails).
6. **Plot Architecture** checks macro escalation logic. This audit checks whether *stakes specifically* (not just events) escalate across narrative time.

### Activation conditions

Activate when either:

1. user explicitly requests stakes analysis,

or

2. at least 2 of the following are true:
   - beta readers report "I didn't feel what was at stake" or "I don't care what happens,"
   - pacing feels flat despite frequent conflict events,
   - climax feels smaller than build-up,
   - stakes language appears high but consequence feels low,
   - manuscript relies on one repetitive risk type,
   - character maintains high-stakes language while actually facing static pressure,
   - multiple stated risks never manifest as concrete cost.

### Inputs required

1. Contract and genre calibration outputs.
2. Pass 1 Reader Experience findings.
3. Pass 2 Structural Mapping findings.
4. Pass 3 Rhythm/Modulation findings (if available).
5. Pass 5 Character Architecture findings.
6. Optional Emotional Craft and Decision Pressure findings.
7. Optional mode modules (Thriller, Mystery, Romance, Horror, Literary, SFF, Cozy, Satire).

### Outputs produced

1. Stakes Ladder Map.
2. Stakes Axis Distribution Table.
3. Claim-to-Payment Ledger.
4. Climax Stakes Reconciliation Grid.
5. Flag inventory with severity/frequency/blast radius.

8. Pass 11 readiness-impact handoff.

## §2. Stakes Delivery Framework

### 2.1 Core channels

Assess six channels:

1. `STX` Stakes Type Breadth
2. `PC` Personal Coupling
3. `IM` Immediacy Gradient
4. `EG` Escalation Geometry
5. `MP` Manifestation and Payment
6. `CL` Climax Load Integrity

### 2.2 Channel ratings

| Rating | Operational meaning |
|---|---|
| **Integrated** | Channel reliably creates escalating pressure and consequence architecture under narrative load |
| **Partial** | Channel works in pockets but collapses in high-load sections or final act |
| **Detached** | Channel appears as surface signal with weak structural effect; stakes stated but pressure not manifested |

### 2.3 Stakes centrality profile

Before assigning severity,
classify stakes burden:

1. **Low-stakes centrality**
   - few high-risk events,
   - intentional smallness,
   - high craft demand per stakes event,
   - strong coupling and payment required.
2. **Medium-stakes centrality**
   - recurring risk layers,
   - escalation and payment must be consistent,
   - typical thriller/mystery/romance profile.
3. **High-stakes centrality**
   - stakes are primary propulsion engine,
   - constant escalation pressure,
   - plateaus/regressions are high-impact defects.

### 2.4 Quick diagnostic statement

**Stakes architecture fails when risk language rises faster than consequence load.**

### 2.5 Channel definitions

#### A) STX: Stakes Type Breadth

**Question:** Are multiple risk axes active where the contract requires them?

Primary axes:

1. external (life, freedom, resources),
2. relational (attachment, trust, belonging),
3. identity (self-concept, integrity),
4. moral (ethical self-concept, complicity),
5. bodily autonomy (where relevant).

**Integrated when:**

1. manuscript establishes 2-3+ consequence types where contract promises layered stakes,
2. each type is visible and trackable,
3. stakes compound rather than merely accumulate (second axis tightens as first axis pressures),
4. by climax, character cannot escape one stakes type without colliding with another,
5. axis selection fits mode and contract.

**Detached when:**

1. stakes depend entirely on one consequence axis,
2. secondary stakes are asserted but remain background,
3. character can potentially satisfy one stakes type while abandoning others,
4. stakes appear to multiply without actually compounding,
5. axis changes are rhetorical rather than consequential.

#### B) PC: Personal Coupling

**Question:** Are stakes bound to the protagonist's demonstrated values, desires, and vulnerabilities?

**Integrated when:**

1. stakes target character-specific fears or commitments reader has witnessed,
2. escalating stakes force character to choose between conflicting commitments,
3. stakes pressure is felt as personal even when external threat is impersonal,
4. character's position tightens because character *cares*, not only because exterior danger rises,
5. walk-away vulnerability escalates: early character could theoretically exit; late character cannot exit without abandoning something they cannot abandon.

**Detached when:**

1. stakes are generalized external threats with no relational anchor,
2. character is interchangeable; different protagonist would face same stakes identically,
3. character's values or relationships never bridge to stakes pressure,
4. stakes feel imposed rather than rooted in character's specific position,
5. protagonist could withdraw with minimal personal change.

#### C) IM: Immediacy Gradient

**Question:** Do stakes move from abstract/looming to concrete/immediate?

**Integrated when:**

1. early stakes are temporally distant or low-specificity threats,
2. temporal/material proximity tightens across major units,
3. late narrative creates real-time or near-real-time threat immediacy,
4. escalation is felt as urgency increases—reader's threat-appraisal changes as time window shrinks.

**Detached when:**

1. stakes remain temporally vague throughout,
2. urgency language escalates without timeline compression,
3. threats remain abstract near climax,
4. immediacy appears only in language and vanishes between set pieces.

#### D) EG: Escalation Geometry

**Question:** Do stakes evolve in kind, not only in intensity language?

**Integrated when:**

1. character's position tightens across major units (options narrow, cost for misstep rises, error margin shrinks),
2. escalation is structural: earlier choices lock character into higher-stakes positions,
3. midpoint stakes are visibly higher than act-one stakes; climax stakes exceed midpoint,
4. later units carry heavier or transformed consequence load,
5. escalation includes kind-shifts (not only "more of same risk").

**Detached when:**

1. intensity language grows but character's actual situation remains structurally stable,
2. stakes plateau in middle narrative or regress unexpectedly,
3. escalation announces itself without architecture supporting the claim,
4. character maintains equivalent options or escape routes across acts despite escalating language,
5. consequences remain same category and magnitude.

#### E) MP: Manifestation and Payment

**Question:** Are claimed stakes shown through consequence and visible cost?

**Integrated when:**

1. stakes are not only asserted but manifested in character behavior, environment damage, or material loss,
2. consequences are concrete (physical, relational, resource, social, psychological) not only thematic,
3. cost accumulates: early stakes payment makes later escalation harder,
4. reader never needs exposition to understand what the manuscript claims as stakes—reader *sees* stakes taking price,
5. consequences persist beyond immediate scene and change future pressure.

**Detached when:**

1. stakes are stated and never shown taking cost,
2. consequences are announced or implied but reader doesn't witness manifestation,
3. character declares "everything is at stake" but survives major events with minimal damage,
4. cost is rhetorical without structural proof,
5. costs reset quickly.

#### F) CL: Climax Load Integrity

**Question:** Does climax carry the manuscript's earned stakes burden?

**Integrated when:**

1. climax stakes are structurally higher than midpoint stakes,
2. climax pays off or transforms the escalating pressures the manuscript has built,
3. when multiple stakes axes converge, climax manages the convergence as unified pressure,
4. climax consequence is proportionate to build-up; reader feels escalation culminates rather than resets,
5. key risk axes reconcile at payoff.

**Detached when:**

1. climax stakes are lower than or equal to midpoint stakes without intentional anti-climax design,
2. climax avoids or sidesteps the primary escalated stakes,
3. major stakes threads are resolved before climax or abandoned at climax,
4. climax feels like a departure from stakes architecture rather than its culmination,
5. midpoint feels like real peak.

## §3. Named Diagnostic Flags

22 flags across six families.

Severity defaults: `Could-Fix` (local, low blast radius), `Should-Fix` (repeated, multi-scene), `Must-Fix` (systemic, climax-critical, or contract-breaking).

Blast radius tags: `Local`, `Multi-scene`, `Systemic`.

### STX — Stakes Type (3 flags)

**STX-1: Single-Rail Stakes**
Stakes depend entirely on one consequence axis. Character must defeat antagonist, or protect relationship, or avoid exposure—but only one of these truly escalates.
*Detect when*: one risk type dominates >80% of major pressure beats; removing one stakes type would collapse the entire pressure field; secondary stakes are window-dressing.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix` (upgrade to `Must-Fix` if contract promises multi-axis stakes).

**STX-2: Axis Drift**
Stakes axis shifts without causal bridge. Dominant risk axis changes abruptly between units without enacted transition; prior axis is abandoned without payment.
*Detect when*: dominant risk axis changes between major units without causal connection; transition is announced but not dramatized; prior axis disappears without consequence.
*Default severity*: `Could-Fix` (upgrade to `Should-Fix` by frequency).

**STX-3: Phantom Axis**
A risk axis is promised early but never becomes operational. Setup names specific risk domain, but ladder/ledger shows minimal or no manifestation. Climax omits that axis entirely.
*Detect when*: setup establishes a risk domain; claim-to-payment ledger shows no payment for that domain; climax resolution ignores it.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`.

### PC — Personal Coupling (4 flags)

**PC-1: Borrowed Stakes**
Manuscript imports external stakes without tying them to protagonist's personal vulnerability. Character is motivated by plot necessity, not personal coupling. Same plot could run unchanged with generic lead.
*Detect when*: protagonist could withdraw with minimal personal change; manuscript asserts importance without specific personal consequence; character's stake in the outcome is unclear.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`.

**PC-2: Walk-Away Vulnerability Deficit**
Character could plausibly exit the stakes situation at any point. No architectural mechanism—values, commitments, relationships, entanglements—binds them. Reader wonders why the character is still engaged.
*Detect when*: character stays in danger because the plot requires it; there is no scene or structural mechanism showing why exit is impossible or prohibitively costly; character has options that would reduce pressure but doesn't take them without established reason.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix` (upgrade to `Must-Fix` if sustained through final act).

**PC-3: Substitution Flaw**
Protagonist is interchangeable with any other character. The specific character's values, history, relationships, and vulnerabilities do not determine the stakes. Stakes are entirely plot-driven.
*Detect when*: swapping the protagonist for a generic character of similar capability would not change the stakes structure; character's unique psychology does not shape which stakes register as pressure; personal history is disconnected from consequence domains.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`.

**PC-4: Orphaned Identity Stakes**
Manuscript asserts stakes to character's identity, values, or meaning without establishing clear character commitments in earlier narrative. Reader doesn't understand what identity is threatened.
*Detect when*: climax reveals character was fighting for their identity/values, but early narrative gave no indication what those values were; character's core commitment is unclear until the moment stakes are applied.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`.

### IM — Immediacy (3 flags)

**IM-1: Temporal Fog**
Stakes timeline remains vague throughout early-to-middle narrative. Reader cannot construct a threat-arrival model. Danger exists but when it will manifest is undefined.
*Detect when*: threat is described as "coming" without specificity; character references timeline without reader understanding it; stakes pressure builds without temporal urgency structure.
*Default severity*: `Could-Fix` (upgrade to `Should-Fix` if contract promises ticking-clock tension).

**IM-2: Urgency Without Clock**
Intensity language escalates but temporal window does not compress. Time pressure is rhetorical, not architectural.
*Detect when*: character declares increasing urgency while timeline remains stable or unknown; prose tone accelerates without objective time shrinking; deadlines appear but do not materially tighten.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`.

**IM-3: Urgency Leak**
Immediate stakes are established, then repeatedly suspended without cause. Declared urgency disappears in intervening scenes; time/resource constraints stop shaping decisions; re-escalation requires repeated restatement.
*Detect when*: urgent threat is established then ignored for multiple scenes; character behaves as if time pressure paused; urgency must be re-declared to restore pressure.
*Default severity*: `Could-Fix` (upgrade to `Should-Fix` by frequency).

### EG — Escalation Geometry (4 flags)

**EG-1: Peak Prematurity**
Stakes reach their highest structural load before the climax. The most intense consequences, narrowest options, and most irreversible pressure all arrive at midpoint or late-middle narrative. Everything after is denouement dressed as climax.
*Detect when*: character's options and consequence-level are equivalent or higher at midpoint than at climax; stakes ladder shows peak before final act; reader feels story peaked early.
*Default severity*: `Must-Fix`.

**EG-2: Escalation Mirage**
Language of stakes grows more intense across narrative, but character's actual position—options, resources, threat proximity—remains structurally constant.
*Detect when*: "the stakes have never been higher" appears while character's capability and access to solutions remain unchanged; intensity language escalates while consequences don't compound; no new irreversible pressure appears.
*Default severity*: `Must-Fix`.

**EG-3: Regression Pocket**
Stakes escalate to a peak, then character receives unexpected aid, luck, or solution that materially lowers pressure. Reader cannot track escalation when stakes unexpectedly reverse.
*Detect when*: character is cornered then finds unanticipated resource; escape route appears without prior seeding; clear pressure drop appears before climax.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix` (upgrade to `Must-Fix` if repeated or climax-impacting).

**EG-4: Plateau Strip**
Escalation reaches peak in middle narrative, then holds flat through final act. Character's position equilibrium stabilizes. Maximum pressure is felt at midpoint, not climax.
*Detect when*: ladder shows no meaningful increase or transformation across 2+ major units; scene conflict repeats same consequence load; new stakes don't introduce greater pressure.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix` (downgrade if cyclical/thematic design is intentional and successful).

### MP — Manifestation and Payment (3 flags)

**MP-1: Stated-Not-Shown Risk**
Consequences are asserted as speech act or internal monologue without manifestation. Character declares "if I fail, X happens," but reader never sees X take cost on page.
*Detect when*: claim-to-payment ledger shows repeated long gaps; character narrates stakes frequently but actual consequences remain abstract; risk terms appear without cost scenes.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`.

**MP-2: Cost Amnesia**
Character experiences consequences early narrative, but later narrative ignores the cost and treats character as unaffected. Consequence is paid in one scene and forgotten by the next major unit.
*Detect when*: character suffers injury, relational rupture, or resource loss, then subsequent scenes show no behavioral or capability change; paid costs vanish in next units; manuscript resets pressure repeatedly.
*Default severity*: `Must-Fix` when systemic.

**MP-3: Consequence Evasion**
Manuscript creates setup for consequences but character avoids them through action, luck, or author intervention. Stakes are tested but not paid.
*Detect when*: severe risk resolves via unearned relief; promised consequence is softened without proportional cause; narrative repeatedly protects characters from announced costs.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix` (upgrade to `Must-Fix` at climax).

### CL — Climax Load (5 flags)

**CL-1: Climax Underload**
Climax stakes are lower in load or consequence than stakes character faced in middle narrative. Final confrontation feels smaller than earlier pressures.
*Detect when*: reconciliation grid shows reduced axis load at climax; character's position at climax is actually looser than at midpoint; climax consequences are narrower than setup implied.
*Default severity*: `Must-Fix` (unless anti-climax as deliberate form).

**CL-2: Payoff Misalignment**
Climax resolves one stakes type but leaves primary escalated stakes unaddressed. Manuscript's escalation trajectory doesn't match climax focus.
*Detect when*: character has been escalating around X goal but climax revolves around Y goal; key promised stakes axes remain unpaid; primary threat is resolved off-page.
*Default severity*: `Must-Fix`.

**CL-3: Axis Collapse at Climax**
When multiple stakes axes converge at climax, they're handled as separate problems rather than unified pressure. Character resolves one axis, then another, rather than facing convergent bind.
*Detect when*: climax scenes separate stakes resolution by geography or time; character satisfies relational stakes separately from safety stakes; prior layered consequences are flattened to single-axis resolution.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix` (upgrade to `Must-Fix` if convergence was promised).

**CL-4: Climax Authority Collapse**
Climactic stakes depend on threat credibility that the manuscript hasn't established. Reader doesn't believe the threat is real because narrative hasn't built threat authority.
*Detect when*: antagonist threatens stakes reader believes antagonist won't actually enforce; consequence is announced but narrative signals it won't manifest; threat-credibility is low relative to stakes claims.
*Default severity*: `Must-Fix`.

**CL-5: Climax Consequence Evaporation**
Climax threatens major consequence that doesn't materialize. Character faces the narrative's highest-stated stakes and survives with minimal or no lasting damage.
*Detect when*: climax builds to maximum threat then resolves without proportionate cost; character emerges from climax functionally unchanged; resolution feels unearned relative to build-up.
*Default severity*: `Must-Fix`.

## §4. Tracking Artifacts

### 4.1 Artifact A: Stakes Ladder Map (required)

| Major Unit | Dominant Risk Axis | Secondary Axis | Immediacy | Trajectory Shift | Cost Paid | Manifestation Clarity | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Rules:

1. Map by act or equivalent major structural unit.
2. "Trajectory shift" must be one of: increase, transform, cycle, plateau, regress.
3. Mark any unit with plateau/regress for threshold checks.
4. If Trajectory Shift is "plateau" or "regress," flag as candidate EG-4 or EG-3.
5. Cost Paid: what does character concretely lose or commit at this unit's climax?

### 4.2 Artifact B: Stakes Axis Distribution Table (required)

| Risk Axis | Frequency (# scenes) | First Appearance | Peak Intensity Location | Personal Coupling Quality | Manifestation Count | Persistence | Over/Underused |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Rules:

1. Axes: External / Relational / Identity / Moral / Bodily Autonomy / Other.
2. Personal Coupling Quality: does this axis connect to protagonist's demonstrated values? (High/Medium/Low)
3. Manifestation Count: how many times does this axis actually cost something on page?
4. Persistence: does cost from this axis carry into subsequent units?
5. Single axis dominating is candidate STX-1. Promised axis absent is candidate STX-3.
6. Compare to contract and mode expectations before flagging.

### 4.3 Artifact C: Claim-to-Payment Ledger (required)

| Claimed Risk | Claim Location | First Concrete Manifestation | Payment Quality | Gap Length | Persistence |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Payment quality:

1. none,
2. weak,
3. adequate,
4. strong.

Rules:

1. Track stakes claimed in character speech, thought, or narrator assertion.
2. Manifestation = first time reader sees the risk actually take cost (behavior, loss, damage, changed relationship).
3. Gap Length: how many scenes between claim and payment?
4. If Gap Length is more than 20 scenes or manifestation never occurs, flag candidate MP-1 or CL-2.
5. Track whether payment persists beyond scene.
6. Any major risk claim with no manifestation by climax is candidate MP-1 or CL-2.

### 4.4 Artifact D: Climax Stakes Reconciliation Grid (required)

| Stakes Axis | Build Path | Climax Expression | Resolved / Transformed / Abandoned | Consequence Manifestation | Integrity Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Rules:

1. List the 3-5 stakes axes the manuscript escalated throughout narrative.
2. How does climax engage each?
3. Resolved = climax pays the stakes. Transformed = climax changes what stakes mean. Abandoned = climax ignores this stakes thread.
4. "Abandoned" requires explicit justification; otherwise flag CL-2.
5. Consequence Manifestation: does reader see the stakes taking consequence, or is it narrated?
6. Integrity Verdict: Does climax feel like culmination or departure?
7. This artifact determines hard-gate checks.

## §5. Distinguish Framework

### 5.1 Required tests

1. **Scale Fit Test**: Does stakes size match declared mode and contract?

2. **Manifestation Test**: Can reader specify what character concretely stands to lose at each major story beat, in sequence? (Intentional modest stakes still pass if losses are specific, if small.)

3. **Escalation Test**: Does each major unit force character into higher-consequence position than previous unit? (Cyclical design passes if each cycle reveals new depth.)

4. **Coupling Test**: Are stakes bound to protagonist-specific vulnerabilities or commitments? Does walk-away impossibility escalate?

5. **Payment Test**: Is claimed risk paid by concrete cost or irreversible shift in character's position?

6. **Climax Integrity Test**: Does climax carry the manuscript's highest-resolved stakes load? (Anti-climax as design passes if the low-stakes climax answers the narrative's central question.)

### 5.2 Classification matrix

| Test Profile | Classification | Editorial Action |
|---|---|---|
| All tests pass; low-stakes manuscripts show intentional scale calibration | **Intentional and Successful** | Preserve architecture; note technique; validate against contract |
| Intentional strategy visible; 1-2 channels unstable | **Intentional but Unstable** | Targeted revision on unstable channels |
| 3-4 pass; pattern ambiguous; may indicate emerging craft | **Ambiguous/Developmental** | Flag with specific tests for author evaluation |
| Multiple failures; no stable pressure logic | **Accidental Failure** | Structural stakes recast required |

### 5.3 False-positive controls

1. **Modest stakes are not underpowered stakes** if consequence is concrete at the chosen scale and escalates within that scale.
2. **Cyclical stakes design is not stakes plateau** if each cycle provides new information about stakes or character's relationship to them.
3. **Delayed-stakes revelation is not vague setup** if textual evidence supports the shape of unrevealed stakes.
4. **Thematic/identity stakes are not abstraction** if grounded in character's demonstrated values and choices.
5. **Anti-climax as deliberate form is not payoff collapse** if the low-stakes climax answers the narrative's central operating question faithfully.
6. **Quiet consequence is not cost amnesia** if reader sees behavioral evidence that character carries the cost forward.
7. **Small stakes can pass** if coupling and payment are strong.
8. **Delayed manifestation can pass** if anticipation pressure is active and legible.

## §6. Mode Calibration Matrix

| Mode | Integration Promise | Named Failure Mode | Tighten On | Loosen On |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Thriller/Suspense** | Escalating external threat + narrowing options + real-time urgency | **Deadline Wallpaper**: high-stakes language + constant motion without escalation geometry; urgency without stakes compounding | Immediacy gradient, consequence accumulation, resource depletion, time window compression, decision irreversibility, regression checks, climax load | Velocity of action; exhaustive thematic articulation; dread intensity |
| **Mystery/Investigation** | Stakes through truth exposure + consequence of failure to solve | **Puzzle Without Cost**: investigation drives forward momentum while personal stakes remain abstract; solving becomes goal, consequence becomes secondary | Personal coupling to investigation outcome, consequence to each revelation, payment at reveal, investigative progress manufacturing escalating stakes | Continuous high-intensity threat; layering complexity |
| **Romance** | Emotional/relational stakes with attachment risk escalation | **Feelings Without Price**: emotional tone sustained at intensity without escalating emotional demand; same core fear throughout without tightening | Personal coupling, escalating emotional vulnerability requirement, relational payment persistence, convergence of personal and relational stakes, impossibility of avoiding climax choice | Frequency of intimate scenes; emotional vocabulary; external jeopardy scale |
| **Horror** | Dread + consequence accumulation + violation of boundary systems | **Dread Without Damage**: rotating threat types without binding them to escalating architecture; shock multiplies without stakes deepening | Escalation through embodiment, boundary-system violation progression, manifestation of damage in character state, threat-logic binding, manifestation and persistence, climax burden integrity | Intensity of individual scares; gore quantity; tactical realism detail |
| **Literary/Upmarket** | Existential/moral stakes made dramatically legible | **Elegant Stakes Fog**: theme is clear but character arc doesn't escalate the stakes; contradiction explored but character's forced position doesn't tighten | Manifestation clarity, narrowing character's available options for resolving thematic contradiction, irreversibility of identity consequence, relational consequence manifestation, climax reconciliation | Thematic sophistication; density of exploration; overt ticking-clock mechanics |
| **SFF Epic/Adventure** | Layered personal + systemic stakes across scale | **World in Peril, Hero Unmoved**: threat grows in scope without escalating character's personal consequence; character's role and position remain constant despite enlarged threat | Character's personal tightening as world-state escalates, personal coupling to systemic risk, resource depletion as challenge grows, magic/power cost escalation, escalation geometry | Scale of threat; epic scope language; strict one-axis progression |
| **Cozy/Low-Violence** | Bounded threat with high belonging/repair stakes | **Gentle Drift**: mystery solved but stakes never materialize in actual social cost; character maintains standing throughout | Community-standing consequence accumulation, relational stakes visibility, reputation-damage manifestation, belonging stakes tightening, repair consequence | Warmth of relationships; community acceptance baseline; high bodily danger requirements |
| **Satire/Comedy Hybrid** | Stakes preserved through tonal modulation; exposure risk escalation | **Joke Shield Stakes**: facade increasingly obvious but no relational consequence; character is exposed but no one cares or confronts; comedy masks absent consequences | Payment integrity, escalating difficulty of facade maintenance, relational consequence of exposure, decision consequences, climax load | Absurdity escalation; comedy density; solemn threat language density |

## §7. Severity Hard Gates

Apply these floors regardless of prose quality.

| Condition | Minimum Severity |
|---|---|
| Climax stakes load lower than or equal to midpoint load, no intentional anti-climax design | `Must-Fix` |
| Climax escalated stakes language without structural escalation in character's position | `Must-Fix` |
| Cost paid in one scene and contradicted by character's unchanged behavior in subsequent unit | `Must-Fix` when systemic |
| Multi-axis contract collapses to single-axis payoff | `Should-Fix` (upgrade to `Must-Fix` when it breaks promised reading experience) |
| Major stakes claims remain abstract across 2+ acts without manifestation | `Should-Fix` (upgrade to `Must-Fix` if contract promises high-stakes intensity) |
| External stakes unlinked to personal consequence in core arc | `Should-Fix` |
| Repeated stakes regression across major units | `Should-Fix` (upgrade per contract burden) |
| Repeated plateau/regression spans 2+ major units in high-stakes manuscripts | `Should-Fix` |
| Single-axis stakes dependent on one consequence type across entire manuscript | `Should-Fix` (upgrade to `Must-Fix` if contract implies multi-axis architecture) |
| Manifestation gap longer than 20 scenes between claim and payment | `Should-Fix` |
| Consequences repeatedly paid then reset (cost amnesia) | `Must-Fix` when systemic |

## §8. Pattern Interpretation Protocol

### 8.1 Reading the flag map

When flags cluster, read the combination before prescribing.

1. **STX + PC flags — Shallow rooting.** Stakes are asserted but not anchored to character or personal vulnerability. Author knows what the external stakes are but hasn't bound them to character-specific reasons they matter. Revision priority: coupling repair before escalation architecture.

2. **IM + EG flags — Urgency without escalation.** Pressure language grows but structural tightening doesn't follow. Common in manuscripts where prose style escalates faster than plot architecture. Revision priority: escalation geometry.

3. **MP flags clustering — Manifestation failure.** Consequences are stated and never shown. Gap between authored stakes-intention and on-page visibility. Revision priority: artifact-level reconstruction of cost.

4. **CL flags + strong earlier channels — Climax conversion failure.** Stakes architecture works through middle narrative but climax doesn't culminate it. Often indicates climax was written for spectacle rather than escalation payoff. Revision priority: reconciliation grid and payoff redesign before line edits.

5. **EG-4 (Plateau) + strong middle escalation — Plateau in final act.** Character's position tightens through act two, then equilibrium establishes in act three. Reader feels maximum pressure at midpoint. Suggests author lost stakes architecture in final-act pacing. Revision priority: final-act stakes redesign.

6. **PC flags across all channels — Coupling failure throughout.** Stakes are external, abstract, and unbound to character psychology. Requires foundational coupling repair before escalation architecture can be built. Revision priority: character coupling is prerequisite.

7. **IM flags in non-thriller — Time-pressure misapplication.** Author is calibrating stakes to thriller immediacy when contract specifies other mode. May need mode recalibration rather than removal. Revision priority: mode recalibration.

8. **STX-3 (Phantom Axis) + CL-3 (Axis Collapse) — Convergence failure.** Multiple stakes axes don't compound; climax handles them separately or drops them. Indicates manuscript designed stakes in isolation rather than as integrated pressure system. Revision priority: axis integration.

9. **EG plateau + Pass 3 flat rhythm — Structural pressure stall.** Not just sentence-level pacing issue. Stakes architecture and rhythm architecture are failing together. Revision priority: structural before line.

10. **Strong stakes claims + weak Decision Pressure — Rhetorical pressure field.** Pressure field exists in language but choices are not constrained. Coordinate with Decision Pressure Audit. Revision priority: Decision Pressure drives.

11. **Strong stakes ladder + weak Emotional Craft — Transmission failure.** Architecture may be sound but affect transmission is failing. Revision priority: Emotional Craft drives.

12. **High external stakes + weak personal coupling — Plot importance without reader attachment.** Revision priority: coupling repair.

### 8.2 Special caution zones

1. **Emotional Craft overlap**: When stakes carry emotional transmission burden, both audits apply. Stakes Architecture evaluates whether consequences manifest; Emotional Craft evaluates whether consequences are *felt*. A consequence can manifest (Stakes passes) and fail to transmit affect (Emotional Craft fails).

2. **Decision Pressure Audit overlap**: When stakes create choice pressure, coordinate diagnostics. Stakes Audit evaluates pressure field; Decision Pressure evaluates choice plausibility within field. High stakes without plausible choice indicates coupling problem (PC) rather than decision-pressure failure.

3. **Character Architecture downstream**: Escalating stakes that force character into contradiction territory should cross-reference Character Architecture for motive coherence and agency arc.

4. **Plot Architecture upstream**: If escalation is missing, verify Plot Architecture isn't failing to manifest causal chains that would tighten stakes naturally.

5. **Mode module calibration**: Stakes in cozy feel like non-stakes in thriller. Before flagging low-stakes, verify mode-calibrated expectations are applied.

## §12. What This Audit Is Not

1. **Not a "bigger stakes are always better" doctrine.** Evaluates craft, not intensity. Modest well-executed stakes score higher than inflated poorly-architected stakes.
2. **Not a replacement for Decision Pressure Audit.** Diagnoses pressure field, not choice plausibility within that field.
3. **Not a genre-enforcement tool blind to mode calibration.** Cozy, literary, and satire modes calibrate stakes expectations differently.
4. **Not a demand for perpetual intensity escalation.** Cyclical design, delayed revelation, and thematic stakes are valid non-linear architectures.
5. **Not a prose rewrite mechanism.** Diagnoses architectural failure, not line-level intensity language.
6. **Not a plot invention directive.** Names where stakes fail, not what plot events should fix them.
7. **Not indifferent to intentionality.** False-positive controls distinguish intentional design from accidental failure.
8. **Not a proxy for prose quality.** A beautifully written manuscript can have detached stakes architecture.

## §13. Firewall Compliance

This audit remains diagnostic.

**Allowed:**

1. identifying stakes architecture failures,
2. classifying severity and blast radius,
3. specifying escalation scope categories,
4. recommending revision priorities by architecture level.

**Forbidden:**

1. inventing new plot events,
2. writing replacement scenes,
3. drafting emotional beats or dialogue,
4. mandating specific consequence types,
5. drafting replacement stakes sequences.

This audit says what stakes architecture breaks, where it breaks, why it breaks, and what revision scope is required. It does not write the manuscript for the author.
