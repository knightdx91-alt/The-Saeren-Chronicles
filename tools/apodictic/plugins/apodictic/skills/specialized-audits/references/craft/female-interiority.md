# Specialized Audit: Female Interiority
## Version 0.4.8
*Last Updated: February 2026*
*Synthesis: Three-model collaboration (Opus, Codex, Gemini)*

---

## Purpose

This audit evaluates whether female characters maintain specific, psychologically credible inner lives across all scene types — or whether their interiority thins, flattens, or disappears when the narrative needs them to function rather than experience.

**The core problem is not "male gaze." It is interiority thinning.** Male gaze is one mechanism — the most visible one — but the broader failure is a female character whose inner life appears and disappears based on what the narrative demands of her. She thinks during emotional scenes, becomes a body during action scenes, becomes a function during caregiving scenes, becomes a reaction during confrontation scenes. Her interiority is conditional rather than persistent.

The audit's fundamental question: **Does this character have a persistent inner life, or does her interiority appear and disappear based on what the narrative needs from her?**

**Deficit-First Diagnostic Rule:** Do not evaluate female interiority by validating scenes where she thinks about the plot. You must hunt for the *absence* of non-relational cognition, the *absence* of independent motive, and the *thinning* of interiority when male characters enter the scene. An active female character with no internal cost or independent friction is a failure. You are auditing for omission, not presence.

This is a craft audit, not an ideological checklist. Complex, flawed, even villainous female characters are fine. Characters whose interiority is deliberately thinned for thematic reasons are fine. The audit identifies *unintentional* thinning — places where the author stopped simulating the character's mind without knowing it.

**When to activate:**
- Any work featuring significant female characters (any genre, any author gender)
- Work where female perspective is central to the narrative
- Any time characterization concerns are raised about female characters
- Ensemble casts where gender-differential interiority may appear
- Work where female characters function in high-pressure scenes (action, professional, political)

---

## The Interiority Thinning Framework

Interiority is not binary. Characters don't simply "have" or "lack" inner lives. Interiority *thins* — becomes less specific, less surprising, less psychologically grounded — in predictable patterns. The audit tracks four levels:

| Level | What it looks like | Diagnostic signal |
|-------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Full interiority** | Character's inner life is specific, surprising, psychologically grounded. Her thoughts are *hers*, not generic "woman thoughts." She infers, predicts, withholds, reframes, chooses. | Internal verbs beyond emotion shorthand. Non-relational thought bandwidth. Distinctive conflict logic. |
| **Thinned interiority** | Inner life becomes generic, reactive, or predictable. She thinks what "a woman in this situation" would think rather than what *this specific woman* would think. | Emotion labels replace thought processes. Reactions track plot needs rather than character logic. Voice becomes indistinguishable from narrator. |
| **Functional interiority** | Character has just enough inner life to justify her actions, but no more. She exists to serve the scene. | Motives appear only when needed to explain behavior. No post-scene processing. No between-the-lines thinking in dialogue. |
| **Absent interiority** | Character is pure function: action-taker, reaction-generator, body-in-space. No inner life visible. | External description only. Character could be replaced by a lamp with a note on it (the Sexy Lamp test). |

### Where Thinning Happens

The key diagnostic insight: thinning is usually *contextual*. A female character may have full interiority in "her" scenes (domestic, emotional, relational) and thin dramatically in scenes coded differently (action, professional, intellectual, political). The pattern reveals what the author believes women's inner lives contain.

**Seven pressure contexts** where interiority commonly thins:

1. **Action/violence scenes** — She becomes a body executing moves. Fear becomes generic rather than specific to her psychology. Competence erases interiority (she's effective but we don't know what it costs her).
2. **Professional/intellectual scenes** — Expertise becomes a trait rather than an experience. She has conclusions but not the *feeling* of thinking. Professional self feels performed rather than inhabited.
3. **Emotional confrontation scenes** — She becomes a reaction-generator for the other character's arc. Her anger/grief/joy is "what a woman would feel," not what *she* would feel.
4. **Caregiving/domestic scenes** — She becomes a function (nurturer, homemaker, mother) rather than a person doing those things. The narrative knows what caregiving provides but not what it costs.
5. **Dialogue-heavy scenes** — No running inner life between lines. She exists only when speaking. No thought about what she's choosing not to say.
6. **Group/ensemble scenes** — She thins when surrounded by male characters. Her perspective narrows to "the woman in the room." Individual voice compresses under male-cast density.
7. **Intimate/erotic scenes** — Body becomes surface observed from outside. Desire is purely responsive. Interiority vanishes during physical experience and returns only afterward.

### Severity Hard Gates

These gates apply regardless of prose quality elsewhere:

1. If a protagonist female character scores Functional or Absent in 2+ high-load scene classes → minimum Must-Fix.
2. If the same thinning flag appears in 3+ chapters across 2+ scene classes → classify as systemic.
3. If male comparator characters retain full interiority in matched conditions → escalate severity by one tier.
4. If thinning contradicts the manuscript's stated contract (e.g., "female-led thriller") → cap readiness accordingly.

## Named Diagnostic Flags

Each flag identifies a specific pattern of interiority failure. Flags can co-occur. Severity depends on frequency, pattern, and whether the flag represents a systematic authorial assumption or an isolated lapse.

### Interiority Thinning Flags

**IT-1: "The Thermostat"**
Female character exists primarily to regulate others' emotional temperature — comforting, soothing, mediating, managing feelings. Her own emotional temperature is never the point.
- *Detect:* Character's interior access opens only when another character is in distress. Her thoughts are about their needs, never her own.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if systematic across the manuscript.

**IT-2: "Competence Without Cost"**
Female character is skilled and effective but we never see the inner experience of exercising that skill. She's a function-delivery mechanism: hacks the system, wins the fight, solves the problem, and the narrative moves on without registering what it was like *for her*.
- *Detect:* Action/professional scenes rendered in external description. Male characters in equivalent scenes retain internal process. Her expertise is summarized; theirs is dramatized.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if it creates a gender-differential pattern.

**IT-3: "The Witness"**
Female character's primary narrative function is to observe and react to what male characters do. Her interiority is entirely responsive — she has feelings *about* men's actions but no independent inner momentum.
- *Detect:* Remove all male characters' actions from the scene. Does she still have anything to think about? If not, she's a witness.
- *Default severity:* Must-Fix if systematic. Could-Fix if isolated to a single scene with structural justification.

**IT-4: "Reactive Desire"**
In intimate or romantic contexts, female desire activates only in response to male initiation. No autonomous wanting, fantasy, or sexual selfhood. She wants because she is wanted.
- *Detect:* Every instance of female desire is preceded by male action. She never initiates, fantasizes, or wants outside the presence of the desire object.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Could-Fix if isolated to a single encounter with character-logic justification.

**IT-5: "The Personality Transplant"**
*(Cross-reference: Character Architecture audit)*
Female character's psychology shifts to serve plot needs. Bold in chapter 3, timid in chapter 7, angry in chapter 12 — not because she's complex, but because the scene needs that reaction from "a woman."
- *Detect:* Track her dominant trait per scene. Do shifts follow internal logic (growth, trauma, decision) or external demand (the plot needs someone angry/scared/supportive)?
- *Default severity:* Must-Fix. This is a characterization failure regardless of gender, but gendered patterns (she shifts to accommodate; he stays consistent) compound it.

**IT-6: "Interiority Vacation"**
Character has full inner life in some scene types and near-zero in others. The pattern reveals which contexts the author considers "female" (she thinks during emotional scenes) versus not (she acts during action scenes, works during professional scenes).
- *Detect:* Map interiority level across scene types using the Interiority Map artifact. A gendered pattern of presence and absence is the signal.
- *Default severity:* Must-Fix if the pattern is gendered. Should-Fix if limited to a single scene type.

**IT-7: "The Performed Ideal"**
Character's interiority has been hollowed out and replaced by audience-appeal compliance. She's effortlessly cool, never angry, accommodating without cost. The narrative presents this as her authentic self rather than a performance.
- *Detect:* No internal friction, no private dissatisfaction, no gap between public self and private thought. Character never thinks something she wouldn't say.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if the character is a protagonist.

**IT-8: "Competence as Costume"**
Character is described as highly capable, but her competence never actually drives plot outcomes. She's introduced as "the smart one" or "the tough one," but when the climax arrives, male characters formulate the plan, make the decisions, or rescue her.
- *Detect:* Expertise is asserted in dialogue or exposition but never used to drive pivots. She provides analysis; others choose and execute. Plot rewards her label without showing operative thinking.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix in mystery/crime/SFF where expertise is core currency.

**IT-9: "Caregiver Erasure"**
Character's role as mother, nurse, or caretaker overrides her personhood. The narrative thoroughly tracks what her care provides to the plot or to other characters, but entirely ignores what the caregiving costs her, or what she thinks about while doing it.
- *Detect:* She performs care labor with no fatigue, resentment, boundary, or reflective signal. Domestic scenes frame what others receive, not what she gives up.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix when caregiving role is central identity axis.

**IT-10: "Ensemble Fade"**
Character thins or vanishes in mixed-gender group scenes, especially when male cast density rises. Solo scenes restore depth, but group scenes erase it.
- *Detect:* She loses initiative, interruption rights, or inference lines in ensemble dialogue. Her POV narrows to spectator status while male characters exchange strategy.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if ensemble scenes carry major plot turns.

**IT-11: "Borrowed Stakes"**
Character's stakes are consistently derivative of another character's arc, not self-originating. She acts, but her reasons are always about him.
- *Detect:* Her risk language is mostly about him/them, not self-defined losses. High-stakes decisions justified by loyalty without self-accounting. In climactic scenes, her objective collapses into support function.
- *Default severity:* Must-Fix for protagonist/co-protagonist. Should-Fix for supporting roles.

**IT-12: "Refrigerated Consequence"**
Female suffering, injury, or loss is used primarily to catalyze another character's growth. Her pain receives low interior bandwidth; his response receives high bandwidth.
- *Detect:* Narrative aftermath tracks his transformation, not her experience. Her arc endpoint is instrumental rather than autonomous. Her inner experience of the harm is either absent or perfunctory.
- *Default severity:* Must-Fix in most contexts. Downgrade only when critique framing is explicit and structurally sustained.

### Gaze and Observation Flags

**GO-1: "Self-Objectification Without Grounding"**
In female POV, character notices her own appearance in ways that serve an external viewer rather than her psychology. Physical self-description reads like someone else looking at her.
- *Detect:* Self-aware body descriptions that aren't motivated by character psychology (vanity, anxiety, body dysmorphia, habitual self-surveillance). Description organized for visual consumption rather than self-experience.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix per instance. Must-Fix if pattern.

**GO-2: "Body-Part Inventory"**
Female character introduced or described primarily through a catalog of physical features — breasts, legs, hair, skin — fragmented into separate objects rather than integrated into a person.
- *Detect:* Physical description organized as list of parts. Appearance described before personality, thought, or action. Compare with how male characters are introduced.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Could-Fix if the observing character's psychology motivates it.

**GO-3: "Scenery Woman"**
Female character present in scene for aesthetic value, not narrative function. She decorates the background. Could be replaced by a piece of furniture.
- *Detect:* Remove the character from the scene. Does anything change? If not, she's scenery.
- *Default severity:* Could-Fix if isolated. Should-Fix if pattern across walk-on female characters.

**GO-4: "The Sexy Lamp"**
*(Imported/Adapted from Kelly Sue DeConnick)*
Character's entire presence could be replaced by an inanimate object without altering the plot. Zero interiority, zero agency — she exists solely as prize, hostage, or scenery.
- *Detect:* Replace her with a lamp. Does the plot still work? If yes, she's the lamp.
- *Default severity:* Must-Fix.

### Agency Flags

**AG-1: "Satellite Character"**
Female character defined entirely by her relationship to a male character — wife of, mother of, love interest of. No independent goals that survive outside the relationship.
- *Detect:* List her goals. Are any of them hers alone? Does she have desires, fears, or ambitions that exist when the male character isn't present?
- *Default severity:* Must-Fix if protagonist or major secondary. Should-Fix for minor characters.

**AG-2: "Emotional Labor as Characterization"**
The only way the narrative shows her depth is through her emotional management of others. She's complex because she *cares*, not because she *thinks, wants, or acts*.
- *Detect:* Inventory her scenes of demonstrated depth. Are they all scenes of caregiving, comforting, or managing? Does she ever demonstrate intellectual, strategic, creative, or self-directed depth?
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if she's the only significant female character.

**AG-3: "Punished for Agency"**
Female character's active choices lead to punishment the narrative frames as deserved. Independence coded as character flaw. Bold women humbled by plot.
- *Detect:* Map female characters' choices and consequences. Compare with male characters' equivalent choices. Is she punished for the same boldness that's rewarded in male characters?
- *Default severity:* Must-Fix if systematic. Should-Fix if isolated but reinforced by narrative framing.

**AG-4: "The Smurfette"**
In a male ensemble, the sole (or token) female character functions as "the moral center" or "the voice of reason" for the group rather than acting as an individual with her own agenda.
- *Detect:* Does she influence via persuading men to act, rather than acting herself? Is her role to humanize the group rather than pursue independent objectives?
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if she is positioned as a major character.

### Relationship Flags

**RF-1: "The Bechdel Floor"**
No significant female-female relationships. Women in competition for male attention. Female friendships that are about men.
- *Detect:* Identify all female-female interactions. What do they discuss? Do they have relationship stakes independent of male characters?
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix if the cast allows for female-female interaction. (Single-POV male-dominated settings may not trigger this.)

**RF-2: "Disposable Bonds"**
Female friendships and rivalries that lack the narrative weight given to male-male or male-female relationships. Male friendships tested by plot events; female friendships decorative.
- *Detect:* Compare narrative investment (scene time, emotional weight, plot consequence) in female-female vs. male-male relationships.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Could-Fix if genre conventions limit relationship scope.

## Tracking Artifact: The Interiority Map

Fill out one map per significant female character. The map creates a heat-map of interiority across scene types, making contextual thinning visible.

```
Character: [Name]
Role: [Protagonist / Major Secondary / Minor Secondary]

| Scene Type | Scenes Present | Interiority Level | Flags Triggered | Notes |
|------------|---------------|-------------------|-----------------|-------|
| Action/violence | | F / T / Fn / A | | |
| Professional/intellectual | | F / T / Fn / A | | |
| Emotional confrontation | | F / T / Fn / A | | |
| Caregiving/domestic | | F / T / Fn / A | | |
| Dialogue-heavy | | F / T / Fn / A | | |
| Group/ensemble | | F / T / Fn / A | | |
| Intimate/erotic | | F / T / Fn / A | | |
| Solo/reflective | | F / T / Fn / A | | |

Key: F = Full, T = Thinned, Fn = Functional, A = Absent
```

### Reading the Map

- **Consistent F across scene types** → Strong characterization. Inner life is persistent.
- **F in relational scenes, T/Fn/A in action/professional** → Gendered interiority assumption. Author knows what she feels but not what she thinks under pressure.
- **F only in solo scenes, T/Fn elsewhere** → The Solitude Spike. Character can't maintain self in company. Interiority collapses under social pressure.
- **F only with male lead present, T/Fn elsewhere** → Relational Dependency. She thinks, but only thinks about/because of him. Her interiority is a satellite of his presence.
- **F in "her" scenes, A in ensemble** → Ensemble Fade (IT-10). She disappears when male characters are present.
- **Mixed T/Fn with no A** → Moderate thinning. May be addressable through revision passes targeting specific scene types.

### Severity Calibration from the Map

A single Absent cell is not necessarily a problem (some scenes legitimately don't require interiority). The map reveals *patterns*:

- **Gendered pattern** (F in female-coded scenes, T/A in male-coded scenes): Must-Fix
- **Pressure pattern** (F in low-stakes, T/A in high-stakes): Must-Fix
- **Ensemble pattern** (F alone, T/A in groups): Should-Fix
- **Relational pattern** (F only with love interest, T/A otherwise): Must-Fix
- **Isolated gap** (one scene type, explainable by narrative logic): Could-Fix
- **Consistent thinning across all types**: Character Architecture problem, not just interiority (cross-reference Character Architecture audit)

## The Distinguish Framework

Not all interiority thinning is a problem. Some of the best fiction thins interiority deliberately to explore trauma, oppression, performance, or the erasure of female consciousness. The audit must tell the difference.

### Intentional Thinning (not a problem)

**Dissociation as theme.** Character's inner life thins because trauma, conditioning, or shock has thinned it — and the narrative *knows* this. The thinning is a symptom, not an oversight.
- *Signal:* Onset and offset are visible. Interiority narrows in specific triggered contexts and returns with explanatory continuity. The psychological cost of thinning is rendered.

**Performance / code-switching.** Character suppresses interiority in certain contexts because that's what the character would do — a woman in a hostile professional environment performing competence without revealing inner cost.
- *Signal:* Compensating scenes reveal active private processing. The gap between public self and private thought is the *point*.

**POV constraint.** Another character's POV can't access her interiority. That's a POV limitation, not a characterization failure.
- *Signal:* When she does get POV access (even briefly), full interiority is present.

**Deliberate critique.** The narrative is *about* the erasure of female interiority. The thinning is the subject being examined, not the author's blindspot.
- *Signal:* The text creates friction around the thinning — other characters notice, the narrative voice registers it, consequences accumulate. The reader is made uncomfortable by the absence, not invited to accept it.

### Accidental Thinning (problem)

- Author doesn't know what this character thinks in this context (default to generic)
- Author interested in what's happening *to* her, not what's happening *in* her
- Character's inner life tracks what the plot needs rather than what her psychology would produce
- Pattern is gendered: male characters maintain interiority in equivalent scenes
- **Silent Erasure:** The thinning happens seamlessly — no cost, no acknowledgment, no friction. The simulation of the character's mind has simply stopped without anyone noticing.

### The Five Distinguish Tests

1. **Registration test:** Does the narrative *notice* the thinning? If it happens silently, it's probably accidental.
2. **Distribution test:** Does thinning cluster by scene type (gendered assumption) or by specific thematic beats (intentional)?
3. **Parity test:** Do male and female characters receive equivalent interiority under equivalent pressure?
4. **Recovery test:** Does interior specificity return with explanatory continuity, or does it just reappear without acknowledgment?
5. **Cost test:** When competence or action is shown, is cost, tradeoff, or interpretation present?

### Decision Matrix

| If tests show... | Classification | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Registered, bounded, recovering, parity-consistent | Intentional craft choice | Note only; no penalty unless overused |
| Mixed evidence, partial registration, uneven recovery | Ambiguous | Assign Could-Fix or low Should-Fix; monitor for pattern |
| Unregistered, clustered, parity-skewed, no recovery | Accidental thinning | Assign Should-Fix or Must-Fix based on blast radius |

A manuscript passes the intentionality gate when thinning is bounded, registered, and thematically legible. It fails when thinning is silent, systematic, and gender-skewed.

**Special caution zones** (require stronger evidence before assigning systemic failure): unreliable narrators, dissociation/trauma narratives, horror and dark erotics with intentional discomfort, historical settings with constrained external agency.

## Gaze Analysis

Gaze analysis is a *subset* of interiority analysis, not a separate concern. Male gaze intrusions are a specific form of interiority thinning: the character's inner life is replaced by an external viewing perspective.

### In Female POV — Flag When:

- **Self-objectification without grounding** (GO-1): Character sees herself as an audience would, without psychological motivation for self-surveillance
- **Body-part fragmentation** (GO-2): Physical description organized as inventory of parts for visual consumption
- **Appearance anxiety as default**: Female character constantly aware of how she looks, without this being rooted in specific psychology
- **Costume focus**: Detailed clothing description serving visual imagination rather than character in contexts where it's inappropriate (life-threatening situation, she notices her dress)

### In Male POV Observing Women — Flag When:

- **Appearance-first introduction**: Female characters introduced through physical inventory before personhood
- **Sexualized framing of non-sexual contexts**: Every woman assessed for attractiveness regardless of scene function
- **Women as scenery** (GO-3): Female characters present for aesthetic value, not narrative function

### The Gaze/Interiority Connection

**Male gaze from male character:** Appropriate — that's how he sees. Not an interiority flag unless it's the *only* way women are rendered.

**Male gaze bleeding into female POV:** Interiority failure — unless the character is self-surveilling, playing to gaze, or otherwise psychologically motivated (and the narrative registers this).

**Female gaze on male character:** Should include her internal response, not just his body described. Absence of her response = interiority thinning.

**Any POV during high-intensity scenes:** Should maintain that character's interiority, even if also observing others. See Interiority Preservation audit for the gender-neutral version.

## Genre-Specific Calibration

Each genre produces its own characteristic version of interiority thinning. Severity thresholds shift by genre, but the diagnostic framework applies universally. Each genre's failure mode has a name for use in editorial reports.

| Genre | Named Risk | Core Failure | Tighten Severity On | Loosen Severity On |
|-------|-----------|-------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Romance / Erotica | Desire Dependency | Heroine's desire activates only in response to hero's | Desire autonomy, boundary cognition, post-encounter processing | Stylized sensual description that remains internally owned |
| Horror | Generic Terror | Female fear rendered as spectacle rather than lived cognition | Lived terror cognition, aftershock processing, violation framing | Controlled dissociation where narratively registered |
| Thriller / Mystery | The Case as Personality | Investigative thought summarized; competence is label, not process | Investigative process from inside, cost and bias in procedural scenes | Functional exposition segments if parity holds |
| SFF | The Gender-Swapped Archetype | Worldbuilding displaces cognition; "chosen one, now female" shell | World/system impact on female cognition, ensemble authority | Minimalist lore passages if interiority parity holds |
| Literary Fiction | The Aestheticized Woman | Beautiful, symbolically loaded, psychologically thin | Epistemic authority, non-relational cognition, voice specificity | High-distance narration with stable authority cues |
| Historical Fiction | Minds in Corsets | Period constraints on action mistaken for constraints on thought | Inner-life density under external limits, friction between desire and role | Reduced overt defiance if interior resistance is rich |
| Action / Adventure | Body-Without-Mind | She kicks ass but we never know what it's like to be her | Action rendered from inside, physical cost specific to her psychology | Brief low-reflection action bursts if recovery follows |

### Genre Notes

**Romance / Erotica:** Core risk is desire dependency, not explicitness. Require evidence that female desire originates internally at least sometimes. Penalize dependency and ownership loss, not heat level.

**Horror:** The Final Girl may survive but never be granted the interiority of her terror. Fear can fragment language, but still require subject-owned terror signal, not only observed body distress.

**Thriller / Mystery:** "She is smart" is not sufficient. A brilliant female detective whose entire inner life is restricted to the clues of the case is structurally identical to a love interest whose entire inner life is restricted to the hero. Competence is not a substitute for an inner life. Require visible investigative reasoning, hypothesis revision, and personal cost logic.

**SFF:** Worldbuilding pressure should make interiority *more* specific, not less. If lore is rich but female cognition is generic, escalate. Gender irrelevant to inner life in a gendered world is a flag.

**Literary Fiction:** The prose may be exquisite while the characterization is hollow. Women as symbols, the "muse" dynamic, observation substituting for consciousness. Check for selective depth: rich relational interiority, thin strategic/intellectual interiority.

**Historical Fiction:** Period restrictions may limit action, but should often *increase* interior density — the gap between what she can think and what she can publicly do is a source of psychological richness. A woman who cannot legally own property should still possess a vast and potentially furious internal life.

**Action / Adventure:** The "tough woman" whose only vulnerability is romantic. Action sequences in external description only. No inner experience of violence, fear, or physical cost.

## Audit Output

### Summary Assessment

Provide:
1. **Overall interiority assessment:** Does the manuscript sustain female interiority across scene types, or does thinning follow a gendered pattern?
2. **Gaze assessment:** Whose perspective dominates description of female characters? Does it shift appropriately by POV?
3. **Agency assessment:** Do female characters drive plot through their own choices, or are they acted upon?
4. **Relationship assessment:** Do female characters have meaningful connections beyond romantic interests?

### Interiority Maps

Complete one Interiority Map per significant female character. These are the audit's primary evidence artifact. Do not issue final severity without them.

### Flag Inventory

For each triggered flag, provide:
- **Flag code and name** (e.g., IT-2: Competence Without Cost)
- **Location** (scene/chapter)
- **Evidence** (specific passage reference, ≤25 words, or structural paraphrase)
- **Severity** (Must-Fix / Should-Fix / Could-Fix)
- **Pattern note** (isolated instance or part of a systematic pattern?)
- **Blast radius** (local / multi-scene / systemic)

### Pattern Analysis

From the Interiority Maps and flag inventory, identify:
- Does thinning follow a gendered pattern (specific scene types)?
- Does thinning cluster around specific characters or situations?
- Do male characters in equivalent scenes maintain interiority?
- Is there a systematic authorial assumption about what women's inner lives contain?

### Recommendations

Stated in abstract structural terms (per editorial firewall):
- "This character needs interiority sustained through [scene type] — currently she thins to functional."
- "The parity gap between [female character] and [male character] in [context] needs closing."
- "Physical descriptions in female POV need grounding in character psychology, not visual consumption."

**NOT:** Specific rewrites, new scenes, or content invention.

## What This Audit Is Not

1. **Not a demand for "positive" representation.** Complex, flawed, even villainous female characters are fine. The question is whether they have interiority and agency, not whether they're likeable.

2. **Not a prohibition on male gaze.** Male characters observing female bodies is in character for those characters. The audit identifies when *POV interiority* disappears, not when male characters look at women.

3. **Not a requirement for female POV.** Works can be excellent without female POV. The audit applies to how female characters are rendered regardless of POV structure.

4. **Not an ideological checklist.** The goal is craft — fully realized characters — not political correctness. A feminist novel with thinned female characters fails this audit. A conservative novel with fully realized women passes it.

5. **Not about eliminating gendered observation.** A woman playing to male gaze can be intentional character work. A man observing a woman's body is in character. The issue is whether interiority persists or vanishes.

6. **Not limited to intimate scenes.** The most visible interiority failures happen during sex, but the most *damaging* ones happen everywhere else — in action sequences, professional settings, group dynamics, and caregiving — because they're invisible until you look for them.
