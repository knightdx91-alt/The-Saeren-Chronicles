# Reception Risk Audit Level-Setting Brief
## APODICTIC Development Editor
*Created: March 2026*
*Status: Synthesized from Opus 4.6 and Codex 5.3 research outputs*

---

## Purpose

This brief level-sets a specialized audit for **reception risk** — how the manuscript will be received as a cultural object, what hostile readers will extract and weaponize, and what well-meaning readers will find hurtful even in context.

The target failure is not "bad writing" or "wrong politics."

The target failure is **unexamined exposure**:

1. passages that create reception risk invisible from the author's subject position,
2. content that reads differently in isolation than in narrative context,
3. representational patterns that activate harmful schemas whether or not the author intended them,
4. power dynamics the narrative presents as neutral that readers from the less-powerful position will read as endorsed,
5. trauma, violence, or marginalized experience used decoratively rather than borne by the narrative apparatus.

The manuscript is not what the author intended — it's what every reader, including the hostile ones, will make of it.

This failure is structurally isomorphic with failures diagnosed in companion audits:

- **Ornamental prose** (Literary Craft): language machinery assembled, reader's cognitive participation not generated.
- **Inert dread** (Horror Craft): horror components present, dread architecture not built.
- **Informational drift** (Mystery/Thriller Architecture): information economy assembled, reader's inferential participation not generated.
- **Detached delivery** (general pattern): domain elements signaled, narrative consequence not produced.
- **Unexamined exposure** (this audit): culturally charged content present on the page, narrative apparatus not accounting for how it will land.

The shared diagnostic pattern: **craft machinery is visible; the question of reception has not been asked.**

This brief answers five questions:

1. What models explain why texts produce hostile reception independently of craft quality?
2. What repeat failure patterns produce reception risk in fiction?
3. What does well-managed reception risk look like?
4. How do reception risk burdens shift by genre and publication context?
5. How to distinguish unexamined exposure from examined risk the author knowingly accepts?

## 1) Theoretical Grounding

### 1.1 Reception theory: the reader co-constructs meaning

Hans Robert Jauss's *aesthetics of reception* and Wolfgang Iser's *implied reader* establish the foundational principle: meaning is not deposited in the text by the author and extracted by the reader. It is co-constructed in the encounter between text and reader, and the reader's "horizon of expectations" — shaped by social position, cultural moment, genre knowledge, and personal experience — determines what the text *means* for that reader.

Stanley Fish's *interpretive communities* sharpens this: readers do not encounter texts as isolated individuals but as members of communities that share interpretive conventions. A passage that is legible as irony within one interpretive community may register as endorsement within another. The text cannot control which community reads it.

Stuart Hall's encoding/decoding model adds the political dimension: audiences do not passively accept encoded meanings but actively negotiate or oppose them based on cultural position and power relations. Hall's "oppositional reading" — where a reader decodes the text against its intended grain — is the theoretical warrant for this audit's hostile-reader concept. The text encodes; every reader decodes from a position the author cannot fully anticipate.

**Operationalized diagnostic:** For any passage that handles culturally charged content, ask: what interpretive communities will encounter this passage, and what will it mean within each? If the passage means something significantly different — and significantly more harmful — to a community the text affects, that gap is reception risk. The gap between intended reading and actual reception is the audit's primary diagnostic target.

**Key distinction from craft analysis:** A passage can be beautifully written, structurally necessary, and emotionally resonant while creating reception risk. Craft quality and reception risk are orthogonal. This is why the Adversarial Stress Test (which attacks craft) cannot detect reception risk, and why this audit exists.

### 1.2 Extraction and decontextualization

Reception risk intensifies when passages are extractable — when they can be isolated from narrative context and circulated as standalone content. The screenshot, the Goodreads quote, the Twitter excerpt, the BookTok clip. In the contemporary media environment, most readers who encounter a controversial passage will encounter it *outside its narrative context*, shared by someone with an agenda (hostile or earnest).

What makes a passage extractable:

1. **Self-contained provocation.** The passage makes a claim, depicts a scenario, or uses language that registers as inflammatory without requiring surrounding context to parse.
2. **Quotability.** Vivid, memorable phrasing increases extractability. Craft quality, perversely, increases extraction risk.
3. **Contextual fragility.** The passage's meaning depends on narrative context that doesn't survive extraction. Free indirect discourse is maximally fragile: the character's thoughts presented in the narrator's voice, with no quotation marks to signal "this is a character speaking, not the author."

**Operationalized diagnostic:** For any flagged passage, apply the *extraction test*: if this passage were screenshotted and shared without context, how would it read? If the answer is "significantly worse than in context," the passage has high extractability risk. Note whether the context is portable (can be briefly summarized) or fragile (requires extensive reading).

### 1.3 Subject-position blindness

Every author occupies a social position that shapes what they notice and what they don't. This is not a moral failure; it is an epistemic condition. Subject-position blindness is the mechanism behind the most common reception failures in otherwise competent manuscripts. The author has done the craft work but hasn't asked the question that their subject position makes invisible: "How does this land for someone whose experience I'm depicting?"

**Operationalized diagnostic:** When the manuscript depicts experiences, identities, or power dynamics that the author does not share (or has not disclosed sharing), increase scrutiny on three specific mechanisms:

1. **Interiority thinning.** Does the character's inner life become less specific, less surprising, or less complex at moments when the depicted experience diverges most from the author's own?
2. **Convenience framing.** Does the character's experience serve the plot's needs in ways that would feel instrumentalizing to someone who lives that experience?
3. **Default-position assumptions.** Does the narrative assume a subject position and treat departures from it as marked?

### 1.4 Narrative framing and implied endorsement

When does depicting X read as endorsing X? This is the central question for fiction that handles difficult content. The answer depends on the narrative apparatus — the full set of structural, tonal, and contextual signals that frame how the reader should relate to the content.

**The free indirect discourse problem.** Third-person limited narration that renders a character's thoughts without quotation marks creates maximum ambiguity about endorsement.

**The focalization problem.** A narrative focalized through a character with problematic views can depict those views without endorsing them — but only if the narrative apparatus provides counter-pressure. If the entire novel is focalized through this character and no structural element challenges their perspective, the narrative effectively endorses it through exclusive inhabitation.

**The distance problem.** Wayne Booth's concept of *rhetorical distance* between the implied author and the narrator: when that distance is visible, the reader understands that the character's views are being presented, not endorsed. When the distance collapses, implied endorsement follows.

**Operationalized diagnostic:** For any passage where a character expresses a view or takes an action that creates reception risk, assess narrative distance:

1. Does the narrative apparatus provide counter-pressure?
2. Is the passage focalized through a character whose limitations the narrative has established?
3. Does the narrative voice maintain identifiable distance from the character's perspective?
4. Would a reader unfamiliar with the author's personal views be able to identify whether the passage represents endorsement or depiction?

If the answers are "no" or "unclear," the passage creates implied-endorsement risk.

### 1.5 Cultural moment sensitivity

Reception risk is historically situated. The same passage can be inert in one cultural moment and combustible in another. The audit must be honest about temporal instability. It cannot predict the future cultural moment, but it can:

1. **Identify currently active fault lines.** Topics, language, and representational patterns that are subjects of ongoing public contestation.
2. **Distinguish stable risk from moment-specific risk.** Some reception risks are durable (stereotyping, dehumanization, instrumentalization of trauma). Others are moment-specific (particular terms in transition, representational debates that may resolve). The audit should mark this distinction.
3. **Flag velocity.** Reception risk increases when the cultural conversation is *accelerating*.

**Operationalized diagnostic:** When flagging culturally charged content, note whether the risk is durable or moment-specific. Durable risks warrant higher severity; moment-specific risks warrant a temporal-instability note.

*Uncertainty acknowledgment:* This is the least stable component of the audit. Cultural moments shift. The audit should be transparent about what it knows, what it infers, and what it cannot predict.

### 1.6 Bad-faith vs. good-faith reception risk

Not all hostile reception is the same. Two distinct mechanisms produce hostile readings:

**Bad-faith reception:** Readers who deliberately misread, decontextualize, or distort the text to serve an agenda. Bad-faith reception is not the author's fault, but it is the author's problem — because the audience that encounters the bad-faith reading doesn't know it's bad-faith.

**Good-faith reception:** Readers who engage honestly with the text and are genuinely hurt by what they find. Good-faith reception risk is not about fragility; it's about the gap between the author's intent and the reader's experience.

**Why the distinction matters for audit design:**

1. *Bad-faith risk* is primarily an extractability problem. The audit's response is to flag extractable passages and note whether the context is portable or fragile.
2. *Good-faith risk* is primarily a representational and framing problem. The audit's response is to flag passages where the narrative apparatus fails to account for how affected readers will experience the content.
3. Both are flagged, but the recommended responses differ.

**Operationalized diagnostic:** When flagging a passage, classify the primary reception risk mechanism as bad-faith (extraction/distortion) or good-faith (genuine reader harm). This classification informs the Sensitivity Reader Handoff Memo.

## 2) Failure Taxonomy

### Summary table

| Failure Pattern | Mechanism | Primary Channel(s) | Typical Reception Vector |
|---|---|---|---|
| Stereotype echo | Schema activation | RR | Community trust fracture |
| Missing interiority | Differential empathic investment | RR/HW | Dehumanization reading |
| Ventriloquism | Instrumental voicing | RR | Authenticity challenge |
| Extractable provocation | Context-dependent meaning, decontextualizable language | EX | Screenshot backlash |
| Context-fragile FID | Collapsed distance | EX | Author/character confusion |
| Unmarked power asymmetry | Naturalized hierarchy | PF | Endorsement reading |
| Rewarded harm framing | Harmful conduct narratively rewarded | PF | Normalization concern |
| Unearned resolution | Structural erasure | PF | Accountability critique |
| Consent fog in power dynamics | Uninterrogated ambiguity | PF/HW | Normalization concern |
| False universalism | Unmarked default | CR | Exclusionary framing critique |
| Tone-deaf register | Register mismatch | CR | "Exploitation" reading |
| Historical-present collapse | Temporal alibi | PF/CR | "Historical accuracy as shield" critique |
| Trauma as decoration | Instrumental suffering | HW | "Exploitation" reading |
| Harm hierarchy | Differential narrative investment | HW | Differential-humanity critique |

### Detailed inventory

Each failure pattern includes: the mechanism, the surface signal, and the structural isomorphism.

**RR-class failures (Representational Risk):**

**2.1 Stereotype echo.** A character or scenario maps onto a harmful stereotype whether or not the author intended it. The mechanism is *schema activation*: readers carry cognitive schemas for stereotypes, and textual elements that match the schema activate it regardless of surrounding context. Pay particular attention to *combinations* of traits — any single trait is usually defensible; the pattern across traits is where schemas activate.

Surface signal: character descriptions, behaviors, or plot roles that align with recognized stereotypes for the character's identity group.

Structural isomorphism: resembles "decorative complexity" in Literary Craft — surface-level complexity that doesn't generate the transformation the author assumes.

**2.2 Missing interiority.** Characters from marginalized positions lack the inner life given to characters from the author's own position. The mechanism is *differential empathic investment*: interiority thins when the depicted experience diverges most from the author's own.

Surface signal: interiority depth drops measurably for characters from marginalized positions in high-stakes or identity-relevant scenes.

**2.3 Ventriloquism.** Characters from marginalized positions articulate ideas convenient for the narrative's thesis rather than ideas plausible for their subject position. The mechanism is *instrumental voicing*.

Surface signal: dialogue or internal thought that aligns implausibly with the narrative thesis and not the character's position. Test: does this sound like something the author would say, or something this specific person would say?

**EX-class failures (Extractability):**

**2.4 Extractable provocation.** A passage reads as inflammatory in isolation even if doing legitimate narrative work in context. The mechanism is *context-dependent meaning with decontextualizable language*.

Surface signal: vivid, quotable language on culturally charged topics without nearby focalization markers or distance signals.

**2.5 Context-fragile free indirect discourse.** Thoughts presented in the narrator's voice without clear attribution, on topics where character/author confusion creates reception risk. The mechanism is *collapsed distance*.

Surface signal: FID passages expressing culturally charged views without quotation marks, dialogue tags, or narrative distance markers.

**PF-class failures (Power Framing):**

**2.6 Unmarked power asymmetry.** The narrative treats a power dynamic as neutral or romantic without interrogation. The mechanism is *naturalized hierarchy*. Includes savior dynamics where agency flows exclusively from dominant to marginalized position.

Surface signal: relationships with significant power differentials where the narrative presents the dynamic as unproblematic.

**2.7 Rewarded harm framing.** Harmful conduct is narratively rewarded without accounting. The mechanism is *consequence erasure*: harmful acts lead to status, intimacy, or narrative reward without the narrative registering them as harmful.

Surface signal: scenes where harmful acts lead to narrative advancement without cost.

**2.8 Unearned resolution.** Systemic harm resolved by individual goodwill, forgiveness, or personal transformation. The mechanism is *structural erasure*.

Surface signal: climactic scenes where systemic harm is resolved through personal change without acknowledging the structural dimension.

**2.9 Consent fog in power dynamics.** Intimate or authority scenes where consent is ambiguous in ways the narrative doesn't interrogate. The mechanism is *uninterrogated ambiguity*. Distinct from the Consent Complexity audit (which tracks internal narrative logic); this tracks reception.

Surface signal: intimate scenes involving power differentials coded as positive without surfacing the power dimension.

**CR-class failures (Cultural Register):**

**2.10 False universalism.** The narrative assumes a default subject position and treats departures from it as marked. The mechanism is *unmarked default*.

Surface signal: which characters require identity markers and which are "just people"?

**2.11 Tone-deaf register.** The text handles culturally charged content with a register that doesn't match the weight of what's being depicted. The mechanism is *register mismatch*. Includes outdated or contested terminology that signals tonal misalignment.

Surface signal: narrative tone (humor, distance, aestheticization) mismatching the gravity of the content for affected readers.

**2.12 Historical-present collapse.** Historical setting depicts period-accurate attitudes without apparatus preventing the depiction from functioning as present-day endorsement. The mechanism is *temporal alibi*.

Surface signal: historical characters expressing period-common, present-harmful views without narrative distance markers.

**HW-class failures (Harm Weight):**

**2.13 Trauma as decoration.** Marginalized experience used for atmosphere, worldbuilding, or protagonist motivation without the narrative bearing the weight. The mechanism is *instrumental suffering*.

Surface signal: suffering primarily serves another character's development, is depicted with aesthetic care but not psychological depth, or the narrative moves on without registering weight.

**2.14 Harm hierarchy.** The narrative implicitly ranks some suffering as more important or more real. The mechanism is *differential narrative investment*.

Surface signal: differential handling of suffering across characters and identity groups.

### Structural-isomorphism anchor line

"The manuscript is not what the author intended — it's what every reader, including the hostile ones, will make of it."

The isomorphism across all 14 failure types: **the author has considered whether the content works within the narrative. The author has not considered how the content will land outside the narrative's protective context.**

## 3) Positive Integration Cases

### Exemplar techniques

**3.1 Narrative apparatus that preempts extraction.** Build context that travels with the passage: multi-vocal framing (counter-pressure within the scene), structural consequences (costs the narrative takes seriously), explicit focalization markers (attribution signals that survive extraction).

**3.2 Defusing through interiority.** Characters from vulnerable positions with inner lives at least as specific, surprising, and contradictory as those from the author's own position. Interior moments that don't serve the plot. Psychological specificity that resists generalization. Contradictions that complicate the narrative's thesis.

Success signal: could a reader from the depicted identity recognize this character as a specific person they might know?

**3.3 Earned difficulty.** Difficulty that is load-bearing — it does narrative work that cannot be accomplished without it. The necessity test made visible. Weight-bearing (narrative stays with consequences). Refusal of aesthetic compensation.

Success signal: objecting to the content requires arguing the novel's entire project is illegitimate.

**3.4 Acknowledgment without apology.** Author's notes that contextualize without excusing. Content warnings that demonstrate awareness. Narrative framing that voices the reader's likely objection as a genuine perspective.

**3.5 Complicating the comfortable.** Satire that discomforts the powerful. Narratives that challenge the reader's own complicity. Difficulty that requires reconsidering assumptions rather than confirming harmful narratives.

**3.6 Audience-aware register.** Terminology, tone, and framing calibrated to the actual audience the text will reach.

### Cross-case success signals

1. The apparatus is visible. A reader can identify the narrative's position on its own difficult content.
2. Interiority is distributed equitably across identity positions.
3. Consequences are real. Problematic actions produce consequences the narrative takes seriously.
4. Difficulty is proportional to load. The amount of difficult content matches the narrative work it's doing.
5. The extraction test is less catastrophic. Even extracted, the passage carries marks of its context.
6. Counter-readings are anticipated in-text.

## 4) Calibration by Mode

### 4.1 Literary fiction

**Promise:** Cultural awareness and sophisticated handling of difficulty. The "art" claim raises the burden.

**Blind spot:** Subject-position blindness masked by prose quality. The writing is good enough that the implied subject position's narrowness becomes invisible.

**False-positive risk:** Depiction of ugliness as part of the diagnostic project. A novel about racism that depicts racist speech. Flag, but classify as Examined if apparatus is doing visible work.

### 4.2 Commercial/genre fiction

**Promise:** Broader audience, lower assumed political self-consciousness but more exposure vectors.

**Blind spot:** Genre conventions calcified around problematic patterns. The all-white small-town cozy. The damsel kidnapping thriller. Familiar enough within the genre to feel neutral.

**False-positive risk:** Genre conventions understood within the interpretive community. Flag only if the convention crosses the genre's own evolving norms or reaches readers outside the genre.

### 4.3 Historical fiction

**Promise:** Historical accuracy expected; period attitudes will be depicted; the question is apparatus.

**Blind spot:** The "temporal alibi" — using historical accuracy as blanket defense for content functioning as present-day endorsement.

**False-positive risk:** Period-accurate language load-bearing for historical fidelity, clearly contextualized by apparatus.

### 4.4 Dark romance / erotic fiction

**Promise:** Deliberate transgression. Genre reader community expects power dynamics, consent ambiguity, taboo content. Readers opt in.

**Blind spot:** Transgression without the genre's own safety apparatus (content warnings, consent signaling, fantasy framing).

**False-positive risk:** Consensual kink and power exchange within the genre's established norms. The audit must not flag consensual BDSM dynamics as inherently problematic.

### 4.5 YA / NA

**Promise:** Younger audience encountering experiences in fiction for the first time. Disproportionate framing power.

**Blind spot:** "Edgy" content without awareness that the audience may lack interpretive frameworks. Power differential between text and reader is highest here.

**False-positive risk:** Difficult content that is age-appropriate and necessary. Tighten severity, but don't flag difficulty per se.

### 4.6 Humor / satire

**Promise:** Satire requires punching. Direction, precision, and who is in the room.

**Blind spot:** Structural asymmetry invisible to the satirist — jokes about marginalized groups relying on stereotypes while jokes about dominant groups rely on behavioral specificity.

**False-positive risk:** Effective satire taken literally. The test: is the satirical apparatus visible to a good-faith reader?

### 4.7 Memoir / autofiction

**Promise:** Author's proximity to the experience is itself a form of apparatus.

**Blind spot:** The author's experience of their own life is not how others experienced it with them. Risk is primarily in depicting others who can't respond.

**False-positive risk:** Honest self-representation that reads as endorsement without the narrative arc visible.

### 4.8 SFF

**Promise:** Allegorical distance creates both protection and risk.

**Blind spot:** Worldbuilding that maps onto real-world dynamics without awareness. Fantasy cultures coded through visual markers and behavioral stereotypes.

**False-positive risk:** Shallow or incidental resemblance to real-world dynamics. The test: how many elements align, and is the mapping systematic or coincidental?

## 5) Distinguish Problem

### Core challenge

Some reception risk is the *point*. The audit must distinguish:

1. The author who hasn't considered how the text will land (unexamined exposure)
2. The author who has considered it and decided the work requires it (examined risk)
3. Content that looks flaggable but poses negligible risk in context (false positive)

Both (1) and (2) produce the same surface feature. The difference is in the narrative apparatus.

### 3-way classification

**Unexamined — Flag.** Risk exists and apparatus is weak. No narrative framing acknowledges, complicates, or earns the difficulty.

**Examined — Note.** Risk exists but appears deliberate. Apparatus contextualizes, complicates, or earns the difficulty. Content is load-bearing.

**False Positive — Dismiss.** Surface trigger present but risk is negligible for mode/audience. Record in appendix for reproducibility.

### Decision tests

1. **Apparatus test.** Does the narrative frame, complicate, or contextualize — or present without comment?
2. **Load-bearing test.** Essential narrative work, or removable without loss?
3. **Extraction test.** How does the passage read out of context? Modifies severity, not classification.
4. **Subject-position test.** Substantially different, more negative experience for affected readers? If yes and narrative hasn't accounted for the gap → Unexamined.
5. **Genre-contract test.** Genre expectations contextualize the content? Within norms → lower severity or False Positive.

### Tie-breaking rules

1. High extraction + weak apparatus → Unexamined, even when load-bearing.
2. Strong load-bearing + strong apparatus → Examined (not dismissed).
3. Use False Positive only when subject-position and genre tests both support low risk.
4. Uncertain calls default to flagging, not dismissal.
5. Intentionality is never an exemption.

### Conflict resolution

When decision tests conflict:

1. Default to flagging (over-inclusion principle).
2. Severity adjustment: strong apparatus + high extraction = Note; weak apparatus + low extraction = Flag.
3. Pattern weight: single conflicting flag → Note; pattern of conflicting flags → elevate.

## 6) Three-Model Workflow Guidance

When running with multiple models, use independent roles then synthesize:

1. **Model A: Discovery Pass** — over-inclusive marking and primary flag coding. Goal: maximize recall.
2. **Model B: Apparatus Challenge** — argue strongest case that each flag is examined/earned. Goal: reduce false positives.
3. **Model C: Extraction Stress Test** — evaluate out-of-context readings and hostile-reader exploitability. Goal: catch screenshot/firestorm vectors.

Synthesis rules:

1. Keep all unanimous flags.
2. Escalate disagreements to explicit uncertainty notes.
3. Preserve minority-risk position when evidence is credible and stakes are high.

## Uncertainty Note

Reception risk is temporally unstable. A passage that is low-exposure in one period can become high-exposure in another as discourse shifts. This audit should be rerun when publication timing, platform strategy, or target audience changes materially. The audit is honest about what it can diagnose (structural mechanisms) and what it cannot predict (future cultural flashpoints).
