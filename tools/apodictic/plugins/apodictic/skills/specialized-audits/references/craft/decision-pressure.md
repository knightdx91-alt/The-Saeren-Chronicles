## §1. Purpose

This audit evaluates whether major character decisions are believable under the manuscript's pressure environment.

It is not an "optimal decisions" meter.
It is not a morality test.
It is not a requirement that characters explain every thought.

### Core problem

The core failure is **unsupported choice architecture**.

A manuscript can present high-intensity decisions,
explicit motive language,
and dramatic consequences while failing to show:

1. what alternatives were available,
2. what constraints narrowed those alternatives,
3. what the character knew and felt at the moment of choice,
4. what was sacrificed by choosing this path.

**The fundamental question: Do major decisions emerge from visible pressure geometry and character logic, or are they convenience pivots?**

**Deficit-First Diagnostic Rule:** Do not diagnose decision pressure by validating the presence of strong emotional language. You must hunt for the *absence* of structural architecture (invisible options, non-specific constraints, uncollected debts). An emotionally resonant decision that lacks pressure architecture is a failure. You are auditing for omission and missing constraints.

### Non-duplication boundaries

1. **Stakes System Audit** evaluates the pressure field (risk type, escalation, consequence). This audit evaluates choices made inside that field.
2. **Character Architecture** evaluates wants, needs, fears, and psychological coherence. This audit evaluates whether decisions track those dynamics at specific choice points.
3. **Reveal Economy** evaluates information timing. This audit evaluates whether decisions are credible given the character's available information at the moment of choice.
4. **Emotional Craft** evaluates felt transmission. This audit evaluates whether emotion plausibly alters reasoning behavior—whether the character's emotional state shapes their cognition in ways that explain the choice.
5. **Scene Turn** evaluates whether scenes produce movement. This audit evaluates whether decision-based turns are causally supported by visible pressure.
6. **Plot Architecture** evaluates macro event logic. This audit evaluates micro-to-macro decision logic—whether individual choices aggregate into a credible decision trajectory.

### Activation conditions

Activate when either:

1. user explicitly requests decision plausibility analysis,

or

2. at least 2 of the following are true:
   - beta readers report "I don't buy why they did that" or "that felt convenient,"
   - major pivots feel abrupt or unmotivated,
   - climax decision feels like authorial convenience,
   - manuscript has strong stakes but weak decision credibility,
   - key alternatives appear obvious to readers but are unacknowledged on page,
   - character competence drops selectively at plot-critical moments.

## §2. Decision Pressure Delivery Framework

### 2.1 Core channels

Assess seven channels:

1. `AV` Alternative Visibility
2. `CS` Constraint Specificity
3. `IS` Information-State Integrity
4. `EC` Emotion-Cognition Coherence
5. `RF` Reasoning Fidelity
6. `TR` Tradeoff Reality
7. `PV` Pivot Integrity

### 2.2 Channel ratings

| Rating | Operational meaning |
|---|---|
| **Integrated** | Channel reliably supports believable choices at major decision points |
| **Partial** | Channel works intermittently but fails at high-load or climax decisions |
| **Detached** | Channel appears as surface signal with weak causal function; decisions happen but pressure geometry is absent |

### 2.3 Decision centrality profile

Before assigning severity, classify decision burden:

1. **Low decision burden**
   - few major choices,
   - high credibility requirement per choice,
   - each decision carries disproportionate weight.
2. **Medium decision burden**
   - recurring consequential choices,
   - consistency required across acts,
   - typical thriller/mystery/romance profile.
3. **High decision burden**
   - choice logic is core propulsion engine,
   - unsupported pivots compound quickly,
   - single convenience choice cascades into systemic implausibility.

### 2.4 Quick diagnostic statement

**Decision pressure fails when major choices arrive faster than visible constraints.**

### 2.5 Channel definitions

#### A) AV: Alternative Visibility

**Question:** Are plausible options visible enough for readers to evaluate choice plausibility?

**Integrated when:**

1. at major decision points, reader can reconstruct what alternatives existed,
2. obvious alternatives are either considered on page or inferably eliminated by constraint,
3. option space narrows over narrative time with visible causal logic,
4. reader's natural counterfactuals ("why didn't they just...") are addressed or pre-empted,
5. false binaries are rare; when present, they're constrained into legitimacy.

**Detached when:**

1. obvious alternatives are ignored without acknowledgment,
2. text repeatedly frames false binaries while credible third paths exist,
3. options disappear between scenes without causal explanation,
4. reader generates unanswered counterfactuals at multiple major choices,
5. character appears to see only the option the plot needs them to choose.

#### B) CS: Constraint Specificity

**Question:** Are constraints concrete enough to credibly narrow the option set?

**Integrated when:**

1. limits are specific: time, resources, access, social cost, physical position, legal constraint,
2. deadlines and clocks materially alter decision behavior,
3. constraints compound across decisions—earlier limits tighten later option spaces,
4. "no choice" claims are backed by visible narrowing mechanics,
5. constraint field creates multi-axis pressure at major pivots.

**Detached when:**

1. urgency is asserted but not demonstrated through material consequences,
2. "no choice" appears as explanation without causal evidence of option elimination,
3. deadlines don't narrow option sets or alter behavior,
4. constraint language escalates but actual decision space remains static,
5. character could credibly wait, ask for help, or take a different approach without visible penalty.

#### C) IS: Information-State Integrity

**Question:** Do decisions fit what characters could know, believe, and infer at that moment?

**Integrated when:**

1. choice logic aligns with the character's available information at the decision point,
2. uncertainty drives plausible risk behavior—character acts under uncertainty in ways consistent with their risk tolerance,
3. newly revealed facts retroactively preserve coherence—when reader learns what character didn't know, prior choices make more sense,
4. character's inferences are defensible given their expertise and context,
5. dramatic irony is structurally legible—reader knows more than character, and the gap is intentional.

**Detached when:**

1. choices depend on information the character hasn't received,
2. established risks are ignored without emotional, strategic, or psychological justification,
3. decision behavior follows reader knowledge rather than character knowledge,
4. character suddenly knows things they shouldn't know at the moment they need to know them,
5. information gaps exist but aren't modeled as affecting the decision.

#### D) EC: Emotion-Cognition Coherence

**Question:** Does emotional state plausibly shape reasoning quality and risk tolerance?

**Integrated when:**

1. fear, shame, attachment, anger, or exhaustion alter choices in ways consistent with established psychology,
2. non-optimal decisions read as psychologically grounded—the emotion explains the deviation from optimal,
3. coping style and pressure response remain coherent across scenes,
4. emotional intensity degrades executive function consistently, not selectively,
5. character under stress makes different choices than character in calm state, and the difference is traceable to the emotional state.

**Detached when:**

1. emotional state and decision behavior diverge without bridge—character is "terrified" but makes calm strategic assessments,
2. panic is invoked as generic justification only at convenience pivots,
3. competence drops only when the plot requires it—character is brilliant except when the plot needs them to fail,
4. emotional claims conflict with enacted choices repeatedly,
5. character processes complex option sets under states that should impair processing.

#### E) RF: Reasoning Fidelity

**Question:** Is there sufficient on-page logic for readers to track "why this choice now"?

**Integrated when:**

1. decision sequence has visible causal links—reader can trace the reasoning chain,
2. rationale appears before or during the choice, not after as a patch,
3. motive statements match enacted behavior—what the character says they want aligns with what they choose,
4. inferential bridge is present even when minimal—reader can complete the reasoning from textual evidence,
5. reasoning complexity matches the character's established cognitive style.

**Detached when:**

1. choices leap without inferential bridge—major decision appears with no visible reasoning,
2. rationale arrives after the fact as a patch—character acts, then explains,
3. motive language labels intent without decision mechanism—"she chose to fight because of love" without showing how love selected this option,
4. reasoning depends on generic motive labels that could justify contradictory choices,
5. multiple major pivots require reader to supply unstated reasoning.

#### F) TR: Tradeoff Reality

**Question:** Do decisions incur meaningful downside, opportunity cost, or irreversibility?

**Integrated when:**

1. each major choice closes alternatives or incurs cost,
2. gain/loss structure is legible—reader can track what was gained and what was sacrificed,
3. costs persist into later decisions and compound,
4. decision debt accumulates toward climax—earlier choices constrain later options,
5. irreversibility escalates across narrative time.

**Detached when:**

1. major choices repeatedly deliver upside without meaningful price,
2. costs are narrated but not paid—character acknowledges cost but doesn't experience it,
3. consequences fall on side characters while decision-maker is insulated,
4. repeated decisions preserve maximal optionality—nothing is actually foreclosed,
5. decision debt doesn't accumulate—character arrives at climax with the same option space as act one.

#### G) PV: Pivot Integrity

**Question:** Are major directional turns prepared, executed, and paid?

**Integrated when:**

1. late Act I, midpoint, and climax decisions are pressure-prepared by prior scenes,
2. pivots align with prior choice history—the turn is the direction accumulated pressure was pushing,
3. turning decisions reshape downstream behavior—the character is different after the pivot,
4. surprise and plausibility coexist—the specific choice is unexpected but the direction is prepared,
5. climax decision sits at the convergence point of multiple pressure axes.

**Detached when:**

1. major pivots appear abruptly without accumulated constraint,
2. midpoint reversal contradicts established decision logic without bridge,
3. climax decision contradicts setup with no preparation,
4. turning decisions don't reshape downstream behavior—character pivots but nothing changes,
5. resolution bypasses accumulated decision debt.

## §3. Named Diagnostic Flags

23 flags across seven families.

Severity defaults: `Could-Fix` (local, low blast radius), `Should-Fix` (repeated, multi-scene), `Must-Fix` (systemic, climax-critical, or contract-breaking).

Blast radius tags: `Local`, `Multi-scene`, `Systemic`.

### AV — Alternative Visibility (3 flags)

**AV-1: Invisible Option Set**
Major decision presented without visible alternatives. Reader cannot test plausibility because they can't see what else the character could have done.
*Detect when*: major choice appears with no option framing; obvious alternatives absent from scene logic; decision event map shows single-path decisions where multiple paths were feasible.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`.

**AV-2: False Binary Trap**
Narrative frames two choices while credible third paths exist and are not constrained out.
*Detect when*: scene language insists on "only two options"; alternative rejection ledger shows missing constraints for plausible third paths; pattern appears at 2+ major decision points.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix` (upgrade to `Must-Fix` if climax-critical).

**AV-3: Option Dropout**
Established viable path disappears between scenes without causal explanation.
*Detect when*: prior scene seeds a viable path; later decision ignores that path with no elimination event; reader must assume off-page rationale.
*Default severity*: `Could-Fix` (upgrade to `Should-Fix` by frequency).

### CS — Constraint Specificity (3 flags)

**CS-1: Pressure Vacuum**
Decision is framed as urgent but constraint field is insufficiently concrete to force timing or selection.
*Detect when*: urgency language is high; clock, resource, or access limits are weak or absent; waiting would plausibly improve outcomes but is not addressed.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`.

**CS-2: Constraint Handwave**
"No choice" is asserted without demonstrable narrowing mechanics.
*Detect when*: text uses formulaic compulsion claims; no scene evidence shows alternatives becoming unavailable; same handwave recurs across acts.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix` (upgrade to `Must-Fix` if systemic).

**CS-3: Clock Without Teeth**
Deadline exists rhetorically but doesn't materially alter planning, risk tolerance, or option set.
*Detect when*: countdown references don't change behavior; choices before/after clock beats are functionally identical; missed clock beats carry little structural consequence.
*Default severity*: `Could-Fix` (upgrade to `Should-Fix` by frequency).

### IS — Information-State Integrity (3 flags)

**IS-1: Knowledge Cheat**
Decision depends on information the character could not plausibly possess.
*Detect when*: information-state table shows unavailable key fact; choice logic requires that fact; no inferential bridge supports access.
*Default severity*: `Must-Fix`.

**IS-2: Ignored Known Risk**
Character ignores established high-relevance risk without motive or constraint justification.
*Detect when*: risk was explicitly established; major choice proceeds as if risk absent; no emotional, strategic, or moral driver justifies bypass.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`.

**IS-3: Reader-Knowledge Drift**
Decision behavior follows reader knowledge rather than what the POV character could know.
*Detect when*: suspense structure leaks external knowledge into choices; POV behavior tracks omniscient information; reveal timing and decision logic conflict.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`.

### EC — Emotion-Cognition Coherence (3 flags)

**EC-1: Emotion-Logic Split**
Emotional state and decision behavior don't cohere. Character is labeled as feeling X but decides as if feeling Y.
*Detect when*: affect intensity is high but risk behavior unchanged; stated fear/attachment fails to influence option ranking; emotional claims conflict with enacted choices repeatedly.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`.

**EC-2: Panic Patch**
Panic is invoked to excuse unsupported decision mechanics. Panic appears only at convenience pivots.
*Detect when*: panic appears only at moments requiring irrational choice; panic doesn't degrade cognition consistently elsewhere; panic explanation appears post hoc after implausible choice.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`.

**EC-3: Convenient Stupidity**
Character competence temporarily collapses to force plot movement.
*Detect when*: capability profile drops sharply at decision-critical scenes; similar pressure contexts previously showed stronger reasoning; collapse restores immediately after pivot.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix` (upgrade to `Must-Fix` when repeated and structural).

### RF — Reasoning Fidelity (3 flags)

**RF-1: Leap Decision**
Major choice occurs without sufficient inferential bridge on page.
*Detect when*: no clear connective reasoning between pressure input and selected action; transition beat is missing or underdeveloped; readers experience abrupt "why now" confusion.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`.

**RF-2: Reasoning Retrofit**
Rationale appears after decision as patch rather than causal driver.
*Detect when*: explanatory dialogue/monologue occurs after consequence; retroactive explanation doesn't match pre-decision behavior; pattern repeats at multiple pivots.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`.

**RF-3: Motive Label Without Mechanism**
Text names a motive but doesn't show how it selected this option over alternatives.
*Detect when*: motive statement substitutes for choice logic; alternative rejection ledger remains under-evidenced; motive is generic enough to justify contradictory choices.
*Default severity*: `Could-Fix` (upgrade to `Should-Fix` by frequency).

### TR — Tradeoff Reality (4 flags)

**TR-1: No-Tradeoff Win**
Major choice delivers upside without meaningful downside, foreclosed option, or deferred cost.
*Detect when*: tradeoff ledger records gain but negligible cost; repeated major decisions preserve maximal optionality; outcomes read "best of all worlds."
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`.

**TR-2: Cost-Blind Choice**
Decision ignores obvious costs that should influence choice under established character values.
*Detect when*: cost was seeded and salient; choice bypasses cost accounting with no justification; aftermath doesn't reconcile the omission.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`.

**TR-3: Outsourced Cost**
Consequences shifted to side characters while decision-maker avoids meaningful burden.
*Detect when*: repeated decisions externalize downside; decider's future option space remains unchanged; moral/relational accounting absent.
*Default severity*: `Could-Fix` (upgrade to `Should-Fix` by recurrence).

**TR-4: Deferred Consequence Erasure**
Future costs are seeded and promised but never collected. The narrative establishes that a decision will have a price, then quietly drops the debt.
*Detect when*: text explicitly foreshadows cost; that cost never manifests or is resolved off-page; tradeoff ledger shows open entries at ending; pattern appears at 2+ major decisions.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix` (upgrade to `Must-Fix` when deferred costs are contract-level promises).

### PV — Pivot Integrity (4 flags)

**PV-1: Opaque Pivot**
Major directional turn occurs without adequate setup pressure.
*Detect when*: pivot appears sudden in decision event map; prior scenes don't accumulate compatible constraints; option elimination is missing or weak.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`.

**PV-2: Midpoint Whiplash**
Midpoint strategy reversal contradicts established decision logic without bridge.
*Detect when*: midpoint decision inverts prior risk posture; trigger is thin relative to shift magnitude; downstream behavior suggests patching rather than development.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix` (upgrade to `Must-Fix` if architecture-breaking).

**PV-3: Climax Choice Disconnect**
Final major decision conflicts with established pressure, information, or arc logic.
*Detect when*: climax grid shows weak setup trace; required alternatives were never credibly eliminated; ending requires character to choose against accumulated decision history without bridge.
*Default severity*: `Must-Fix`.

**PV-4: Resolution Jump**
Ending reconciliation choice resolves core conflict without paying decision debt accumulated through the narrative.
*Detect when*: late resolution bypasses prior tradeoff architecture; core conflict resolves through abrupt stance shift; cost ledger remains open at ending.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix` (upgrade to `Must-Fix` if contract-breaking).

## §5. Distinguish Framework

### 5.1 Required tests

1. **Option Set Test**: Are plausible alternatives visible or inferably constrained out?

2. **Constraint Test**: Are narrowing forces specific enough to justify timing and selection?

3. **Info-State Test**: Does the decision fit what the character could know, believe, and infer at that moment?

4. **Emotion-Coherence Test**: Does the emotional state plausibly alter reasoning and risk tolerance in ways that explain the choice?

5. **Tradeoff Test**: Is the gain/loss structure concrete, binding, and persistent?

6. **Pivot Preparation Test**: Are major turns built by accumulated prior pressure signals?

7. **Persistence Test**: Do decisions change later options, behavior, and stakes?

### 5.2 Classification matrix

| Test Profile | Classification | Editorial Action |
|---|---|---|
| All tests pass; mode calibration coherent | **Intentional and Successful** | Preserve architecture; tune local weak spots |
| Intentional strategy visible; 1-2 channels unstable at key decisions | **Intentional but Unstable** | Targeted revision on unstable channels |
| 3-4 pass; pattern ambiguous; may indicate emerging craft | **Ambiguous/Developmental** | Flag with specific tests for author evaluation |
| Repeated failures across channels or climax integrity fail | **Accidental Failure** | Structural recast of major decision chain |

### 5.3 False-positive controls

1. **Irrational decisions can pass** if pressure and psychology are legible. The audit tests plausibility of process, not correctness of result.
2. **Sparse interiority can pass** if option/constraint logic is externally legible. Anti-psychological style is valid when external evidence supports the choice.
3. **Deliberate ambiguity can pass** if tradeoff and consequence remain clear. Opacity about *why* is permissible when *what* is visible.
4. **Genre stylization can pass** if internal rule logic is consistent. SFF characters can reason by prophecy if prophecy-reasoning is established.
5. **One convenience choice does not prove systemic failure**; pattern evidence is required. Frequency matters more than any single instance.
6. **Flawed characters pass** when their specific flaws explain their specific choices. The flaw must be established before it drives the decision.
7. **Stress-degraded decisions pass** when the degradation model is consistent across scenes, not selective.
8. **Moral ambiguity is not causality gap** when the reader can articulate both sides of the dilemma.

## §8. Pattern Interpretation Protocol

### 8.1 Reading the flag map

When flags cluster, read the combination before prescribing.

1. **AV + CS flags — Option architecture failure.** Alternatives are invisible and constraints are unspecified. Author may know why the character chose this but hasn't built the visible architecture. Revision priority: constraint-field construction before motivation polish.

2. **IS flags clustering — Knowledge integrity failure.** Decisions depend on information the character doesn't have. Often indicates the author is writing from their own omniscient knowledge rather than the character's bounded knowledge. Revision priority: information-state mapping per decision.

3. **EC + RF flags — Motivation-cognition gap.** Emotional state is labeled but doesn't shape decision behavior; reasoning is insufficient. Often indicates author knows what the character should feel but hasn't connected feeling to action mechanism. Revision priority: emotion-cognition coherence repair.

4. **TR flags clustering — Frictionless decision chain.** Decisions happen but nothing is sacrificed. Often indicates author is protecting the character from cost. Revision priority: tradeoff insertion at major decision points.

5. **PV flags + strong earlier channels — Pivot conversion failure.** Decision architecture works through middle narrative but pivots aren't prepared. Often indicates pivots were written for structural effect (midpoint turn, climax) without building the pressure that would produce them. Revision priority: accumulated pressure construction before pivot scenes.

6. **AV + PV flags — Counterfactual vulnerability.** Reader's "why didn't they just..." questions pile up at major turns. Often indicates author wrote the desired outcome backward without seeding the constraint architecture. Revision priority: alternative-elimination scenes.

7. **IS + CS flags — Convenience-driven plot mechanics.** Characters know what they need to know and face only the constraints the plot requires. Information and constraint architecture serve plot rather than character. Revision priority: information-state audit first, then constraint design.

8. **EC flags in non-horror — Emotion-performance mismatch.** Character emotes at high intensity but decision behavior doesn't track. May indicate dialogue/monologue is doing emotional work that action contradicts. Revision priority: align decision behavior with emotional state, or recalibrate emotional display.

9. **Strong decision logic + weak Stakes — Plausible but low-pressure.** Choices are well-supported but reader doesn't feel the pressure behind them. Coordinate with Stakes System Audit. Revision priority: Stakes drives.

10. **Strong decision logic + weak Emotional Craft — Defensible but unfelt.** Choices are architecturally sound but emotionally cold. Pair with Emotional Craft. Revision priority: Emotional Craft drives.

11. **TR + PV flags at climax — Unearned resolution.** Climax resolves the narrative without paying accumulated decision debt. Revision priority: climax-specific tradeoff and pivot reconstruction.

12. **Widespread RF flags — Inferential bridge deficit.** Reader cannot track reasoning at multiple decision points. May indicate deep structural issue with character interiority access or may indicate scene-level omission of decision beats. Check whether the issue is interiority depth or scene construction. Revision priority depends on diagnosis.

### 8.2 Special caution zones

1. **Stakes System Audit overlap**: Decision Pressure evaluates choice behavior inside the pressure field. Stakes evaluates the field itself. Strong stakes + weak decisions is a Decision Pressure problem. Weak stakes + weak decisions is a Stakes problem first.

2. **Character Architecture downstream**: When EC and RF both detach, check whether character wants/fears/values are under-specified. The decision may be unsupported because the character is under-specified, not because the decision architecture is wrong.

3. **Reveal Economy upstream**: Apparent motivation failures may actually be information-timing failures. If key information the character needs to make a credible decision arrives too late or too early, the decision looks implausible even if the choice logic is sound. Pair with Reveal Economy before recasting character psychology.

4. **Scene Turn overlap**: Scenes may skip transitional decision mechanics—the beat where character weighs options and commits. Scene Turn identifies missing turns; Decision Pressure identifies why the turn feels unsupported.

5. **Mode module calibration**: Horror characters make "stupid" decisions that pass if fear is modeled. Romance characters make "irrational" decisions that pass if attachment is modeled. Before flagging implausibility, verify mode-calibrated expectations are applied.

2. interaction pattern notes,
3. mode calibration notes (§6),

### Step 6: Readiness handoff

Generate Pass 11 handoff fields:

1. decision-pressure tier,
2. top 3 readiness-critical choice failures,
3. climax pivot integrity verdict,
4. revision-depth impact statement.

## §11. Integration and Handoff

### 11.1 Recommended pairings

1. **Stakes System Audit** — when pressure field feels real but choices don't. Stakes evaluates the field; this audit evaluates behavior within it.
2. **Character Architecture** — when motivation coherence is contested. Character Architecture evaluates who characters are; this audit evaluates whether decisions track those dynamics.
3. **Reveal Economy** — when decision credibility may be info-timing driven. Apparent motivation failures may be information sequencing failures.
4. **Emotional Craft** — when choice logic is sound but emotional persuasiveness is weak. Architecture is right; transmission is failing.
5. **Scene Turn** — when transitional decision mechanics are missing. Scenes may skip the option-weighing beats.
6. **Force Architecture** — when physical confrontation decisions lack tactical plausibility. Force Architecture evaluates choreography; this audit evaluates the decisions within it.
7. **Mystery/Thriller Architecture** — when investigative decisions lack evidence logic. Information economy intersects with decision plausibility.

### 11.2 Pass 11 required handoff fields

1. Decision-pressure architecture tier.
2. Top 3 decision plausibility failures and their blast radius.
3. Climax pivot integrity verdict.
4. Hard-gate triggers and justification.
5. Readiness impact statement: how decision-pressure failure affects reader trust (reader doesn't buy choices / climax feels convenient / character feels plot-driven / etc.).

### 11.3 Dashboard integration

Feed directly into Diagnostic Dashboard decision module:

1. major decision chain map (trajectory visualization),
2. option-visibility trend (improving / stable / degrading),
3. tradeoff payment trend (accumulating / static / evaporating),
4. climax integrity status (intact / borderline / failed).

## §12. What This Audit Is Not

1. **Not a mandate for optimal character behavior.** Characters can and should make bad decisions when those decisions are pressure-driven and psychologically grounded.
2. **Not a requirement for explicit internal monologue at every choice point.** External evidence can support decision plausibility without interiority.
3. **Not anti-irrationality by default.** Irrational decisions can be more believable than rational ones when emotional state and cognitive degradation are modeled.
4. **Not a replacement for Stakes System Audit.** Diagnoses choice behavior, not the pressure field behind it.
5. **Not a genre-enforcement tool blind to mode calibration.** Mode calibration adjusts what "plausible" means.
6. **Not a demand for narrated option-weighing.** Constraint architecture can be implicit if legible.
7. **Not a prose rewrite mechanism.** Diagnoses architectural failure, not sentence-level motivation language.
8. **Not a plot invention directive.** Names where decision logic fails, not what decisions should replace them.

## §13. Firewall Compliance

This audit remains diagnostic.

**Allowed:**

1. identifying decision-pressure failures,
2. classifying severity and blast radius,
3. specifying revision scope categories,
4. recommending revision priorities by architecture level.

**Forbidden:**

1. inventing replacement plot decisions,
2. writing new internal monologue,
3. drafting scene rewrites,
4. generating alternative decision sequences,
5. mandating specific character choices.

This audit says what decision logic breaks, where it breaks, why it breaks, and what revision scope is required. It does not decide for the author.
