# Reception Risk Audit
## APODICTIC Development Editor
*Created: March 2026*
*Status: Synthesized from Opus 4.6 and Codex 5.3 research outputs*

---

## §1. Purpose

This audit identifies passages, patterns, and structural choices that create cultural, political, or ideological reception risk — content that hostile readers could weaponize or that well-meaning readers could find hurtful. It is an **over-inclusive flagging diagnostic**: the operating instruction is *flag everything a human sensitivity reader should consider, with enough context for them to make a judgment call.*

**Core problem:** Manuscripts can be aesthetically accomplished and structurally sound while containing unexamined reception risk. Core passes and the Adversarial Stress Test evaluate craft; they do not evaluate how the text will function as a cultural object. This audit fills the gap between "does the text work?" and "how will the text land?"

**Fundamental question:** What passages, patterns, or structural choices in this manuscript are likely to produce hostile reception, cultural backlash, or reader harm — and has the narrative apparatus done the work to earn, complicate, or contextualize the difficulty?

**Activation:**

- Author requests a sensitivity pre-read or reception risk assessment
- Manuscript handles identity, power, trauma, or politically charged content
- Representation Context research mode surfaces active community contestation relevant to the manuscript's content
- Editor or beta reader flags reception concern
- Author asks "how will this land?" or "will this be a problem?"

**Required inputs:**

- Complete manuscript text
- Genre and target audience identification (from intake or Pass 0)
- Publication context (trad/indie/serial/platform)
- Author's stated identity and subject position (if voluntarily disclosed — the audit operates without this but is more accurate with it)
- Representation Context research output (if available — pairs well, not required)

**Outputs:**

- Risk Map artifact (primary — passage-level flags with context, severity, and classification)
- Reception Risk audit report with flag inventory and pattern analysis
- Sensitivity Reader Handoff Memo (if human review is recommended)

**What this audit is not:**

- Not content policing (does not tell the author what they can or cannot write)
- Not a sensitivity read replacement (flags passages for human review; does not adjudicate)
- Not a political opinion audit (does not evaluate whether the author's views are correct)
- Not a craft diagnostic (the Adversarial Stress Test handles hostile craft readings)
- Not gatekeeping (does not assess who is "allowed" to write what)
- Not generic "sensitivity" advice (every flag must point to specific manuscript evidence)
- Not legal or public-relations counsel (can surface reputation-exposure patterns, but is not legal advice, crisis strategy, or compliance review)

**Non-duplication with core passes and related audits:**

- **Pass 5 (Voice and Register):** catches tone-deaf moments at the prose level. This audit catches cultural-register problems at the reception level.
- **Adversarial Stress Test:** hostile readers attack craft (pacing, logic, prose, promise). This audit's hostile readers attack politics, representation, and cultural positioning.
- **Dialectical Clarity:** evaluates whether arguments are fair. This audit evaluates whether the text will be *received* as fair — regardless of whether it is.
- **Banister (Epistemic Humility):** checks internal rhetorical honesty. This audit checks vulnerability to external extraction.
- **Representation Context:** surfaces community discourse (research mode). This audit diagnoses specific passages in the manuscript (diagnostic audit).
- **Consent Complexity:** tracks consent dynamics within the narrative's internal logic. This audit tracks how consent dynamics will be received externally.

## §2. Reception Risk Framework

Five channels diagnose different mechanisms of reception exposure. They are intentionally separable so findings can be reproduced by a second editor.

**Channel 1: Representational Risk (RR)**

How characters from marginalized or vulnerable positions are depicted, voiced, and functionally used. Stereotype activation, missing interiority, instrumental voicing, differential empathic investment.

- *Examined:* Characters from marginalized positions receive interiority comparable to other characters. Depictions that intersect with stereotypes are contextualized by narrative apparatus. The text demonstrates awareness of representational dynamics.
- *Partial:* Some characters are richly rendered; others thin at critical moments. The awareness is inconsistent.
- *Unexamined:* Characters from marginalized positions serve instrumental roles. Interiority thins when the character's experience diverges from the author's position. Stereotypical patterns are deployed without apparatus.

Detection: Track role function (agent, foil, victim, thesis vehicle, worldbuilding marker) and interiority access by group. Compare psychological complexity across characters of different identity positions, especially in high-stakes scenes. Flag persistent asymmetry in pivotal scenes.

**Channel 2: Extractability (EX)**

How vulnerable the text is to decontextualization. Passages that read differently — and more harmfully — in isolation than in narrative context.

- *Examined:* Culturally charged passages carry extraction-resistant signals: visible focalization markers, nearby counter-pressure, portable context.
- *Partial:* Some passages are extraction-resistant; others rely on fragile context (requires reading 50+ pages to understand).
- *Unexamined:* Culturally charged passages use vivid, quotable language without focalization markers or contextualizing apparatus. Extracted, they read as endorsement.

Detection: Run screenshot test on candidate passages using only 1–3 surrounding sentences. If likely public interpretation diverges sharply from in-context meaning, flag. Note whether context is portable or fragile.

**Channel 3: Power Framing (PF)**

How the narrative apparatus frames power dynamics. Whether asymmetry is marked or naturalized, whether hierarchy is interrogated or endorsed by default.

- *Examined:* Power is marked, consequences are visible, narrative distance avoids accidental endorsement. Agency is distributed.
- *Partial:* Power asymmetry acknowledged intermittently but not structurally.
- *Unexamined:* Harmful asymmetry is normalized, rewarded, or resolved without narrative accounting. Agency flows one way along power lines.

Detection: Map scenes with authority/intimacy/class/race/gender asymmetry. Track whether the narrative marks asymmetry, assigns consequences, and gives affected characters epistemic weight.

**Channel 4: Cultural Register (CR)**

Whether the text's treatment of culturally charged content matches the awareness level its mode and audience expect. Register mismatches, false universalism, default-position assumptions.

- *Examined:* Register shows contextual awareness and precise targeting. Terminology is current and appropriate for target audience.
- *Partial:* Register mostly aligned but slips during high-load moments.
- *Unexamined:* Tone/terminology repeatedly misalign with audience norms and discourse realities.

Detection: Mark culturally charged terminology, jokes, metaphors, and universalizing claims. Compare usage to mode calibration and target audience assumptions.

**Channel 5: Harm Weight (HW)**

Whether trauma, violence, or marginalized experience is load-bearing or decorative. Does the narrative bear the weight of the harm it depicts?

- *Examined:* Harm has aftermath, perspective, and structural consequence proportional to depiction. Harm to marginalized characters receives at least as much narrative investment as harm to dominant-position characters.
- *Partial:* Some harms are borne; others are decorative or unevenly weighted.
- *Unexamined:* Harm is used as atmosphere, motivation fuel, or shock without narrative weight. Suffering is distributed along identity lines.

Detection: For each major harm event, audit: who experiences consequences, whose interiority is present, and whether downstream structure acknowledges the event.

**Channel overlap and deduplication rule:**

Channels can diagnose the same passage through different mechanisms. When this happens:

1. **Assign a primary flag** based on the dominant mechanism. The primary flag determines the Risk Map entry.
2. **Note secondary flags** as corroborating evidence. Do not create separate Risk Map entries for the same passage.
3. **Cross-channel corroboration increases severity.** Two-channel corroboration elevates Note → Flag; three-channel corroboration elevates Flag → Alert.

Each channel must be observable in manuscript evidence, not theoretical mood. "This could be read as..." claims must point to specific passages.

## §3. Named Diagnostic Flags

Seventeen flags across five channels.

**Representational Risk (RR):**

| Code | Name | Definition | Detection Criteria | Default Severity |
|------|------|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|
| RR-1 | **Stereotype Echo** | Character or scenario maps onto a harmful stereotype whether or not the author intended it | Role + trait cluster reproduces a known stereotype and no adjacent scene complicates it. Pay attention to *combinations* of traits | Flag |
| RR-2 | **Interiority Gap** | Character from marginalized position lacks the inner life given to comparably important characters from the author's position | Pivotal scenes grant interiority to dominant-group characters but not to affected-group characters. Interiority thins at moments when the depicted experience diverges most from the author's | Flag |
| RR-3 | **Instrumentalized Identity** | Character's marginalized identity exists primarily to serve another character's development or the narrative's project | Character's identity is the primary thing that matters about them *to the plot*; they lack story function independent of their identity category | Flag |
| RR-4 | **Ventriloquism** | Character from marginalized position articulates ideas convenient for the narrative's thesis rather than ideas plausible for their subject position | Dialogue/internal thought aligns implausibly with narrative thesis and not character position. Test: does this sound like the author or the character? | Note |

**Extractability (EX):**

| Code | Name | Definition | Detection Criteria | Default Severity |
|------|------|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|
| EX-1 | **Screenshot-Ready** | Passage reads as inflammatory, endorsing, or gratuitous in isolation regardless of narrative context | 1–3 sentence excerpt plausibly reads as endorsement or provocation to external readers. Vivid/quotable language on culturally charged topic with no focalization markers or visible distance signals | Flag |
| EX-2 | **Collapsed Distance** | Free indirect discourse renders a character's problematic views without attribution markers, on a topic where author/character confusion creates reception risk | Character's views expressed in narrator's voice without quotation marks, dialogue tags, or distance markers, on identity, power, or culturally charged topics | Note |
| EX-3 | **Fragile Context** | Passage's meaning depends on narrative context that won't survive decontextualization | The passage requires reading 50+ pages or understanding a specific subplot/arc to register as intended. Context is not portable. Includes: irony markers that are distant or subtle enough that excerpts read literal | Note |

**Power Framing (PF):**

| Code | Name | Definition | Detection Criteria | Default Severity |
|------|------|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|
| PF-1 | **Naturalized Hierarchy** | Narrative treats a power differential as neutral, inevitable, or romantic without interrogation | Significant power asymmetry without framing cues. Includes savior dynamics where dominant-position character is the primary agent rescuing, enlightening, or liberating characters from marginalized positions | Flag |
| PF-2 | **Rewarded Harm Framing** | Harmful conduct is narratively rewarded without accounting | Harmful act leads to status, intimacy, or narrative reward without consequence. The narrative treats the harm as incidental to the reward | Flag |
| PF-3 | **Unearned Resolution** | Systemic harm resolved through abrupt forgiveness or personal growth alone | Resolution bypasses structural dimension. The "racism was solved because one person changed" ending | Flag |
| PF-4 | **Consent Ambiguity Uninterrogated** | Ambiguous consent in power dynamics is presented without inquiry | Intimate/power scene with ambiguity and no narrative interrogation in-scene or downstream. Distinct from Consent Complexity audit: this tracks *reception*, not internal narrative logic | Flag |

**Cultural Register (CR):**

| Code | Name | Definition | Detection Criteria | Default Severity |
|------|------|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|
| CR-1 | **False Universalism** | One social location is treated as the unmarked "normal" | Unmarked "people/families/real life" language excludes named groups by implication. Whiteness, heterosexuality, able-bodiedness, or middle-class status as invisible default | Note |
| CR-2 | **Tone-Load Mismatch** | Tone undercuts severity of culturally charged material | Humor/lightness/banter overlays harm topic without signaled intentionality. Beautiful prose describing violence against marginalized people. Includes: outdated or contested terminology used without contextual rationale | Flag |
| CR-3 | **Historical-Present Collapse** | Period attitudes depicted "accurately" without apparatus preventing present-day endorsement reading | Period-accurate language or attitudes harmful by contemporary standards, presented without narrative distance markers, consequences, or structural counter-pressure | Note |

**Harm Weight (HW):**

| Code | Name | Definition | Detection Criteria | Default Severity |
|------|------|-----------|-------------------|-----------------|
| HW-1 | **Decorative Trauma** | Trauma is used for mood or edge rather than narrative consequence | Harm event has vivid depiction but no proportionate aftermath or perspective. Harm primarily serves another character's development | Flag |
| HW-2 | **Disproportionate Harm Burden** | One group absorbs harm to advance others' arcs | Pattern where one demographic repeatedly suffers to motivate another. Comparable harms receive asymmetric seriousness by group | Flag |
| HW-3 | **Minor Harm Exploitation** | Harm to children/minors is used as shock propulsion without narrative burden | Minor-targeted harm appears for stakes escalation with thin aftermath/interiority. Non-negotiable across all modes | Alert |

**Severity model** (review urgency, not rewrite urgency):

- `Note` — Low urgency. Include in review scope. The passage creates mild reception risk the author should be aware of.
- `Flag` — Moderate urgency. Recommend sensitivity review. Meaningful reception risk a sensitivity reader should evaluate.
- `Alert` — High urgency. Require human review before publication decision. Significant risk that could produce backlash, harm, or reputational damage.

Severity labels deliberately differ from Could-Fix / Should-Fix / Must-Fix because this audit does not prescribe fixes. It prescribes review priority.

Note: severity indicates urgency of review, not urgency of revision. A Note might be kept as-is. An Alert might also be kept if the author decides it's load-bearing. The author decides.

Flag language must be portable to editorial reports and sensitivity-reader handoff memos.

## §4. Tracking Artifacts

### Risk Map (primary artifact)

Passage-level registry of every flagged item.

```markdown
## Risk Map — [Manuscript Title]

| # | Location | Flag | Severity | Classification | Evidence Anchor | Context Portability | Reviewer Question |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Ch.4, p.63 | EX-1 | Alert | Unexamined | "..." | Fragile | Does surrounding framing survive excerpting? |
| 2 | Ch.9, p.141 | RR-2 | Flag | Unexamined | [summary] | Medium | Why is interiority absent at peak harm scene? |
```

Rules:

1. One passage gets one **primary** flag code.
2. Secondary flags are listed in notes, not duplicated as separate entries.
3. Evidence anchors must include either a short quote or a precise summary tied to location.

Sorted by severity (Alert → Flag → Note), then by manuscript order within severity tier.

### Pattern Summary

Mechanism-level synthesis showing persistence, concentration, and cross-channel compounding.

```markdown
## Pattern Summary

| Pattern ID | Channels | Scope | Count | Severity Lift | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| P-01 | RR + HW | Systemic | 6 scenes | Note/Flag → Flag | Ch.3, Ch.8, Ch.14... |
| P-02 | EX + PF | Local cluster | 3 scenes | Flag → Alert | Ch.11-12 |
```

Pattern escalation rule:

- 1 isolated instance = incident.
- 3+ instances of same mechanism affecting same group or dynamic = pattern.
- Pattern can lift severity by one tier unless already governed by a hard gate.

### Sensitivity Reader Handoff Memo (conditional)

Generated when any `Alert` remains after calibration, or when systemic `Flag` patterns remain.

Required sections:

1. Scope lock (mode, audience, relevant identities/power dynamics).
2. Prioritized concerns by mechanism.
3. Passage packet per concern (location, summary, why flagged, what reviewer should evaluate).
4. Open questions where model confidence is low.

The Risk Map must track persistence. A one-off flag is different from a recurring pattern. The Pattern Summary makes persistence visible.

## §5. Distinguish Framework

### Classification protocol

Three-way classification for every flagged passage:

| Classification | Meaning | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Unexamined — Flag** | Risk exists and narrative apparatus does not adequately contextualize or earn it | Keep in Risk Map at assigned severity; prioritize for human review |
| **Examined — Note** | Risk exists and appears deliberate/earned, but remains socially combustible | Keep in Risk Map with reduced urgency note for reviewer |
| **False Positive — Dismiss** | Surface trigger present, but risk is negligible in mode/audience context | Keep as dismissed record in appendix (for reproducibility), exclude from priority list |

### Decision tests (apply in order)

1. **Apparatus test.** Does nearby narrative framing mark, complicate, or contextualize the risky material? Present and functional → Examined. Absent or inadequate → Unexamined.
2. **Load-bearing test.** Is the risky material essential to character, theme, or plot function? Load-bearing + no apparatus = still Unexamined. Load-bearing + apparatus = Examined.
3. **Extraction test.** If excerpted, is likely public reading materially different from in-context reading? Modifies severity, not classification.
4. **Subject-position test.** Would affected readers plausibly experience this differently than the implied reader, and is that variance anticipated in-text?
5. **Genre-contract test.** Do mode/subgenre expectations legitimately contextualize the material for the target audience?

### Tie-breaking rules

1. If extraction risk is high and apparatus is weak, classify as **Unexamined** even when load-bearing.
2. If load-bearing is high and apparatus is strong, classify as **Examined** (not dismissed).
3. Use **False Positive** only when subject-position and genre tests both support low risk.
4. Record uncertainty explicitly; unresolved uncertainty defaults to flagging, not dismissal.

### No intentionality exemption

Examined risk is still flagged with lower urgency. "The author meant to do this" is not a defense; it's a classification input. Intentional can still land badly.

### Over-inclusion principle

When in doubt, flag. A false positive that gets reviewed and dismissed costs minutes; a missed flag that produces a reception crisis costs careers.

### Lock-then-classify

Commit classification before proceeding. Classification cannot be revised by subsequent self-argument. (Anti-sycophancy / no-self-revise rule canonical in `core-editor/references/output-policy.md §Severity Honesty Protocol`.)

## §6. Mode Calibration Matrix

| Mode | Reception Promise | Named Failure Mode | Tighten On | Loosen On |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Literary fiction** | Difficulty acceptable when formally earned and ethically legible | **Aestheticized Harm** — lyrical treatment masks unexamined harm framing | EX-1, PF-2, HW-1 | CR-2 when irony is structurally clear and sustained |
| **Commercial/genre fiction** | Fast readability with clear value signaling | **Collateral Caricature** — side characters absorb stereotype shorthand for speed | RR-1, RR-2, CR-2 | EX-3 for familiar genre banter clearly in-character and bounded |
| **Historical fiction** | Period fidelity plus present-day readability of framing | **Archive Shield** — "it was historical" used to avoid narrative framing duties | PF-1, RR-1, EX-1 | CR-3 on period terms when framing clearly marks historical distance |
| **Dark romance / erotic** | Transgression central if consent/power framing is legible to target readers | **Transgression Without Containment** — taboo dynamics presented as neutral rather than intentionally bounded | PF-4, PF-2, HW-3 | CR-2 and EX-3 for consensual taboo cues explicit in-scene |
| **YA / NA** | Developmental readability and representational care for younger audiences | **Normalization Drift** — risky power or harm dynamics treated as ordinary relational behavior | PF-4, RR-2, HW-2, HW-3 | EX-3 when didactic framing would overstate risk beyond target-age interpretation |
| **Humor / satire** | Punching has a target; satire must signal direction and stance | **Target Blur Satire** — target ambiguity makes satire read as endorsement | EX-1, CR-2, PF-1 | RR-4 where target is explicit and reinforced across scenes |
| **Memoir / autofiction** | Subjective truth plus ethical framing when depicting others | **Self-Exonerating Frame** — narrator's self-justification suppresses others' subjectivity | RR-2, PF-3, HW-2 | CR-1 for voice-specific diction if harm framing remains explicit |
| **SFF** | Allegorical distance with clear internal ethics | **Allegory Evasion** — coded real-world dynamics used without responsibility for implications | RR-1, PF-1, HW-1 | CR-3 when invented lexicons are internally coherent and non-derogatory |

Mode calibration is especially important here because reception risk norms vary dramatically across genres. What's an Alert in YA may be a Note in literary fiction and a false positive in dark romance.

## §7. Severity Hard Gates

Audit-local non-negotiable severity floors. These five gates are additive to — and never substitute for — the framework-level severity-floor rules canonical in `core-editor/references/output-policy.md §Severity Floor Rules`. Hard gates override the audit's own mode-based loosening and Distinguish-framework downgrades; they raise an audit-internal flag's severity. Synthesis-layer consequence (whether a hard-gate flag becomes a synthesis Must-Fix, Should-Fix, or override-with-rationale) follows the canonical Audit-Signal Propagation Rule in `core-editor/references/run-synthesis.md §Step 2`.

| # | Condition | Minimum Severity | Evidence Requirement |
|---|-----------|-----------------|---------------------|
| 1 | **Systemic representational pattern:** ≥3 RR/HW flags affecting the same identity group across pivotal scenes | Flag | Identify the 3+ flags and the pattern they form |
| 2 | **Extractable hate material:** passage can plausibly function as hate speech, harassment bait, or dehumanizing rhetoric when excerpted | Alert | Quote the passage; demonstrate the extraction reading; note context fragility |
| 3 | **Minor-targeted exploitation:** depiction of sexualized or severe harm to minors used primarily for shock without narrative burden | Alert | Identify the scene; note the narrative treatment |
| 4 | **Repeated unmarked coercive dynamic:** PF-4/PF-2 pattern in ≥2 major scenes with narrative reward or normalization | Flag (escalate to Alert if paired with EX-1) | Identify scenes and pattern |
| 5 | **Alert concentration rule:** ≥2 independent Alert findings surviving calibration | Triggers automatic sensitivity-reader handoff recommendation | List the surviving Alerts |

Hard gates override mode-based loosening. Even in dark romance, certain reception risks have minimum severity floors.

**Constraint:** Hard gates are few and high-threshold. The audit's default mode is over-inclusive flagging with human review. Hard gates exist only for cases where the severity floor is genuinely non-negotiable.

**Relation to canonical floors:** A hard gate triggered here is a Reception-Risk-internal severity event. It does not by itself overwrite the verdict ceiling rules in `output-policy.md §Severity Floor Rules`; instead it produces an audit-internal Must-Fix-floor or Alert-class signal that must propagate to synthesis per the canonical propagation rule. If the canonical floor and a hard gate disagree, the canonical floor governs verdict ceilings; the hard gate governs the audit-internal severity assigned to the offending finding.

**Propagation to synthesis-layer triggers.** Reception Risk Alerts and §7 hard-gate signals also propagate to the Underdiagnosis Retry Loop trigger #2 (hard-gate trigger) in `core-editor/references/run-synthesis.md §Step 9`. See also the canonical Audit-Signal Propagation Rule in `core-editor/references/run-synthesis.md §Step 2` (Wave 1).

## §8. Interaction Patterns

Six high-value channel combinations where joint diagnosis is more accurate than either alone.

**1. RR + HW: "Double Exposure"**

Marginalized characters carry disproportionate harm with low interiority. Combined effect usually lifts one severity tier.

*Review order:* Evaluate HW first (is harm narratively borne?), then RR (who is bearing it and how).

**2. EX + PF: "Screenshot Endorsement"**

Unmarked coercive/power dynamics plus high extractability. The screenshot will be read as the narrative endorsing the hierarchy.

*Review order:* Resolve PF framing first; then retest extractability in excerpt form.

**3. CR + RR: "Prejudice Confirmation Loop"**

Register slips that activate stereotype schemas. The register mismatch makes the stereotype echo worse because the narrative's tone communicates it doesn't understand the weight.

*Review order:* Verify RR mechanism first (actual representational pattern), then CR (language/tone amplifiers).

**4. EX + CR: "Out-of-Context Firestorm"**

Culturally charged wording that loses nuance outside full chapter context. Most likely to produce viral backlash.

*Review order:* Calibrate CR by mode/audience first; then apply screenshot stress test.

**5. PF + HW: "Endorsed Harm Arc"**

Harmful power dynamic is both narratively rewarded and underweighted in aftermath.

*Review order:* Assess PF consequences first, then HW proportionality.

**6. RR + PF: "Voice Without Authority"**

Marginalized characters appear present but lack decision power while dominant actors set the moral frame.

*Review order:* Map PF decision authority, then audit RR interiority/agency gaps.

## §9. Audit Procedure

### Two-pass structure

The audit uses two passes to prevent premature dismissal.

**Step 1: Lock scope.**

- Confirm mode/genre, target audience, and publication context.
- Record any author-supplied subject-position notes (optional input, never required).

**Step 2: Ingest external context.**

- Read Representation Context output if available.
- List active discourse areas relevant to this manuscript.

**Step 3: Pass 1 — Over-inclusive marking.**

- Read full manuscript and mark every candidate passage.
- Do not classify, calibrate, or dismiss in this pass.

**Step 4: Pass 2 — Primary coding.**

- Assign one primary flag code per marked passage.
- Add secondary codes only in notes.
- Assign preliminary severity (Note / Flag / Alert).

**Step 5: Run Distinguish tests.**

- Apply apparatus, load-bearing, extraction, subject-position, and genre-contract tests.
- Classify each passage as Unexamined / Examined / False Positive.
- Lock classification.

**Step 6: Apply mode calibration.**

- Use §6 matrix to tighten/loosen severity where warranted.
- Record every calibration adjustment in notes.

**Step 7: Apply hard gates.**

- Check every entry against §7 floors.
- Hard gates can raise severity; they never dismiss a flag.

**Step 8: Build artifacts.**

- Finalize Risk Map.
- Build Pattern Summary with persistence counts and interaction patterns.
- Generate Handoff Memo if trigger conditions are met.

**Step 9: Synthesize report.**

- Prioritize by severity first, then by pattern significance.
- Identify top 3 reception risks affecting readiness.

**Step 10: Quality check.**

- Verify evidence anchors for all non-dismissed flags.
- Verify tone discipline (descriptive, non-accusatory).
- Verify reproducibility (another editor could rerun from record).
- Run directional check: if every Unexamined flag were addressed, what is the net word-count implication (addition vs. compression)? Flag if recommendations collectively imply >10% word-count increase — the audit may be skewing toward apparatus-building solutions that bloat the manuscript rather than structural solutions that redistribute existing weight. Note: this audit is diagnostic-only and does not prescribe rewrites, so the directional check estimates the *implicit* direction of the findings, not explicit revision instructions.

Procedure must be executable by another editor without hidden assumptions.

## §11. Integration and Handoff

### Recommended pairings

1. **Representation Context research mode** — run before or concurrently. Provides the external community discourse that this audit maps onto the manuscript.
2. **Dialectical Clarity** — for manuscripts with explicit argument, thesis, or political content.
3. **Consent Complexity** — for manuscripts with intimate content involving power dynamics.
4. **Female Interiority** — for manuscripts where interiority thinning along gender lines may create reception risk.

### Sequence position

**After** core passes (needs structural map from Pass 2, voice analysis from Pass 5, character data from Pass 4).

**Before** editorial letter synthesis so findings can inform:
- The Adversarial Stress Test (§8 of the editorial letter) — should acknowledge reception risk findings: "In addition to the craft-level concerns above, the Reception Risk audit identified [n] passages that warrant human sensitivity review before publication."
- The readiness assessment (Pass 11) — reception risk is a readiness factor.
- The rejection memo — reception risk can strengthen or complicate the rejection case.

### Pass 11 handoff fields

1. Number of Alert-level flags
2. Number of Flag-level flags
3. Whether human sensitivity review is recommended (yes/no)
4. Top 3 reception risks affecting readiness (flag code, location, severity, one-sentence summary)

### Finding-driven trigger

Representation Context surfaces active community contestation relevant to the manuscript's content → recommend Reception Risk audit if not already activated.

## §12. What This Audit Is Not

1. **Not content policing.** The audit does not tell the author what they can or cannot write. It surfaces reception risk so the author can make informed decisions. An author who reads the Risk Map and says "I know, and I'm keeping it" has used the audit correctly.

2. **Not a sensitivity read replacement.** The audit flags passages for human review. It cannot perform the work of a sensitivity reader — which requires lived experience, community knowledge, and contextual judgment that a diagnostic framework cannot replicate.

3. **Not a political opinion audit.** The audit does not evaluate whether the author's views are correct, moral, or socially acceptable. Conservative and progressive manuscripts alike can have unexamined reception risk.

4. **Not gatekeeping.** The audit does not assess who is "allowed" to write about which experiences. An author writing outside their experience may have lower reception risk than an author writing within it, depending on the narrative apparatus.

5. **Not a craft diagnostic.** If the hostile reader is attacking pacing, logic, or prose quality → Adversarial Stress Test. If the hostile reader is attacking representation, politics, or cultural positioning → this audit.

6. **Not generic "sensitivity" advice.** Every flag must point to specific manuscript evidence. "This topic is sensitive" is not a finding.

7. **Not legal or public-relations counsel.** The audit can surface reputation-exposure patterns, but it is not legal advice, crisis strategy, or policy compliance review.

## §13. Firewall Compliance

The audit is diagnostic.

**Allowed:**

1. Identify passages that create reception risk.
2. Assign severity and classify as Unexamined / Examined / False Positive.
3. Specify scope for human review.
4. Recommend sensitivity reader engagement.
5. Note whether the narrative apparatus earns, complicates, or fails to address the difficulty.

**Forbidden:**

1. Rewrite passages to reduce reception risk.
2. Generate replacement prose.
3. Tell the author what they should believe or value.
4. Adjudicate whether a flagged passage is morally acceptable.
5. Declare that an author should not write about a particular topic.
6. Reduce severity ratings through self-generated counter-arguments after locking. (Anti-sycophancy / no-self-revise rule canonical in `core-editor/references/output-policy.md §Severity Honesty Protocol`.)

The audit surfaces risk. The author decides what to do about it.

## Generic Flag Coding Pattern

Channel prefixes aligned to the 5 channels:

1. `RR` — Representational Risk
2. `EX` — Extractability
3. `PF` — Power Framing
4. `CR` — Cultural Register
5. `HW` — Harm Weight

Numbered flags: `RR-1`, `RR-2`, `EX-1`, etc.

Rules:

1. Avoid overlapping meanings across codes.
2. One code = one failure mechanism.
3. One passage = one primary code (+ optional secondary notes).
4. Severity is stored separately: `Note`, `Flag`, `Alert`.
5. Classification is stored separately: `Unexamined`, `Examined`, `False Positive`.
6. Pattern IDs use `P-##` and reference grouped evidence entries in the Pattern Summary.
