# Specialized Audit: Force Architecture
## APODICTIC Development Editor v0.4.14.3
## Three-Model Synthesis (Opus46 + Codex53 + Gemini)
*Audit Classification: Cross-Genre Craft*

---

## §1. Purpose

This audit evaluates whether physical conflict functions as a coherent narrative engine.

It is not an "action amount" meter.
It is not a violence permissibility test.

### Core problem

The core failure is **inert force**.

A manuscript can deliver kinetic scenes, weapons, injury, tactical variation, and visceral prose while leaving agency, stakes, theme, and reader understanding unchanged.

**The fundamental question: Do force events produce legible, causal, persistent, meaningful change, or are they spectacle loops?**

### Non-duplication boundaries

1. **Scene Turn** checks whether scenes turn. This audit checks force-specific turn quality—legibility, tactical causality, competence-risk tension, cost persistence, escalation architecture, and meaning conversion within physical conflict.
2. **Emotional Craft** checks affect transmission. This audit checks force-specific cost and conversion—does the event generate and sustain the emotional architecture it requires?
3. **Horror Craft** checks dread architecture. This audit checks force architecture across horror and non-horror. When force is dread-bearing, both audits apply; they diagnose different dimensions.
4. **Mystery/Thriller Architecture** checks information economy. This audit checks physical confrontation systems—the choreography itself, not the information management around it.
5. **Character Architecture** checks motive/coherence. This audit checks whether force tests and transforms character architecture.

### Activation conditions

Activate when either:

1. user explicitly requests this audit,

or

2. at least 2 of the following are true:
   - repeated action/fight/combat set pieces,
   - beta complaints about unreadable fights,
   - escalation fatigue or "all fights feel the same,"
   - injuries/costs seem to reset,
   - force scenes feel exciting but empty.

### Inputs required

1. Contract and genre calibration outputs.
2. Pass 1 Reader Experience notes.
3. Pass 3 Rhythm/Modulation notes.
4. Pass 5 Character Architecture notes.
5. Pass 6 Scene Function notes.
6. Optional Emotional Craft and AI-Prose overlays.
7. Optional mode modules (Horror, Mystery/Thriller, SFF, Military-adjacent).

## §2. Force Delivery Framework

### 2.1 Core channels

Assess six channels:

1. `LG` Legibility
2. `TC` Tactical Causality
3. `CR` Competence-Risk Tension
4. `CP` Cost Persistence
5. `ES` Escalation Kind-Shift
6. `MC` Meaning Conversion

### 2.2 Channel ratings

| Rating | Operational meaning |
|---|---|
| **Integrated** | Channel reliably shapes comprehension and consequence under pressure |
| **Partial** | Channel works in pockets but collapses in high-load sections |
| **Detached** | Channel appears as surface signal with weak structural effect |

### 2.3 Force centrality profile

Before assigning severity, classify manuscript force burden:

1. **Low-force burden**: few force events; high conversion expectation per event.
2. **Medium-force burden**: recurring force events, mixed scene types; persistence and variation required.
3. **High-force burden**: force is primary experience engine; legibility, escalation, and persistence failures compound quickly.

### 2.4 Channel definitions

#### A) LG: Legibility

**Question:** Can readers track who did what, where, when, and with what result?

**Integrated when:**
1. actor identity and position remain trackable,
2. event order is reconstructable,
3. objective shifts are visible,
4. POV maintains a cognitive anchor—someone is perceiving this.

**Detached when:**
1. space becomes a white room,
2. actor chains blur under speed,
3. scene outcome is understood only after exposition patching,
4. interiority vanishes and text delivers choreography from no particular cognitive position.

#### B) TC: Tactical Causality

**Question:** Do force outcomes follow constraints, resources, capabilities, and environment?

**Integrated when:**
1. plans meet friction and adaptation,
2. outcomes follow seeded capability rules,
3. tactical choices alter next-scene options,
4. environment participates as independent constraint.

**Detached when:**
1. wins rely on unseeded advantage,
2. causality becomes "and then and then,"
3. environment exists decoratively,
4. plans execute without degradation under pressure.

#### C) CR: Competence-Risk Tension

**Question:** Does skill remain exciting without erasing downside?

**Integrated when:**
1. capable characters still face credible loss,
2. threats behave competently enough to pressure choices,
3. victory requires tradeoff,
4. execution degrades realistically under stress.

**Detached when:**
1. protagonist competence nullifies risk,
2. opponents are degraded to preserve momentum,
3. tension depends on fake peril language,
4. survival probability is visibly governed by plot rather than scene causality.

#### D) CP: Cost Persistence

**Question:** Do force events leave durable physical, psychological, relational, resource, or moral cost?

**Integrated when:**
1. injuries and resource loss affect later performance,
2. relational trust/fear changes persist,
3. decision behavior mutates after major force events,
4. collateral damage is acknowledged and consequential.

**Detached when:**
1. injury resets after scene break,
2. cost is narrated but not enacted,
3. aftermath never changes planning or relation structure,
4. environmental and civilian damage vanishes from narrative attention.

#### E) ES: Escalation Kind-Shift

**Question:** Does conflict evolve in kind, not only in scale?

**Integrated when:**
1. later force events introduce new strategic problems,
2. escalation deepens moral/relational pressure,
3. climax integrates prior escalation logic,
4. structural weight correlates with narrative significance.

**Detached when:**
1. battles get bigger but not different,
2. set pieces repeat one dominant pattern,
3. escalation collapses at the payoff,
4. minor skirmishes receive more structural weight than major confrontations.

#### F) MC: Meaning Conversion

**Question:** Do force choices convert into character, relationship, and theme movement?

**Integrated when:**
1. force reveals priorities and limits,
2. outcomes alter relationship/power geometry,
3. thematic claims are embodied through force consequences,
4. removal test: excise the event and downstream decisions change.

**Detached when:**
1. violence solves logistics but avoids core conflict,
2. set pieces are cool but narratively non-transformative,
3. manuscript could remove major force events with minimal thematic loss.

### 2.5 Anchor line

**Force architecture fails when event intensity rises faster than consequence and meaning conversion.**

## §3. Named Diagnostic Flags

25 flags across six families.

Severity defaults: `Could-Fix` (local, low blast radius), `Should-Fix` (repeated, multi-scene), `Must-Fix` (systemic, climax-critical, or contract-breaking).

Blast radius tags: `Local`, `Multi-scene`, `Systemic`.

### LG — Legibility (5 flags)

**LG-1: White-Room Melee**
Action occurs without stable spatial model. Environment exerts no influence on choreography.
*Detect when*: reader cannot map positions over 3+ beats; movement vectors missing or contradictory; environment details do not constrain choices.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`.

**LG-2: Teleport Bodies**
Characters appear in impossible positions between adjacent beats.
*Detect when*: beat geometry contradicts prior placement; no transition action explains shift.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`.

**LG-3: Actor Chain Blur**
Reader loses clear actor identity under speed.
*Detect when*: pronoun-heavy chains obscure who acts/reacts; multi-opponent sequences use interchangeable references; consequences assigned after the fact.
*Default severity*: `Could-Fix`.

**LG-4: Sensory Flatline**
Force event narrated through single sensory channel without perceptual shifts stress produces.
*Detect when*: extended action relies exclusively on visual narration; no proprioceptive, auditory, temporal, or pain-state information.
*Default severity*: `Could-Fix` (upgrade to `Should-Fix` if close POV and character has no reason for detachment).

**LG-5: Disembodied Combatant**
POV character's interiority vanishes during force events. Text delivers choreography from no particular cognitive position.
*Detect when*: POV shifts from close narration to camera-like or omniscient perspective without textual justification; character narrates movements they could not perceive from their position.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix` (upgrade to `Must-Fix` if POV consistency is a contract promise).

### TC — Tactical Causality (3 flags)

**TC-1: And-Then Chain**
Events stack sequentially without causal hierarchy.
*Detect when*: 4+ consecutive beats connected only by temporal sequence; action choices do not change threat state.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`.

**TC-2: Ruleless Advantage**
Outcome relies on capability or rule not previously available.
*Detect when*: decisive advantage appears first at payoff point; established constraints selectively suspended; post-scene explanation required to justify outcome.
*Default severity*: `Must-Fix` at climax, `Should-Fix` elsewhere.

**TC-3: Frictionless Execution**
Plans execute without uncertainty, error, or adaptive cost.
*Detect when*: plan succeeds exactly as stated in high-pressure context; no environmental or informational disruption; success cost is rhetorical not structural.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`.

### CR — Competence-Risk (4 flags)

**CR-1: Godmode Drift**
Protagonist competence removes meaningful risk over sustained sections.
*Detect when*: threats fail to constrain options across 3+ encounters; protagonist repeatedly exits with negligible downside; tension depends on narration claiming danger.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`; `Must-Fix` if systemic in high-force manuscripts.

**CR-2: Threat Collapse**
Opponents functionally downgraded to preserve protagonist momentum.
*Detect when*: antagonist choices repeatedly ignore established skill/intelligence; threat behavior downgrades at confrontation moments; conflict difficulty is performance theater.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`.

**CR-3: Plot Armor Leak**
Survival probability logic is visibly external to scene causality.
*Detect when*: implausible escapes recur without new cost; similar secondary characters receive harsher consequences; survival cannot be justified by established rules/resources.
*Default severity*: `Must-Fix` if repeated.

**CR-4: Flawless Execution**
Characters execute complex maneuvers under extreme stress without error or fine-motor degradation.
*Detect when*: narrative fails to account for physiological impact of adrenaline on performance; competence appears frictionless at body level even when tactical friction exists.
*Default severity*: `Could-Fix` (upgrade to `Should-Fix` in military/thriller/crime modes where physiological realism is contract-adjacent).

### CP — Cost Persistence (5 flags)

**CP-1: Rubber-Band Injury**
Injury appears in-scene and vanishes in later performance.
*Detect when*: major wound has no effect on movement/decision in subsequent scenes; recovery timeline absent or impossible by contract.
*Default severity*: `Must-Fix` when systemic.

**CP-2: Resource Theater**
Resource constraints deplete rhetorically, not architecturally.
*Detect when*: resource panic appears without downstream scarcity effects; equipment/logistics reset between scenes.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`.

**CP-3: Trauma Erasure**
Psychological impact of force events produces no behavioral change.
*Detect when*: character experiences events that would produce psychological response but subsequent behavior shows no trace. Not the same as suppressed response (which is itself behavioral change).
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix` (upgrade to `Must-Fix` if text elsewhere claims the character is affected).

**CP-4: Collateral Erasure**
Bystanders, environments, and secondary characters absorb damage never acknowledged.
*Detect when*: environmental destruction, civilian harm, or ally injury absent from subsequent narrative attention.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix` (upgrade per mode—superhero/speculative and crime/noir tighten on collateral).

**CP-5: Relationship Reset**
Force-event relational consequences do not persist.
*Detect when*: betrayal, rescue, or harm does not alter trust behavior; power dynamics return to pre-event baseline; later scenes ignore force-linked relational debt.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`.

### ES — Escalation (4 flags)

**ES-1: Scale-Only Escalation**
Scale rises while conflict type remains unchanged.
*Detect when*: each set piece repeats same tactical/emotional logic; threat count grows without new decision burden.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`.

**ES-2: Combat Monotone**
Action rhythm uses one dominant pattern across multiple events.
*Detect when*: 3+ force events follow same structural template; similar opening, exchange, and exit beats.
*Default severity*: `Could-Fix`; upgrade to `Should-Fix` when multi-scene.

**ES-3: Escalation Cliff**
Build-up underdelivers at major confrontation.
*Detect when*: climax resolves too quickly relative to setup load; key pressures bypassed rather than paid off; culmination does not integrate prior conflict threads.
*Default severity*: `Must-Fix` if climax-critical.

**ES-4: Pacing-Significance Inversion**
Structural weight afforded to minor events exceeds weight allocated to major ones.
*Detect when*: word count and scene elaboration for minor skirmishes exceed those for narrative-critical force events.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`.

### MC — Meaning Conversion (4 flags)

**MC-1: Violence Shortcut**
Fight replaces unresolved moral, relational, or psychological conflict.
*Detect when*: physical resolution ends tension that required non-physical reckoning; post-event core conflict remains structurally unaddressed.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`; `Must-Fix` when repeated near major pivots.

**MC-2: Cool-Kill Drift**
Style emphasis suppresses intended ethical or thematic meaning.
*Detect when*: tonal framing celebrates events manuscript later frames as tragic; narratorial framing conflicts with stated thematic stance.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix`.

**MC-3: Aftermath Null**
Major force events produce no durable interpretive residue.
*Detect when*: no shift in worldview/relationship/strategy follows major event; thematic thread does not absorb force outcome.
*Default severity*: `Must-Fix` when systemic.

**MC-4: Conversion Failure**
Scene is vivid and well-choreographed but does not change decision logic in later scenes.
*Detect when*: apply removal test—excise the force event and nothing downstream changes.
*Default severity*: `Should-Fix` (upgrade to `Must-Fix` if event is positioned as narratively significant).

## §4. Tracking Artifacts

### 4.1 Artifact A: Force Event Ledger (required)

| Event ID | Chapter/Scene | POV | Tactical Objective | Key Constraint | Outcome | Immediate Cost | Abstraction Level |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Rules:
1. Include all major force events and any minor event that changes direction.
2. Mark objective type: survive, escape, capture, protect, delay, defeat.
3. Immediate cost cannot be "none" in high-force manuscripts without justification.
4. Abstraction Level (Granular / Mid / Compressed) tracks authorial choice without penalizing non-granular approaches.

### 4.2 Artifact B: Consequence Continuity Ledger (required)

| Character | Source Event | Physical Cost | Psychological Cost | Relational Cost | Resource Cost | Moral Cost | Persistence Window | Behavioral Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Rules:
1. Track persistence for at least 3 downstream scenes after each major event.
2. If no persistence appears, mark as candidate CP-1, CP-3, CP-5, or MC-3.
3. Include group/system costs when relevant (unit trust, legal risk, collateral pressure).
4. Behavioral Evidence column requires specific citation—not "character was affected" but "character refused to enter the building (Ch. 14), flinched at raised voice (Ch. 16)."

### 4.3 Artifact C: Escalation Ladder Map (required)

| Act/Unit | Dominant Force Mode | Conflict Type | New Problem Introduced | Kind-Shift? | Stakes Shift | Payoff Status | Dominant Flags |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Rules:
1. "Kind-Shift?" cannot be "yes" by scale alone.
2. Require at least one explicit strategic or ethical kind-shift per major act in high-force works.
3. Map unresolved pressure entering climax.

### 4.4 Artifact D: Legibility Snapshot Grid (recommended)

| Scene | Actor Clarity | Spatial Clarity | Sequence Clarity | POV Consistency | Cognitive Load Rating |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Use when beta reports "couldn't follow the fight" or when LG flags cluster. Cognitive Load Rating (Low/Medium/High/Overload) is calibrated to genre expectations.

## §5. Distinguish Framework

### 5.1 Required tests

1. **Reconstructability Test**: Can a careful reader reconstruct the force event's spatial and causal chain? (Intentional abstraction on Hayakawa's Ladder that maintains reconstructability passes.)
2. **Constraint Consistency Test**: Do outcomes obey established capability/resource/environment rules?
3. **Risk Reality Test**: Do skilled characters still face material downside? (Check all threat vectors: physical, environmental, informational, temporal, moral.)
4. **Persistence Test**: Do force costs alter later behavior and relations? (Specific behavioral evidence required.)
5. **Kind-Shift Test**: Does escalation change problem type?
6. **Conversion Test**: Does force alter character/theme pressure? (Apply removal test.)
7. **Cognitive Anchor Test**: Does the force event maintain a legible POV position—someone perceiving, filtering, and responding? (Dissociation, shock, and trained-response narration can be the anchor.)

### 5.2 Classification matrix

| Test Profile | Classification | Editorial Action |
|---|---|---|
| All or nearly all pass; style clearly intentional | **Intentional and Successful** | Preserve architecture; note technique; tune local clarity only |
| Intentional strategy visible; 1-2 channels unstable | **Intentional but Unstable** | Targeted revision on failing channels |
| 3-4 pass; pattern ambiguous; may be emerging craft or unrecognized habit | **Ambiguous/Developmental** | Flag with specific tests for author evaluation |
| Multiple test failures; no stable intentional pattern | **Accidental Failure** | Structural recast of force architecture |

### 5.3 False-positive controls

1. Sparse style can pass if reconstructability is high.
2. Stylized abstraction can pass if persistence and conversion remain strong.
3. High competence can pass if risk is real and costs stick.
4. Quiet aftermath can pass if consequence is behaviorally visible.
5. Protagonist survival can pass if justified by established rules/resources.
6. Withdrawn POV can pass if dissociation/shock is the cognitive anchor.

## §6. Mode Calibration Matrix

| Mode | Integration Promise | Named Failure Mode | Tighten On | Loosen On |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Military/War** | Tactical coherence with command and psychological consequence | **Map-Table Heroics**: omniscient strategic clarity replaces fog-of-war experience | Chain-of-command logic, fog/friction, casualty persistence, unit dynamics, orders-vs-morality tension | Exhaustive hardware detail |
| **Systems-Driven Progression** | Readable progression pressure, combat-as-character-expression | **Stat-Sheet Spectacle**: mechanical escalation without strategic or ethical shift | Kind-shift challenge design, power-system consistency, capability constraints, cost-to-advancement rate | Literal simulation realism if system logic is consistent |
| **Thriller Action** | Urgency, resource strain, competence under uncertain information | **Perpetual Chase Blur**: continuous motion without accumulating consequence | Clock consequence, resource accounting, environmental improvisation, seeded reversals | Full tactical decomposition; long-term psychological rumination |
| **Horror Violence** | Force deepens dread and violation consequence | **Shock Carousel**: rotating gore moments that discharge rather than build dread | Aftermath residue, identity cost, threat-logic progression, dread accumulation | Strict tactical realism if dread logic holds; protagonist tactical failure can be structural necessity |
| **Crime/Noir** | Violence under social corruption and moral compromise | **Clean Hit Myth**: violence resolves cleanly without social/legal/moral fallout | Messiness of close-in violence, legal/social repercussion, reputation dynamics | Full procedural granularity; protagonist outside law is genre-normative if consequences track |
| **Domestic Violence Drama** | Patterned coercion, escalation realism, durable agency effects | **Incident Theater**: domestic violence as isolated incidents rather than pattern system | Control-pattern continuity, isolation/dependency, credibility asymmetry, survivor intelligence, recovery as structural | High explicit-event frequency; kinetic choreography |
| **Literary Violence** | Force as ethical and perceptual transformation | **Aphoristic Carnage**: violence aestheticized into philosophical set pieces without residue | Conversion to recognition, residue in language/structure, moral persistence, image-system mutation, thematic alignment | Explicit tactical granularity; fast pacing |
| **Superhero/Speculative** | Scaled power with consequence architecture | **City-Scale Weightlessness**: catastrophic collateral with no narrative or moral accounting | Collateral accounting, power-rule consistency, moral tradeoff visibility, power-scale legibility | Literal physics; internal system consistency replaces thermodynamic realism |

## §7. Severity Hard Gates

Apply these floors regardless of local prose quality.

| Condition | Minimum Severity |
|---|---|
| Climax force sequence fails Reconstructability Test | `Must-Fix` |
| Major injury/trauma has no downstream behavioral effect | `Must-Fix` |
| Protagonist competence nullifies risk architecture across 2+ acts | `Must-Fix` |
| Climax force event fails Conversion Test | `Must-Fix` |
| Outcome depends on unseeded rule/capability in final confrontation | `Must-Fix` |
| Antagonist capability collapses at climax to enable protagonist victory | `Must-Fix` |
| Escalation is scale-only across 2+ major units | `Should-Fix` (minimum) |
| Repeated major force events fail meaning conversion | `Should-Fix`; upgrade to `Must-Fix` when climax-adjacent |
| Force event POV consistency breaks at key moment without justification | `Should-Fix` (minimum) |

## §8. Pattern Interpretation Protocol

### 8.1 Reading the flag map

When flags cluster, read the combination before prescribing.

1. **LG + TC flags**: Comprehension architecture failure. Text is not readable as action *and* does not follow its own rules. Often indicates the author visualizes the scene but does not translate the visualization to page. Scene-level rewrite, not line-level polish.

2. **CR + MC flags**: Spectacle-without-stakes syndrome. "Good fights, empty book." Characters are impressive but unchallenged; force events don't convert to meaning.

3. **CP flags clustering in bridge scenes**: Consequence-reset pattern. Force events are consequential in-scene but the narrative resets between encounters. Scene-level craft is strong; manuscript-level architecture is weak.

4. **ES flags + Combat Monotone**: Escalation failure. Manuscript adds force events without evolving them. Common in progression fiction and series drafts.

5. **MC flags at climax + clean middle**: Conversion engine works during rising action but fails at the peak. Climax designed for spectacle rather than transformation.

6. **LG-5 (Disembodied Combatant) pattern across manuscript**: Systematic POV failure in force scenes. Author's force-scene technique defaults to a different narrative mode than their non-force technique.

7. **TC-2 (Ruleless Advantage) at resolution points**: Deus ex machina pattern. Author writes toward outcomes and reverse-engineers capability.

8. **CP-4 (Collateral Erasure) + MC-3 (Aftermath Null)**: Consequence-free violence. World doesn't register what happened. Most damaging in modes promising social/environmental consequence.

9. **Strong LG + weak CP**: Choreography is readable but narratively forgettable. Prioritize persistence architecture.

10. **Strong ES in scale + weak kind-shift**: Likely ES-1 even with high energy. Check whether new strategic or ethical problems actually emerge.

### 8.2 Special caution zones

1. **Horror Craft overlap**: When force is dread-bearing, both audits apply. Force Architecture evaluates mechanics; Horror Craft evaluates dread function. A force event can pass this audit and fail Horror Craft (dread discharged rather than deepened).

2. **Mystery/Thriller Architecture overlap**: When force events contain or reveal information, coordinate with Mystery/Thriller audit. A fight that functions as clue delivery must satisfy both force mechanics and information economy.

3. **Character Architecture downstream**: Force events producing CP or MC flags should cross-reference Character Architecture for downstream agency and coherence effects.

4. **Emotional Craft upstream**: Force events failing MC flags may indicate upstream Emotional Craft problems—affect transmission infrastructure wasn't built before the event attempted to use it.

5. **AI-Prose Calibration**: Force scenes are particularly susceptible to AI-prose tells—competent surface, no cognitive anchor, frictionless execution. When action reads fluent but generic, pair with AI-Prose audit.

## §9. Audit Procedure

### Step 1: Claim lock

Record:
1. force centrality profile (low/medium/high),
2. intended mode burden(s),
3. declared thematic stance on force.

### Step 2: Build artifacts

1. Force Event Ledger.
2. Consequence Continuity Ledger.
3. Escalation Ladder Map.
4. Legibility Snapshot Grid (if LG concerns present).

### Step 3: Rate channels

Rate LG, TC, CR, CP, ES, MC as Integrated/Partial/Detached with evidence.

### Step 4: Apply flags

1. Assign flags with frequency and blast radius.
2. Apply hard-gate floors (§7).
3. Run distinguish tests (§5).

### Step 5: Synthesize

Produce:
1. top failure patterns (§8),
2. mode calibration notes (§6),
3. revision-order recommendation by blast radius.

## §11. Integration and Handoff

### 11.1 Recommended pairings

1. **Horror Craft** — when force carries dread burden.
2. **Mystery/Thriller Architecture** — when force and clock/clue systems interact.
3. **Emotional Craft** — when aftermath transmission is disputed.
4. **Character Architecture** — when competence-risk or motive coherence is unstable.
5. **AI-Prose Calibration** — when action reads fluent but generic.

### 11.2 Pass 11 required handoff fields

2. Top 3 systemic force flags.

## §12. What This Audit Is Not

1. **Not a content-censorship mechanism.** Evaluates craft, not appropriateness. Graphic violence can pass; sanitized violence can fail.
2. **Not anti-violence by default.** Diagnoses inert force, not excessive force. Frequent effective violence scores higher than rare hollow violence.
3. **Not a realism-purity test.** Stylized, fantastical, and system-driven force events are evaluated on internal consistency and narrative function.
4. **Not a demand for maximal tactical detail.** Compressed, abstract, and summary-level narration can pass all channels.
5. **Not a replacement for Scene Turn, Emotional Craft, or mode modules.** Each diagnoses dimensions this audit does not attempt.
6. **Not a choreography-aesthetics scorecard.** Beautifully written fights that fail conversion are diagnosed. Rough fights that convert powerfully are praised.
7. **Not a violence-maximalism standard.** The most effective force architecture often involves restraint, compression, and off-page implication.

## §13. Firewall Compliance

This audit remains diagnostic.

**Allowed:**
1. identifying structural failures in force architecture,
2. naming vulnerability patterns,
3. specifying severity and blast radius,
4. recommending revision priorities by architecture level.

**Forbidden:**
1. rewriting action scenes,
2. generating replacement choreography,
3. inventing new plot events,
4. drafting line-level prose fixes.

This audit says what breaks, where it breaks, why it breaks, and what revision scope is required. It does not write the manuscript for the author.
