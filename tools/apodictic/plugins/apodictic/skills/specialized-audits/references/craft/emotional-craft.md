# Specialized Audit: Emotional Craft Diagnostics
## Version 1.1
*Last Updated: February 2026*

---

## Purpose

Detect and diagnose when a manuscript is structurally competent but emotionally flat, or when emotion exists on the page but does not transmit to the reader. Provide specific flags and revision targets without inventing content.

**Core claim:** Emotion on the page is not a label ("she was furious"). It is a meaning pipeline—perception → interpretation → judgment → impulse → choice—that the reader can experience. When the pipeline is intact, the reader feels. When it breaks, the reader is told.

**Deficit-First Diagnostic Rule:** Do not evaluate emotional craft by validating the presence of feeling-words, affectively charged scenes, or emotionally loaded dialogue. You must hunt for the *absence* of the meaning pipeline — the *absence* of perception, interpretation, judgment, and impulse that convert labeled emotion into transmitted experience. A scene where "she was devastated" appears without embodied perceptual evidence fails the transmission diagnostic, even if the prose is grammatically accomplished. You are auditing for where the current dies, not where the vocabulary gestures at feeling.

**What this audit is:** A transmission diagnostic. It identifies where emotional current dies and names the mechanism.

**What this audit is not:** A substitute for Plot Architecture (spine mechanics) or Character Architecture (psychology/agency). Those audits determine whether the story works structurally and whether characters are psychologically coherent. This audit determines whether that structural and psychological competence reaches the reader as felt experience.

**When to activate:**
- Pass 1 (Reader Experience) flags emotional distance, flatness, or "technically good but not moving"
- Pass 4 (Emotional Value Tracking) finds static scenes on valence/intensity/certainty axes
- Character Architecture shows solid AQ/CQ but choices don't land emotionally
- Dialogue passes functional checks but lacks subtext or consequence
- Author or beta readers report "I don't care about the characters" despite sound structure
- High exposition density where reader emotion drains out
- Narrative voice feels generic or interchangeable

**Optional spot-check use:** Run on 1–2 representative scenes without full trigger conditions. The quiet bridge scene (conversation, morning after, walk to the car) is often the more diagnostic target—intense scenes usually have some emotional transmission from event pressure alone. It's the bridge scenes where the pipeline breaks down and slack accumulates.

## Vocabulary

### Emotional Current

The continuous felt pressure that makes the reader lean forward even between big plot turns. Not "high stakes" but felt consequence in the moment. The sensation that something is forming, shifting, or at risk on every page.

### Microtension

Line-to-line friction created by contradiction, misalignment between what's said and meant, a suppressed impulse, an uneasy interpretation, ambiguity with stakes, or a value judgment forming.

Microtension ≠ conflict scene. It is the page never going emotionally smooth.

### Meaning Pipeline

The chain through which emotion transmits from page to reader:

**Perception** → **Interpretation** → **Judgment** → **Impulse** → **Choice/Action** → **Consequence**

- **Perception:** What the POV notices (sensory detail, social cue, environmental selection)
- **Interpretation:** What it means to them (not neutral observation—filtered, biased, loaded)
- **Judgment:** Value stance ("unfair," "dangerous," "humiliating," "beautiful," "wrong")
- **Impulse:** Urge (attack, withdraw, confess, hide, protect, test, provoke, submit)
- **Choice/Action:** What they do or don't do
- **Consequence:** What changes (even slightly—a relational shift, a lost opportunity, a revealed truth)

When all links are present, the reader experiences the emotion. When a link is missing, the reader is informed about it.

### Emotional Mode

The channel used to transmit feeling:

| Mode | Mechanism | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Behavioral** | Action, gesture, choices | She set the cup down without drinking. |
| **Perceptual** | What the POV notices/filters | He noticed the exit first, then the people. |
| **Cognitive** | Interpretation, reasoning | This was the third time she'd said "fine." |
| **Somatic** | Body sensation | Her jaw ached from holding it still. |
| **Relational** | Status, attachment, power shifts | He used her first name for the first time. |
| **Symbolic** | Objects/imagery carrying emotion | The permit was still in her purse, unsigned. |

### Emotional Layering

Multiple simultaneous emotions, especially:
- Stated vs. felt
- Desired vs. feared
- Pride vs. shame
- Love vs. resentment
- Duty vs. longing

Single-note emotion reads as simple. Layered emotion reads as human.

### Moral Stakes

The values at risk: fairness, dignity, loyalty, betrayal, mercy, cowardice, cruelty, debt, obligation, responsibility, autonomy, belonging. Moral stakes convert events into meaning.

Distinguished from **plot stakes** (danger, deadline, consequence). A scene can have high plot stakes and zero moral stakes—mechanically tense but emotionally inert.

### Emotional Turn

A change in the emotional state or stance of the POV character, or in the reader's stance toward what's happening. Softening, hardening, resolve, disgust, admiration, grief, relief—any shift in the felt register.

### Emotion Labeling

Direct naming of emotion ("angry," "sad," "devastated," "hurt") or stock physiological signals used as substitutes for the meaning pipeline. The label replaces the experience.

## Relationship to Existing Passes

This audit does not duplicate existing infrastructure. It refines and extends specific passes:

**Pass 4 (Emotional Value Tracking) detects the symptom; this audit diagnoses the mechanism.** Pass 4 tracks whether scenes shift on valence, intensity, and certainty axes. When Pass 4 flags a static scene, this audit explains why—where the meaning pipeline breaks, which slack types are present, whether moral stakes are absent.

**Character Architecture's Interiority Function Check identifies coarse-grain problems; this audit provides fine-grain diagnosis.** The Interiority Function Check tags passages as "reveals character," "builds pressure," "delays," "substitutes." This audit's Meaning Pipeline Audit explains *where within* a flagged passage the chain collapses—perception without interpretation, judgment without impulse, choice without felt cost.

**Pass 7 (Voice/POV) identifies voice indistinctness; this audit identifies the mechanism.** Pass 7's Blind Swap test and Interiority Markers analysis detect interchangeable voices. This audit's microtension scan (particularly S9: Filterless POV) diagnoses *why*—the POV isn't selecting perceptions under emotional pressure, so everything gets equal descriptive weight.

**Pass 1 (Reader Experience) provides the trigger; this audit provides the investigation.** Pass 1 flags "flat," "distant," "I don't care." This audit explains the craft-level cause.

## Scope Selection

This audit runs on selected scenes, not necessarily the full manuscript.

### Default Scope: 2 Scenes

Pick one of these pairs:

| Pair | Scene A | Scene B | Why |
|------|---------|---------|-----|
| **Intensity test** | A scene that should be intense (confrontation, revelation, climax) | A quiet bridge scene (conversation, transition, aftermath) | Tests both ends of the emotional register |
| **Opening + midpoint** | Opening scene/chapter | One midpoint scene | Tests whether the manuscript establishes and sustains emotional current |
| **User-nominated** | The section the author/reader identifies as "flat" | One scene the author considers successful | Compares working vs. non-working craft in the same manuscript |

### When to Expand Scope

- If both sample scenes show the same slack types → the problem is likely manuscript-wide; expand to 4–6 scenes
- If sample scenes show different problems → the issue is local; targeted fixes may suffice
- If the manuscript is uniformly flat across Pass 4 → run the full audit on representative scenes from each act

### When NOT to Run This Audit

- Material has no text yet (notes, premise only) → use Franklin Pathway instead
- Structural problems dominate (no spine, no protagonist) → fix structure first; emotional craft diagnosis on a structurally broken manuscript wastes effort
- The manuscript is emotionally effective but structurally unsound → the emotion is working; the other passes are more urgent

## The Diagnostic Procedure

### Step A: Pass-Linked Symptom Summary

Before running any new analysis, compile what earlier passes already found. Each scene audit begins by quoting upstream signals—this prevents re-deriving what the Editor has already diagnosed and ensures the Emotional Craft audit starts from evidence, not from scratch.

**For each selected scene, collect:**

```
Scene [X]: [Title/location]
  Pass 1 (Reader Experience): [relevant flags — "flat," "told," "mechanical," "generic," or "no flags"]
  Pass 4 (Emotional Value Tracking): [valence/intensity/certainty findings for this scene, or "not yet run"]
  Character Architecture: [interiority function flags, AQ notes, or "no flags"]
  Pass 7 (Voice/POV): [Blind Swap results, interiority markers, or "no flags"]
  Other signals: [beta reader reports, author self-diagnosis, or "none"]
```

**If a pass has not yet run:** Note "not yet run" and proceed. The Symptom Summary captures what's available, not what's mandatory.

**What this step prevents:** Running a full Emotional Intent Map that re-derives what Pass 4 already found. Running a full pipeline audit on scenes that Pass 1 flagged as effective. Wasting diagnostic effort on scenes where the problem is structural (routed to Pass 2 or Plot Architecture), not emotional craft.

**Output:** One Symptom Summary block per selected scene. This summary anchors all subsequent steps.

### Step B: Emotional Intent Map

This step identifies the *designed reader emotion* for each scene—what the scene is trying to make the reader feel. This is the layer Pass 4's valence/intensity/certainty axes don't capture.

**This is not a re-run of Pass 4.** Pass 4 tracks whether scenes shift on measurable axes. This step names the intended emotional experience and checks whether the scene turns emotionally. Where Pass 4 has already produced findings for a scene, reference them; do not re-derive.

For each selected scene, answer:

**1. Dominant intended reader emotion(s)** (1–3):
Dread, tenderness, anger, admiration, disgust, grief, shame, awe, relief, yearning, hope, unease, complicity, pity, contempt, protectiveness, helplessness, recognition, longing, claustrophobia, vertigo.

**2. Secondary/contrast emotion(s):** What complicates the dominant emotion? (If nothing: flag as single-note.)

**3. Emotional arc within the scene:** Start state → shift → end state. Does the scene turn emotionally, or does it end where it began?

**4. Emotional turn location:** If the scene turns, where? If it doesn't, flag: "No emotional turn—scene is emotionally static."

**Failure signal:** The scene has no emotional intent beyond "clarify plot" or "deliver information."

**Output format:**
```
Scene [X]: [Title/location]
  Intended emotion(s): [list] | Evidence: [specific textual markers]
  Contrast emotion(s): [list or "none—single-note"]
  Emotional arc: [start] → [shift] → [end]
  Turn location: [specific passage] or "No turn"
```

### Step C: Microtension Scan

Read line-to-line through each selected scene and mark spans where the emotional surface becomes frictionless—where the prose could be skimmed without the reader losing felt experience.

For each slack span, assign one or more causes from the Slack Taxonomy:

#### Microtension Slack Taxonomy

| Code | Name | Description |
|------|------|-------------|
| **S1** | Pure reportage | Events narrated with no interpretation or judgment. Things happen; nothing means anything. |
| **S2** | Explanation slab | Context delivered without pressure. Information the reader needs but with no emotional current carrying it. |
| **S3** | Emotion labeling | Emotion named directly ("angry," "heartbroken," "terrified") instead of embodied through the meaning pipeline. |
| **S4** | Generic signals | Stock physiological tells ("heart pounding," "stomach dropped," "breath caught") doing all emotional work. |
| **S5** | No consequence | Actions occur but nothing risks changing. Events without felt cost. |
| **S6** | No relational charge | Interactions lack status, attachment, or power stakes. People occupy the same space without affecting each other. |
| **S7** | No contradiction | Character is emotionally single-track. No competing impulses, no internal friction. |
| **S8** | Unarmed dialogue | Characters say what they mean. No subtext, no avoidance, no hidden agenda, no stakes. |
| **S9** | Filterless POV | The voice doesn't select meaningfully. Everything described with equal weight, equal attention, equal affect. |
| **S10** | Over-smoothing | Transitions and clarifications remove uncertainty too early. The prose explains away tension before the reader can feel it. |

**Output format:**
```
Slack span: [quote excerpt or location reference]
  Type(s): [S-code(s)]
  What it costs the reader: [specific emotional loss]
```

**Firewall note:** Identify what's missing (friction type), not what to invent.

### Step D: Meaning Pipeline Audit

For at least 3 key beats per scene, fill the pipeline template. Prioritize beats flagged in Step A (Symptom Summary) or identified as slack spans in Step C.

```
Beat [N]: [brief description]
  Perception:      [what POV notices]
  Interpretation:  [what it means to them]
  Judgment:        [value stance]
  Impulse:         [urge]
  Choice/Action:   [what they do]
  Consequence:     [what changes]

  Breakpoint:      [B-code or "none"]
```

#### Pipeline Breakpoint Codes

Parallel to the S-codes for microtension slack, the B-codes name where the meaning pipeline collapses. Each code identifies a specific missing link in the chain.

| Code | Name | Description | Severity |
|------|------|-------------|----------|
| **B1** | No interpretation | Events without meaning; perception present but POV assigns no significance. The character notices but doesn't process. | High — this is where most "competent but inert" prose breaks |
| **B2** | No judgment | Meaning without values; interpretation present but no stance taken. The character processes but doesn't care. | High — removes moral stakes |
| **B3** | No impulse | Flat cognition; character thinks and judges but doesn't want to act. Insight without urgency. | Medium — can be acceptable in reflective passages if brief |
| **B4** | Choice without cost | Action taken but no consequence registered. The character acts but nothing changes. | High — erodes reader investment |
| **B5** | Consequence stated not felt | Change named but not experienced by POV or reader. "Everything was different now." | Medium — often caused by S3 (labeling) |
| **B6** | Pipeline present but generic | All links present but none are particularized to this character, this situation, this moment. Any character would process it the same way. | Medium — produces competent but impersonal prose |

**Cross-reference with Character Architecture:** If the Interiority Function Check flagged specific passages as "substitutes for action" or "delays," the Meaning Pipeline Audit explains *where* those passages break down structurally. B1 and B2 are the most common breakpoints in passages flagged as "substitutes."

**Cross-reference with Step A:** If the Symptom Summary shows Pass 4 flagged a scene as static, the B-codes explain the mechanism—which pipeline link is failing. If the Symptom Summary shows Pass 1 flagged "told" or "mechanical," B5 and B4 are the likely culprits.

### Step E: Moral Stakes Audit

For each scene, identify:

**1. Which values are in play?**
Dignity, loyalty, fairness, mercy, status, belonging, autonomy, responsibility, honesty, courage, compassion, justice, duty, self-preservation, integrity.

**2. Who is judging whom, and by what standard?**
The POV character judges others (and is judged) against implied values. Name the standard.

**3. What would be shameful/noble/cruel/brave here?**
As implied by the POV voice and the narrative stance. If nothing would be shameful or noble, the scene has no moral stakes.

**Failure signal:** Scene has plot stakes (danger, money, deadline) but no value stance. Events happen; nothing means anything morally. This is the "mechanically tense" scene—technically competent, emotionally inert.

**Output format:**
```
Scene [X]: Moral stakes [PRESENT / ABSENT / GENERIC]
  Values in play: [list]
  Judgment: [who judges whom, by what standard]
  Evidence: [quote or reference where the value surfaces in the prose]
```

**GENERIC** means values are implied by situation (any reasonable person would feel X) but the POV voice doesn't particularize them. Generic moral stakes produce generic emotion.

### Step F: Layering & Contradiction Audit

For each scene, classify the emotional register:

| Register | Description | Microtension? |
|----------|-------------|---------------|
| **Single-note** | One declared feeling sustained without complication | Low—reads as simple |
| **Layered** | Two or more competing feelings present simultaneously | High—reads as human |
| **Masked** | Expressed feeling differs from underlying feeling (anger covering hurt, compliance covering rage) | High—creates dramatic irony |
| **Oscillating** | Shifts between emotions without clear causal trigger | Flag—may indicate inconsistent characterization rather than complexity |

**Look for:**
- Defended emotion (anger covering vulnerability, humor covering fear)
- Conflicted desire (wants X and fears X simultaneously)
- Simultaneous impulses (protect and punish, flee and confront, confess and conceal)
- Contradiction between body and speech, between thought and action

**Failure signal:** Characters react on rails. Stimulus → single predictable response → no internal friction. The reader knows what the character will feel before they feel it.

**Output format:**
```
Scene [X]: [Single-note / Layered / Masked / Oscillating]
  Evidence: [specific passages]
  Produces microtension: [Y/N; how]
```

### Step G: Revision Targets

Produce firewall-safe surgery targets. These specify the *type* of move needed at a specific location, not the content.

#### Allowed Move Types

| Move | What It Does |
|------|-------------|
| **Replace label with pipeline** | Swap direct emotion naming for meaning pipeline evidence (interpretation + judgment). |
| **Increase perceptual selectivity** | Make what the POV notices carry emotion—select details under emotional pressure rather than reporting neutrally. |
| **Convert generic to personal** | Replace stock signals (heart pounding) with the specific personal logic of why this detail matters to this character. |
| **Re-arm dialogue** | Identify where dialogue is too explicit; target for subtext, avoidance, misdirection, or hidden agenda. |
| **Insert uncertainty** | Identify where the prose explains away tension prematurely; target for delayed resolution. |
| **Attach consequence** | Identify actions without felt cost; target for even small relational, psychological, or situational shifts. |
| **Add contradicting layer** | Identify single-note passages; target for a second, competing emotional register. |
| **Particularize moral stakes** | Identify generic value situations; target for the specific value judgment this POV character would make. |

**Output format:**
```
Target: [scene/paragraph/line location]
  Problem: [slack type or pipeline breakpoint]
  Allowed move(s): [from the list above]
```

**Firewall compliance:** No invented scenes. No new plot events. No specific dialogue or prose. The audit identifies what the prose needs to do differently; the writer provides the content.

## Common Patterns

Named diagnostic patterns this audit is designed to catch.

### Pattern 1: Competent But Inert

**Signature:** S1 (reportage) + S5 (no consequence) + B1 (no interpretation).

The prose is clean, the events are clear, the structure works. But the POV doesn't interpret, doesn't judge, doesn't want. Events happen without meaning. The reader understands everything and feels nothing.

**Typical cause:** The writer is narrating from outside the character's emotional logic—describing what happens rather than what it means to the person it's happening to.

### Pattern 2: Melodrama / Overwrite

**Signature:** S3 (labeling) + S4 (generic signals) + B6 (pipeline present but generic).

The emotion is on the page—loudly, insistently, with exclamation. But the meaning pipeline is bypassed: instead of perception → interpretation → judgment, the prose jumps to label + stock body response. "She was devastated. Her heart shattered. Tears streamed." The reader is told the intensity; they don't experience the cause.

**Typical cause:** The writer feels the emotion and assumes naming it transmits it. The fix is almost always backing up to perception and interpretation—*what did she notice, and what did it mean to her?*

### Pattern 3: Cinematic But Empty

**Signature:** S1 (reportage) + S9 (filterless POV) + B1 (no interpretation) + B2 (no judgment).

The prose reads like a screenplay: action, dialogue, setting, movement. Vivid, kinetic, well-paced. But no interiority—no interpretation, no judgment, no impulse. The camera is on but no one is behind it. The reader watches but doesn't feel.

**Typical cause:** The writer has strong visual/dramatic instincts but hasn't built the POV's emotional filter into the narration.

### Pattern 4: Neutral Voice

**Signature:** S9 (filterless POV) + perceptual selectivity absent + generic moral stakes.

The prose describes everything with equal attention, equal affect. Details don't carry weight because they're not selected under emotional pressure. The voice could belong to anyone in any situation.

**Cross-reference:** Pass 7's Blind Swap test often flags this same problem at the voice level. The Emotional Craft audit identifies the mechanism: the POV isn't filtering through values, fears, or desires.

### Pattern 5: Informational Dialogue

**Signature:** S8 (unarmed dialogue) + S6 (no relational charge) + S5 (no consequence).

Characters exchange information efficiently. Questions get answered. Exposition is delivered. But no one has a hidden agenda, no one is avoiding a subject, no one's status shifts, and nothing said has consequences for the relationship. Dialogue functions as data transfer, not as action.

**Typical cause:** The writer is using dialogue to solve plot problems (character needs to learn X) without loading the exchange with relational or moral stakes.

## Genre Calibration

The audit's expectations adjust by genre. What constitutes appropriate microtension and emotional transmission differs across genre contexts.

### By Genre Module

**Romance / Erotic:** Perceptual selectivity and relational charge are critical. S6 (no relational charge) is a high-severity flag. The meaning pipeline should foreground desire, vulnerability, and power shifts. Somatic and relational emotional modes carry primary weight. Layering (want vs. fear, desire vs. distrust) is the engine.

**Horror (Psychological):** Perceptual selectivity drives dread. S9 (filterless POV) is high-severity—in horror, *what the character notices* is the primary fear mechanism. The pipeline's interpretation link should generate unease, not clarity. Certainty axis from Pass 4 is the key cross-reference. Single-note emotion (pure dread) can be acceptable for sustained passages if it's escalating.

**Thriller / Suspense:** S1 (reportage) in action sequences may be acceptable—lean, kinetic narration doesn't always need interpretation at every step. But bridge scenes between action sequences need full pipeline engagement. S5 (no consequence) between set pieces is high-severity. Decision density (from the Thriller genre module) should correlate with moral stakes density.

**Literary Fiction:** Full pipeline expected throughout. S1 is high-severity in any passage. Perceptual selectivity is the primary voice mechanism. Moral stakes should be particularized, not generic. Layering and contradiction are baseline expectations, not bonuses. S7 (no contradiction) is higher-severity here than in any other genre.

**Mystery / Investigation:** Pipeline engagement varies by scene type. Investigation scenes may legitimately foreground cognitive mode (interpretation, deduction). But S6 and S8 in interview/interrogation scenes are high-severity flags. The moral stakes audit is critical for distinguishing mechanical puzzle-solving from felt investigation.

**Science Fiction / Fantasy:** Worldbuilding exposition (S2) requires extra scrutiny. The Context Smother Diagnostic from the Franklin Pathway may be relevant here. Pipeline engagement should be maintained even during world-exposition by filtering technical/speculative content through the POV's emotional logic.

## Integration with Core Framework

### Module Position

This is a specialized audit that runs after or alongside relevant passes, producing its own output section. It stacks with genre modules and other specialized audits.

### Loading Trigger

Activate when ≥2 of the following appear:

1. Pass 1 flags emotional distance/flatness
2. Pass 4 finds static scenes (no movement on valence/intensity/certainty)
3. Character Architecture shows solid AQ but choices don't land
4. Pass 7 flags voice indistinctness that may stem from emotional craft
5. Author/beta reader reports of "technically good but not moving"
6. High exposition density across multiple scenes

### Pass Modifications

**Pass 1 (Reader Experience):** When flagging emotional distance, tag the specific quality: "flat" (no emotion present), "told" (emotion labeled but not transmitted), "mechanical" (events have stakes but no felt consequence), "generic" (emotion present but impersonal). These tags help the Emotional Craft audit target its investigation.

**Pass 4 (Emotional Value Tracking):** When a scene scores as static on all three axes, note whether the stasis appears to be structural (scene lacks a turn) or craft-based (scene has a turn that doesn't transmit). Structural stasis routes to Pass 2 or Plot Architecture. Craft-based stasis routes here.

**Pass 5 / Character Architecture:** When the Interiority Function Check flags passages as "delays" or "substitutes for action," cross-reference with the Meaning Pipeline. A passage that "substitutes" may have a pipeline breakpoint at interpretation (events described without meaning) rather than being structurally unnecessary.

### Output Delivered

The full audit produces:

1. **Pass-Linked Symptom Summary** (per scene: what earlier passes already found)
2. **Trigger rationale** (which earlier signals fired and scope selected)
3. **Emotional Intent Map** (per scene: intended emotions, contrast, arc, turn)
4. **Microtension Scan** (slack spans with S-codes and reader cost)
5. **Meaning Pipeline Audit** (3+ beats per scene with B-codes flagged)
6. **Moral Stakes Audit** (per scene: present/absent/generic with evidence)
7. **Layering & Contradiction Audit** (per scene: register classification with evidence)
8. **Revision Targets** (firewall-safe: location + problem + allowed move type)

All outputs anchored to specific passages in the manuscript.

### Coaching in the Editorial Letter

The diagnostic procedure above identifies *what* is broken and *where*. When the editorial letter is written, it may include coaching guidance for writers who need it—explaining *why* a B-code or S-code matters, offering the logic behind an allowed move type, or providing examples of the kind of craft move that addresses a specific pattern. This coaching belongs in the deliverable (the editorial letter), not in the diagnostic specification. The audit stays diagnostic; the letter can teach.

---

*This audit diagnoses emotional transmission—why structurally competent prose doesn't reach the reader as felt experience. It identifies where the meaning pipeline breaks (B-codes), where microtension goes slack (S-codes), and where moral stakes are absent or generic. The system diagnoses craft-level mechanisms; the writer provides the emotional content.*
