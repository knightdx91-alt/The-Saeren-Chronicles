## Version 0.1.0
*Drafted: March 2026*
*Synthesized from five-model research (Claude, Codex, Gemini, GPT, Claude research)*
*Status: Specialized audit — genre-specific (horror subgenre)*

## §1. Purpose

This audit evaluates whether a manuscript's supernatural apparatus produces the felt experience of violated reality — wrongness, otherness, haunting pressure, sacred dread, impossible consequence — or merely delivers supernatural-coded content: ghosts, demons, curses, haunted spaces, possessions, revenants, and occult systems that move plot without altering the reader's model of the world.

**The core problem is not unbelievability. It is inert supernaturality.** A manuscript can contain apparitions, possession, ritual, sacred language, family curses, uncanny architecture, and metaphysical threat while remaining experientially naturalized. The supernatural exists in the summary, perhaps even in the lore, but not in the reading experience. The ghost is there. The wrongness is not.

The audit's fundamental question:

**Does the supernatural in this manuscript feel like a breach of order, or does it function as genre furniture?**

### Anchor insight

Supernatural horror fails when the manuscript confirms the impossible in plot terms but never forces the reader to live under its consequences. This is structurally parallel to decorative worldbuilding and ornamental literary craft: the labor is visible, the genre markers are present, the intended effect is detached. A haunting that could be replaced by "and then the ghost did something eerie" without changing the reader's felt relation to reality is the supernatural equivalent of lore that never changes decision-making or prose that never changes perception.

This is a mechanism audit, not a belief audit. The question is not whether ghosts, demons, saints, curses, ancestral spirits, or sacred forces are "real" in any external sense. The question is whether the manuscript constructs them as load-bearing pressures inside its own contract.

### Relationship to existing infrastructure

**Horror Craft Integration** asks whether horror lands as dread, uncertainty, consequence, atmosphere, and ending residue. This audit asks a narrower and more specific question: **does the horror feel supernaturally wrong?** It inherits Horror Craft's concern with pressure but focuses on belief thresholds, supernatural otherness, haunting phenomenology, rule pressure, and the management of impossible escalation. Horror Craft is the parent audit; this is the child. Almost always pair them.

**SFF Worldbuilding Integration** handles internal consistency of speculative systems. This audit does not replace that work. It asks whether a consistent supernatural system produces dread rather than merely competence-puzzle interest. A perfectly coherent ghost system can still fail this audit.

**Stakes System** tracks cost and consequence. This audit tests whether the supernatural changes the character and reader's sense of reality, not just the plot's risk profile.

**Emotional Craft** diagnoses transmission of fear, awe, despair, and revulsion at a general level. This audit diagnoses supernatural-specific transmission failures: when the impossible is explained, displayed, or named without becoming experientially wrong.

**Consent Complexity** may need to pair when the supernatural operates through bodily invasion, possession, coercive haunting, or autonomy collapse.

**Reception Risk** may need to pair when religious, spiritual, or culturally specific supernatural systems are central. This audit evaluates craft delivery, not doctrinal or representational appropriateness.

### Hard boundary

This audit ONLY evaluates ontological violation mechanics — whether the supernatural produces felt breach of reality. It does not evaluate general pacing (Horror Craft: DA), general atmosphere (Horror Craft: AP), or general stakes (Stakes System). Overlap is inevitable at the edges, but if a flag could fire equally in a non-supernatural horror manuscript, it belongs to the parent audit.

### When to activate

- Any manuscript where supernatural horror is the primary contract
- Ghost stories, possession narratives, haunted-house novels, curse narratives, demonic/religious horror, mythic horror, dark fairy tales, and agency-bearing cosmic horror
- Dark fantasy or gothic manuscripts where the author wants the supernatural to feel frightening rather than merely atmospheric
- Cases where beta readers say "I know the book is supernatural, but I didn't feel haunted"
- Cases where the supernatural seems decorative, overexplained, or oddly consequence-free

### Required inputs

1. Pass 1 Reader Experience notes or equivalent felt-response observations
2. Pass 10 entity/state tracking for supernatural elements when available
3. Horror Craft Integration audit if already run
4. SFF Worldbuilding notes if the manuscript uses explicit supernatural rules

### Outputs

This audit produces:

1. Channel ratings for the supernatural delivery system
2. Named flag inventory with severity and blast radius
3. Belief-threshold and rule-ledger findings
4. Distinguish classification: working, restrained/ambiguous, mode-appropriate but unstable, or inert
5. Mode calibration notes
6. Structural revision priorities

### Exclusions

Do not run when:

1. The manuscript uses one or two supernatural flourishes but does not claim a supernatural-horror burden
2. The work is still pre-draft ideation only
3. The primary problem is simple world-rule inconsistency with no horror ambition
4. The author wants only theological or folklore fact-checking

## §2. Supernatural Delivery Framework

Supernatural horror is not just horror plus magic. It is a pressure system with a distinct burden: the manuscript must convert the impossible into an experienced disturbance of reality. That burden breaks into seven observable channels.

### Seven Integration Dimensions

**1. BT: Belief Threshold Management**
The transition from ordinary explanation to supernatural acceptance.

*Producing when:* The manuscript establishes a world ordinary enough for violation to register, then manages a progressive narrowing of natural explanations. The reader crosses into supernatural acceptance with increased dread, not decreased immersion. Hesitation is productive rather than static. Three threshold strategies are valid: *gradual bleed* (ambiguity narrows to confirmation), *immediate violation* (supernatural present from page one, accepted within the operative belief world), and *oscillation* (confirmation never fully arrives, and the sustained uncertainty is the method). Each has distinct failure modes — gradual bleed risks belief crash, immediate violation risks normalization, oscillation risks Scooby-Doo drift.

*Inert when:* The supernatural is asked for too early, too abruptly, or too vaguely. The reader either refuses the premise or stops caring whether the premise is supernatural at all. Token skepticism persists after it stops generating pressure.

**2. WO: Wrongness and Otherness**
Whether the supernatural feels like a breach of order rather than a dangerous object with paranormal delivery.

*Producing when:* The impossible disturbs presence, absence, causality, category boundaries, taboo, or sacred/profane order. The threat feels other, not merely hostile. Wrongness manifests through two channels: the *weird* (presence of that which does not belong — cognitive excess, category violation) and the *eerie* (failure of presence or absence — agency where none should exist, void where life should be). In modes that promise numinous terror, Otto's *tremendum* (overwhelming dread before the wholly other) and *fascinans* (magnetic allure of the unknown) coexist. The body registers wrongness before the mind comprehends it: trembling, fleeing, closing doors, somatic revolt.

*Inert when:* The supernatural becomes familiar furniture, monster-of-the-week behavior, or generic danger with a spooky label. The entity is present in plot terms but absent as ontological wrongness. Or: the supernatural is rendered with such composed, polished prose that the narrator's rhetorical control implicitly reassures the reader that the impossible is manageable.

**3. HP: Haunting Propagation**
Whether supernatural contact contaminates body, mind, behavior, relationship, and environment beyond the set-piece where it appears.

*Producing when:* Encounters alter sleep, speech, ritual behavior, domestic routine, trust, self-recognition, family geometry, and subsequent scene logic. The haunting is not episodic; it propagates. In possession narratives, the host retains traceable interiority — remnants of self, horrifying lucidity within the occupation — so the mode's intimacy survives. The supernatural spreads through proximity, penetration (it gets inside — space, mind, body), and inevitability (it becomes unavoidable).

*Inert when:* Apparitions and manifestations occur, but characters recover into ordinary scene behavior. The supernatural affects incident count more than lived condition. In possession narratives, the host vanishes into blank puppetry and the mode loses its core horror: the witness within the occupied self.

**4. RL: Rule Pressure and Ritual Cost**
The management of supernatural rules, operative constraints, lore delivery, and attempted remedies.

*Producing when:* The manuscript teaches enough about the supernatural to sharpen fear, narrow options, and force costly choices. Rules create trap-logic: they close escape routes and constrain characters into worse positions. Rituals, investigations, exorcisms, protections, and bargains intensify dread rather than solving it cheaply. Lore arrives through traumatic trial and error, not convenient exposition. Information that confirms the worst suspicion while suggesting something worse beyond it increases dread.

*Inert when:* Exposition outruns experience, the rules over-regularize the threat, or the resolution becomes procedural. Rules create game-logic: they provide a winning strategy. Knowledge makes the supernatural smaller instead of worse. Remedy demands technique rather than irreversible cost. Explanation functions as a solvent that dissolves the supernatural's power.

**5. MS: Moral / Historical / Belief Substrate**
Whether the supernatural is attached to a load-bearing pressure system rather than dropped in as swappable plot machinery.

*Producing when:* The haunting belongs to a specific family, place, institution, history, taboo, grief, injustice, inheritance, oath, sacrilege, or belief world. The supernatural feels necessary to this manuscript, not portable to any manuscript. Religious, folkloric, or spiritual systems shape behavior, interpretation, and consequence — the cosmology is inhabited, not displayed. Two failure extremes must be avoided: moral vacuum (the ghost is random, the haunting meaningless) and moral overcoding (the ghost is didactic, understanding the moral logic resolves the dread).

*Inert when:* The ghost, curse, or demon has backstory but not substrate. The story can explain the entity without giving the manifestation deeper necessity. Belief systems serve atmosphere or exotic texture rather than operative worldview.

**6. IE: Impossible Escalation**
How the manuscript deepens threat when the threat is not constrained by natural means.

*Producing when:* Each escalation changes the reader's relationship to the supernatural. Exits narrow, reality grows less sealable, ordinary coping proves inadequate in new ways. The manuscript escalates through three vectors: *intimacy* (the threat gets closer personally — it knows the character's name, then fears, then memories), *ontological threat* (the threat changes what reality is — the character's identity becomes uncertain), and *moral implication* (the threat reveals something about the character worse than the threat itself). The macro-structure is spiral, not linear: returning to the same ground with deeper and darker implications each time. The environment thickens — atmosphere coagulates, paths loop back, reality becomes impeded by unseen forces, and ordinary lateral movement away from the threat becomes impossible.

*Inert when:* Escalation occurs by addition only: more signs, more phenomena, more bodies, more lore, more hauntings, but no deeper violation or narrower horizon. Characters keep solving, escaping, rationalizing, or outrunning the threat as if it were merely difficult rather than supernaturally asymmetrical. Or: once the explanatory pattern is uncovered, the supernatural threat becomes structurally smaller — the house, ghost, or curse loses appetite once understood.

**7. RR: Residue and Resealing Failure**
Whether the ending leaves the reader feeling that reality has been altered, contaminated, or only partially resealed.

*Producing when:* The ending preserves or productively transforms supernatural pressure. Explanation does not shrink the threat. The reader carries residue: dread, wrongness, uncertainty, awe, or the sense that some breach remains. In the strongest supernatural horror, temporal retroactivity operates — the "it was always here" revelation changes the meaning of earlier events, violating the reader's timeline as well as the character's.

*Inert when:* The ending cleans the world too thoroughly, explains away the pressure, or resolves the supernatural through operational procedure without lasting cost or altered reality. Late explanation makes the threat smaller rather than deeper. The reader closes with understanding but without dread.

### Integration Levels

| Level | Operational definition |
|---|---|
| **Producing** | Channel actively generates supernatural pressure. Removing it would reduce the felt breach of reality. |
| **Partial** | Channel works intermittently. Present in setup or isolated scenes but unstable under stress. |
| **Inert** | Channel is present in summary terms but detached in experience. Could be reduced to synopsis without major loss of supernatural force. |

### Quick diagnostic statement

**Supernatural horror fails when the text verifies the impossible but never makes the reader inhabit a world where the impossible has become consequential.**

## §3. Named Diagnostic Flags

Severity scale: `Could-Fix` (local), `Should-Fix` (repeated or multi-scene), `Must-Fix` (systemic, climax-critical, or contract-breaking).

### BT Flags (Belief Threshold Management)

**BT-1: Belief Crash**
The text asks the reader to accept the supernatural before credibility, anomaly pattern, or ordinary-life grounding has been earned.
- *Detect:* The first major supernatural claim lands before the manuscript has given the reader either a stable normal model or a progressive anomaly track. Readers report disbelief rather than dread.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if the crash happens at the manuscript's main threshold crossing.

**BT-2: Threshold Stall**
The manuscript withholds confirmation so long that hesitation stops being productive and becomes drag.
- *Detect:* The reader can no longer tell whether the book wants supernatural uncertainty or simple ambiguity drift. Suspicion does not intensify across successive anomalies. More than one act passes with no genuine narrowing of explanations.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if sustained through the primary threshold sequence.

**BT-3: Courtesy Skepticism**
Token natural explanations are kept alive out of habit rather than pressure design.
- *Detect:* Skeptical alternatives persist formally after the manuscript has ceased making them interesting, plausible, or consequential. The reader feels the story is "pretending" to hedge. The text seems embarrassed by its own premise.
- *Default severity:* Could-Fix to Should-Fix depending on recurrence.

### WO Flags (Wrongness and Otherness)

**WO-1: Supernatural Tourism**
The manuscript displays eerie or occult phenomena for fascination value rather than felt wrongness.
- *Detect:* The reader is shown striking supernatural imagery but experiences curiosity or admiration more than ontological disturbance. Scene energy comes from coolness or visual spectacle, not breach. Entities are encountered with awe but no *tremendum*; characters observe the impossible without somatic or psychological destabilization.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix.

**WO-2: Numinous Collapse**
The supernatural loses otherness and shrinks into an ordinary antagonist with paranormal methods.
- *Detect:* A demon, god-touched force, curse, or sacred threat behaves with overly legible motive, familiar banter, or ordinary villain logic. Awe, taboo, or metaphysical scale drop out. A cosmic entity monologues about its desire for revenge or power, rendering its psychology disappointingly mundane.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix in demonic, religious, cosmic, and mythic modes.

**WO-3: Reported Haunting**
The prose reports supernatural events instead of enacting altered perception, embodiment, or cognition.
- *Detect:* Key encounters are summarized from outside — "she saw a ghost" rather than the rendered perceptual experience of encountering the impossible. The reader knows something impossible happened but does not undergo the destabilization of contact. The processing gap where the character's rational framework fails is skipped.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if the manuscript depends on immersive supernatural experience.

**WO-4: Static Wrongness**
The same eerie register repeats until the reader habituates.
- *Detect:* Atmosphere, anomaly type, and supernatural pressure remain tonally unchanged over long stretches. The manuscript can signal "this is haunted" but not escalate how haunting feels. Wrongness does not vary in kind.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix.

**WO-5: Abject Sterilization**
Encounters with the dead, possessed, or supernaturally transformed ignore the somatic reality of boundary violation.
- *Detect:* Possession, haunting, or bodily transformation is rendered without the physical wrongness of category collapse — a body that is simultaneously self and other, living and dead, inside and outside. Possession features glowing eyes and deep voices but ignores the horror of a body physically turning against itself. The supernatural is sanitized of its boundary-breaking elements.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix in possession, body-invasion, and transformation narratives.

**WO-6: Empathy Dissolution**
The supernatural entity is given so much sympathetic backstory that it ceases to function as a source of horror.
- *Detect:* Understanding the entity's pain, motivations, and tragic origins converts it from threat to character. Comprehension entirely extinguishes dread rather than complicating it. The reader's empathy displaces their fear.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Could-Fix if the empathy serves a deliberate genre-hybrid contract (gothic romance, literary ghost story).

### HP Flags (Haunting Propagation)

**HP-1: Consequence Compartmentalization**
The supernatural affects plot outcomes but not interior life, relationship geometry, or scene behavior.
- *Detect:* Characters witness or survive supernatural contact and then behave as though they experienced only an unusual event, not a changed reality. Consequence stays local to the scene. A ghost manifests, and in the very next scene characters are eating breakfast and discussing logistics without signs of ontological shock.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if repeated across major manifestations.

**HP-2: Decoration Haunting**
Manifestations remain atmospheric ornaments rather than structural forces.
- *Detect:* Apparitions, sounds, cold spots, visions, or poltergeist incidents do not redirect decisions, alter stakes, or mutate relational dynamics. Creepy occurrences happen constantly but do not change the characters' situations or force them into corners.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix.

**HP-3: Host Hollowing**
In possession narratives, the possessed character loses too much interiority and becomes a blank puppet.
- *Detect:* After possession begins, the host has no traceable inner presence, no remnants of self, and no meaningful witness response to that loss. The mode's intimacy — the horror of watching selfhood erode from within — vanishes. Binary switching (normal/possessed) replaces gradient erosion.
- *Default severity:* Must-Fix in possession-primary manuscripts.

**HP-4: Backstory Over Appetite**
The supernatural past is vivid, but the present-tense hunger of the haunting is weak.
- *Detect:* The manuscript can explain who the ghost was or what happened historically, yet current manifestations lack urgent desire, pattern, or agency. The ghost has become an archive problem rather than a lived threat.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix.

### RL Flags (Rule Pressure and Ritual Cost)

**RL-1: Premature Taxonomy**
The ghost, demon, curse, or occult system is classified too completely too early.
- *Detect:* Naming, ranking, mythologizing, or cataloging the supernatural converts dread into manageable information before pressure has matured. Characters uncover ancient texts or experts who perfectly explain the entity's history and rules before the midpoint. The text converts the unknowable into the merely unknown, then into the known, collapsing the dread gradient.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if classification occurs before the manuscript's primary atmospheric peak.

**RL-2: Lore Dump Exorcism**
Explanation scenes discharge supernatural pressure by converting felt wrongness into lore transfer.
- *Detect:* Archive scenes, priest-scholar scenes, family-history speeches, or mythology explanations reduce fear and increase administrative clarity. Each fact delivered is a unit of dread subtracted. The reader's posture shifts from fearful uncertainty to intellectual processing.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if clustered near midpoint or climax.

**RL-3: Rule Defanging**
The supernatural rule set is so explicit that the threat becomes strategically solvable.
- *Detect:* Once the characters understand the rules, the reader's fear drops because the manuscript has effectively turned haunting into compliance management or procedural puzzle. Rules create game-logic (enabling solutions) rather than trap-logic (closing escape routes).
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix when the climax depends on rule application.

**RL-4: Ritual Without Sacrifice**
Countermeasures exist, but they demand technique rather than irreversible cost.
- *Detect:* Exorcism, burial, appeasement, destruction of object, confession, or bargain resolves the threat without meaningful loss, moral compromise, or altered life. Reading a Latin incantation perfectly destroys the demon, leaving the protagonist unchanged, unharmed, and untraumatized. Resolution is procedural rather than sacrificial.
- *Default severity:* Must-Fix at the primary resolution.

### MS Flags (Moral / Historical / Belief Substrate)

**MS-1: Moral Vacuum Haunting**
The supernatural is active but insufficiently anchored in grief, guilt, taboo, injustice, inheritance, belief, or history.
- *Detect:* The haunting could be relocated to a different family, house, town, or institution with little change. The entity has activity but not necessity. The ghost has no metaphorical weight; its haunting is entirely random.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix in ghost, curse, folk-horror, and diasporic spiritual modes.

**MS-2: Belief Wallpaper**
Religious, folkloric, or spiritual systems function as atmosphere or exotic texture rather than operative worldview.
- *Detect:* The manuscript names rituals, saints, ancestors, taboos, or local beliefs, but these do not shape behavior, interpretation, or consequence. The cosmology is displayed, not inhabited.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix in culturally specific or spiritually grounded modes.

**MS-3: Moral Overcoding**
Understanding the relationship between the supernatural threat and the moral substrate makes the horror smaller rather than larger.
- *Detect:* The supernatural becomes a simple punishment mechanism. Understanding why the ghost haunts removes all dread because the solution is obvious. The ghost becomes a plot device with a legible ledger.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix.

### IE Flags (Impossible Escalation)

**IE-1: Escalation by Addition**
The manuscript escalates through more phenomena rather than deeper violation.
- *Detect:* More ghosts, more visions, more symbols, more possessions, more incidents, but the reader's relationship to the supernatural does not change in kind. The climax features an army of spirits, rendering them cannon fodder.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix.

**IE-2: No-Exit Failure**
The supernatural never fully earns its impossible status because ordinary coping remains too viable.
- *Detect:* Characters keep solving, escaping, rationalizing, or outrunning the threat as if it were merely difficult rather than supernaturally asymmetrical. The protagonist easily leaves the haunted space, goes shopping, and returns, breaking claustrophobic accumulation. The atmosphere fails to thicken.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix.

**IE-3: Solved-House Syndrome**
Once the explanatory pattern is uncovered, the supernatural threat becomes structurally smaller.
- *Detect:* Revelation turns the haunting into a case file. The house, ghost, or curse loses appetite once understood. Knowledge of the supernatural's cause becomes sufficient to dispel it, reducing horror to mystery with a supernatural veneer.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if revelation is the manuscript's late-act engine.

**IE-4: Chosen One Domestication**
The protagonist is revealed to be specially suited — through lineage, power, or destiny — to face the supernatural.
- *Detect:* The protagonist's special status undermines horror by converting a universal threat into a specialized problem. Horror requires the supernatural threaten ordinary existence; the "chosen one" structure implies containability. The supernatural becomes addressable rather than inescapable.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if the special status removes the need for sacrifice or cost.

### RR Flags (Residue and Resealing Failure)

**RR-1: Clean Reseal**
The ending restores normalcy too completely for the mode's contract.
- *Detect:* The supernatural is resolved and reality snaps back into place with little residue, altered worldview, or enduring contamination. The reader closes the book feeling "that's done" rather than "that's in me now."
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix in haunting, cosmic, and ambiguity-forward contracts.

**RR-2: Diminishing Revelation**
Late explanation makes the threat smaller rather than deeper.
- *Detect:* Final answers resolve more pressure than the ending can rebuild. The reader closes with understanding but without dread. The explanation is less frightening than the mystery.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix when explanation is the ending's centerpiece.

**RR-3: Tonal Whiplash at Resolution**
The manuscript's tonal register shifts so sharply at the ending that accumulated supernatural pressure is accidentally discharged.
- *Detect:* The ending moves from dread into sentimentality, triumph, or domestic normalcy without managing the tonal transition. Supernatural pressure is released by tonal accident rather than transformed by design.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix.

## §4. Tracking Artifacts

### Artifact A: Belief Threshold Ledger (required)

| Scene Unit | Anomaly | Natural Model Available | Supernatural Pull | Threshold Status | Reader Position | Dominant Flags | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ch/Scene | Event/signal | Plausible explanation | Low/Med/High | Open/Narrowing/Crossed/Collapsed | Skeptical/Divided/Accepting | BT codes | Summary |

Usage rules:

1. Track every major anomaly and threshold-crossing beat.
2. Mark when natural explanations stop being sufficient, not merely when characters say they do.
3. Note whether the crossing increases dread or reduces it.
4. Identify the threshold strategy in use (gradual bleed, immediate violation, or oscillation) and assess whether the manuscript executes it consistently.

### Artifact B: Wrongness and Otherness Log (recommended)

| Scene Unit | Pressure Type | Perceptual Stage | Category Breached | Agency Signal | Visibility | Pressure Delta | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ch/Scene | Weird/Eerie/Numinous/Abject/Direct Manifestation | Sensory anomaly/Failed explanation/Somatic response/Cognitive dissonance/Impossible recognition | Life-death, self-other, sacred-profane, time, causality, etc. | Weak/Med/Strong | Hidden/Partial/Exposed | Rising/Flat/Dropping | Summary |

Usage rules:

1. Distinguish eerie absence from direct manifestation.
2. Track whether wrongness varies in kind or repeats in one register.
3. In religious or mythic horror, note whether awe remains present alongside threat.
4. Track the perceptual cascade: does the text render the five stages (sensory anomaly, failed rational explanation, somatic response, cognitive dissonance, impossible recognition) or skip to summary?

### Artifact C: Rule and Ritual Ledger (required when rules matter)

| Rule or Remedy | Source of Claim | Confirmed by Text? | Cost | Limit | Contradiction? | Effect on Dread |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Statement | Lore / witness / event | Yes/No/Partial | None/Low/High/Irreversible | Condition | Note | Intensifies/Neutral/Defangs |

Usage rules:

1. Separate what characters believe from what the manuscript confirms.
2. Mark every contradiction between stated and enacted rules.
3. Flag any remedy that lowers fear more than it raises cost.
4. Note whether rules create trap-logic (closing escape routes) or game-logic (enabling solutions).

### Artifact D: Aftermath Chain (required)

| Character | Supernatural Event | Immediate Response | Next-Scene Shift | Delayed Cost | Relationship Impact | Persistence |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Name | Ch/Scene | In-moment reaction | What changes right after | What lingers | What mutates socially | Transient/Durable |

Usage rules:

1. Include all major characters touched by the supernatural.
2. Require at least one delayed-cost observation for each major event.
3. Mark absence of persistence explicitly rather than leaving it blank.

### Artifact E: Haunting Substrate Map (recommended)

| Locus | Underlying Pressure | Manifestation Form | Who Pays | Necessity Test | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Family/House/Land/Institution/Body/Belief world | Grief/Guilt/Injustice/Taboo/Inheritance/History/Faith/etc. | Ghost/curse/possession/space corruption/etc. | Character/group | Could this haunting relocate without loss? | Summary |

### Pattern interpretation protocol

After artifacts are complete, run:

1. **Threshold Check:** Does the manuscript cross from ordinary to impossible with pressure or with strain?
2. **Wrongness Check:** Is the supernatural altering ontology, or merely providing paranormal incident?
3. **Propagation Check:** Do encounters contaminate later scenes?
4. **Rule Pressure Check:** Does knowledge intensify fear or solve it?
5. **Substrate Check:** Does the haunting belong to this manuscript's deeper pressure system?
6. **Escalation Check:** Does each escalation change the reader's relationship to the supernatural, or just the quantity of phenomena?
7. **Resealing Check:** Does the ending preserve residue or close the breach too neatly?
8. **Pressure Gradient Check:** Is tension rising over time, even in scenes where nothing supernatural is shown?

### Reading the artifacts

- **Producing across most channels** → Integrated supernatural horror. The breach feels real.
- **Producing in BT and RL, inert in HP** → The manuscript can establish and explain the supernatural but cannot make it lived.
- **Producing in HP and MS, inert in BT** → Consequences and substrate are strong, but belief crossing is shaky or underbuilt.
- **Producing in WO and AP-adjacent texture, inert elsewhere** → Beautiful haunting surface without structural conversion.
- **Producing early, collapsing in RR** → Strong supernatural engine undercut by clean reseal or late explanation.
- **Producing in RL, inert in WO** → Competence-puzzle interest in the occult system without felt wrongness. The supernatural is interesting, not terrifying.
- **Inert across channels** → Paranormal plotting without supernatural pressure.

### Blast radius classes

1. `Local` — isolated scene or sequence
2. `Multi-scene` — repeated in one act or one channel
3. `Systemic` — cross-act, climax-critical, or ending-critical

## §5. Distinguish Framework

Supernatural horror uses tools that can look like failures from the outside. Ambiguity, delayed manifestation, sacred language, ritual repetition, cultural opacity, and deliberate restraint are often working features, not defects. The audit must separate productive restraint from inert supernatural delivery, and it must recognize that unfamiliar modes can look like failures to readers calibrated for Western secular supernatural horror.

### Distinguish classes

1. **Working / Integrated** (Pass)
2. **Deliberately Restrained / Ambiguous** (Pass with note)
3. **Mode-Appropriate but Unstable** (Conditional)
4. **Inert / Execution Failure** (Fail)

### Required distinguish tests

1. **Belief Threshold Test**
At the moment the narrative asks the reader to accept the supernatural, has the manuscript earned that leap? Understated but earned: the text established reality so firmly that small deviations feel enormous (Jackson, James). Understated and unearned: reality isn't established firmly enough for the supernatural to register as violation. Overt but earned: emotional investment makes explicit supernatural events feel true (King, Morrison). Overt and unearned: supernatural events drop into an insufficiently established world.

2. **Wrongness Test**
Does the supernatural alter the reader's model of reality, or does it behave like familiar genre equipment? Wrongness is a prose-level phenomenon, not a plot-level one. A text can have supernatural events and no wrongness (failure), or no confirmed supernatural events and profound wrongness (success). If wrongness is present, the text may be restrained or in an unfamiliar mode. **If wrongness is absent, the text is likely genuinely failing.**

3. **Propagation Test**
Do encounters alter later behavior, relationships, rituals, or worldview? Even in ambiguous modes, the supernatural changes things.

4. **Withholding Productivity Test**
If the manuscript withholds visibility or confirmation, does that withholding generate hypotheses, pressure, and anticipation? Productive restraint: the reader's imagination fills gaps with something worse (reader leans in). Evasive restraint: the reader simply forgets the threat exists (reader leans back). **The pressure gradient test:** is tension rising even when nothing supernatural is shown?

5. **Rule Pressure Test**
Do rules and remedies trap characters more tightly, or simply hand them instructions?

6. **Substrate Necessity Test**
Why is this haunting here, in this family, house, body, or community? If that answer is weak, integration is weak.

7. **Residue Test**
After the ending, does the supernatural feel partially unresolved, metabolized into cost, or too neatly resealed?

8. **Valence Calibration Test**
If the supernatural is protective, ambivalent, sacred, or culturally specific rather than purely malevolent, does it still produce mode-appropriate wrongness and consequence? Do not confuse mixed valence with failure.

### Decision matrix

| Test profile | Classification | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Threshold earned, wrongness active, propagation durable, rules trapping, substrate necessary, residue strong | Working / Integrated | Pass with calibration notes |
| Confirmation partial but pressure high; restraint clearly productive | Deliberately Restrained / Ambiguous | Pass with note; avoid false positives |
| Some tests strong, others weak; mode visible but unstable under stress | Mode-Appropriate but Unstable | Could/Should revisions |
| Threshold unearned or stalled, wrongness flat, propagation absent, rules solving, substrate weak, reseal clean | Inert / Execution Failure | Should/Must by gates |

### The master heuristic

A reader who mistakes an unfamiliar mode for failure will report *boredom* — the text isn't producing affect they recognize. A reader encountering productive restraint or an unfamiliar mode will feel *unsettled* — something is wrong but they can't articulate what. A reader encountering genuine failure will feel *uninvested* — the text simply fails to produce affect. **Unsettlement is evidence of craft; indifference is evidence of failure.**

### False-positive guardrails

Before calling systemic failure, confirm:

1. You did not mistake deliberate ambiguity for threshold stall. Can a reader form and revise plausible models?
2. You did not penalize low apparition count in ghost-story or eerie modes where less is the method.
3. You did not mistake explicit doctrine, ritual, or inherited belief for didactic excess in religious or spiritual horror where such language is part of the pressure system.
4. You did not assume supernatural force must be purely evil. Protective or ambivalent spirits can still generate horror.
5. You did not demand total explanation from cosmic, mythic, or anti-explanatory modes.
6. You did not impose a default modern-skeptical worldview on manuscripts rooted in live supernatural belief systems. Western supernatural horror assumes a secular baseline that the supernatural violates; Japanese, Latin American, and West African/diaspora traditions operate from fundamentally different assumptions about where the supernatural begins.
7. You did not mistake the gendering of a supernatural threat for craft failure. Possession, haunting, and cursing operate through bodily/spiritual invasion that is frequently gendered. The question is whether the gendering taps genuine anxieties about autonomy and origin or merely reproduces stereotype.
8. You assessed the reading *experience* of supernatural wrongness, not the plot *events* of supernatural content.

### Special caution zones

- **Quiet ghost stories** — often low on overt manifestation, high on eerie propagation
- **Possession with unreliable testimony** — POV instability may be functional
- **Religious horror** — explicit cosmology is not automatically overexplanation
- **Diasporic / ancestral spiritual horror** — belief systems may be protective, warning-bearing, or morally mixed; spirits that are simultaneously protective and dangerous are normative, not paradoxical
- **Cosmic-supernatural hybrids** — explanation thresholds differ from ordinary hauntings
- **Latin American supernatural realism** — the supernatural may be ambient and indigenous to the landscape, requiring no "earning" in the Western sense
- **Japanese kaidan / J-horror** — inevitability replaces escapability; passive protagonists and unresolved mechanisms are features; beauty coexists with terror through *mono no aware*

## §6. Subgenre Calibration Matrix

| Subgenre | Integration Promise | Named Failure Mode | Tighten On | Loosen On |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ghost story / haunting | The past intrudes; space, object, or relationship proves permeable to what should be gone | **Case File Ghost** — the haunting becomes an archive problem instead of a lived one | BT, HP, RR | Apparition frequency, full confirmation |
| Possession | Selfhood is violated from within; the body becomes an occupied site | **Vacant Vessel** — the host vanishes into puppetry and the mode loses intimacy | HP, RL, WO | POV stability, narrative neatness |
| Demonic / religious horror | Sacred-profane order is breached; cosmological threat enters ordinary life | **Catechism Over Dread** — doctrine and ritual explanation replace terror | WO, RL, RR | Explicit theology, liturgical language |
| Haunted space | Environment acts as antagonist, memory-bank, appetite, or contagion | **Static House** — creepy architecture without escalating pressure | HP, IE, RR | Early pace, high event density |
| Curse / folk horror | Communal, inherited, or place-bound dread closes ordinary exits | **Puzzle Curse** — the curse becomes a solvable mechanism rather than a trap | MS, RL, IE | Repetition, delayed clarity, nihilistic inevitability |
| Cosmic horror with supernatural agency | Incomprehensible force exerts enough agency to wound the human world | **Abyss With Motives** — the vast threat becomes too understandable and too character-like | WO, IE, RR | Explanatory completeness, ordinary character motive |
| Dark fairy tale / mythic horror | Archetypal terror, taboo, and transformation through symbolic inevitability | **Archetype Without Bite** — mythic distance becomes emotionally anesthetic | WO, HP, RR | Literal realism, naturalistic dialogue |
| Diasporic / ancestral spiritual horror | Haunting as inheritance, warning, judgment, continuity, or pressure within a live belief world | **Heritage Wallpaper** — spirituality serves texture but not structure | MS, HP, belief specificity | Pure malevolence assumption, Western skepticism norms |

### Calibration principles

1. Apply the mode's own promise, not a generic supernatural standard.
2. Tighten on the channel that carries the mode's primary burden.
3. Loosen on surface features that the mode legitimately minimizes.
4. Never loosen on consequence propagation or ending alignment.
5. In culturally specific supernatural systems, calibrate against the manuscript's operative belief world rather than against default secular realism.

## §7. Severity Hard Gates

These gates improve reproducibility. If triggered, minimum severity applies regardless of local strengths.

**Gate 1: Threshold Failure**
Trigger: BT overall rated Inert, or BT-1/BT-2 persistent through the primary threshold sequence.
Minimum: Must-Fix if supernatural acceptance is core to the contract.

**Gate 2: Otherness Void**
Trigger: WO overall rated Inert while supernatural content is plot-confirmed.
Minimum: Must-Fix. The manuscript has paranormal content without violated reality.

**Gate 3: Aftermath Void**
Trigger: HP-1 or HP-2 fires across 2+ major supernatural events.
Minimum: Should-Fix. Upgrade to Must-Fix in haunting, possession, or domestic-supernatural modes.

**Gate 4: Rule Collapse**
Trigger: RL-1, RL-2, and RL-3 cluster in the same act or around the climax.
Minimum: Must-Fix in rule-heavy manuscripts.

**Gate 5: Substrate Void**
Trigger: MS overall rated Inert in ghost, curse, folk, or diasporic spiritual horror.
Minimum: Must-Fix. The supernatural lacks load-bearing necessity.

**Gate 6: Addition Escalation Loop**
Trigger: IE-1 persists across Act II and Act III without deeper category shift.
Minimum: Should-Fix. Upgrade to Must-Fix if the manuscript is long-form and escalation is the main engine.

**Gate 7: Host Erasure**
Trigger: HP-3 fires in a possession-primary manuscript.
Minimum: Must-Fix.

**Gate 8: Costless Resolution**
Trigger: RL-4 fires at the primary supernatural resolution.
Minimum: Must-Fix.

**Gate 9: Clean Reseal**
Trigger: RR-1 or RR-2 fires at the ending in a manuscript promising haunting residue, ambiguity, cosmic disturbance, or sacred wrongness.
Minimum: Must-Fix.

**Gate 10: Domesticated Numen**
Trigger: WO-2 fires before the third act, converting the supernatural threat from horror to dark fantasy.
Minimum: Should-Fix. Upgrade to Must-Fix in demonic, religious, cosmic, and mythic modes.

## §10. What This Audit Is Not

1. **Not a theology-truth audit.** It does not judge whether a manuscript's supernatural beliefs are doctrinally correct.
2. **Not a mythology encyclopedia test.** It does not reward lore volume for its own sake.
3. **Not anti-ambiguity.** Ambiguity often makes supernatural horror stronger.
4. **Not anti-rules.** Rules can sharpen dread when they constrain rather than solve.
5. **Not a replacement for Horror Craft Integration.** It is a specialized child audit, not a parent framework.
6. **Not a realism test locked to secular skepticism.** Belief worlds vary, and the audit must calibrate to them.
7. **Not a content-sensitivity verdict.** Representational or spiritual-risk questions belong with Reception Risk or adjacent modules.
8. **Not limited to ghosts.** It covers hauntings, curses, possession, sacred terror, spiritual invasion, and related impossible-pressure forms.
9. **Not anti-quiet.** Quiet supernatural horror — through suggestion, omission, atmospheric accumulation — is fully valid. The audit measures ontological pressure, not manifestation volume.
10. **Not a demand for the numinous.** The numinous register (Otto's *mysterium tremendum et fascinans*) is mode-specific, not universal. Morrison's *Beloved* achieves its effects through abjection and moral architecture rather than numinous awe; Enriquez's social-realist ghosts feel present rather than transcendent. The audit tests for the numinous without requiring it.

## Final Diagnostic Question

After artifacts, flags, tests, and gates, answer directly:

**Does the supernatural in this manuscript alter the reader's reality model, or does it only decorate the plot with impossibility?**

That answer determines whether the manuscript needs local tightening, systemic supernatural revision, or a different genre-positioning claim.
