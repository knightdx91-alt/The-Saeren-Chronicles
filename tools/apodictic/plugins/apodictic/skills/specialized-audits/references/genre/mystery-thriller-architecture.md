## Version 0.4.11
*Last Updated: February 2026*
*Synthesized from three-model research (Opus, Codex, Gemini)*
*Status: Specialized audit — genre-specific*

---

## §1. Purpose

This audit evaluates whether a mystery/thriller manuscript operates as a coherent information-pressure machine — whether its clues, misdirection, clock mechanics, investigation logic, and reveal sequencing generate the reader's active cognitive and emotional participation, or merely assemble genre components without producing inference, urgency, or satisfying surprise.

**The core problem is not complexity. It is informational drift.** A manuscript can contain clues, suspects, reversals, red herrings, and ticking deadlines while still failing to generate the reader's hypothesis formation, prediction revision, felt urgency, or the "click" of surprise-with-inevitability. The puzzle components are on the page. The reader's mind isn't working them.

The audit's fundamental question: **Does this manuscript's information architecture generate inference, or does it only deliver information?**

### Anchor insight

Informational drift is to cognitive engagement what inert dread is to felt fear and ornamental prose is to structural meaning. In all three cases: the craft machinery is assembled, the genre components are present, the reader's active participation isn't generated. A clue the reader can't infer from is the mystery equivalent of a dread sequence that doesn't produce anxiety. A clock that doesn't constrain behavior is the thriller equivalent of atmosphere that doesn't generate pressure. A solution that requires unseen evidence is the mystery equivalent of a horror ending that domesticates through explanation. **Information that the reader possesses but isn't working with is the architecture equivalent of prose the reader admires but doesn't need.**

This is an architecture audit, not a content audit. Cozy mysteries with gentle pacing are within scope alongside high-velocity conspiracy thrillers. The question is never whether the puzzle is complex enough or the clock fast enough. It is whether the information-pressure system — whatever its mode — generates the cognitive and emotional engagement it promises.

### Relationship to existing infrastructure

**Genre Module: Mystery / Investigation** handles contract calibration, intake questions (crime, fair play, investigation architecture), pass modifications, and a flat flag list (12 flags). This audit deepens the diagnostic apparatus: named flags with detection criteria, tracking artifacts, severity gates, distinguish framework, and subgenre calibration with named failure modes. The genre module asks "Is the mystery contract coherent?" This audit asks "Does the information architecture *run*?"

**Genre Module: Thriller / Suspense** handles contract calibration, intake questions (threat architecture, protagonist constraints, escalation), pass modifications, stakes system audit, and a flat flag list (15 flags). This audit addresses the information-pressure side of thriller: how deadlines convert to constraint, how threat information is managed, how reversals operate architecturally.

**Pass 8: Reveal Economy** remains the primary input pass. This audit deepens it with dedicated ledgers, hard gates, and pattern-level diagnostics that Pass 8's general reveal tracking can't provide.

**Emotional Craft** diagnoses general emotional transmission. This audit diagnoses the specific emotional operations of mystery/thriller: cognitive engagement (inference, prediction), suspense (anxiety about outcomes), and the satisfaction architecture of surprise-with-inevitability.

**Horror Craft Integration** diagnoses dread-pressure delivery. This audit shares the pressure-system diagnostic framework but applies it to information-pressure specifically: hypothesis formation replaces dread architecture, fairness replaces consequence, revelation replaces reveal-as-horror.

### When to activate

- Any manuscript where mystery or investigation is the primary genre contract
- Any manuscript where thriller or suspense is the primary genre contract
- Mystery-thriller hybrids where both puzzle and pressure must work
- Literary fiction with mystery architecture (whodunit, investigation, revelation structure)
- Manuscripts where beta readers say "I figured it out at page 50" or "the ending felt like a cheat" or "I stopped caring who did it"
- Any time an author asks "the puzzle is there — why isn't the reader working it?"

### Exclusions

Do not run when: (1) manuscript is pre-draft ideation only, (2) mystery/investigation is incidental to a non-mystery contract (a romance with one suspicious event), (3) author does not claim mystery/thriller burden and does not request this audit.

## §2. Information-Pressure Framework

Mystery/thriller is not a content category. It is an information-pressure system — a set of craft commitments to generating the reader's active cognitive participation through controlled information delivery, productive misdirection, time-based constraint, and resolution that reorganizes understanding. These commitments succeed or fail independently, and they fail in specific, diagnosable ways.

### Six Architecture Dimensions

**1. IE: Information Economy**
The management of clue delivery — what evidence is presented, when, how it's embedded, and whether it supports reader inference at the point of encounter. Not just "are clues present?" but "do clues generate hypothesis formation?"

*Integrated when:* Evidence is distributed across the full narrative. Each major clue supports hypothesis formation at the point of encounter, not just retrospective recognition. Information density is calibrated — enough to reason from, not so much that signal drowns in noise. The reader's knowledge base expands progressively and productively.

*Detached when:* Evidence is dumped, clustered, or obscured beyond inferential use. The reader accumulates facts without being able to reason from them. Clues are technically present but functionally invisible — not because they're hidden cleverly, but because they're not inferentially activated.

**2. RH: Red Herring Integrity**
The quality of misdirection — whether false trails have evidentiary foundation, bounded lifespan, and fair collapse. Not just "are red herrings present?" but "do they earn the reader's serious consideration?"

*Integrated when:* Red herrings are supported by genuine (but ultimately misleading) evidence. False trails have natural lifespans — they emerge from evidence, persist under scrutiny, and collapse when contradicted. The strongest red herring is almost as well-evidenced as the true solution.

*Detached when:* Red herrings are propped up by authorial assertion rather than evidence. Decoys persist after disproof or collapse without explanation. The misdirection system is noise rather than architecture.

**3. IL: Investigation Legibility**
Whether the investigator's reasoning chain is visible enough for reader trust and participation. Not just "does the detective solve it?" but "can the reader follow and check the reasoning?"

*Integrated when:* The reader can track the investigator's logic — what they notice, what they infer, how they connect evidence. The reasoning is legible even when incomplete. Investigation scenes advance both the puzzle and the reader's understanding.

*Detached when:* The investigator arrives at conclusions through opaque leaps, stated intuition, or offscreen reasoning. Investigation scenes demonstrate process without advancing understanding. The detective is a black box that takes in clues and produces answers.

**4. CM: Clock Mechanics**
Whether time pressure — explicit or implicit — converts into scene-level constraint. Not just "is there a deadline?" but "does the deadline change what characters do?"

*Integrated when:* Deadlines constrain character choices. Time pressure removes options, forces suboptimal decisions, creates trade-offs between investigation and action. The clock is enacted — it shapes behavior, not just narration.

*Detached when:* Deadlines are stated but ignored. Characters investigate as though they have unlimited time despite stated urgency. The clock is cosmetic — it exists in the narration but not in the architecture.

**5. RC: Reveal Choreography**
The sequencing of clue, suspicion, reversal, and explanation — how information is progressively disclosed to maximize impact. Not just "when is the solution revealed?" but "does each revelation reorganize understanding?"

*Integrated when:* Each revelation changes the reader's model, not just their data. Partial reveals increase rather than decrease engagement. The final reveal reorganizes earlier events — the reader rethinks scenes in light of the solution.

*Detached when:* Revelations arrive as information dumps rather than model-changers. The sequence of disclosure is chronological rather than architectural. The ending explains rather than reorganizes.

**6. SF: Solution Fairness**
Whether the resolution achieves surprise with inevitability — unexpected yet retrospectively justified. Not just "is the solution correct?" but "does the solution produce the 'click' of recognition?"

*Integrated when:* The solution surprises the reader while satisfying the evidence. Retrospective examination reveals clues that pointed to the truth. The reader experiences the "click": the moment when disparate evidence reorganizes into a coherent picture.

*Detached when:* The solution depends on information not available before the reveal. Or the solution is technically fair but doesn't reorganize understanding. Or the solution is obvious from early on. Or the explanation fails to account for planted evidence.

### Integration Levels

| Level | Operational definition |
|---|---|
| **Integrated** | Dimension actively generates reader cognitive/emotional participation. The architecture is running. |
| **Partial** | Dimension functions intermittently. Inference generation in some scenes, informational drift in others. |
| **Detached** | Dimension is present (clues exist, clock is stated, investigation occurs) but doesn't generate participation. Could be summarized without felt loss. |

### Quick diagnostic statement

**A mystery/thriller fails when information moves, but inference does not.**

## §3. Named Diagnostic Flags

Each flag identifies a specific pattern of information-architecture failure. Flags can co-occur. Severity depends on frequency, pattern, and the manuscript's genre contract.

Severity scale: `Could-Fix` (local, non-patterned), `Should-Fix` (repeated, multi-scene), `Must-Fix` (systemic, climax/ending impact, contract breach).

### IE Flags (Information Economy)

**IE-1: Fog of Facts**
High information density, low inferential utility. The reader knows many things but can't do anything with them. Evidence accumulates as data, not as material for reasoning.
- *Detect:* At key investigation points, can the reader articulate what the evidence suggests? If the answer is "I know a lot of stuff" rather than "I suspect X because of Y," the fog is active. Test: could you remove 40% of the factual detail without changing what the reader can infer? If yes, the surplus is fog.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Could-Fix if limited to early establishment chapters.

**IE-2: Clue Starvation**
Insufficient fair anchors before the reveal. The reader is asked to participate in the puzzle but isn't given the tools. The solution, when it arrives, feels unfair — not because it was hidden cleverly, but because the evidence wasn't provided.
- *Detect:* Audit the Clue Ledger. For each clue essential to the solution, when was it introduced? If essential evidence appears only in the final act, the reader was starved. Check: at the 60% mark, could a careful reader have formed a hypothesis pointing toward the true solution? If not, starvation is active.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if the genre contract promises fair-play.

**IE-3: Asymmetry Collapse**
Reader and investigator information tracks diverge unfairly. The investigator knows things the reader doesn't, without structural justification. Includes deliberate POV censorship: the narrative confirms observation without description (e.g., "She read the name on the file and gasped, quickly burning the paper").
- *Detect:* Compare the investigator's knowledge at key deduction points with the reader's knowledge. If the detective reasons from evidence the reader hasn't seen and no structural reason explains the gap, asymmetry has collapsed fairness.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if the collapse is at the resolution.

**IE-4: Evidence Island**
Clues clustered in one section rather than distributed across the narrative. The reader's inference work is compressed into a short span rather than sustained.
- *Detect:* Map clue introduction across the manuscript's timeline. If >60% of essential clues appear in a single act or investigation sequence, evidence is islanded.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Could-Fix if early acts compensate with strong establishment.

**IE-5: Signal Collapse**
True-path clues repeatedly buried beneath non-functional noise. Even retrospectively, clue relevance cannot be inferred. Reader tracking load exceeds reasonable mode burden.
- *Detect:* Clue relevance cannot be recovered even on re-read. Noise volume outpaces meaningful data systematically, not just locally.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix.

### RH Flags (Red Herring Integrity)

**RH-1: Noise Cannon**
Decoy proliferation overwhelms core logic. So many false trails compete for attention that the reader stops trying to track them. The signal-to-noise ratio defeats engagement.
- *Detect:* Count active red herrings at midpoint. If >5 simultaneously active with none definitively eliminated, the cannon is firing. Check: can the reader rank suspects by evidential strength? If all seem equally plausible (or implausible), noise has defeated architecture.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if fairness collapses.

**RH-2: Decoy Without Spine**
Red herring unsupported by believable evidence. The text tells the reader to suspect someone, but the suspicion is based on emphasis or character description rather than actual evidence.
- *Detect:* For each red herring, can you articulate the *evidence* (not just the *emphasis*) that supports it? If plausibility depends on authorial tone rather than factual basis, the spine is missing.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Could-Fix if limited to minor suspects.

**RH-3: Immortal Herring**
False trail persists after its evidence has been contradicted. The red herring should be dead but the narrative continues treating it as a live option without new evidence reviving it.
- *Detect:* Track each red herring's lifespan. When introduced? When contradicted? Is it still treated as active after contradiction?
- *Default severity:* Could-Fix. Should-Fix if the immortal herring occupies significant page time.

**RH-4: Orphaned Thread**
Red herring introduced but never formally collapsed or explained. The false trail simply disappears from the narrative without disproof event or resolution. The reader is left wondering what happened to that suspect or theory.
- *Detect:* Check each red herring in the Red Herring Ledger for a definitive disproof point. If none exists, the thread is orphaned.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix.

**RH-5: Herring Hierarchy**
Red herrings of vastly different quality or plausibility. One false trail is carefully evidenced while others are transparently implausible, making the "real" false trail identifiable by quality rather than evidence. The reader solves the puzzle by meta-reading.
- *Detect:* Compare evidentiary depth across suspects/theories. If one false trail is dramatically more developed than others, the hierarchy signals the answer.
- *Default severity:* Could-Fix. Should-Fix in fair-play contracts.

### IL Flags (Investigation Legibility)

**IL-1: Leap Detective**
Major deductions without observable reasoning chain. The investigator concludes X from evidence A, B, and C, but the logical steps connecting them are unstated.
- *Detect:* At the 3 most important deduction moments, can the reader reconstruct the reasoning from presented evidence? If the chain requires unstated steps or offscreen information, the leap is active.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if the leap occurs at the solution.

**IL-2: Procedure Theatre**
Process scenes that add realism flavor but no puzzle progress. The investigation *appears* to be happening but the reader's understanding is unchanged after the scene.
- *Detect:* After each investigation scene, state what the reader now knows or suspects that they didn't before. If "nothing" for 3+ consecutive scenes, procedure is theatrical.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Could-Fix if limited to early establishment.

**IL-3: Oracle Investigator**
Detective arrives at conclusions through stated "intuition," "a feeling," or "something didn't sit right" rather than inferrable evidence. The investigator is a black box. Distinct from Leap Detective (where evidence is present but reasoning chain unstated) — here, the *basis* for the deduction is invisible.
- *Detect:* When the investigator explains reasoning, do they cite specific evidence the reader has encountered? Or unspecified instinct? Specific evidence = legible. Unspecified instinct = oracle.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if the solution depends on an oracle deduction.

**IL-4: Blind-Side Investigator**
Investigator misses obvious inferences repeatedly beyond credible character limits. The reader outruns the detective on core clues, and the misses are not explained by bias, pressure, or expertise limits.
- *Detect:* Reader repeatedly arrives at inferences the investigator misses. Delayed realization exists mainly to preserve twist timing rather than serving character or plot logic.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if credibility collapses.

**IL-5: Passive Catalyst**
Protagonist does not actively deduce the solution. The climax relies on unmotivated confession, secondary character delivering the answer, or pure accidental discovery rather than logical deduction.
- *Detect:* Identify how the solution is reached. If the protagonist's active reasoning is not the primary mechanism, the catalyst is passive.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if the genre contract promises investigation-driven resolution.

### CM Flags (Clock Mechanics)

**CM-1: Cosmetic Clock**
Deadline stated, behavior unchanged. The text establishes urgency but characters investigate as though they have unlimited time. The clock is narration, not architecture.
- *Detect:* Does the stated deadline change any character's decision in any scene? Does it force suboptimal choices, eliminate options, or create trade-offs? If the investigation would proceed identically with or without the clock, the clock is cosmetic.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Could-Fix if limited to early acts before the clock tightens.

**CM-2: Clock Freeze**
Urgency disappears for extended sections. The narrative establishes a live deadline, then enters scenes where time pressure is neither enacted nor mentioned. When the clock returns, it feels arbitrary.
- *Detect:* Map clock references. If 3+ consecutive chapters pass without the deadline affecting behavior or narration, the clock has frozen. Distinguish from deliberate false-safety (characters *think* they have time) vs. authorial amnesia (text forgets).
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix.

**CM-3: Clock Without Cost**
The deadline approaches or passes without consequence. The threat was established but when the moment arrives, nothing happens. The reader learns that the clock isn't real.
- *Detect:* Does the clock produce actual consequence at milestones? If repeatedly approached without consequence, the clock is toothless.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if the clock is the primary suspense engine.

**CM-4: Deadline Detour**
Major subplot or detour blocks clock conversion at critical phase. Clock-dependent decisions postponed by low-yield content, requiring abrupt acceleration later.
- *Detect:* Detour scenes delay key actions without new pressure value. Urgency recovery requires sudden acceleration that feels unearned.
- *Default severity:* Could-Fix. Should-Fix if repeated.

### RC Flags (Reveal Choreography)

**RC-1: Reverse Gear Reveal**
The resolution introduces key facts absent from earlier architecture. The explanation scene contains information the reader encounters for the first time — not hidden clues being recontextualized, but new data arriving at the moment of solution.
- *Detect:* For each fact the solution depends on, when was it first available to the reader? If essential facts debut during the explanation (or in the final 10% without prior establishment), the reveal is running in reverse.
- *Default severity:* Must-Fix. The cardinal architectural failure.

**RC-2: Twist Before Foundation**
Reversal arrives without inferential preparation. The surprise is real, but it's arbitrary rather than reorganizing — it comes from outside the reader's inferential space.
- *Detect:* For each major reversal, can the reader retrospectively trace evidence pointing toward the new understanding? If the twist requires retroactive information planting that doesn't hold on re-read, it's ungrounded.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if the twist is the primary structural event.

**RC-3: Premature Closure**
Key mystery resolved too early, leaving remaining pages anticlimactic. The puzzle is solved with significant narrative left to run, and the remaining chapters don't introduce a deeper or second-order mystery.
- *Detect:* Where does the primary puzzle resolve? If before the 80% mark and remaining narrative doesn't escalate, closure is premature.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Could-Fix if post-closure material carries thematic weight.

**RC-4: Reveal Pile-Up**
Multiple reveals in rapid succession, preventing the reader from processing any one of them. The climax compresses identity revelation, motive explanation, method disclosure, and thematic statement into a single breathless sequence.
- *Detect:* Count distinct revelations in the climactic sequence. If >3 major reveals in a single chapter without processing space, pile-up is active.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix.

**RC-5: Explain Patch Ending**
Final explanation compensates for missing architecture. The explanation scene introduces several unseeded causal links via monologue compression. Reader trust depends on accepting untestable assertions.
- *Detect:* Explanation scene introduces multiple causal links not previously available. Reveal relies on monologue telling rather than architectural showing.
- *Default severity:* Must-Fix.

### SF Flags (Solution Fairness)

**SF-1: Cheat Ending**
Solution requires unseen evidence or withheld POV-available fact. The cardinal sin. The reader couldn't have gotten there because the necessary information was never available.
- *Detect:* For each essential element of the solution, trace it to its first appearance. If any essential element has no prior appearance, the ending cheats. Special case: POV characters who withhold knowledge they naturally possess without structural justification (unreliable narrator design, trauma) are committing POV-level cheating.
- *Default severity:* Must-Fix. No exceptions.

**SF-2: Invisible Culprit**
The person responsible has insufficient page presence to be satisfying. The solution points to someone the reader barely encountered.
- *Detect:* What percentage of scenes include the culprit? If <10% and the culprit isn't a deliberate absence-presence (someone whose *absence* is a clue), they're invisible. Satisfying solutions typically require >15% scene presence, disguised by role rather than absence.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix in fair-play contracts.

**SF-3: Obvious Culprit Drift**
Reader identifies the correct answer too early, and nothing meaningful complicates that identification. The puzzle is "solved" at the 25% mark and the remaining investigation is going through motions.
- *Detect:* When does the correct answer become the reader's primary hypothesis? If correct from initial introduction and no intervening evidence challenges it, drift is active.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Could-Fix if the manuscript compensates with strong "how/why" investigation.

**SF-4: Retroactive Collapse**
The reveal fails to reconcile planted clues. The solution explains some evidence but leaves other planted clues unaccounted for or contradicted.
- *Detect:* After the solution, return to the Clue Ledger. Does the solution account for every planted clue? Do any clues contradict the solution? If planted evidence is orphaned or contradicted, retroactive coherence has collapsed.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if 2+ major clues are unreconciled.

## §4. Tracking Artifacts

No systemic verdict without required artifacts.

### Artifact A: Clue Ledger (required)

Track every piece of evidence essential to the solution:

| Clue | Plant Location | Surface Meaning | True Meaning | Concealment Method | Payoff Location | Reader-Tractable? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Description | Ch/Scene | What reader initially thinks | What it actually means | How it's hidden | Ch/Scene where significance revealed | Yes / Retrospective only / No |

Usage rules: (1) Complete for all solution-essential evidence. (2) The "Reader-Tractable?" column is critical — distinguish between clues that support inference *at the point of encounter* (Yes) and clues that only make sense in retrospect (Retrospective only). A fair-play puzzle needs most essential clues tractable prospectively. (3) Tag clues as `core` or `supporting`.

### Artifact B: Red Herring Ledger (required)

Track every significant false trail:

| Decoy | Entry Point | Evidentiary Basis | Disproof Point | Lifespan | Reader Cost | Quality vs. True Solution |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Description | Ch/Scene | What evidence supports this false trail | Ch/Scene where contradicted | Intro → Disproof span | Attention/investment cost | Comparable / Weaker / Stronger |

Usage rules: (1) "Evidentiary Basis" tests RH-2 (Decoy Without Spine). (2) "Quality vs. True Solution" tests RH-5 (Herring Hierarchy). The strongest red herring should be comparable to the true solution in evidentiary support. (3) Each red herring must have an explicit collapse/disproof event; absence triggers RH-4 (Orphaned Thread).

### Artifact C: Clock Pressure Map (required)

| Scene Unit | Clock State | Constraint Added | Options Removed | Behavior Changed | Urgency Felt | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ch/Scene | Active/Frozen/Absent | What new time pressure | What can no longer be done | What characters do differently | Low/Med/High | |

Usage rules: (1) Track clock state across all acts. (2) Flag stretches where Clock State is Active but Behavior Changed is "none" — this is Cosmetic Clock. (3) Require explicit option-depletion evidence. (4) Flag safety islands >2 consecutive high-clock scenes with low urgency.

### Artifact D: Fairness Matrix (required)

| Solution Element | First Available | Reader-Accessible? | Investigator-Accessible? | Prospectively Tractable? | Fair? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Each fact essential to solution | Ch/Scene | Yes/No | Yes/No | Yes / Retrospective / No | Yes / Borderline / No |

Usage rules: (1) Evaluate every payoff-critical reveal element. (2) Require explicit justification for any Borderline. (3) Any "No" element triggers SF hard-gate review.

### Pattern interpretation protocol

After artifacts are complete, run:

1. **Distribution Check:** Are essential clues distributed across acts or islanded?
2. **Tractability Check:** What percentage of essential clues are prospectively tractable (vs. retrospective only)?
3. **Herring Quality Check:** Is the strongest red herring comparable in evidentiary support to the true solution?
4. **Clock Conversion Check:** Does the clock change behavior in >50% of scenes where it's active?
5. **Legibility Check:** Can the reader reconstruct the investigator's reasoning at the 3 most important deduction points?
6. **Fairness Check:** Does the solution depend on any element not available before the reveal?

### Reading the Map

- **Integrated across dimensions** → The machine is running. Information generates inference, clock generates urgency, solution generates the click.
- **Integrated in IE/RH/SF, Detached in CM** → The puzzle works but lacks urgency. Common in mystery without thriller elements. May be genre-appropriate for classic whodunit or cozy.
- **Integrated in CM, Detached in SF** → The thriller works but the puzzle cheats. Common in thrillers that use mystery elements as plot devices without architectural commitment.
- **Integrated in early acts, declining through Act III** → Premature Closure pattern. Architecture spent its best material early.
- **Integrated in RH, Detached in IE** → Misdirection without foundation. Working hard to mislead but hasn't provided enough true evidence for the misdirection to matter.
- **Detached across all dimensions** → Assembled Components. The genre furniture is present. The architecture isn't running.
- **Partial across everything** → The Promising Draft. Architectural instincts are real but need deepening.
- **CM and IE integrated, IL Detached** → Pressure-Puzzle Split. Clock operates in action scenes, puzzle operates in investigation scenes, the two never interact.

### Blast radius classes

1. `Local` — one scene/one clue
2. `Multi-scene` — repeated in one act or one dimension
3. `Systemic` — cross-act or ending-critical

## §5. Distinguish Framework

Mystery/thriller makes intentional choices that can look like craft failures. Noir deliberately frustrates clean solutions. Literary mystery deliberately refuses closure. Unreliable narrators deliberately withhold. The audit must separate design from failure.

### Distinguish classes

1. **Intentional and Effective** (Pass)
2. **Intentional but Unstable** (Pass with note)
3. **Ambiguous / Developmental** (Conditional)
4. **Execution Failure** (Fail)

### Required distinguish tests

1. **Accessibility Test:** Was core solution evidence available to the reader before the reveal? If yes → fair. If no → check whether withholding is structural design (unreliable narrator, trauma) or a cheat.

2. **Inference Test:** Could a skilled reader form plausible true-model hypotheses at the midpoint? If yes → tractable. If no → starvation (unless mode deliberately refuses tractability, as in literary mystery).

3. **Clock Conversion Test:** Does the deadline change character choices, not just narration? If yes → enacted. If no → cosmetic (unless mode doesn't claim clock burden, as in classic whodunit).

4. **Retroactive Coherence Test:** Do prior scenes improve under the final explanation? Does the solution make the reader want to re-read? If yes → deep surprise. If no → arbitrary surprise.

5. **Misdirection Foundation Test:** Do red herrings have evidentiary basis a careful reader could evaluate? If yes → fair misdirection. If no → noise.

6. **Architecture-Ambiguity Test:** Does unresolved material produce interpretive richness or just unanswered questions? If the former → designed ambiguity. If the latter → unfinished architecture.

7. **POV Fairness Test:** If unreliable POV, was withholding character-credible and bounded by psychology? Does re-reading reveal a consistently flawed perspective driven by character psychology, or sheer authorial concealment?

### Decision matrix

| Test profile | Classification | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Accessible, tractable, clock enacted, retroactively coherent, misdirection grounded, ambiguity designed, POV fair | Intentional and Effective | Pass with calibration notes |
| Mixed results — some tests pass, others wobble | Intentional but Unstable | Targeted Could/Should revisions |
| Tractability unclear, fairness ambiguous, developmental | Ambiguous / Developmental | Could-Fix with diagnostic notes |
| Inaccessible, untractable, clock cosmetic, retroactively incoherent, misdirection baseless, POV cheating | Execution Failure | Should/Must by hard gates |

### False-positive guardrails

Before calling systemic failure, confirm:
1. You didn't impose fair-play whodunit standards on noir or literary mystery. Check contract.
2. You didn't penalize deliberate anti-closure that produces interpretive richness. Test: does the ambiguity generate reader work or just reader frustration?
3. You didn't flag unreliable narration as a cheat when the unreliability IS the information system. Check: does the text eventually reveal the unreliability and integrate it?
4. You didn't demand explicit clock from modes (cozy, literary, inverted) where urgency operates differently.
5. You didn't mistake a deliberately loose puzzle (noir, literary) for a broken one. Check thematic purpose.
6. You assessed the reader's inference *capacity* at key points, not just the factual fairness of the ending.

### Special Caution Zones

- **Cozy mystery** — Operates at gentler pace with emphasis on community and charm. Clock may be implicit or absent. Investigation legibility is high (amateur sleuth the reader identifies with). Don't impose thriller pacing. Evaluate the puzzle on cozy terms.
- **Noir/hardboiled** — May deliberately frustrate clean solutions. The investigation reveals the moral landscape more than the factual truth. Don't demand strict fair-play. Evaluate whether the thematic investigation is rigorous even if the puzzle is loose.
- **Literary mystery** — May refuse closure entirely. The point may be that some things can't be known. Don't penalize anti-closure. Evaluate whether the refusal produces interpretive richness.
- **Inverted mystery** — Reader knows the culprit. The puzzle is the method and the investigation, not the identity. Don't flag "Obvious Culprit" — it's the design. Evaluate the pleasure of watching the investigation approach known truth.
- **Psychological thriller** — Information operates through identity and psychology, not physical evidence. The "clues" are behavioral; the "red herrings" are performed identities. Don't demand forensic evidence. Evaluate the psychological information economy.

## §6. Subgenre Calibration Matrix

Each mystery/thriller mode has its own integration promise, characteristic failure pattern, and standards for what counts as informational drift.

| Subgenre | Integration Promise | Named Failure Mode | Tighten On | Loosen On |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Classic Whodunit | Fair-play puzzle with elegant, reorganizing solution | **The Locked Room Lecture** — puzzle mechanics on display without reader participation; the solution is impressive but the reader was never playing | SF, IE (tractability), RC | CM (may be implicit or absent) |
| Cozy | Puzzle embedded in community charm and amateur investigation | **Tea-Shop Stall Loop** — charming scenes that advance atmosphere but not the puzzle; the mystery is a pretext for the community | IL (amateur sleuth must be followable), SF | CM, procedural realism, RH sophistication |
| Procedural | Process credibility driving cumulative case logic | **Clipboard Carousel** — investigation demonstrates methodology without advancing understanding; scenes of "doing police work" that don't change the reader's model | IL, IE (each step must advance), RC | Pacing speed, solution surprise (process > twist) |
| Hardboiled/Noir | Investigation under moral corrosion; the detective is also investigating themselves | **Smoke Without Signal** — atmosphere and voice substituting for actual investigation; style masking structural emptiness | Thematic integration, character investigation, atmosphere-as-evidence | Strict fair-play, CM |
| Psychological Thriller | Mind-level manipulation and epistemic pressure | **Unreliable Escape Hatch** — unreliable narration without inferential structure; the reader can't form hypotheses because the rules keep changing without pattern | Uncertainty design, IL, RC (midpoint reorganization) | Physical evidence, procedural realism |
| Conspiracy/Political | Institutional threat, paranoia, narrowing options | **The Bulletin Board** — connections proliferating without narrowing; more threads don't increase tension because none resolve | IE (multiple information streams), CM, RH (paranoia grounded in actual misdirection) | Single-culprit solution, full resolution |
| Domestic Thriller | Intimate threat, trust erosion, familiar spaces made dangerous | **Kitchen Twist Roulette** — twist dependency with weak clue plumbing; proximity without information architecture | IE (behavioral clues, relationship evidence), CM (escalating domestic danger), IL | External investigation, procedural elements |
| Inverted Mystery | Culprit known; pleasure in watching investigation approach truth | **The Transparent Trap** — reader can see detective's path immediately; no pleasure in the chase because the method is obvious | IL (detective's method must compel), RC (progressive tightening), CM | Fair-play (reader already knows), identity-level surprise |
| Literary Hybrid | Mystery architecture carrying interpretive depth | **The Beautiful Fog** — literary ambiguity substituting for mystery architecture; opacity is aesthetic choice rather than puzzle design | Thematic integration, IE, ending coherence (ambiguity designed not accidental) | Genre-standard pacing, clean resolution, strict fair-play |

### Calibration principles

1. Apply the subtype's own integration promise, not a universal standard.
2. Tighten on dimensions where the subtype's satisfaction *depends* on architectural delivery.
3. Loosen on dimensions where the subtype legitimately de-prioritizes.
4. Never loosen on solution fairness in modes that promise fair-play or on clock conversion in modes that promise urgency.

## §7. Severity Hard Gates

These gates improve reproducibility. If triggered, minimum severity applies regardless of local strengths.

**Gate 1: Architecture Void**
Trigger: 4+ of 6 dimensions rated Detached overall.
Minimum: Must-Fix (Systemic). The information-pressure machine isn't running.

**Gate 2: Cheat Ending**
Trigger: SF-1 fires at the manuscript's primary resolution.
Minimum: Must-Fix. The cardinal architectural failure.

**Gate 3: Inferential Inaccessibility**
Trigger: IE-1 (Fog of Facts) + IE-2 (Clue Starvation) both fire — high noise AND low signal.
Minimum: Must-Fix.

**Gate 4: Clock Disconnect**
Trigger: CM-1 (Cosmetic Clock) fires across 2+ acts in a manuscript claiming thriller urgency.
Minimum: Should-Fix. Upgrade to Must-Fix if the clock is the primary suspense mechanism.

**Gate 5: Investigation Opacity**
Trigger: IL-1 (Leap Detective) or IL-3 (Oracle Investigator) fires at 3+ major deduction points.
Minimum: Should-Fix. Upgrade to Must-Fix in fair-play contracts.

**Gate 6: Red Herring Dominance**
Trigger: The strongest red herring is better evidenced than the actual solution.
Minimum: Must-Fix.

**Gate 7: Retroactive Incoherence**
Trigger: SF-4 fires for 2+ major clues — the solution doesn't account for planted evidence.
Minimum: Must-Fix.

**Gate 8: Culprit Underrepresentation**
Trigger: SF-2 fires AND the culprit has <10% page presence in a non-inverted, non-absence mystery.
Minimum: Must-Fix.

**Gate 9: Reveal Timing Failure**
Trigger: RC-1 (Reverse Gear Reveal) fires at the primary resolution.
Minimum: Must-Fix. (Overlaps with Gate 2 but catches the specific mechanism.)

**Gate 10: Hybrid Contract Breach**
Trigger: In hybrid manuscripts, literary/atmospheric apparatus repeatedly suppresses core mystery-thriller architecture.
Minimum: Should-Fix.

## §9. Integration with Core Framework

### Required run sequence

1. Mystery genre module AND/OR Thriller genre module (contract and calibration).
2. Core DE pass set (rhythm, emotional tracking, reveal economy).
3. Mystery/Thriller Architecture audit.
4. Pass 11 synthesis.

### Core pass connections

- **Pass 1 Reader Experience:** Supplies engagement symptoms — "I stopped caring who did it," "I figured it out too early," "the ending felt unfair."
- **Pass 3 Rhythm:** Mystery/thriller requires specific pacing patterns — investigation oscillation, clock compression, false-safety periods.
- **Pass 4 Emotional Value Tracking:** Certainty axis is primary in mystery; intensity axis primary in thriller.
- **Pass 6 Scene Function:** Mystery/thriller-specific function tags (clue planting, red herring, deduction, revelation, explanation). A scene that passes basic function requirements may still fail Fog of Facts if its function doesn't advance puzzle architecture.
- **Pass 8 Reveal Economy:** Primary input pass. This audit deepens it.
- **Pass 9 Thematic Coherence:** What the mystery/investigation *means* beyond the puzzle.

### Cross-audit pairings

- **Emotional Craft** (craft/) — Joint-run recommended when tension exists but affect doesn't transmit.
- **Horror Craft Integration** (genre/) — For mystery-horror hybrids. Both share the pressure-system diagnostic framework.
- **Literary Craft Deep Dive** (craft/) — For literary mystery/thriller. Both address literary ambition degrading genre architecture.
- **AI-Prose Calibration** (craft/) — When investigation reasoning reads as generically fluent rather than specifically deductive.
- **Character Architecture** (craft/) — When investigator or culprit characterization is the architectural weak point.

### Dynamic literary mode trigger

If the primary module is identified during intake as Literary Fiction, creating a Literary Hybrid, strictness on Solution Fairness flags is dynamically recalibrated to prioritize thematic resonance over rigid puzzle mechanics, governed by the Subgenre Calibration Matrix.

### Synthesis handoff

When handing into Pass 11 synthesis, provide:
1. Top 3 architecture failures + blast radius.
2. Clue Ledger summary — distribution, tractability ratio.
3. Clock conversion rate (if applicable).
4. Distinguish classification and rationale.
5. Contract-impact statement.

## §10. What This Audit Is Not

1. **Not a demand that readers must solve the case.** The goal is "could have" not "will." The puzzle should reward attention, not require it.

2. **Not anti-red-herring.** Misdirection is essential to mystery. The audit evaluates red herring *quality*, not red herring *presence*.

3. **Not anti-ambiguity.** Literary mystery may deliberately refuse closure. The audit evaluates whether ambiguity is *designed* or *accidental*.

4. **Not a requirement for explicit clocks.** Many mystery modes operate without time pressure. The audit evaluates clock mechanics only when the contract promises urgency.

5. **Not a replacement for genre modules.** The genre modules handle contract calibration. This audit handles whether the architecture *delivers*.

6. **Not anti-atmosphere.** Character scenes, atmospheric passages, and relationship development are valid if they carry architectural load — planting clues, building suspicion, or establishing the emotional stakes that make the puzzle matter.

7. **Not a strict fair-play enforcer.** Noir, literary mystery, and psychological thriller may deliberately bend fair-play rules. The audit evaluates whether bending serves architectural or thematic purposes.

8. **Not a formula.** There is no required number of clues, red herrings, suspects, or reveals. The audit evaluates architecture, not recipe compliance.

9. **Not hostile to character interiority.** Psychology and atmosphere are valued — provided their inclusion supports rather than replaces the inferential engine.

## Final Diagnostic Question

After artifacts, flags, tests, and gates, answer directly:

**Does this manuscript's information architecture make the reader a participant, or only a spectator?**

That answer determines revision depth, readiness, and positioning confidence.
