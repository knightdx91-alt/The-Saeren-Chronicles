# Specialized Audit: Horror Craft Integration
## Version 0.4.10
*Last Updated: February 2026*
*Synthesized from three-model research (Opus, Codex, Gemini)*
*Status: Specialized audit — genre-specific*

---

## §1. Purpose

This audit evaluates whether a manuscript's horror apparatus produces sustained horror *experience* — dread, destabilization, felt consequence, meaningful fear — or merely delivers horror-coded *content*: events that are dark, strange, or violent without generating the psychological pressure that makes horror work.

**The core problem is not darkness. It is inert dread.** A manuscript can contain extreme events, unreliable perception, body violation, cosmic threat, and psychological dissolution while remaining emotionally and cognitively unthreatening. The horror is present in the plot summary but absent from the reading experience. The events are dark. The effect is flat.

The audit's fundamental question: **Does this manuscript produce sustained horror pressure, or does it only deliver horror-adjacent content?**

### Anchor insight

Horror apparatus that doesn't produce horror experience is structurally parallel to literary craft that doesn't do work and worldbuilding that doesn't integrate. In all three cases: the labor is visible, the craft is coherent, the effect is detached. A dread sequence that could be replaced by a summary of "and then something terrible happened" without changing the reader's felt experience is the horror equivalent of a prose style that could be replaced by plain English without changing what the reader understands. **A threat that escalates in facts but not in felt consequence is the horror equivalent of a romance with plot beats but no attachment arc.**

This is a craft audit, not a content audit. Quiet horror (Jackson, Machado) is within scope alongside extreme horror (Barker, Ketchum). The question is never whether the horror is intense enough. It is whether the horror apparatus — whatever its mode — produces the pressure it promises.

### Relationship to existing infrastructure

**Genre Module: Horror** handles contract calibration, intake questions, pass modifications, and a flat flag list. This audit provides the deep diagnostic apparatus: named flags with detection criteria, tracking artifacts, severity gates, distinguish framework, and subgenre calibration with named failure modes. The genre module asks "Is the horror contract coherent?" This audit asks "Does the horror *land*?"

**Emotional Craft** diagnoses general emotional transmission failures. This audit diagnoses horror-specific transmission failures — where the horror-relevant emotions (dread, revulsion, destabilization, awe, despair) fail to transmit even when the emotional craft pipeline is otherwise functional.

**AI-Prose Calibration** diagnoses generic texture. This audit diagnoses horror effect failure that can occur in stylistically strong prose — beautiful sentences that don't produce fear.

**Literary Craft Deep Dive** diagnoses ornamental literary apparatus. This audit shares the load-bearing/ornamental framework but applies it to horror-specific craft: atmosphere, dread architecture, consequence embodiment.

**Consent Complexity** handles consent boundary tracking. This audit handles horror that operates through violation, conditioning, or body autonomy — where the horror *is* the consent architecture collapsing.

### When to activate

- Any manuscript where horror is the primary genre contract
- Literary horror where craft quality is high but dread impact is low
- Dark fantasy or Gothic fiction where atmospheric ambition exceeds atmospheric effect
- Psychological thrillers that claim horror territory
- Work where beta readers say "it's dark but I'm not scared"
- Any time an author asks "the events are horrifying — why isn't the reader horrified?"

### Exclusions

Do not run when: (1) manuscript is pre-draft ideation only, (2) horror is incidental to a non-horror contract (a thriller with one scary scene), (3) author does not claim horror burden and does not request this audit.

## §2. Horror Delivery Framework

Horror is not a content category. It is a pressure system — a set of craft commitments to producing specific psychological states in the reader through sustained, escalating, carefully managed exposure to threat, uncertainty, and consequence. These commitments succeed or fail independently, and they fail in specific, diagnosable ways.

### Six Integration Dimensions

**1. DA: Dread Architecture**
The shape of fear across the manuscript. Is dread cumulative, oscillating, and directional — building in *kind* as well as degree? Or is it flat, repetitive, and shapeless?

*Producing when:* Dread escalates not just in intensity but in type (unease → suspicion → confirmation → inescapability). Tension oscillates with purpose — relief periods make the next compression harder. The reader's anxiety is directional (moving toward something) not ambient (hanging in the air). The text manipulates the reader's predictive processing, sustaining anticipatory anxiety through the tension between what is known and what is feared.

*Inert when:* Dread stays at the same register for extended stretches. Escalation is repetitive (same threat, louder) rather than progressive (new threat, deeper). Tension doesn't oscillate — it plateaus or drops without rebuilding. Anticipatory anxiety is repeatedly dissipated before it reaches productive peaks.

**2. UD: Uncertainty Design**
The management of what the reader knows, suspects, and can't determine. Productive uncertainty generates interpretive activity (the reader forms hypotheses, revises them, experiences the revision as unsettling). Unproductive uncertainty generates confusion (the reader gives up trying to track what's real).

*Producing when:* The reader can form hypotheses about what's happening. Those hypotheses shift under new evidence. The *shifting* produces discomfort. The reader isn't confused — they're destabilized. They can track what's being questioned even when they can't resolve it.

*Inert when:* The reader can't form hypotheses (too chaotic). Or hypotheses are too easily confirmed (too stable). Or uncertainty is asserted rather than enacted — the narrator says "nothing was certain" but the reader's experience is perfectly clear. Or the text provides contradictory evidence without pattern, shutting down the reader's predictive processing rather than heightening it.

**3. TC: Threat Choreography**
The management of threat visibility — what's shown, what's hidden, when the reveal happens, what remains permanently obscured. Effective choreography generates the maximum dread per unit of information.

*Producing when:* Withholding creates anticipatory dread (the reader fears what they can't see). Reveals escalate understanding of the threat rather than simply displaying it. Partial visibility is more frightening than full exposure. What remains unknown serves the horror better than what's known.

*Inert when:* The threat is shown too early or too completely (nothing left to fear). Or the threat is withheld too long without consequence (the reader stops caring). Or reveals are anticlimactic — the full picture is less frightening than the partial one. Or reveal/withholding timing follows narrative convenience rather than pressure optimization.

**4. CE: Consequence Embodiment**
Whether horror has *cost*. Effective horror leaves traces — psychological damage, relationship corruption, physical change, identity erosion, worldview collapse. The horror happened *to* someone, and it shows.

*Producing when:* Characters are measurably changed by horror encounters. Damage persists across scenes and chapters. Behavioral patterns shift. Relationships fray. The reader can track the cost accumulating. Horror isn't an event — it's a condition.

*Inert when:* Characters experience horror and bounce back. Trauma is stated but not enacted. Damage resets between chapters. Horror events have plot consequences (the monster is released) but not psychological consequences (the character who witnessed it is unchanged). Extreme violence leaves no trace on the character's decision-making, interiority, or relationships.

**5. AP: Atmosphere and Image Pressure**
Whether the manuscript's sensory systems, image patterns, and environmental textures are doing horror work — building threat through accumulation, corrupting familiar images, making the physical world feel wrong — or are merely providing Gothic set dressing.

*Producing when:* Atmospheric details recur with escalating wrongness. Sensory information carries threat signals. The physical environment participates in the horror (spaces shrink, sounds shift, familiar objects become unfamiliar). Image patterns corrupt over time — a motif that was neutral becomes menacing through context accumulation. Setting operates as an active, hostile participant that mirrors or exacerbates the protagonist's internal state.

*Inert when:* Atmosphere is rich but static — the same "creepy" register from page one to page three hundred. Sensory detail is decorative rather than diagnostic. Image patterns repeat without corrupting. Environment functions as passive stage rather than pressure engine.

**6. ER: Ending Residue**
Whether the ending delivers the horror contract's promised payoff — whether that's catharsis, tragic destruction, deliberate denial, or lingering haunt. And whether the ending leaves *residue*: the persistent unease, altered perception, or disturbed recognition that distinguishes horror that lands from horror that merely concludes.

Possible residue models: cathartic release, pyrrhic survival, tragic collapse, ambiguous haunt, denied catharsis.

*Producing when:* The ending matches or productively subverts the stated catharsis model. Something lingers after the last page — an image, a question, a destabilized assumption. The reader's relationship to something (their body, their home, their certainty about reality) has been altered. Dread energy is transformed, not accidentally discharged.

*Inert when:* The ending resolves events without resolving (or deliberately refusing to resolve) the horror pressure. The reader closes the book feeling "that's done" rather than "that's *in* me now." Catharsis arrives without being earned. Denial of catharsis arrives without being purposeful. Late explanation domesticates the horror.

### Integration Levels

| Level | Operational definition |
|---|---|
| **Producing** | Dimension actively generates horror pressure in high-load scenes. Removing it would reduce felt horror. |
| **Partial** | Dimension functions intermittently. Contributes to pressure in some scenes but drops out in others. Setup-heavy and stress-fragile. |
| **Inert** | Dimension is present (dark content, atmospheric description, stated trauma) but doesn't produce horror pressure. Could be stripped to summary without felt loss. |

### Quick diagnostic statement

**Horror fails when threat facts escalate faster than felt consequence.**

## §3. Named Diagnostic Flags

Each flag identifies a specific pattern of horror craft failure. Flags can co-occur. Severity depends on frequency, pattern, and the manuscript's horror contract.

Severity scale: `Could-Fix` (local, non-patterned), `Should-Fix` (repeated, multi-scene), `Must-Fix` (systemic, climax/ending impact, contract breach).

### DA Flags (Dread Architecture)

**DA-1: Static Dread Loop**
Dread stays at the same register for extended stretches. The manuscript repeats the same type of threat at the same intensity, escalating in *event* (louder noises, more bodies) but not in *kind* (the reader's relationship to the threat doesn't change). The reader habituates.
- *Detect:* Map dread type and intensity across chapters. If the reader's psychological position vis-à-vis the horror is identical in Chapter 3 and Chapter 15, the loop is static. Multiple scares produce same affect beat despite different events.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if sustained across two or more acts.

**DA-2: Kindless Escalation**
Escalation increases magnitude but not category of threat. Bigger incidents repeat the same implication. Stakes rise numerically but not psychologically. Each escalation is additive, not transformative.
- *Detect:* Compare successive horror events. Do they change the reader's *relationship* to the threat, or just the *scale*? If the third body is scary for the same reasons as the first, escalation is kindless.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix.

**DA-3: Escalation Cliff**
Build-up collapses into an underpowered climactic confrontation. The reader's anticipatory dread exceeds the delivered horror. The partial was more frightening than the full.
- *Detect:* Compare the dread level in the build-up chapters to the dread level at the climax. If the climax is less frightening than the anticipation, the cliff has dropped. Common cause: the threat was more terrifying when incompletely understood.
- *Default severity:* Must-Fix when the climax is the manuscript's primary horror payoff.

**DA-4: Dread Fatigue**
Relentless high-intensity horror without oscillation. No relief, no false safety, no return to baseline. The reader's fear response exhausts rather than intensifies. Horror becomes noise.
- *Detect:* Map intensity across 10+ consecutive scenes. If there are no valleys (scenes where tension deliberately drops to create contrast), the manuscript is producing fatigue rather than escalation. Effective horror breathes — compression requires decompression.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix.

**DA-5: Anticipatory Deflation**
The text consistently dissipates tension before a peak is reached by inserting inappropriate comedic relief, sudden perspective shifts, or premature safety markers. This breaks the dread plateau and undermines anticipatory anxiety.
- *Detect:* Identify moments where dread is building toward a confrontation or revelation. Does the text interrupt the build with tonal shifts, comfort signals, or diversions that bleed off pressure before the peak? Distinguish from deliberate relief-before-storm (which serves horror) vs. chronic tension bleeding (which defeats it).
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix.

### UD Flags (Uncertainty Design)

**UD-1: Confusion Smog**
The reader cannot form meaningful hypotheses about what's happening. Uncertainty is total rather than productive. The effect is fog rather than vertigo — the reader stops trying to track reality rather than being disturbed by its instability.
- *Detect:* At key uncertainty moments, can the reader articulate at least two competing interpretations? If the reader can't form *any* hypothesis (as opposed to not being able to *choose* between hypotheses), the uncertainty is smog, not design. Evidence is too sparse, contradictory without pattern, or arbitrarily distributed.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if the horror contract depends on epistemic dread.

**UD-2: Cheap Certainty**
Uncertainty is raised and then resolved too quickly or too cleanly. The strange thing happens, the character investigates, the explanation arrives. The horror moment becomes a puzzle with a solution rather than a destabilization without resolution.
- *Detect:* Track uncertainty lifespan. If strange events are explained within 1-2 scenes, the uncertainty isn't accumulating. Effective horror uncertainty compounds — each answer raises two new questions. If certainty is restored without cost, the uncertainty was a stall tactic.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Could-Fix if limited to early chapters.

**UD-3: Stated Instability**
The narrative tells the reader that reality is uncertain without enacting uncertainty through the reading experience. "She couldn't be sure what was real" — but the reader *can* be sure, because the narration is perfectly clear. The character is destabilized; the reader is not.
- *Detect:* Identify passages that assert uncertainty. Is the reader's experience genuinely uncertain (contradictory evidence, perceptual conflicts, unreliable memory) or is the uncertainty confined to the character's stated feelings?
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if epistemic horror is the primary mode.

**UD-4: Hypothesis Starvation**
Text withholds inferential anchors needed for dread participation. Clues don't permit model revision. Repeated unknowns have no discriminating signals. The reader cannot test any prediction.
- *Detect:* At peak mystery points, does the text provide enough signal for the reader to form, test, and revise hypotheses? If not — if the unknowns are featureless rather than patterned — the reader's predictive engagement is starved rather than heightened.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if coupled with ending incoherence.

### TC Flags (Threat Choreography)

**TC-1: Monster Before Mystery**
The threat is fully displayed too early. Once the reader sees the whole picture, anticipatory dread collapses. The monster/mechanism/truth is revealed, and everything after is just watching it operate.
- *Detect:* Identify the point of maximum threat visibility. Is it before the midpoint? If so, does the manuscript have a second-order threat (the *meaning* of the monster, not just the monster) that sustains dread after the reveal? If not, the choreography front-loaded its best material.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Could-Fix if the manuscript has a deliberate second-phase horror structure.

**TC-2: Mystery Without Teeth**
Threat is withheld but nothing happens while the reader waits. The withholding creates a theoretical "something might happen" without actual pressure. Mystery without consequence.
- *Detect:* During peak withholding periods, is the reader's anticipatory dread increasing (because consequences are accumulating even without full knowledge) or stalling (because the narrative is treading water)? Effective withholding has *cost* — people are affected, things change, the unknown is demonstrably dangerous even before it's understood.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if the withholding occupies >30% of the manuscript.

**TC-3: The Diminishing Reveal**
Each new piece of information about the threat makes it *less* frightening. The partial glimpse was terrifying; the full explanation is mundane, mechanical, or disappointingly logical. Understanding domesticates the horror.
- *Detect:* Track reader dread level across the reveal sequence. If each reveal reduces rather than redirects dread, the choreography is diminishing. The best horror reveals make the threat worse by making it understood — the explanation is more horrifying than the mystery.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if the final reveal is the climactic moment.

**TC-4: Visibility Drift**
Threat visibility shifts by convenience, not architecture. Reveal/obscure toggles are unmotivated. Exposure level does not track act progression. POV access and threat visibility are misaligned.
- *Detect:* Map when the threat is visible vs hidden across the manuscript. Is the pattern architectural (building toward strategic reveals) or opportunistic (shown when the plot needs action, hidden when the plot needs suspense)?
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix.

### CE Flags (Consequence Embodiment)

**CE-1: Trauma Without Trace**
Horror events occur but leave no measurable mark on the characters who experience them. Damage is stated in the moment and absent in the next scene. Characters live in an emotional reset loop.
- *Detect:* After the manuscript's three most significant horror events, track the affected character across 3-5 subsequent scenes. Is there behavioral change? Altered decision-making? Persistent symptoms? If post-horror behavior is indistinguishable from pre-horror behavior, trauma has left no trace.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if consequence embodiment is part of the horror contract (psychological, body, domestic horror).

**CE-2: Immunity Bubble**
The protagonist passes through horror that damages or destroys other characters but remains functionally intact. The horror is real for side characters and theoretical for the lead. The reader notices the asymmetry and stops believing in the threat.
- *Detect:* Compare the protagonist's horror response to other characters' responses. If secondary characters show realistic damage while the protagonist maintains functionality, the bubble is active.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Could-Fix if the immunity is explicitly marked as abnormal (the character's lack of response is itself horrifying). Must-Fix if climax depends on unearned immunity.

**CE-3: Damage as Decoration**
Consequences are present but superficial — the character has nightmares, flinches at loud noises, "couldn't stop thinking about it" — without these effects changing what happens in the story. The damage is acknowledged without being structural. This includes graphic gore that fails to register psychological or thematic cost (the "cosmetic splatter" variant).
- *Detect:* For each stated consequence, ask: does this change a decision? Alter a relationship? Close off or open a narrative path? If the damage is present only as color and could be removed without changing any subsequent plot event, it's decorative.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Could-Fix if limited to minor characters.

### AP Flags (Atmosphere and Image Pressure)

**AP-1: Gothic Wallpaper**
Atmosphere is rich, detailed, and consistently "creepy" but doesn't generate pressure. The same dark, moody register persists from beginning to end. The atmosphere is a setting rather than a system — it creates ambiance without creating dread. The environment functions as passive stage rather than active, hostile participant.
- *Detect:* Does atmospheric intensity modulate in response to narrative events? Does it escalate? Does the atmosphere of Chapter 15 feel different from Chapter 3 — not just in detail but in pressure? If the atmospheric register is constant, it's wallpaper.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Could-Fix if atmospheric consistency is genre-appropriate and dread work is carried by other dimensions.

**AP-2: Symbolic Static**
Horror images and motifs recur without corruption or evolution. The creepy doll appears in Chapter 2 and Chapter 12 and means the same thing both times. Motifs restate their threat rather than deepening it.
- *Detect:* Track recurring horror images across appearances. Does the image change in meaning, valence, or power? Does it accumulate new associations? Or does it simply repeat? Static symbols bore; corrupting symbols disturb.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Could-Fix if the repetition serves ritual or obsessive psychology.

**AP-3: Sensory Anesthesia**
The manuscript describes horror events in language that doesn't produce sensory engagement. Clinical, removed, or generic description of things that should trigger visceral response. The prose observes the horror from a safe distance.
- *Detect:* At peak horror moments, does the prose engage specific senses (not just sight)? Does the language produce physical responses (revulsion, tension, discomfort) or merely convey information? If the horror description could be read aloud in a monotone without losing anything, the prose is anesthetized.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix if the manuscript's horror contract depends on embodied experience (body horror, visceral horror).

**AP-4: Beautiful Distance**
Literary prose that aestheticizes horror into something admirable rather than frightening. The language is gorgeous, the descriptions are artful, and the effect is appreciation rather than dread. The prose creates a frame around the horror rather than putting the reader inside it.
- *Detect:* At peak horror moments, is the reader's primary response "that's beautifully written" or "I'm afraid"? If the prose consistently converts horror into aesthetic experience, distance has replaced immersion. Compare with Literary Craft Deep Dive's Defamiliarization Test — here the question is whether literary technique produces *horror-perception* or only *craft-appreciation*.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix in literary horror where the contract promises both literary quality AND horror impact.
- *Cross-reference:* Literary Craft Deep Dive, Beautiful Rot calibration.

### ER Flags (Ending Residue)

**ER-1: Catharsis Betrayal**
The ending violates the horror contract's catharsis model without productive purpose. A manuscript that promises cathartic resolution denies it arbitrarily. A manuscript that promises denied catharsis delivers neat resolution. The mismatch feels arbitrary rather than meaningful.
- *Detect:* State the catharsis model the manuscript establishes. Does the ending deliver it? If not, does the subversion serve a thematic purpose? Purposeful catharsis subversion is valid; arbitrary catharsis mismatch is contract violation.
- *Default severity:* Must-Fix.

**ER-2: Aftertaste Null**
The ending resolves events without leaving residue. The horror is over. Nothing lingers — no disturbed image, no altered assumption, no persistent question, no residual unease. The horror was an experience bounded by the pages rather than one that bleeds past them.
- *Detect:* After the final chapter, what does the reader carry away? If the answer is only "the plot resolved," the aftertaste is null. Effective horror endings leave something: an image you can't unsee, a question you can't answer, an assumption about safety that's been destabilized.
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix for haunt/denial contracts.

**ER-3: Resolution Collapse**
The ending explains the horror so thoroughly that the explanation domesticates it. Everything is accounted for. The mystery is solved. The threat is understood. And the horror, which depended on the unknown, evaporates. Includes late-act explanation patches that add causal logic absent from earlier architecture.
- *Detect:* How much of the horror's mechanism is explained in the final act? If the ending answers every question and the answers make the horror smaller rather than deeper, the resolution has collapsed the pressure. If the final 15% introduces explanatory blocks that neutralize uncertainty, the collapse is specifically a "final-page explain patch."
- *Default severity:* Should-Fix. Must-Fix in cosmic or epistemic horror where the unknowable is the point.

## §4. Tracking Artifacts

### Artifact A: Horror Pressure Map (required)

Track horror craft delivery across the manuscript's major scenes:

| Scene Unit | Dread Level | Dread Type | Certainty Shift | Threat Visibility | Cost Signal | Dominant Flags | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ch/Scene | Low/Med/High | Unease/Suspicion/Confirmation/Inescapability | Up/Down/Stable | Hidden/Partial/Exposed/Over-Exposed | None/Stated/Enacted/Structural | Codes | Summary |

Usage rules: (1) complete map across all acts, (2) mark major reversals, (3) note where pressure changes in kind, (4) track oscillation (valleys as well as peaks).

### Artifact B: Consequence Ledger (recommended)

Track how horror events register across characters:

| Character | Horror Event | Immediate Response | Delayed Cost | Behavioral Change | Relationship Impact | Persistence |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Name | Ch/Scene | How they react in moment | What shows up later | What they do differently | How connections change | Transient / Durable |

Usage rules: (1) include all major characters, (2) require at least one delayed-cost observation per major event, (3) mark absent persistence explicitly.

### Artifact C: Uncertainty Traction Log (optional, recommended when UD flags cluster)

| Unit | Plausible Models Available | Model Revision Trigger | Reader Position | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ch/Scene | count/list | clue/event | Informed/Uncertain/Disoriented | Traction quality |

### Pattern interpretation protocol

After artifacts are complete, run:

1. **Waveform Check:** Does dread escalate in kind across acts, or only repeat in degree?
2. **Oscillation Check:** Are there deliberate relief periods creating contrast, or is intensity constant?
3. **Traction Check:** UD quality at midpoint and climax — is the reader engaged or lost?
4. **Persistence Check:** Do consequences compound across the manuscript, or reset between events?
5. **Atmosphere Conversion Check:** AP cues translating into pressure or remaining decorative?
6. **Residue Check:** Does the ending's dread level match or productively exceed the build-up?

### Reading the Map

- **Producing across dimensions and acts** → Fully integrated horror craft. The apparatus delivers. The reader is afraid.
- **Producing in Atmosphere, Inert in Consequence** → Gothic Wallpaper pattern. Beautiful dark world, nothing at stake.
- **Producing in Dread Architecture, Inert in Ending** → The Build Without Payoff. Excellent escalation, disappointing resolution.
- **Producing in early acts, declining through Act III** → Horror Fatigue or Escalation Cliff. The horror burned its best material early.
- **Producing in Consequence only** → The Damage Report. Characters suffer convincingly but the reader isn't afraid — they're sympathetic.
- **Inert across all dimensions** → Content Without Pressure. The manuscript contains horror elements but doesn't produce horror experience.
- **Partial across everything** → The Promising Draft. Horror instincts are real and partially realized but need deepening.

### Blast radius classes

1. `Local` — one scene/one event
2. `Multi-scene` — repeated in one act or channel
3. `Systemic` — cross-act or ending-critical

## §5. Distinguish Framework

Horror makes intentional choices that can look like craft failures. The audit must separate deliberate horror strategies from execution problems.

### Distinguish classes

1. **Intentional and Effective** (Pass)
2. **Intentional but Unstable** (Pass with note)
3. **Ambiguous / Developmental** (Conditional)
4. **Execution Failure** (Fail)

### Required distinguish tests

1. **Interpretive Traction Test:** Can the reader form and revise plausible horror interpretations at key uncertainty points? If yes → productive ambiguity. If no → confusion.

2. **Contract Coherence Test:** Does the ending mode match the stated or implied horror contract? Deliberate subversion has thematic purpose. Accidental mismatch doesn't.

3. **Cost Continuity Test:** Does damage persist and alter behavior across scenes? Deliberate reset (time-loop horror, amnesia horror) is marked as a mechanism. Accidental reset is sloppy.

4. **Pressure Integrity Test:** Does horror pressure hold or increase at climax and ending? Deliberate anticlimax (horror wins so completely that the ending is quiet despair) is a valid horror mode. Unintentional deflation is a craft failure.

5. **Transgression Purpose Test:** Does extreme content serve a diagnostic, thematic, or experiential function? Transgression as argument passes. Transgression as spectacle may pass in splatter/transgressive subgenre but fails elsewhere.

6. **Affect Calibration Test:** Is flat emotional register intentional (numbness as horror, dissociation as defense) or accidental? Signal: does the flatness escalate, shift, or break at key moments? Does the text frame the non-reaction as a psychological symptom through contrasting interiority or external observation?

### Decision matrix

| Test profile | Classification | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Traction productive, contract coherent, cost persistent, pressure holds, transgression purposeful, affect calibrated | Intentional and Effective | Pass with calibration notes |
| Mixed results — some tests pass, others wobble | Intentional but Unstable | Targeted Could/Should revisions |
| Traction unclear, contract ambiguous, developmental rather than failed | Ambiguous / Developmental | Could-Fix with diagnostic notes |
| Traction absent, contract violated, cost resets, pressure deflates, transgression purposeless | Execution Failure | Should/Must by hard gates |

### False-positive guardrails

Before calling systemic failure, confirm:
1. You did not mistake deliberate ambiguity for confusion. Can the reader form hypotheses? If yes, the ambiguity is productive.
2. You did not penalize denied catharsis. Horror that deliberately refuses resolution is a valid mode — flag only if the denial has no thematic function.
3. You did not demand consequence from characters designed to be already broken. If the character enters the narrative damaged, the horror may operate through different channels.
4. You did not impose one subgenre's standards on another. Body horror and cosmic horror have fundamentally different consequence models.
5. You assessed the reading *experience* of horror, not the plot *events* of horror. A disturbing summary doesn't mean a disturbing read.

### Special Caution Zones

- **Quiet horror** — Horror that operates through implication, omission, and atmosphere (Jackson, Machado, Enriquez). May score low on Dread Architecture by event-based metrics while producing intense horror through accumulation and subtext. Evaluate on its own terms.
- **Cosmic horror** — Horror through scale, incomprehensibility, and epistemological rupture. Consequence Embodiment may operate differently: the cost isn't psychological damage but the permanent alteration of what the character understands to be possible.
- **Horror-comedy** — Deliberate tonal oscillation between horror and humor. Dread fatigue rules don't apply in the same way — humor *is* the relief valve. Evaluate whether the horror moments land within the comedic framework.
- **Transgressive fiction** — Horror that pushes content boundaries deliberately. The audit evaluates craft (does the extremity do work?) not content (is the extremity acceptable?).

## §6. Subgenre Calibration Matrix

Each horror mode has its own integration promise, characteristic failure pattern, and standards for what counts as inert.

| Subgenre | Integration Promise | Named Failure Mode | Tighten On | Loosen On |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Psychological | Mind-level dread, reality destabilization, identity erosion | **Mind Maze Without Thread** — elaborate destabilization with no interpretive traction; the reader is lost, not afraid | UD, CE, protagonist interiority | Event-level intensity |
| Cosmic | Epistemic rupture, scale-induced insignificance, knowledge as damage | **Abyss on a Leash** — cosmic scale displayed but human agency and comprehension remain intact; the sublime without the terror | UD, ER, dread-through-understanding | Character-level consequence (may be obliterated rather than damaged) |
| Body | Embodied violation, identity through flesh, loss of bodily autonomy | **Meat Without Meaning** — anatomical spectacle without psychological or identity cost; gore as texture rather than crisis | CE, AP (sensory engagement), interiority during transformation | Plot-level consequence (the body IS the plot) |
| Domestic | Familiar made predatory, safety inversion, intimate threat | **Haunted Furniture Syndrome** — creepy house, creepy family, but relational stakes are static; domestic drama adorned with occasional disconnected spooky events | DA (escalation within familiar), CE (relationship damage), normalcy establishment | Action-beat density expectations |
| Folk | Cultural dread, inherited structures, collective horror, ritual as trap | **Ritual Postcard** — cultural/pagan elements displayed as set dressing without operating as an active, inescapable system that threatens to assimilate the protagonist | AP (cultural specificity), TC (folk horror's slow reveal tradition), collective consequence | Individual character consequence, Western horror pacing |
| Splatter/Transgressive | Extremity carrying thematic/psychological load, shock as argument | **Shock Treadmill** — escalating taboo and violence without engaging empathy, thematic intellect, or structural pacing; interchangeable and mathematically repetitive | CE, ER, extremity function test | Explicitness per se, atmospheric subtlety |
| Gothic/Atmospheric | Mood as pressure engine, environment as character, accumulated dread | **Velvet Rot** — gorgeous atmosphere, zero pressure; the setting is a painting, not a trap; excessive word count on architecture and weather with no threat progression | AP (atmosphere must escalate, not just persist), DA (mood must build, not sustain) | Event density, rapid-plot pacing demands |
| Literary Horror | Recognition architecture through dread, meaning through fear | **The Artful Flinch** — literary distance that converts horror into aesthetic appreciation; afraid of its own material; beautifully written but devoid of horror pressure | AP-4 (Beautiful Distance), ER (residue and recognition), Literary Craft integration | Genre-horror event pacing, jump-scare density |
| Sci-Fi Horror | Technological unknown, isolation, paranoia about what's human, internal invasion | **The Laser-Gun Safari** — terrifying threats mitigated by overabundant technology; horror reduced to military combat simulation; the protagonist's arsenal neutralizes dread | DA (isolation must constrain resources), CE (paranoia must alter trust), UD (who/what is compromised) | Hard science accuracy |

### Calibration principles

1. Apply the subgenre's own integration promise, not a universal horror standard.
2. Tighten on dimensions where the subgenre's horror *depends* on craft delivery.
3. Loosen on dimensions where the subgenre legitimately de-prioritizes.
4. Never loosen on ending alignment or consequence embodiment in the subgenre's primary channel.

## §7. Severity Hard Gates

These gates improve reproducibility. If triggered, minimum severity applies regardless of local strengths.

**Gate 1: Pressure Void**
Trigger: 4+ of 6 dimensions rated Inert overall.
Minimum: Must-Fix (Systemic). The horror contract is unfulfilled.

**Gate 2: Ending Contract Failure**
Trigger: ER-1 (Catharsis Betrayal) fires at the manuscript's primary resolution.
Minimum: Must-Fix.

**Gate 3: Consequence Void**
Trigger: CE-1 or CE-2 fires for the protagonist across 2+ major horror events.
Minimum: Should-Fix. Upgrade to Must-Fix if the horror contract depends on psychological cost.

**Gate 4: Dread Stasis**
Trigger: DA-1 (Static Dread Loop) persists across 3+ act boundaries with no qualitative escalation.
Minimum: Should-Fix. Upgrade to Must-Fix in manuscripts >60K words.

**Gate 5: Epistemic Failure**
Trigger: UD-1 (Confusion Smog) or UD-3 (Stated Instability) fires in manuscripts claiming epistemic/psychological horror as primary mode.
Minimum: Must-Fix. The core horror mechanism is broken.

**Gate 6: Climax Underperformance**
Trigger: DA-3 (Escalation Cliff) combined with TC-3 (Diminishing Reveal) at the manuscript's climactic horror sequence.
Minimum: Must-Fix.

**Gate 7: Reveal Timing Failure**
Trigger: TC-1 (Monster Before Mystery) + TC-2 (Mystery Without Teeth) both persistent, indicating simultaneous overexposure and weak withholding.
Minimum: Should-Fix.

**Gate 8: Atmosphere Without Engine**
Trigger: AP rated Producing while DA, CE, and UD are rated Inert. Beautiful atmosphere carrying no other horror dimension.
Minimum: Should-Fix.

**Gate 9: Residue Null in Haunt Contract**
Trigger: Contract expects ambiguous/denied/haunting residue and ER-2 (Aftertaste Null) fires.
Minimum: Must-Fix.

## §10. What This Audit Is Not

1. **Not a content audit.** The audit evaluates whether horror *works*, not whether horror *should*. Darkness, violence, body horror, transgression, and extremity are evaluated for craft delivery, not appropriateness.

2. **Not a scare-count test.** The audit doesn't measure how many horror events occur. One scene of quiet dread can outperform ten scenes of graphic violence.

3. **Not anti-ambiguity.** Ambiguous horror is often the strongest horror mode. The audit penalizes ambiguity that produces confusion, not ambiguity that produces dread.

4. **Not a mandate for catharsis.** Horror that deliberately denies resolution is genre-appropriate. The audit evaluates whether the ending *matches* the contract, not whether it provides comfort.

5. **Not anti-quiet.** Quiet horror — through implication, omission, atmospheric accumulation — is fully valid. The audit measures pressure, not volume.

6. **Not a replacement for the genre module.** The genre module handles contract calibration. This audit handles whether the horror *delivers*.

7. **Not anti-transgression.** Extreme content has a place. The audit evaluates whether extremity serves craft goals or exists for its own sake.

8. **Not a demand for explanation.** Horror that refuses to explain may be stronger for its refusal. The audit penalizes explanation that domesticates horror, not absence of explanation.
