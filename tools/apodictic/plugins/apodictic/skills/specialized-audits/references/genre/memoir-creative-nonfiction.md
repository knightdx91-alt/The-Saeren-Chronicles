# Specialized Audit: Memoir & Creative Nonfiction
## Version 0.4.4.1
*Last Updated: February 2026*

---

## Purpose

This audit evaluates craft challenges specific to memoir and creative nonfiction—the tension between narrative and factual truth, ethical obligations to living persons, scene reconstruction from memory, and the particular relationship between author, narrator, and subject. Applies to memoir, personal essay, narrative nonfiction, and any work presenting lived experience as its primary material.

**When to activate:**
- Memoir (book-length personal narrative)
- Personal essay (shorter form)
- Narrative nonfiction with significant personal content
- "Based on true events" fiction drawing heavily from author's life
- Family history or generational narratives
- Illness/recovery narratives
- Travel memoir
- Work where author and protagonist are the same person

**This audit does not judge the ethics of writing about one's life. It evaluates craft—whether the work achieves its intended relationship with truth while fulfilling narrative obligations.**

---

## Core Questions

The audit addresses these fundamental questions:

1. **Truth Contract:** What relationship to factual truth is the work promising? Is that promise clear to the reader?
2. **Memory as Material:** How does the work handle the inherent unreliability of memory?
3. **Living Persons:** Are ethical obligations to real people being met?
4. **Scene Reconstruction:** Are recreated scenes plausible and narratively honest?
5. **The Narrator's "I":** What is the relationship between the author, the narrator, and the past self?
6. **Transformation:** Does the narrator achieve insight, or merely report events?

---

## The Truth Spectrum

Different works promise different relationships to factual truth. Establish during intake:

### Truth Positions

| Position | Promise | Example |
|----------|---------|---------|
| **Journalistic** | Verifiable facts, documented sources, no invented scenes | Narrative journalism |
| **Memoir Standard** | Emotional truth, scenes from memory, compressed timelines | Most memoir |
| **Impressionistic** | Truth of experience over factual precision | Lyric essay |
| **Explicitly Reconstructed** | "Based on" with acknowledged invention | Some family histories |
| **Autofiction** | Blurred line between memoir and fiction | Genre-crossing work |

**Intake Addition:**
```
TRUTH POSITION: [Where on spectrum]
DISCLOSED MODIFICATIONS: [What reader knows is altered—names, timeline, composites]
UNDISCLOSED MODIFICATIONS: [What's altered without explicit disclosure—evaluate ethically]
VERIFICATION LEVEL: [How much is documentable vs. memory-dependent]
```

### The Truth Contract Test

For any scene or claim, ask:
1. Could this be verified by external sources?
2. If not, is it presented as memory (inherently imperfect) or fact?
3. Would a reader feel deceived if they learned how this was constructed?

**Red Flag:** Presenting invented details as verified fact
**Acceptable:** Presenting reconstructed dialogue as "what I remember" or "something like"

## Memory as Material

### The Unreliability Problem

All memoir is constructed from imperfect memory. The ethical writer acknowledges this; the craft question is how.

**Memory Handling Strategies:**

**1. Direct Acknowledgment**
- "I don't remember exactly what she said, but..."
- "My memory of that year is fragmented..."
- Author's note explaining reconstruction approach

**2. Structural Acknowledgment**
- Present tense for certainty, past tense for reconstruction
- Fragmented structure mirroring fragmented memory
- Multiple versions of the same event from different memory-moments

**3. Implicit Acknowledgment**
- Avoiding false precision
- Using "perhaps" and "I imagine" appropriately
- Not over-specifying details that couldn't be remembered

### The Specificity Trap

**Problem:** Memoir that reads like fiction—precise dialogue from decades ago, exact descriptions of rooms, play-by-play action.

**Diagnostic Questions:**
1. Is this level of detail plausible from memory?
2. If reconstructed, is that clear to the reader?
3. Does the precision serve the story or create false authority?

**Acceptable specificity:** "The kitchen smelled of cinnamon" (sensory memory, plausible)
**Suspicious specificity:** "She said, 'I've been thinking about this for exactly three weeks, and I've decided we need to talk about your father's drinking'" (dialogue too precise for memory)

### When Memory Fails

The most honest memoirs often acknowledge gaps:
- "I don't know what happened next"
- "My mother's version of this differs"
- "I've told this story so many times I'm no longer sure what I actually remember"

**Flag:** Memoir that never acknowledges uncertainty may be inventing certainty.

## Living Persons

### Ethical Framework

Writing about real people creates obligations. Track:

**Categories of Person:**

| Category | Obligation Level | Considerations |
|----------|------------------|----------------|
| **Self** | Full autonomy | But consider: future self, public persona |
| **Willing Participants** | Informed consent | Did they agree to appear? Know how they'd be portrayed? |
| **Family** | Complex obligations | Privacy vs. truth; their story vs. your story |
| **Unwilling Subjects** | High ethical bar | Did they consent to being written about? |
| **Public Figures** | Lower obligation | But still accuracy and fairness concerns |
| **Dead** | Reduced but not zero | Cannot consent; surviving family may be affected |
| **Minors** | Special protection | Cannot meaningfully consent; identity protection |

### The Consent Spectrum

**Explicit consent:** Person knows they'll appear, has seen portrayal, agrees
**Implicit consent:** Person knows author writes memoir, hasn't objected
**No consent:** Person unaware they'll appear or has objected
**Cannot consent:** Dead, estranged, minors, cognitively impaired

**Intake Addition:**
```
LIVING PERSONS: [List significant real people appearing]
CONSENT STATUS: [For each: explicit / implicit / none / cannot]
DISGUISE LEVEL: [Names changed? Details altered? Composites?]
RELATIONSHIP STATUS: [Current relationship with author—affects obligation]
```

### The "Would They Recognize Themselves?" Test

For each significant real person:
1. Would they recognize themselves?
2. Would they object to how they're portrayed?
3. If they would object, is your portrayal fair and necessary?
4. What is your relationship now, and does the book risk that?

**This audit does not adjudicate these questions—it surfaces them for author decision.**

### Composite Characters

Creating one character from multiple real people:

**When Defensible:**
- Protects privacy of individuals
- Streamlines narrative without distorting truth
- Disclosed to reader

**When Problematic:**
- Creates false attribution (composite did something only one person did)
- Invents relationship dynamics that didn't exist
- Hidden from reader who would expect real individuals

**Flag:** Undisclosed composite who takes significant action in the narrative.

## Scene Reconstruction

### The Building Blocks Problem

Memoir requires scenes, but memory doesn't provide scenes—it provides fragments.

**Scene Reconstruction Hierarchy (most to least defensible):**

1. **Documented scenes:** Video, audio, contemporaneous writing
2. **Corroborated scenes:** Multiple witnesses, consistent accounts
3. **Vivid memory scenes:** Author's clear, detailed recollection
4. **Reconstructed scenes:** Built from fragments, research, plausibility
5. **Imagined scenes:** Author wasn't present; constructed from other sources
6. **Invented scenes:** Pure fiction presented as memoir

**Positions 1-4:** Generally acceptable in memoir
**Position 5:** Requires disclosure or careful framing
**Position 6:** Ethically indefensible unless work is autofiction

### Dialogue Reconstruction

The hardest problem in memoir. No one remembers exact dialogue from years ago.

**Defensible Approaches:**
- "Something like" framing: *He said something like, "I never wanted this."*
- Essence capture: Present the meaning, not the words
- Acknowledged reconstruction: Author's note explaining approach
- Minimal dialogue: Use less direct quote, more summary and indirect

**Indefensible Approaches:**
- Extended dialogue presented as verbatim
- Dialogue for scenes author wasn't present for (without disclosure)
- Dialogue that serves narrative but doesn't reflect actual dynamic

### The "Could This Be True?" Test

For any reconstructed scene:
1. Is this consistent with documented facts?
2. Is this consistent with what author could plausibly remember?
3. Is this consistent with the real people's known character?
4. Would participants recognize this as substantially accurate?

## The Three "I"s

### Author, Narrator, Past Self

Memoir involves three distinct entities:

**The Author:** The person writing now, with current understanding
**The Narrator:** The voice telling the story, making meaning
**The Past Self:** The person who lived the events, with limited understanding

**Key Craft Question:** What is the distance between these three?

### Distance as Technique

**Close Narrator/Past Self:**
- Present tense, immersive
- Reader experiences events as past self did
- Limited retrospective insight
- Works for: trauma narrative, coming-of-age, immediacy

**Distant Narrator/Past Self:**
- Reflective, essayistic
- Narrator comments on past self's errors
- Heavy retrospective insight
- Works for: intellectual memoir, recovery narrative, wisdom literature

**Variable Distance:**
- Moves between close and distant
- Tracks growing understanding
- Most sophisticated but hardest to control
- Works for: complex transformation narratives

### The "Insight Gradient"

Track: How does the narrator's understanding change through the book?

**Beginning:** Narrator knows what past self didn't
**Middle:** Narrator discovers what author didn't know at start of writing
**End:** Reader, narrator, and author converge (or deliberately don't)

**Problem Sign:** Narrator who knows everything from page one (no discovery)
**Problem Sign:** Narrator who never achieves insight (just reports)

## Transformation and Arc

### The Memoir Arc

Memoir requires transformation—not just "things happened to me" but "I became someone who understands differently."

**Memoir Arc Formula:**
Past Self (limited understanding) → Events → Narrator (transformed understanding)

**The transformation can be:**
- Intellectual: "I now understand what I didn't then"
- Emotional: "I can now feel what I couldn't then"
- Relational: "I now see others as I couldn't then"
- Identity: "I now know who I am"

### The "So What?" Test

For any memoir:
1. Why does this story matter beyond the author's life?
2. What does the reader gain from this narration?
3. What universal truth or question does the particular illuminate?

**Flag:** Memoir that assumes reader interest without earning it

### Earned vs. Unearned Insight

**Earned insight:** Emerges from the events, feels discovered
**Unearned insight:** Arrives from outside, feels imposed

**Test:** Does the climactic understanding come FROM the narrative or TO the narrative?

## Structure in Memoir

### Viable Structures

**Chronological:** Events in order
- Simplest to follow
- Requires inherent narrative arc in actual events
- Risk: Events don't naturally build

**Thematic:** Organized by theme, not time
- Allows connections across time
- Requires strong thematic coherence
- Risk: Reader loses narrative thread

**Braided:** Multiple timelines interwoven
- Past events + present reflection
- Childhood + adulthood
- Requires clear signposting

**Fragmented:** Deliberately non-linear
- Mirrors memory or trauma
- Requires accumulation toward meaning
- Risk: Reader gives up

**The Quest:** Built around seeking (person, truth, identity)
- External journey structures internal journey
- Clear forward momentum
- Risk: Forced frame

### What Memoir Cannot Borrow from Fiction

- **Omniscient narration:** You can only know what you experienced
- **Multiple POV:** You can only inhabit your own perspective
- **Dramatic irony:** Only in retrospect
- **Closed endings:** Life continues; closure must be earned differently

## Meaning Engine & Narrating Intelligence (Gornick Layer)

**Based on:** Vivian Gornick's *The Situation and the Story*, adapted as a formal diagnostic for the meaning-making engine in memoir and personal narrative.

The ethics layer above handles what can be responsibly claimed and reconstructed. This layer handles a different question: **is the meaning engine working?** Does the piece have a story beneath the situation — an evolving understanding, not just a sequence of events? Is there a narrating intelligence shaping selection toward meaning? Is the braid of scene and interpretation functional?

### When to Activate

**Primary activation:** Run when manuscript is memoir, personal essay, autobiographical nonfiction, lyric nonfiction, or narrator-forward reported work.

**Trigger conditions (run when ≥2 are true):**
- Diagnostic Flags fire: "No Transformation," "Therapy Memoir," or "Insight Dump"
- Pass 1 reader experience flags: "so what?" / "why this, why now?" / "unclear what changed"
- Franklin Pathway returns Classification 4 (Not Storyable) but material is personal narrative rather than topical/analytic
- Franklin Pathway Narrative Stance Diagnostic flags "Situation Without Story" or "Static Narrator"
- Ending reads as summary or moral banner rather than recontextualization

**Routing:**
- If no narrator stance exists because the piece is fully reported and narrator-invisible → route to Narrative Nonfiction Craft audit (Hart) instead
- If the piece is primarily argument with vignettes → route to Dialectical Clarity Audit and run this layer only on vignettes if they function as narrative segments

### Firewall Compliance

**Allowed:**
- Situation/story separation statements extracted from existing text
- Diagnostic stance classification + distance profile
- Evidence-based failure coding (where the braid breaks)
- Structural requirements ("needs revaluation evidence," "distance collapsed," etc.)
- Selection/compression diagnosis (what is redundant vs. missing function)

**Not Allowed:**
- Inventing "the lesson" the narrator should draw
- Inventing new events or rewriting scenes
- Coaching exercises (journaling prompts, "write a letter to your younger self")

### Code Namespace Note

This layer uses GS-codes (situation/story gate), NI-codes (narrating intelligence), SC-codes (stance coherence), TD-codes (temporal distance), BR-codes (braid integrity), SEL-codes (selection/compression), and EP-codes (ending stance proof).

**BR** is used instead of B to avoid collision with Emotional Craft Diagnostics B-codes (B1–B3 breakpoints). **EP** is used instead of E to avoid collision with Narrative Nonfiction Craft E-codes (E0–E3 ending payoff).

**No collisions with existing code systems:**
- Scene Turn Diagnostics: G/C/O/Sq/H/U/P
- Emotional Craft Diagnostics: S/B
- Narrative Nonfiction Craft: F/QS/I/ST/SW/AS/A/LC/E
- Character Architecture Part 9: M/W/N/DN/OCA/PW/SR/MC/TP
- Dialectical Clarity: AT/CL/SM/BP/OB/NE

All seven code systems in this layer may appear in the same editorial letter alongside codes from other audits.

### Scope

**Default scope:**

| Component | What to Sample |
|-----------|---------------|
| Opening | First 10–15% of the piece |
| Pressure moments | 1–2 scenes where the narrator confronts the material's difficulty |
| Ending | Final section |

**Expanded scope (optional):** Full braid map across all major sections for long-form memoir.

### Vocabulary

These terms supplement the existing Memoir audit vocabulary.

- **Situation:** What happened — the external events and circumstances.
- **Story:** What the narrator comes to understand — the evolving interpretation or judgment.
- **Narrating intelligence:** The felt presence of a mind selecting and shaping material toward meaning. Not self-display; not confessional outpouring. A mind at work.
- **Distance control:** Deliberate management of "then" (experiencing self, limited knowledge) vs. "now" (narrating self, retrospective understanding).
- **Braid:** The alternating or entwined movement between situation material and story-making meaning. Each feeds the other.

### The Diagnostic Procedure

#### Step 1: Situation/Story Gate (GS-codes)

The core gate. Produce two statements:

```
SITUATION: In [time/place], [I/we] [did/experienced X], which led to [Y].
  → Concrete external circumstances and events.

STORY: Over the course of the piece, the narrator learns/admits/revises that [Z].
  → Evolving interpretation, judgment, or self-knowledge.
```

**Classify:**
- **PASS:** Both are extractable from the text, stable, and distinct
- **PARTIAL:** One exists but is thin, unstable, or generic
- **FAIL:** Cannot state one without inventing

**Situation/Story failure codes:**

| Code | Name | Description |
|------|------|-------------|
| **GS0** | Situation without story | Events are reported; understanding does not evolve. The narrator at the end knows what they knew at the beginning. |
| **GS1** | Story without situation | Reflection dominates; events are thin or generic. The meaning claims float free of concrete experience. |
| **GS2** | Unstable story claim | The "lesson" shifts or contradicts itself across the piece without the shift being part of the story. |
| **GS3** | Story claim too broad | Generic moral ("be yourself," "love is complicated") not earned by the particulars of this situation. |

**Cross-reference:** This formalizes the Situation/Story Distinction already present in Franklin's Narrative Stance Diagnostic, which has logic gates for "Situation Without Story" and "Static Narrator" but no formal failure codes. GS-codes provide the diagnostic precision.

#### Step 2: Narrating Intelligence Check (NI-codes)

Test whether the text shows a distinct narrating intelligence — Gornick's "writer on the page" — selecting and shaping material toward meaning. This is not self-display. It's interpretive agency.

**Look for:**
- **Interpretive moves:** contrasts, pattern recognition, causal claims, moral judgments, recontextualization (not just feelings)
- **Purposeful selection:** what's included and omitted signals intention
- **Distance control:** the narrator knows what they know now and uses it deliberately

```
NARRATING INTELLIGENCE:
  Interpretive moves present: [Y/N — with examples]
  Selection feels purposeful: [Y/N]
  Distance deployed deliberately: [Y/N]
  Code: [NI-code or PASS]
```

**Narrating Intelligence failure codes:**

| Code | Name | Description |
|------|------|-------------|
| **NI0** | Transcript voice | Recounting without interpretation. Events are reported in sequence; the narrator doesn't shape or select. Reads like a diary log. |
| **NI1** | Therapy voice | Insight language without concrete pressure-testing. "I realized..." appears frequently, but the realizations aren't earned by or anchored to specific events. |
| **NI2** | Lecture voice | Interpretation overwhelms the scene. The reader can't see the evidence because the narrator won't stop explaining what it means. |
| **NI3** | Neutrality mask | Narrator pretends not to interpret, but selection implies judgment. The stance is covert — the reader senses an argument the narrator won't own. |

**Cross-reference with existing Diagnostic Flags:** "Therapy Memoir" → NI1. "Insight Dump" → NI2. The NI-codes provide formal classification where the existing flags are informal.

#### Step 3: Stance Coherence (SC-codes)

Test whether the narrator maintains a coherent attitude toward the past self and events — and whether any shift feels earned.

**Extends Franklin's Stance Classification** (Witness/Investigator/Advocate/Participant/Invisible) with a formal coherence test. Gornick adds: stance is not just role — it's attitude + distance.

```
STANCE:
  Classification: [Witness / Investigator / Advocate / Participant / Invisible]
  Attitude toward past self: [tender / ironic / condemnatory / bewildered / protective / clinical]
  Consistent: [Y/N]
  If shifts — earned: [Y/N — what triggers the shift?]
  Code: [SC-code or PASS]
```

**Stance Coherence failure codes:**

| Code | Name | Description |
|------|------|-------------|
| **SC0** | Stance drift | Tone and attitude swing without narrative reason. The narrator is tender in one section, contemptuous in the next, with no event explaining the shift. |
| **SC1** | Stance mismatch | Claims one relationship to material, demonstrates another. Narrator claims humility but writes with certainty — or claims certainty but hedges constantly. |
| **SC2** | Contempt problem | Narrator sneers at past self or others, undermining reader trust. The distance between narrator and past self becomes a weapon rather than a tool. |
| **SC3** | Self-exculpation | Narrator uses stance to evade moral responsibility. The reader senses the narrator is performing innocence or victimhood rather than reckoning. |

**Cross-reference:** Franklin's Stance Consistency Check has logic gates (stance consistent → PASS, drift without acknowledgment → FLAG). SC-codes replace the informal FLAGS with formal failure codes.

#### Step 4: Temporal Distance Control (TD-codes)

Test whether the relationship between "experiencing self" (then) and "narrating self" (now) is cleanly managed.

```
DISTANCE PROFILE:
  Then markers: [clear / unclear / absent]
  Now markers: [clear / unclear / absent]
  Balance: [THEN-HEAVY / NOW-HEAVY / BALANCED / WHIPLASH]
  Code: [TD-code or PASS]
```

**Temporal Distance failure codes:**

| Code | Name | Description |
|------|------|-------------|
| **TD0** | Collapsed distance | Narrator knows too much "then." Later insight is present in the moment; discovery is flattened. The reader never experiences the past self's limited understanding. |
| **TD1** | Overdistance | Narrator is always "above" the moment. No lived immediacy — no sensory detail, no confusion, no felt experience. The past is reported rather than entered. |
| **TD2** | Whiplash | Switches between then and now without control. The reader loses track of which self is speaking. Tense shifts without purpose. |
| **TD3** | Retcon effect | Later insights are inserted into earlier scenes as if known at the time. "I knew even then that..." when the narrator clearly didn't. |

**Cross-reference with "The Three I's":** The existing section on Author/Narrator/Past Self and Distance as Technique provides the conceptual framework. TD-codes provide the formal diagnostic.

#### Step 5: Two-Stories Braid Integrity (BR-codes)

Test whether situation scenes and story-meaning moves braid together, each feeding the other.

```
BRAID CHECK:
  Do situation segments create questions that narrating intelligence addresses? [Y/N]
  Are interpretive passages anchored to specific event-details? [Y/N]
  Code: [BR-code or PASS]
```

**Braid Integrity failure codes:**

| Code | Name | Description |
|------|------|-------------|
| **BR0** | Parallel tracks | Scenes and reflection alternate but don't inform each other. The narrative runs on two rails that never meet. |
| **BR1** | Evidence gap | Interpretation lacks scene evidence. The narrator makes meaning claims that the dramatized events don't support. |
| **BR2** | Scene gap | Scenes occur but the narrator doesn't process their meaning. Events accumulate without interpretation — the braid's meaning strand goes silent. |
| **BR3** | Redundant processing | Narrator keeps drawing the same conclusion from different events. No evolution of understanding — the braid repeats rather than builds. |

#### Step 6: Selection & Compression (SEL-codes)

Test whether the chosen material is the right evidence for the story claim. This is Franklin's "surgery list" philosophy applied to memoir selection.

```
For each major dramatized event:
  Event: [description]
  Necessary to story claim: [Y/N]
  Escalates meaning: [Y/N — does this scene produce new understanding, or repeat?]
  Code: [SEL-code or PASS]
```

**Selection & Compression failure codes:**

| Code | Name | Description |
|------|------|-------------|
| **SEL0** | Wrong evidence | Vivid events that don't support the story claim. Compelling scenes that belong in a different memoir. |
| **SEL1** | Missing crucible | The story claim implies a turning point or test that isn't shown. The narrator claims transformation, but we don't see the moment of crisis. |
| **SEL2** | Repetition plateau | Multiple scenes produce the same meaning without escalation. Each scene is individually well-crafted; together they don't build. |
| **SEL3** | Context smother (memoir variant) | Backstory precedes pressure. The reader gets the narrator's history before they have any reason to care about it. |

**Cross-reference:** SEL3 is the memoir-specific form of Franklin's Context Smother Diagnostic (CLI > 0.70). Franklin measures information proportion; SEL3 diagnoses the same problem in memoir-specific terms. Also cross-references the Franklin Pathway's Dramatization Test (dramatize the turns; summarize the rest).

#### Step 7: Ending Stance Proof (EP-codes)

Test whether the ending proves a changed stance — not just states it.

```
ENDING ASSESSMENT:
  Recontextualizes earlier material: [Y/N — does the reader see the opening differently?]
  Narrator's stance has shifted or clarified: [Y/N]
  Concluding move feels earned by the braid: [Y/N — inevitable from what preceded, not appended]
  Code: [EP-code or PASS]
```

**Ending Stance Proof failure codes:**

| Code | Name | Description |
|------|------|-------------|
| **EP0** | Summary ending | Recaps what happened rather than what it means now. The narrator finishes with events, not understanding. |
| **EP1** | Moral banner ending | Tells the lesson without recontextualizing evidence. "And that's when I learned..." without the scene work to earn it. |
| **EP2** | No stance closure | Narrator finishes where they started. The story claim promised transformation; the ending delivers none. |
| **EP3** | New claim at end | Introduces a fresh interpretation not earned by the preceding material. The ending feels like a pivot to a different essay. |

**Cross-reference:** The Narrative Nonfiction Craft audit's E-codes (E0–E3) diagnose nonfiction endings generally. EP-codes diagnose memoir endings specifically — testing whether the stance has moved, not just whether the CQ was answered. Both may apply to memoir; they diagnose different dimensions. E-codes ask "did the ending pay off the reader contract?" EP-codes ask "did the ending prove a changed understanding?"

### Common Patterns

Named diagnostic patterns this layer is designed to catch.

**FM-G1: Situation Without Story**
Signature: GS0 + NI0 or NI1.
Events reported faithfully, often in vivid detail. But the narrator hasn't found the story — the evolving understanding that turns situation into meaning. The narrating intelligence is either absent (NI0 transcript) or performing insight without earning it (NI1 therapy voice). Common in early memoir drafts and in material written primarily for the author's own processing.

**FM-G2: Story Without Situation**
Signature: GS1 + SEL1.
The narrator has an interpretation, often a sophisticated one, but the concrete events are thin or generic. The meaning claims float free. The crucible that would test the story claim is missing — the narrator tells us what they learned without showing us the pressure that forced the learning.

**FM-G3: Therapy Voice Drift**
Signature: NI1 + BR1.
The narrator uses insight language ("I realized," "I came to understand," "I now see that") but the realizations aren't anchored to specific dramatized events. The braid's evidence strand is missing. The piece reads as processed rather than discovered. Common when the writer has done extensive therapeutic work on the material — the insights are real, but the connection to scene has been internalized and not reproduced on the page.

**FM-G4: Distance Collapse**
Signature: TD0 or TD3.
The narrator-now keeps appearing in the experiencing-self's scenes. Later knowledge is inserted into earlier moments. The effect is a flattened temporality — the reader never experiences the past self's confusion, fear, or limited understanding because the narrating self can't stop annotating. The discovery arc is killed by premature explanation.

**FM-G5: Parallel Tracks**
Signature: BR0 + EP0 or EP1.
Scene material and reflective material alternate but don't inform each other. The braid isn't braiding — it's two essays interleaved. The ending either summarizes (EP0) or moralizes (EP1) because the meaning was never woven into the scenes. Common in memoir structured by chronology rather than by the narrator's evolving understanding.

**FM-G6: Redundancy Plateau**
Signature: SEL2 + BR3.
Multiple scenes produce the same meaning. The narrator keeps drawing the same conclusion from different events. Each scene is well-crafted in isolation, but together they don't build. The braid repeats rather than escalates. Common in trauma memoir (where the pattern is genuinely repetitive in life) and in memoir by writers who found their story claim early and can't stop proving it.

### Genre Calibration

**Short personal essays (< 3,000 words):** Expect fewer braid turns. Require at least one clear revaluation or stance clarification. GS and EP are the critical tests — the essay must have a story claim and the ending must prove it. SEL is less diagnostic (fewer events to select from).

**Memoir chapters (standalone or in a book):** GS and SEL become critical — is this chapter contributing to the book's story claim, or repeating? TD errors are common in memoir chapters (retcon and whiplash increase as the author manages multiple timeframes across a longer work).

**Lyric nonfiction:** Allow looser situation density — lyric nonfiction may be impressionistic rather than event-heavy. But NI and BR must still hold: interpretation must be anchored to specific particulars, even if the particulars are sensory or imagistic rather than narrative.

**Refusal arcs (no growth):** Some pieces are designed as refusal narratives — the narrator recognizes something but refuses transformation. This can pass if: the story statement is "the narrator recognizes X but refuses Y," and the ending proves the refusal-stance rather than drifting. GS2 does not apply when the instability IS the story claim.

### Output Delivered

**Gornick Summary Block (always produced):**
1. Situation statement
2. Story statement
3. Stance classification + attitude + distance profile
4. Braid verdict (PASS / PARTIAL / FAIL)

**Test Results (per step):**
For each of Steps 1–7: PASS / PARTIAL / FAIL + evidence references + code(s) + firewall-safe structural requirement.

**Failure Mode Summary:** Named patterns (FM-G1 through FM-G6) with code signatures.

**Revision Targets (firewall-safe):**
Targets are framed as structural requirements, not content:
- "Story statement requires a demonstrable revaluation under pressure."
- "Interpretive moves must be anchored to concrete scene evidence."
- "Then/Now distance must be stabilized; avoid inserting later knowledge into then-scenes."
- "Identify at least one crucible moment where the story claim is tested."
- "Compress repetitive scenes that produce no new meaning turn."
- "Ending must recontextualize earlier events rather than summarize."

### Orchestration with Other Layers and Audits

**With the Ethics Layer (Memoir audit sections above):** The ethics layer asks what can be responsibly claimed and reconstructed. The Gornick layer asks whether the meaning engine works. They operate on the same material but diagnose different problems. A memoir can be ethically rigorous (truth contract honored, living persons protected) and still fail the Gornick tests (no story beneath the situation). The reverse is also possible: brilliant meaning-making that takes indefensible liberties with truth.

**With Franklin Pathway (Narrative Stance Diagnostic):** Franklin's Narrative Stance section includes the Situation/Story Distinction and Stance Consistency Check — both citing Gornick. The Gornick layer provides the formal code taxonomies (GS/SC/TD) that upgrade Franklin's informal logic gates. When Franklin flags "Situation Without Story" or "Static Narrator," the Gornick layer provides the detailed diagnostic. Also: when Franklin returns Classification 4 (Not Storyable) for personal narrative, run the Gornick layer before issuing redirect — the piece may have a story at the stance level (narrator's revaluation) even if it lacks a story at the event level.

**With Narrative Nonfiction Craft Audit (Hart):** Hart diagnoses reader contract, central question, information timing, and status pressure in reported scenes. Gornick diagnoses narrating intelligence, stance, distance, and meaning braid. For nonfiction that includes both narrator stance and heavy reporting, run Hart first for contract clarity, then Gornick for meaning engine coherence. Hart's SW-codes (meaning line) correspond to GS-codes (situation/story) — SW diagnoses whether a meaning line exists in nonfiction generally; GS diagnoses whether a story-beneath-the-situation exists in memoir specifically.

**With Emotional Craft Diagnostics:** A memoir scene can pass the Gornick braid test (BR passes — scene creates pressure that meaning addresses) but fail emotional transmission (Emotional Craft S-codes fire). The Gornick layer tests structural integration of scene and meaning; Emotional Craft tests whether the prose transmits feeling. Both may apply to the same scenes.

**With Character Architecture Part 9:** If the memoir has a moral argument — the narrator's revaluation involves a moral position — Part 9's M-codes can apply to the story claim. The Gornick story statement may map to Part 9's Moral Argument Hypothesis. But Part 9 tests moral argument through character action (weakness → need → opponent → choice); the Gornick layer tests meaning through narrating intelligence and stance. They diagnose different mechanisms.

## Diagnostic Flags

### Must-Fix Floor — Hard Gates

The four flags below are **audit-internal hard gates**. When any one fires, it carries an audit-internal **Must-Fix floor** that propagates to synthesis severity per the canonical Audit-Signal Propagation Rule in `core-editor/references/run-synthesis.md §Step 2`. Synthesis cannot downgrade a hard-gate flag below Must-Fix without an explicit override marker recording rationale. These hard gates are why the Memoir & Creative Nonfiction audit is Auto-run for memoir and creative-nonfiction manuscripts: the audit's gates are definitional to the truth-contract of the form.

**"Memory Fraud"** *(Hard Gate)*
- Memoir presents invented scenes as factual
- Extended precise dialogue from events decades ago
- Details that couldn't plausibly be remembered, presented without acknowledgment
- **This is an ethics flag, not just craft**

**"Living Person Harm"** *(Hard Gate)*
- Identifiable person portrayed in damaging way without consent
- No protection for minors or vulnerable subjects
- Potential legal exposure
- **Recommend: Legal review, not just editorial**

**"No Transformation"** *(Hard Gate)* *(→ Gornick: GS0, EP2)*
- Narrator at end has same understanding as beginning
- Events reported but not interpreted
- Reader asked to care without reason given

**"False Precision"** *(Hard Gate)*
- Dialogue, detail, and scene presented with novelistic certainty
- No acknowledgment of memory's limits
- Creates false authority that genre doesn't support

### Should-Fix Flags

**"Therapy Memoir"** *(→ Gornick: NI1, FM-G3)*
- Written for author's healing, not reader's experience
- Assumes reader investment in author's life
- Lacks distance from material

**"Everyone Else Was Wrong"** *(→ Gornick: SC3)*
- Author/narrator always justified
- Other people serve as villains or props
- No genuine self-examination

**"Timeline Confusion"** *(→ Gornick: TD2)*
- Reader can't track when events occurred
- Jumps without signposting
- Multiple timeframes compete without clarity

**"Insight Dump"** *(→ Gornick: NI2, BR1)*
- Understanding arrives in blocks of reflection
- Not dramatized through scene
- Tells reader what to conclude
