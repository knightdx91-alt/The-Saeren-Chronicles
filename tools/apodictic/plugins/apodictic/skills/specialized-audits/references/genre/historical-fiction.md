# Specialized Audit: Historical Fiction
## Version 0.4.4
*Last Updated: February 2026*

---

## Purpose

This audit evaluates the craft challenges specific to historical fiction—balancing period authenticity with accessibility, handling research without showing off, navigating historical attitudes without endorsing them, and managing the relationship between known history and fictional invention. Applies to all fiction set in a period significantly before the author's lifetime.

**When to activate:**
- Work set more than ~50 years before composition
- Work featuring real historical figures
- Work where period setting is essential (not incidental)
- Work engaging with historical events, movements, or social structures
- Work where "getting it right" matters to target readers
- Work deliberately playing with anachronism (pairs with Comedy/Satire audit)

**This audit does not judge historical accuracy per se. It evaluates craft—whether the work achieves its intended relationship with the historical record and period texture.**

---

## Core Questions

The audit addresses these fundamental questions:

1. **Does the period feel inhabited or researched?**
2. **Is the language register consistent and appropriate to intent?**
3. **Where does the work take liberties, and are those liberties earning their keep?**
4. **Are historical attitudes depicted without being endorsed?**
5. **Is research serving the story or competing with it?**

## Historical Stance Identification

First, identify the work's relationship to history:

### Accuracy Spectrum

**Rigorous Historical**
- Prioritizes accuracy above all
- Readers expect fidelity to record
- Departures require justification
- *Examples: Hilary Mantel, Patrick O'Brian*

**Emotionally Accurate**
- Prioritizes period "feel" over pedantic detail
- Accurate in spirit, flexible in specifics
- Research invisible; immersion primary
- *Examples: Sarah Waters, Anthony Doerr*

**Deliberately Anachronistic**
- Uses modern sensibility intentionally
- Anachronism serves thematic or comedic purpose
- Must be consistent to work
- *Examples: Sofia Coppola's Marie Antoinette, A Knight's Tale, The Favourite*

**Historical Fantasy**
- Uses history as launching point
- Magic, alt-history, or significant departures
- Different accuracy contract
- *Examples: Jonathan Strange & Mr Norrell, Outlander*

**Secondary World Historical**
- Fantasy world with historical flavor (not our history)
- Uses period aesthetics without period facts
- Different accuracy contract: internal consistency matters, historical accuracy doesn't
- *Examples: The Goblin Emperor, Curse of Chalion, many epic fantasies*

### Calibration During Intake

Establish:
- Where does this work sit on the spectrum?
- What does the target audience expect?
- What departures are intentional vs. accidental?

## Period-Specific Challenges

Different eras present different craft challenges:

### Ancient World (pre-500 CE)
- **Source scarcity:** Less documentation means more invention is necessary
- **Alien mindset:** Worldviews genuinely different (polytheism, slavery as normal, etc.)
- **Language distance:** No "period voice" readers will recognize
- **Material culture gaps:** Many everyday details simply unknown
- *Risk: Projecting modern or medieval assumptions backward*

### Medieval (500-1500 CE)
- **Modern misconceptions:** Readers think they know medieval life (often wrong)
- **Religious saturation:** Christianity/Islam permeated everything; hard to render without alienating
- **Class rigidity:** Social mobility limited; modern sensibilities clash
- **Literacy/communication:** Most people couldn't read; information traveled slowly
- *Risk: Renaissance Faire medieval—sanitized and modern*

### Early Modern (1500-1800)
- **Better documentation:** More sources, higher accuracy expectations
- **Recognizable but foreign:** Familiar institutions, unfamiliar assumptions
- **Scientific revolution:** Knowledge changing rapidly; what did people believe when?
- **Colonial contexts:** Global history increasingly unavoidable
- *Risk: Costume drama—pretty clothes, modern minds*

### 19th Century
- **Abundant sources:** Letters, newspapers, photographs—accuracy verifiable
- **Familiar genres:** Regency, Victorian, Western all have established conventions
- **Industrial disruption:** Technology changing everything; what exists where/when?
- **Class and empire:** Rigid hierarchies, colonial contexts ever-present
- *Risk: Nostalgia (romanticizing) or condescension (judging)*

### Early 20th Century (1900-1945)
- **Living memory edge:** Some readers remember or have parents who do
- **World wars:** Define the period; hard to avoid, heavy to include
- **Media explosion:** Film, radio, recorded music—sensory environment changes
- **Social change acceleration:** What was radical when? What was normal?
- *Risk: WWII fatigue; Holocaust representation challenges*

### Mid-20th Century (1945-1980)
- **Within living memory:** Readers who lived it may be reading
- **Cultural touchstones:** Music, TV, events readers think they know
- **Recent enough to seem easy:** But attitudes genuinely different
- **Political minefields:** Civil rights, Vietnam, feminism—contested history
- *Risk: Nostalgia or judgment; getting cultural references wrong*

## Period Texture Analysis

### The "Inhabited vs. Researched" Test

**Inhabited period** feels like characters live there:
- Details emerge naturally from character perception
- Setting is texture, not display
- Reader absorbs rather than learns
- Unfamiliar elements explained through action/context

**Researched period** feels like author visited:
- Details feel curated or highlighted
- "Look what I found" energy
- Reader taught rather than immersed
- Explanations interrupt flow

### Detail Integration Assessment

For a sample of period-specific details, assess:

**Organic integration:**
- Detail serves scene purpose beyond accuracy
- Character would notice/mention this
- Flows without stopping narrative

**Neutral:**
- Accurate, present, neither helps nor hurts
- Could be cut without loss

**Research flag:**
- Detail present primarily to prove research
- Stops narrative to explain
- Character wouldn't actually notice this
- "As you know, Bob" delivery

### Material Culture Tracking

How does the work handle:
- **Objects:** Tools, furniture, clothing, food
- **Technology:** Transportation, communication, medicine
- **Money:** Currency, prices, economic realities
- **Space:** Architecture, urban/rural, domestic arrangements
- **Time:** Calendars, seasons, daily rhythms, lifespans

**Red flags:**
- Modern convenience assumptions
- Distances/travel times that don't work
- Casual references to unavailable technology
- Prices that don't reflect period economics

## Language Register Analysis

### Diction Spectrum

Identify the work's language strategy:

**Full Period Voice**
- Attempts period-appropriate syntax and vocabulary
- Archaic constructions, obsolete words
- Risk: Alienation, "forsooth" syndrome
- *Works for: Literary historical, immersive epic*

**Modified Period**
- Period flavor without full commitment
- Avoids obvious anachronisms
- Modern syntax, selective period vocabulary
- *Works for: Most commercial historical*

**Transparent Modern**
- Modern language throughout
- Period conveyed through content, not diction
- Deliberately accessible
- *Works for: YA historical, high-concept, deliberately anachronistic*

**Strategic Code-Switching**
- Different registers for different purposes
- Formal/informal, public/private distinctions
- Period elements at key moments
- *Works for: Literary works with thematic purpose*

### Register Consistency Check

Once strategy is identified:
- Is register maintained throughout?
- Do slips occur under pressure (action scenes, emotional peaks)?
- Are different characters' registers appropriately distinct?
- Does dialogue register match narration strategy?

### Anachronism Detection

**Hard anachronisms** (factual impossibilities):
- Words that didn't exist yet
- Concepts not yet articulated
- References to future events/inventions
- Idioms from later periods

**Soft anachronisms** (plausibility stretches):
- Attitudes more modern than typical
- Knowledge characters shouldn't have
- Conveniences that simplify plot
- Social mobility beyond period norms

**Intentional anachronisms** (if declared):
- Are they consistent?
- Do they serve purpose?
- Are rules clear to reader?

## Historical Dialogue Craft

Dialogue in historical fiction carries extra weight—it's where period voice is most audible and where anachronism is most jarring.

### The Dialogue Dilemma

Historical people didn't speak like us, but:
- Actual period speech would be incomprehensible or alienating
- "Forsooth" and "prithee" are parody, not accuracy
- Modern idiom breaks immersion
- The goal is *flavor*, not phonetic reproduction

### Dialogue Strategies

**Avoidance Register**
- Know what to avoid more than what to include
- No modern slang, no contemporary idioms
- No references to future concepts
- Creates period feel through absence of anachronism

**Selective Archaism**
- Occasional period words/constructions for flavor
- "I shall" vs. "I will" distinctions
- Period-appropriate oaths and exclamations
- Formal address patterns (thee/thou, titles)

**Syntax Variation**
- Word order slightly different from modern
- More complex sentence structure
- Fewer contractions (varies by period/class)
- Rhythm that reads as "older"

**Class and Education Markers**
- Period class distinctions in speech
- Latin phrases for educated characters (pre-20th century)
- Regional/dialect markers where appropriate
- Servants, tradespeople, aristocrats spoke differently

### Dialogue Red Flags

**Obvious anachronisms:**
- "Okay" (20th century American)
- "No problem" (late 20th century)
- Therapy-speak ("boundaries," "toxic," "closure")
- Business jargon ("leverage," "optimize," "impactful")
- Current slang (obviously)

**Subtle anachronisms:**
- "I feel like..." (contemporary hedge)
- Casual blasphemy where it would have been serious
- Gender-neutral language before its time
- Democratic assumptions in hierarchical societies

**The "thee/thou" trap:**
- If using, know the rules (thee = intimate/inferior, you = formal/superior)
- Inconsistent use worse than no use
- Most readers don't know the rules, so violations may not register
- Consider whether it's worth the effort

### Dialogue Assessment Questions

1. Read dialogue aloud: Does it sound "of the period"?
2. Are there words/phrases that would pull a knowledgeable reader out?
3. Do different characters sound appropriately different (class, education, region)?
4. Is the dialogue style consistent throughout?
5. Where narration meets dialogue, do registers match?

## Historical Attitudes Navigation

### The Depiction/Endorsement Distinction

Historical fiction must often depict attitudes the author doesn't share (racism, sexism, religious intolerance, class prejudice, etc.). The craft question is how.

**Approaches:**

**Immersive Depiction**
- Characters hold period attitudes unremarked
- Reader must do moral work
- No authorial thumb on scale
- *Risk: Readers mistake depiction for endorsement*

**Distanced Depiction**
- Period attitudes shown but framed
- POV character or narrator provides perspective
- Reader guided but not lectured
- *Risk: Can feel anachronistic or preachy*

**Protagonist Exception**
- Main characters hold modern attitudes
- Surrounding world holds period attitudes
- Tension between protagonist and society
- *Risk: Historical tourism; protagonist as tourist*

**Thematic Engagement**
- Period attitudes are the subject
- Work interrogates historical mindset
- Neither pure immersion nor simple critique
- *This is hardest and often most rewarding*

### Assessment Questions

For each significant historical attitude depicted:
- How is it framed?
- What work is the reader asked to do?
- Does the text assume modern reader will recognize the problem?
- Is critique explicit, implicit, or absent?
- Does approach match the work's overall stance?

### Flag Patterns

**Presentism concerns:**
- Protagonist knows things were wrong when contemporaries didn't
- Characters espouse views that would have been radical or nonexistent
- Villains hold period-typical views (making villainy = historical normalcy)
- Moral clarity that didn't exist at the time

**Endorsement concerns:**
- Period attitudes presented without any complicating frame
- Harmful views treated as charming or neutral
- No character or structural element questions the attitude
- Reader positioned to enjoy rather than notice

**Neither is automatically wrong—but both should be intentional.**

## Real Historical Figures

### The Fact/Fiction Interface

When real people appear, additional questions arise:

**Accuracy obligations:**
- What's known vs. invented?
- Does invention contradict established record?
- Would descendants/estates have grounds for complaint?
- Are controversial claims clearly marked as speculation?

**Characterization questions:**
- Is figure reduced to single trait or fully human?
- Does fiction serve the person or use them?
- Are they protagonist, supporting, or cameo?
- Is their inclusion necessary or decorative?

### Integration Assessment

**Seamless integration:**
- Real and fictional characters feel equally real
- Historical figures have interiority, not just reputation
- Invented scenes feel plausible given known character

**Awkward integration:**
- Real figures feel like wax museum appearances
- Famous moments feel obligatory
- Fictional characters seem to exist to witness history

**Historical tourism:**
- Real figures appear primarily for recognition
- "And then I met [famous person]" syndrome
- History as backdrop for fictional protagonist's journey

## Real Historical Events

Distinct from real figures—how to handle documented events (battles, disasters, trials, revolutions, etc.)

### Event Categories

**Major documented events:**
- Battles with known outcomes, casualties, movements
- Trials with transcripts
- Disasters with survivor accounts
- Political events with witnesses

**Background events:**
- Wars, plagues, famines that shape context
- Political changes characters would know about
- Cultural movements, technological changes

**Invented events:**
- Fictional happenings in real settings
- "There was a ball that night" (plausible, not documented)
- Small-scale events between documented ones

### Event Integration Assessment

**For major documented events:**
- Does the depiction match what's known?
- Are liberties taken for dramatic purposes?
- Would a historian object, or understand the license?
- Is the emotional truth of the event honored even if details shift?

**The Forrest Gump Problem:**
- Fictional character conveniently present at every major event
- History becomes backdrop for protagonist's journey
- Events lose their own significance

**The Witness Problem:**
- Where is the character during the event?
- How much can they plausibly see/know?
- How does information about events reach them?
- What's the delay between event and knowledge?

### Sensitive Events

Some historical events require extra care:

**Atrocities and genocide:**
- Whose perspective? (Perpetrator, victim, bystander, survivor)
- Risk of exploitation or aestheticization
- Obligations to historical victims
- Balance between honoring truth and reader endurance

**Contested history:**
- Events where interpretation is politically charged
- "Both sides" framing may be inappropriate
- Whose account is the novel using?

**Living memory events:**
- Survivors or descendants may read the book
- Stakes are different than distant history
- Getting it "wrong" has human cost

## Costume Drama vs. Literary Historical

A useful distinction for calibration:

### Costume Drama Mode

- **Focus:** Plot, romance, entertainment
- **History as:** Backdrop, decoration, obstacle
- **Accuracy:** Surface-level (costumes, settings look right)
- **Attitudes:** Often modern in period dress
- **Liberties:** Freely taken for entertainment
- **Reader expectation:** Fun, escape, romance
- *Examples: Bridgerton, many Regency romances, adventure historicals*

**Not a criticism:** Costume drama is a legitimate mode with its own pleasures.

### Literary Historical Mode

- **Focus:** Character, theme, interrogating the past
- **History as:** Subject, not just setting
- **Accuracy:** Deep (worldview, assumptions, blind spots)
- **Attitudes:** Period attitudes taken seriously
- **Liberties:** Taken carefully, with purpose
- **Reader expectation:** Insight, immersion, emotional truth
- *Examples: Hilary Mantel, Colson Whitehead, Sarah Waters*

### Hybrid Works

Most historical fiction falls somewhere between. During calibration:
- Where does this work intend to sit?
- What does the target audience expect?
- Are there sections that shift mode?
- Is the mode consistent or confused?

## Research Integration Analysis

### The "Iceberg" Principle

Good historical fiction shows 10% of research; 90% creates invisible foundation.

**Signs of proper integration:**
- Period feels solid without explanation
- Details specific but not exhaustive
- Author clearly knows more than they're telling
- Research serves story; story doesn't serve research

**Signs of over-integration:**
- Passages that read like summarized sources
- Details that stop the narrative
- Characters conveniently explain things to each other
- Subplots that exist to include research

### "As You Know, Bob" Detection

Flag dialogue or exposition that exists primarily to convey research:
- Characters explaining common knowledge to each other
- Historical context that no character would articulate
- Convenient questions that prompt infodumps
- Narrative tangents to interesting-but-irrelevant material

### Research-to-Story Ratio

For passages heavy with period detail:
- Is this advancing plot?
- Is this developing character?
- Is this establishing necessary context?
- Or is this because the author found it interesting?

**Author interest is not sufficient justification. Reader interest is required.**

## Known History vs. Fictional Liberty

### Liberty Tracking

Identify significant departures from historical record:
- Timeline compressions or expansions
- Invented characters in real events
- Real characters in invented events
- Altered outcomes or relationships
- Composite characters

### Liberty Justification Assessment

For each significant liberty:
- Does it serve story necessity?
- Does it respect the "spirit" of history?
- Would it mislead readers who trust the novel's history?
- Is it acknowledged (author's note, etc.)?

### The "Reasonable Reader" Test

Would a reader who learned history from this novel:
- Have basically accurate understanding?
- Believe specific falsehoods?
- Understand what's documented vs. invented?

**Historical fiction educates whether it intends to or not. What is it teaching?**

## False Positive Warnings

**What looks like a problem but isn't:**

### Intentional anachronism
Deliberately anachronistic works (flagged during intake) play by different rules. Consistency and purpose matter more than accuracy.

### Protagonist as exception
Characters can hold views ahead of their time—people always have. The question is whether this is plausible for this specific character given their circumstances.

### Strategic omission
Not every historical reality needs inclusion. A novel about 18th-century France needn't constantly reference slavery in the colonies unless that's its subject.

### Modern vocabulary for period concepts
Sometimes modern words better convey period meanings than archaic equivalents that would confuse readers.

### Emotional truth over factual precision
Fiction's job is emotional truth. Minor factual liberties that serve larger truth may be appropriate.

## The Fundamental Question

The audit ultimately asks: **Does this work achieve its intended relationship with history—whatever that relationship is?**

Rigorous historical fiction should feel meticulously accurate. Deliberately anachronistic fiction should feel consistently and purposefully modern. Emotionally accurate fiction should feel inhabited and true even where it takes liberties.

The goal is not historical accuracy per se, but **achieved intention**—a work that does what it sets out to do in its engagement with the past.

Historical fiction, at its best, makes the past feel like a foreign country that is nonetheless recognizably human. The research disappears into texture; the attitudes are navigated with craft; the liberties serve the story. The reader emerges feeling they've been somewhere real, even where the novelist invented.
