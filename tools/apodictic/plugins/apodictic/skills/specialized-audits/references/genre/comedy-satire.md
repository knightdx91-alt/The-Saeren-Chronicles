# Specialized Audit: Comedy and Satire
## Version 0.4.4
*Last Updated: February 2026*

---

## Purpose

This audit evaluates comedic craft in fiction—whether humor is landing, whether it's serving the story, and whether the tonal balance between comedy and emotional stakes is calibrated correctly. Applies to pure comedy, satirical work, and serious fiction that employs significant comedic elements.

**When to activate:**
- Work where comedic voice is a defining feature
- Satire or parody (of genre, institution, or social phenomenon)
- Serious fiction with significant comedic elements (witty narrator, banter-heavy dialogue)
- Work with anachronistic or irreverent sensibility in historical or literary settings
- Any work where "funny" is part of the reader promise
- Work where tonal whiplash between comedy and drama is a concern

**This audit does not judge whether humor is "good enough." It evaluates craft—whether comedic elements are functioning as intended and serving the story.**

---

## Core Questions

The audit addresses these fundamental questions:

1. **Is the humor landing, or is it trying too hard?**
2. **Does comedy serve the story, or compete with it?**
3. **When the work needs to be serious, can it land emotional moments?**
4. **Is the comedic voice consistent, or does it waver?**
5. **If satirizing something, is the satire earning its targets?**

## Comedic Mode Identification

First, identify the comedic mode(s) at work:

### Primary Modes

**Wit-Driven Voice**
- Clever, articulate narrator or protagonist
- Humor emerges from observation, phrasing, unexpected connections
- *Examples: A Gentleman in Moscow, Bridget Jones's Diary, Hitchhiker's Guide*

**Banter and Dialogue Comedy**
- Humor lives in exchanges between characters
- Timing, rhythm, volleying
- *Examples: Nora Ephron films, screwball romance, Aaron Sorkin*

**Situational/Farce**
- Humor from circumstances, escalating absurdity
- Physical comedy translated to prose
- *Examples: P.G. Wodehouse, A Confederacy of Dunces, Catch-22*

**Satire/Parody**
- Humor through critique, exaggeration, ironic distance
- Targets institutions, genres, social phenomena
- *Examples: Don Quixote, Candide, Gulliver's Travels, Space Opera (Valente)*

**Dark Comedy**
- Humor from inappropriate or taboo subjects
- Dissonance between tone and content
- *Examples: A Series of Unfortunate Events, Slaughterhouse-Five, My Year of Rest and Relaxation*

**Absurdist**
- Logic systems that don't match reality
- Humor from internal consistency of nonsensical premises
- *Examples: Alice in Wonderland, Kafka (sometimes), Flann O'Brien*

**Comedy of Manners**
- Humor from social rules, class dynamics, propriety violations
- Characters trapped in systems of etiquette and expectation
- Wit deployed as social weapon or defense
- *Examples: Oscar Wilde, Jane Austen, Edith Wharton, Kevin Kwan*

**Unreliable Narrator Comedy**
- Humor from gap between narrator's perception and reality
- Reader sees what narrator doesn't
- Dramatic irony as comedic engine
- *Examples: Diary of a Nobody, Don Quixote, Where'd You Go Bernadette*

### Hybrid Identification

Most comedic fiction combines modes. Identify:
- **Primary mode:** Where most jokes live
- **Secondary mode(s):** Supporting humor styles
- **Serious-to-comedy ratio:** How much of the work is meant to be funny?

## Joke Economy Analysis

### Density Assessment

Track joke/wit density across the manuscript:

**Calibration questions:**
- Is density consistent, or are there barren stretches?
- Are high-density sections overwhelming?
- Does density appropriately decrease during emotional peaks?

**Red flags:**
- Joke stacking (multiple jokes competing for the same laugh)
- Comedy deserts (long stretches where voice goes flat)
- Uniform density (same rate regardless of scene stakes)

### Landing Rate Assessment

For a sample of jokes/witty moments, assess:

**Clear lands:**
- Setup and payoff connect
- Timing works
- Surprise or recognition present

**Unclear or soft:**
- Joke is present but doesn't quite land
- Timing off
- Too obvious or too obscure

**Misfires:**
- Joke falls flat
- Trying too hard
- Undermines rather than serves

**Landing rate benchmark:** Comedic fiction typically needs 70%+ clear lands to feel "funny." Below 50% and the reader stops trusting the voice.

### Concrete Landing Diagnostics

To assess whether a specific joke lands, apply these tests:

**The Surprise Test**
- Cover the punchline. Can you predict it exactly?
- If yes → too obvious (unless callback or running gag)
- Mild surprise = good; total bafflement = too obscure

**The Economy Test**
- Could the joke be delivered in fewer words?
- If significantly yes → probably overwritten
- Could it use more setup?
- If reader might miss the joke → probably underwritten

**The Necessity Test**
- Remove the joke entirely. What's lost?
- If nothing changes → joke is filler
- If scene loses energy/character/meaning → joke is load-bearing

**The Cringe Test**
- Does the joke produce discomfort?
- If intentional (cringe comedy, dark comedy) → working as designed
- If unintentional (author seems unaware) → misfire

**The Read-Aloud Test**
- Read the passage aloud. Where do you naturally pause?
- Does the prose support comedic delivery?
- Where would an audience laugh? Is there space for it?

**The "Who's Laughing?" Test**
- Is the author laughing at their own cleverness?
- Is the character laughing at their own joke?
- Is only the reader laughing? (Usually best)
- Is no one laughing? (Problem)

### Repetition Assessment

Track repeated joke types, targets, or rhythms:

**Healthy repetition:** Running gags, callbacks, signature phrases (builds)
**Unhealthy repetition:** Same joke beat over and over (exhausts)

## Prose-Level Comedic Rhythm

Comedic timing in prose operates at the sentence and paragraph level. This is the hardest element to systematize, but certain patterns are identifiable.

### Sentence-Level Timing

**The Setup-Punch Structure**
- Setup: Establishes expectation (longer, often complex)
- Punch: Subverts expectation (shorter, often simple)
- The punch typically lands at the end of the sentence

**Flag when:**
- Punch buried in middle of sentence (timing lost)
- Setup too long (reader forgets what they're waiting for)
- Punch over-explained after delivery (stepping on the laugh)

**The Rule of Three**
- Two items establish pattern; third breaks it
- Classic: "X, Y, and Z" where Z is the surprise
- Works at word, phrase, and sentence level

**Flag when:**
- Fourth item added (dilutes)
- Third item isn't surprising enough
- Pattern too obvious from first two items

**The Breath Before**
- Punctuation creates pause before punchline
- Em-dash, colon, or period before the landing
- Gives reader's brain time to anticipate

**Flag when:**
- No breath (joke rushes past)
- Too much breath (momentum dies)

### Paragraph-Level Timing

**The Trailing Punchline**
- Funny line at end of paragraph, then white space
- White space = beat for laugh to land
- Next paragraph starts fresh

**Flag when:**
- Punchline followed immediately by more content in same paragraph
- Next paragraph starts before laugh can land

**The Buried Joke**
- Joke delivered casually within serious content
- Reader almost misses it, then catches it
- Requires deadpan tone and precise placement

**Flag when:**
- Joke too buried (actually missed)
- Surrounding content too frivolous (joke doesn't contrast)

**The Escalation Build**
- Series of jokes building in absurdity
- Each tops the last
- Final joke biggest (or deliberately anticlimactic)

**Flag when:**
- Escalation peaks too early
- Final joke doesn't land because escalation exhausted reader

### Diagnostic Questions for Timing

When assessing whether a joke lands, ask:

1. **Where does the surprising word/phrase fall?** (End of sentence = strongest)
2. **Is there a breath before the punch?** (Punctuation, paragraph break)
3. **Is there space after the punch?** (Room for laugh)
4. **Does the next sentence step on it?** (Continuing immediately can kill timing)
5. **Is the punch the right length?** (Usually shorter than setup)
6. **Read it aloud: where would you pause?** (Prose should support natural delivery)

## Tonal Integration Analysis

### The "Earn the Turn" Test

For each significant shift between comedy and seriousness:

1. **Does the shift feel earned?**
   - Has the work established it can handle both registers?
   - Is there a bridge or is it jarring?

2. **Does seriousness land harder because of surrounding comedy?**
   - Contrast enhances impact
   - Or does comedy undercut the serious moment?

3. **Can the work return to comedy after serious moments?**
   - Graceful return
   - Or tonal whiplash?

### Emotional Stakes Protection

**Flag when comedy undermines:**
- Key emotional revelations
- Character vulnerability
- Relationship turning points
- Climactic moments
- Death, loss, or grief (unless dark comedy is the mode)

**Ask:** Is this joke necessary? What does it cost?

### The Defusing Problem

When is humor used to avoid genuine engagement?

**Flag:**
- Characters making jokes to avoid emotional honesty
- Narrator undercutting moments before they land
- Ironic distance that prevents reader investment
- "Too cool to care" posture

**Note:** Sometimes defusing IS characterization—a character who can't be vulnerable. But the narrative should be aware of this pattern, not enacting it unconsciously.

## Comedic Voice Consistency

### Voice Tracking

For first-person or close-third with strong voice:

**Signature elements:**
- Characteristic phrases or constructions
- Rhythm patterns
- Observation style
- Targets of wit

**Consistency check:**
- Do signature elements appear throughout?
- Are there sections where voice goes generic?
- Does voice hold under stress (when plot gets intense)?

### Register Control

Does the voice maintain appropriate register?

**Flag:**
- Modern slang in historical without anachronistic intention
- Elevated diction that drops inconsistently
- Humor style that changes (witty → slapstick → witty)

### Anachronism Assessment

For works with deliberate anachronistic wit (historical settings, modern sensibility):

**Calibration questions:**
- Is anachronism consistent throughout?
- Are there rules for what's anachronistic and what's period-appropriate?
- Does anachronism serve thematic purpose or just convenience?

**Flag:**
- Inconsistent anachronism (some modern references, others avoided)
- Anachronism that breaks historical plausibility beyond intention
- Modern sensibility that flattens period-specific conflict

## Satire-Specific Analysis

*Skip if work is not satirical.*

### Target Identification

What is being satirized?
- Institution
- Social phenomenon
- Genre conventions
- Ideology or belief system
- Specific work(s) (parody)

### Earning the Target

**Does the satire demonstrate understanding of its target?**
- Accurate identification of target's actual characteristics
- Recognition of what makes target absurd or problematic
- Avoiding strawman versions

**The Informed Satirist Test:** Would someone who loves/works in/believes in the target recognize the critique as fair (even if they disagree)?

### Satirical Stance Assessment

**What is the satirical posture?**
- Gentle mockery (affectionate)
- Sharp critique (corrective)
- Total demolition (hostile)
- Absurdist exaggeration (defamiliarization)

**Is posture consistent?** Mixed signals confuse the reader about intent.

### The Horatian/Juvenalian Spectrum

Classical distinction that remains useful:

**Horatian Satire**
- Gentle, tolerant, amused
- "Look how foolish we all are"
- Targets human nature more than specific villains
- Reader invited to laugh at themselves
- Aims to correct through gentle mockery
- *Examples: Jane Austen, Oscar Wilde, Bridget Jones*

**Juvenalian Satire**
- Bitter, angry, contemptuous
- "Look how corrupt they are"
- Targets specific abuses, institutions, people
- Reader invited to share outrage
- Aims to expose and condemn
- *Examples: Swift, Orwell, Catch-22*

**Most satire blends both**, but knowing the dominant mode helps calibrate:
- Horatian going too harsh loses its warmth
- Juvenalian going too gentle loses its bite
- Mismatch between target and mode creates confusion (gentle satire of atrocity feels wrong; bitter satire of minor foibles feels disproportionate)

### Satirical Overreach

**Flag when satire:**
- Attacks strawman versions of target
- Becomes cruel rather than incisive
- Loses sight of human beings within institutions
- Preaches rather than demonstrates
- Is self-congratulatory ("aren't we clever for seeing this")

## Detection Targets

### The "Trying Too Hard" Problem

**Flag when:**
- Jokes are over-explained
- Multiple jokes serve same beat
- Voice reaches for wit that isn't there
- Labored setups for minimal payoffs
- Humor feels desperate

### The "Too Clever" Problem

**Flag when:**
- Jokes require too much work from reader
- Wit is inaccessible without specialized knowledge
- Author seems to be impressing self
- Cleverness substitutes for emotional truth

### The "Undercutting" Problem

**Flag when:**
- Comedy prevents genuine stakes
- Irony creates distance when closeness is needed
- Nothing is allowed to matter because everything is material
- Sarcasm replaces sincerity

### The "One Note" Problem

**Flag when:**
- Same type of joke throughout
- Same targets, same rhythms
- No variety in comedic approach
- Reader can predict every beat

### The "Comedy Fatigue" Problem

**Flag when:**
- Relentless humor exhausts reader
- No breathing room
- Serious moments feel like relief rather than landing
- Diminishing returns on repeated joke types

## False Positive Warnings

**What looks like a problem but isn't:**

### Acquired taste comedy
Some humor is intentionally niche, dry, or difficult. Not every reader will find it funny, and that's okay if the target audience is well-defined.

### Intentional discomfort
Dark comedy, cringe comedy, and satire often create discomfort deliberately. Discomfort ≠ failure.

### Slow-build comedy
Some joke structures require patience—long setups, delayed payoffs, running gags that compound. Early "misfires" may pay off later.

### Character-specific humor
A character's jokes failing can be characterization. The question is whether the narrative knows the jokes are failing.

### Tonal daring
Ambitious works mix registers in ways that initially feel jarring. Give the work credit for attempting something difficult before flagging as problem.

## Calibration Questions

During intake, establish:

1. **What is the comedic promise?** (How funny should this be?)
2. **What modes are at work?** (Wit, banter, satire, dark comedy, etc.)
3. **What is the serious-to-comedy ratio?**
4. **Are there sacred cows?** (Things that should never be joked about in this work)
5. **What's the target audience's humor tolerance?**
6. **If satirical, what's being satirized and from what stance?**
7. **Does the work intend anachronistic/modern sensibility?**

## Audit Output

### Comedic Mode Map
- Primary and secondary modes identified
- Density tracking (where is humor concentrated?)
- Serious-to-comedy ratio assessment

### Landing Rate Assessment
- Sample analysis of joke effectiveness
- Pattern identification (what's working, what isn't)
- Running gag / callback tracking

### Tonal Integration Assessment
- Major register shifts identified
- "Earn the turn" evaluation
- Emotional stakes protection check
- Defusing pattern analysis

### Voice Consistency Assessment
- Signature elements tracked
- Consistency evaluation
- Register control analysis
- Anachronism audit (if applicable)

### Satire Assessment (if applicable)
- Target identification
- "Earning the target" evaluation
- Stance consistency
- Overreach flags

### Specific Flags
For each flag:
- Location
- Passage reference (≤25 words)
- Issue type
- Suggested recalibration

### Pattern Analysis
- What types of jokes are landing best?
- Where does the work lose confidence?
- What's the strongest comedic section?
- Where does tonal integration succeed/fail?

### Recommendations
Abstract structural terms:
- "Joke density needs reduction in [section] to protect emotional landing"
- "Running gag X has exhausted; consider retiring or transforming"
- "Voice goes flat between chapters Y-Z; signature elements needed"
- "Tonal shift at [location] needs bridge; currently jarring"

**NOT:** What jokes to write, what's funny, whether humor is good enough.

## The Fundamental Question

The audit ultimately asks: **Is the comedy serving the story, or is the story serving the comedy?**

Great comedic fiction uses humor to illuminate character, heighten stakes, provide relief, and deepen theme. The jokes aren't separate from the story—they're part of how the story works.

When comedy and story are integrated, the humor makes emotional moments land harder, not softer. When they're not integrated, the jokes compete with the story for the reader's attention.

**The goal is earned laughter that enhances rather than undercuts.**
