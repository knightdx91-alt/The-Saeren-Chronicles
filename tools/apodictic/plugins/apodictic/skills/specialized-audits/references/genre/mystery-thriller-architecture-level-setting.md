# Mystery/Thriller Architecture — Level-Setting Research
## Version 0.4.11
*Last Updated: February 2026*
*Synthesized from three-model research (Opus, Codex, Gemini)*
*Status: Internal research brief for specialized audit design*

---

## Purpose

This brief establishes the theoretical and critical foundations for the Mystery/Thriller Architecture audit. It shifts the developmental editor's diagnostic lens from "puzzle present / clock ticking" (genre calibration) to "information-pressure machine running" (architecture diagnosis). It provides the vocabulary to prove when a manuscript's information economy, misdirection system, clock mechanics, and solution logic are generating the reader's active cognitive and emotional participation — or merely assembling the components without meshing the gears.

Target failure is not "too simple" or "too confusing" (taste judgments). Target failure is **informational drift**: clues, suspects, timelines, reversals, and deadlines are present but don't convert into the reader's hypothesis formation, prediction revision, urgency, or satisfying surprise. The architecture is assembled. The machine isn't running.

The brief asks:
1. What theoretical frameworks explain why mystery/thriller architecture works as a cognitive-emotional system?
2. What failure modes are stable enough to flag across manuscripts?
3. What positive techniques distinguish effective information-pressure architecture from assembled components?
4. How should standards shift across mystery/thriller subtypes?
5. How can the audit distinguish intentional rule-bending from accidental cheating?

## 1) Theoretical Grounding: What Makes Information-Pressure Architecture Work?

### 1.1 Sternberg's narrative interest triad and cognitive frame mechanics

Meir Sternberg's theory of narrative interest identifies three modes of reader engagement: **curiosity** (what happened? — directed at the past), **suspense** (what will happen? — directed at the future), and **surprise** (that's not what I expected — directed at the present moment of recognition). These are not genre categories; they are cognitive operations the text elicits from the reader.

Building on Brewer and Lichtenstein's cognitive extensions of Sternberg, each mode operates through a specific frame mechanism:

- **Curiosity** operates through **frame-matching**: a significant event (typically the crime) occurs early in the chronological sequence but is omitted from discourse. The reader is aware of the gap and reconstructs the missing frame from subsequent data.
- **Suspense** operates through **frame-completion**: an initiating event introduces a high-stakes scenario, and the narrative places extensive discourse between this event and its outcome, forcing anticipation.
- **Surprise** operates through **frame-shifting**: critical early information is omitted without the reader's knowledge, forcing retroactive reinterpretation upon revelation.

Mystery privileges curiosity: the reader reconstructs a past event from partial evidence. Thriller privileges suspense: the reader anticipates a future threat with incomplete information. The best mystery-thriller hybrids manage all three simultaneously — reconstructing the past (curiosity), anticipating the future (suspense), and periodically having understanding reorganized (surprise).

Diagnostic conversion: a manuscript where the reader is merely *watching* investigation or *observing* threat without performing these cognitive operations has failed at the architecture level. The genre furniture is present; the reader's mind isn't doing the work. At any given moment, the text should be eliciting curiosity, suspense, surprise, or some combination. If none, the architecture is idle.

For audit design: this grounds the distinction between information that the reader *possesses* and information that the reader is *working with*. A clue the reader knows but can't use inferentially doesn't generate curiosity. A deadline the reader knows but doesn't feel emotionally is suspension without suspense. A twist the reader didn't expect but can't retrospectively justify is surprise without satisfaction.

### 1.2 Barthes and the hermeneutic code

Roland Barthes' *S/Z* (1970) identifies the hermeneutic code as the narrative mechanism that poses a question, delays its answer, and manages that delay through specific operations: **snares** (deliberate misleading), **equivocations** (responses that seem to answer but don't), **partial answers** (fragments that advance understanding without resolving), **suspended answers** (deflections that maintain the question's pressure), and finally the **disclosure** (the answer itself).

Diagnostic conversion: this taxonomy of delay is directly diagnostic. A mystery that poses its central question but doesn't manage the delay through these operations — that simply withholds and then dumps — is architecturally impoverished. The reader experiences a gap (question), then stasis (nothing happens to the gap), then filling (answer). The middle section, where the hermeneutic code should be operating at maximum pressure, is empty.

For audit design: Barthes' categories map to specific audit dimensions. Snares → Red Herring Integrity. Equivocations → Investigation Legibility (apparent answers that don't resolve). Partial answers → Reveal Choreography (sequencing of progressive understanding). Suspended answers → Information Economy (what's withheld and why). The hermeneutic code provides the theoretical basis for evaluating whether the space between question and answer is architecturally occupied or merely chronologically extended.

### 1.3 Eco and the model reader

Umberto Eco's concept of the "model reader" (*The Role of the Reader*, 1979) describes the ideal reader constructed by the text — not any particular actual reader, but the cognitive profile the text assumes and depends upon. In mystery, the model reader is someone who *tries to solve the puzzle*. The text constructs this reader by providing inferential material — evidence that rewards attention, that supports hypothesis formation, that permits (even demands) the reader's active cognitive participation.

Diagnostic conversion: a mystery whose model reader is passive — who is expected to wait for the answer rather than pursue it — has failed at the architecture level even if the puzzle is technically fair. The clues may be present, but if the text doesn't construct a reader who is working with them, the information economy is formally sound but experientially inert.

For audit design: this grounds the distinction between **accessibility** (can the reader reach the information?) and **tractability** (can the reader *do something* with it?). A clue that is accessible but not tractable — present on the page but not inferentially useful at the point of encounter — satisfies the letter of fair-play without generating reader participation.

### 1.4 Todorov's typology and temporal direction

Tzvetan Todorov ("The Typology of Detective Fiction," 1966) identifies three forms: the **whodunit** (two stories — the story of the crime and the story of the investigation; the first is absent, the second reconstructs it), the **thriller** (no duality — the story unfolds in present danger), and the **suspense novel** (both — reconstruction of the past AND present danger).

Most manuscripts this audit targets are Todorov's third category — they must manage both temporal directions simultaneously. This maps to a key structural insight about directional movement: **mystery architecture expands** — pacing slows to permit rumination, reconsideration of clues, and reconstruction of a past event. **Thriller architecture narrows** — it funnels characters into increasingly restricted options, accelerating pace and eliminating agency as present threat closes in. Integrated architecture must harmonize the mystery's demand for cognitive breathing room with the thriller's demand for temporal compression.

Diagnostic conversion: manuscripts often manage one temporal direction well and the other poorly. The reconstruction is strong but the present danger is decorative, or the present danger is intense but the puzzle is an afterthought. The audit must evaluate both independently and then assess their integration.

### 1.5 Fair-play traditions and their diagnostic limits

The Knox Decalogue (1929) and Van Dine's "Twenty Rules" (1928) established the fair-play tradition: the reader must have access to the same information as the detective; the solution must be achievable through logic; no supernatural solutions in realistic settings; the criminal must appear early. These rules express a contract principle — **epistemic parity** — but they are poor diagnostic tools in their original form. They focus on what the text must *not do* (withhold, cheat, introduce late) rather than what it must *achieve* (inference, participation, surprise-with-inevitability).

A manuscript can satisfy every Knox rule and still produce an inert puzzle if the clues, while technically present, are not inferentially activated.

Diagnostic conversion: fair-play is necessary but not sufficient. The audit should check fair-play compliance as a hard gate (violation = Must-Fix) but then assess the deeper question: does the architecture *generate inference* or merely *permit it in retrospect*?

For audit design: this grounds the distinction between **retroactive fairness** (the solution checks out on re-read) and **prospective fairness** (the reader has enough to work with going forward). Both matter, but prospective fairness is the architecture-level achievement. Retroactive fairness alone produces "oh, I see it was there" rather than "I should have seen it."

### 1.6 Cognitive psychology of inference and surprise

Reader cognition in mystery/thriller operates through several mechanisms:

**Hypothesis formation and revision.** The reader constructs a mental model of "what happened" or "what will happen" and revises it as new evidence arrives. Effective mystery/thriller architecture provides enough evidence to form hypotheses, enough ambiguity to sustain multiple, and enough surprises to force revision. When this economy fails, readers experience one of two pathologies: **model starvation** (can't build plausible theories — too little evidence) or **model lock** (one obvious answer too early, no meaningful complication). Both reduce engagement, in opposite directions.

**Predictive processing.** As in horror, the reader's brain is a prediction machine. Mystery/thriller exploits this by setting up expectations and then violating them in patterned ways. The violation must be surprising (prediction failed) but retrospectively coherent (prediction *should have* failed, because the evidence pointed elsewhere). Cheap surprise violates predictions randomly. Deep surprise violates predictions in ways that reorganize understanding.

**Misdirection and attention management.** Cognitive research on misdirection (from magic performance studies — Kuhn, Rensink, et al.) shows that attention can be reliably directed away from critical information through: temporal manipulation (placing the critical move during a low-attention moment), spatial manipulation (drawing the eye elsewhere), and social manipulation (directing attention through gaze and gesture). These map directly to clue-hiding techniques in mystery fiction.

**The "click" of recognition.** The moment when disparate evidence reorganizes into a coherent picture is a specific cognitive event. It produces pleasure proportional to the prior work the reader has done. A "click" after sustained inference effort is deeply satisfying. A "click" after passive waiting is tepid. A "click" that doesn't come (because the evidence never coheres) produces frustration.

**Information delivery architecture.** Effective mystery/thriller operates as a "jigsaw puzzle" — clues exist in overlapping but independently meaningful fragments that can be assembled through multiple inferential pathways. The dangerous alternative is "ball of twine" architecture — strictly linear, where one clue leads to the next in sequence. If a single connection fails, the entire chain collapses. The jigsaw model provides inferential resilience; the ball of twine creates fragile architecture where one missed beat stalls everything.

For audit design: these mechanisms ground the dimensional structure. Information Economy maps to hypothesis formation. Red Herring Integrity maps to misdirection quality. Investigation Legibility maps to the reader's ability to follow and check reasoning. Clock Mechanics maps to suspense through time pressure on prediction. Reveal Choreography maps to the sequencing of prediction violations. Solution Fairness maps to the quality of the "click."

### 1.7 Craft practitioner frameworks

**Agatha Christie** (implicit method, extracted from craft): Christie's technique involves presenting every clue in plain sight while exploiting reader assumptions. The best hiding place for a clue is inside a reader assumption. The evidence is visible, but the reader's own cognitive bias renders it invisible. This grounds the audit's treatment of clue hiding — the most sophisticated method is not concealment but assumption exploitation.

**Patricia Highsmith** (*Plotting and Writing Suspense Fiction*): Highsmith emphasizes that suspense comes from the reader caring about what happens to the characters — suspense is an emotional state, not a plot device. A ticking clock in a room of strangers produces less suspense than a quiet conversation between people the reader loves. This grounds the audit's attention to emotional integration: clock mechanics and information economy must be embedded in character stakes, not floating above them.

**Lee Child** (craft interviews): Child's insight about thriller pacing: the key is not "what happens next?" but "what's happening *now*?" The reader must feel that the present moment is dangerous, uncertain, or loaded — not that something interesting will happen eventually. This grounds the distinction between stated urgency and enacted urgency.

**Tana French** (craft practice): French demonstrates that literary mystery can sustain both puzzle architecture and literary ambition. The information economy operates through unreliable memory, social performance, and psychological self-deception — not just physical clues and alibis. This grounds the audit's attention to non-traditional information systems in literary hybrids.

**Gillian Flynn** (craft practice): Flynn demonstrates that domestic thriller can operate through epistemic warfare — characters deliberately managing each other's and the reader's understanding of reality. The "clues" are behavioral inconsistencies; the "red herrings" are performed identities; the "solution" is the revelation of who these people actually are. This grounds recognition that "information" in thriller includes identity, motivation, and relationship truth.

### 1.8 Theoretical synthesis for audit design

Mystery/thriller architecture produces reader engagement when four conditions are simultaneously met:

1. **Inference condition:** The reader can form, test, and revise hypotheses about the central question. Information generates cognitive work, not passive accumulation.
2. **Pressure condition:** Time, threat, or consequence constrains the investigation and converts into scene-level urgency. Clocks are enacted, not stated.
3. **Fairness condition:** The solution is retrospectively justified by available evidence AND prospectively supported by tractable clues. The reader could have gotten there.
4. **Surprise condition:** The resolution reorganizes understanding. The reader's prediction was wrong, and the wrongness is illuminating rather than arbitrary.

Failure of any one condition produces informational drift.

## 2) Failure Modes: Where Information-Pressure Architecture Detaches from Engagement

### 2.1 Core detachment patterns

1. **Information Without Inference** — Clues present but don't support hypothesis-building. Evidence accumulates as data, not material for reasoning.
2. **Urgency Without Constraint** — Clocks stated but don't change what characters do. Time pressure narrated, not enacted.
3. **Misdirection Without Foundation** — Red herrings exist but lack evidentiary basis. They misdirect because the text tells the reader to look at them, not because evidence points there.
4. **Process Without Progress** — Investigation scenes demonstrate methodology but don't advance understanding.
5. **Revelation Without Reorganization** — Twists arrive but don't make the reader rethink earlier events.
6. **Solution Without Inevitability** — The answer is technically correct but doesn't feel earned.

### 2.2 Expanded failure inventory

**Information Economy failures:**
1. **Fog of Facts** — High information density, low inferential utility.
2. **Clue Starvation** — Insufficient fair anchors before the reveal.
3. **Asymmetry Collapse** — Reader and investigator information tracks diverge unfairly. Includes the specific case of deliberate POV censorship: the narrative confirms observation without description.
4. **Evidence Island** — Clues clustered in one section rather than distributed.
5. **Signal Collapse** — True-path clues repeatedly buried beneath non-functional noise. Reader tracking load exceeds reasonable mode burden.

**Red Herring failures:**
6. **Noise Cannon** — Decoy proliferation overwhelms core logic.
7. **Decoy Without Spine** — Red herring unsupported by believable evidence.
8. **Immortal Herring** — False trail persists after evidence has been contradicted.
9. **Orphaned Thread** — Red herring introduced but never formally collapsed or explained. The false trail disappears without disproof.
10. **Herring Hierarchy** — Red herrings of vastly different quality, making the "real" false trail obvious by relative sophistication.

**Investigation Legibility failures:**
11. **Leap Detective** — Major deductions without observable reasoning chain.
12. **Procedure Theatre** — Process scenes that add realism flavor but no puzzle progress.
13. **Oracle Investigator** — Detective arrives at conclusions through stated "intuition" rather than inferrable evidence. The investigator is a black box.
14. **Blind-Side Investigator** — Investigator misses obvious inferences repeatedly beyond credible character limits, mainly to preserve twist timing.
15. **Passive Catalyst** — Protagonist does not actively deduce the solution. Climax relies on unmotivated confession, secondary character delivery, or accidental discovery.

**Clock Mechanics failures:**
16. **Cosmetic Clock** — Deadline stated, behavior unchanged.
17. **Clock Freeze** — Urgency disappears for extended sections without rationale.
18. **Clock Without Cost** — Deadline passes or approaches without consequence. The reader learns the clock isn't real.
19. **Deadline Detour** — Major subplot blocks clock conversion at critical phase, requiring abrupt acceleration later.

**Reveal Choreography failures:**
20. **Reverse Gear Reveal** — Late explanation introduces key facts absent from earlier architecture.
21. **Twist Before Foundation** — Reversal arrives without inferential preparation.
22. **Premature Closure** — Key mystery resolved too early, remaining pages anticlimactic.
23. **Reveal Pile-Up** — Multiple reveals in rapid succession, preventing the reader from processing any one.
24. **Explain Patch Ending** — Final explanation compensates for missing architecture via monologue compression.

**Solution Fairness failures:**
25. **Cheat Ending** — Solution requires unseen evidence or withheld POV-available fact. The cardinal sin.
26. **Invisible Culprit** — Culprit underrepresented until reveal.
27. **Obvious Culprit Drift** — Culprit locked too early without meaningful complication.
28. **Retroactive Collapse** — Reveal fails to reconcile planted clues.

### 2.3 Scene-class concentration points

Informational drift most often appears in: mid-investigation bridge scenes, interview sequences where witnesses provide atmosphere but not evidence, transition scenes between major revelations, parallel-plot sections where urgency drops, resolution sequences where explanation is longer than revelation, and domestic downtime scenes in thrillers where clock pauses invisibly.

### 2.4 Structural isomorphism

**Informational drift is to cognitive engagement what inert dread is to felt fear and ornamental prose is to structural meaning.** In all three cases: the craft machinery is assembled, the genre components are present, the reader's active participation isn't generated. A clue the reader can't infer from is the mystery equivalent of a dread sequence that doesn't produce anxiety. A clock that doesn't constrain behavior is the thriller equivalent of atmosphere that doesn't generate pressure. A solution that requires unseen evidence is the mystery equivalent of a horror ending that resolves through explanation. **Information that the reader possesses but isn't working with is the architecture equivalent of prose the reader admires but doesn't need.**

### 2.5 Hybrid-specific failure: Pressure-Puzzle Split

In hybrid manuscripts, thriller urgency and mystery inference can run in parallel without integrating. The clock operates in action scenes; the puzzle operates in investigation scenes; the two never interact. The reader switches between modes rather than experiencing them as a unified system. This is distinct from any single-dimension failure — it's a system-level failure where each component functions adequately in isolation but produces no combined pressure.

## 3) Positive Cases: What Integrated Information-Pressure Architecture Looks Like

### 3.1 Classic whodunit

**Agatha Christie** (*The Murder of Roger Ackroyd*, *And Then There Were None*): The gold standard for assumption exploitation. Every clue is on the page; the reader's cognitive biases render them invisible. *Ackroyd* demonstrates that fair-play can coexist with devastating surprise. *And Then There Were None* demonstrates that puzzle can operate as both mystery and thriller simultaneously.

**Dorothy L. Sayers** (*Gaudy Night*): Character-driven puzzle where the mystery and emotional arc are the same investigation. The "clue" is behavioral — understanding the criminal requires understanding the community.

Technique pattern: clue hiding through assumption exploitation, distributed evidence, progressive narrowing with false plateaus, solution that recontextualizes character behavior retrospectively.

### 3.2 Police procedural

**Michael Connelly** (Harry Bosch series): Process as revelation engine. Each procedural step advances the puzzle AND deepens character. The investigation *is* the character study.

**Tana French** (procedural-literary crossover): Process scenes always alter suspect probability map. Institutional constraints create tactical and ethical tradeoffs. Deduction distributed across team without losing reader legibility.

Technique pattern: cumulative case logic where each scene changes the reader's model, team dynamics as misdirection, realistic process that never stalls the puzzle.

### 3.3 Hardboiled/noir

**Raymond Chandler** (*The Big Sleep*, *The Long Goodbye*): Atmosphere and moral complexity as information systems. The investigation reveals a moral landscape more than a factual truth. Chandler's admission that he didn't know who killed the chauffeur in *The Big Sleep* suggests noir's puzzle can be deliberately secondary to its ethical investigation.

**Patricia Highsmith** (*The Talented Mr. Ripley*): Inverted psychological noir where the reader knows the culprit. Information economy inverted — the reader has full knowledge and watches the net tighten or fail to tighten.

Technique pattern: moral investigation paralleling factual investigation, atmosphere carrying evidential weight, deliberate puzzle looseness in service of thematic tightness.

### 3.4 Conspiracy/political thriller

**John le Carré** (*Tinker Tailor Soldier Spy*): Information economy as institutional paranoia. Every interaction is potentially a clue or a trap. Suspense can be purely cognitive: minimal physical danger, total epistemic danger.

**Stieg Larsson** (*The Girl with the Dragon Tattoo*): Dual investigation providing two information streams that converge. Clock operates through institutional threat.

Technique pattern: information contamination (every source potentially unreliable), institutional knowledge as puzzle domain, convergent investigations providing cross-validation.

### 3.5 Psychological thriller

**Gillian Flynn** (*Gone Girl*): Epistemic warfare as architecture. The midpoint reversal reorganizes everything the reader thought they knew. The "clues" are personality performance; the "red herrings" are performed identities.

**Patricia Highsmith** (*Strangers on a Train*): Psychological reciprocity as thriller engine. The clock is moral rather than temporal.

Technique pattern: identity as puzzle domain, unreliable narration as architectural feature, behavioral evidence requiring psychological inference, midpoint reorganization.

### 3.6 Domestic thriller

**Tana French** (*In the Woods*): Literary mystery where memory, trauma, and social identity operate as the information system. The investigation uncovers not a criminal but a character's own buried truth.

**Ruth Ware** (*In a Dark, Dark Wood*): Contained setting as information architecture. Physical isolation constrains information flow and forces closed-system puzzles.

Technique pattern: intimacy as information constraint, trust erosion as progressive revelation, domestic routine as clue-hiding mechanism.

### 3.7 Inverted mystery

**Francis Iles** (*Malice Aforethought*): The reader knows the culprit from page one. The pleasure is watching the investigation approach truth.

**Columbo** (television, structurally foundational): Pleasure in watching the detective's method. Each scene tightens the noose. The clock is the culprit's composure.

Technique pattern: transparent culprit with opaque method, progressive constraint on the guilty party, investigation as performance, pleasure in method rather than solution.

### 3.8 Literary hybrid

**Umberto Eco** (*The Name of the Rose*): Full puzzle architecture inside literary-philosophical framework. The mystery is solvable, the solution is thematically resonant, and the investigation is simultaneously an education in semiotics. The investigation serves a dual purpose: solving the literal puzzle while mapping a thematic argument about the nature of truth and signifiers.

**Paul Auster** (*City of Glass*): Anti-mystery where the investigation dissolves rather than resolves. Works because the *expectation* of architecture is established before subversion.

Technique pattern: puzzle architecture carrying thematic weight, investigation as metaphor for interpretation, literary ambiguity that preserves inferential structure, solution (or deliberate non-solution) as philosophical statement.

### 3.9 Cross-subtype success signals

Across exemplars, effective information-pressure architecture shares:
1. **Inference generation, not just information delivery.** Every major clue supports hypothesis formation at the point of encounter.
2. **Progressive model revision.** The reader's understanding changes multiple times, and each change reorganizes rather than merely adds.
3. **Clock-to-behavior conversion.** Time pressure changes what characters do, not just what the narration says.
4. **Misdirection with evidentiary foundation.** Red herrings are false because the evidence genuinely supports them, not because the text asserts them.
5. **Solution that exceeds the puzzle.** The answer reveals something about the world, the characters, or the reader's assumptions.
6. **Emotional integration.** The information architecture is embedded in character stakes. The reader cares about the answer, not just the puzzle.

## 4) Subgenre Calibration Evidence

### 4.1 Classic whodunit
Promise: fair-play puzzle with elegant solution. Tighten on: solution fairness, clue distribution, retroactive coherence. Loosen on: clock mechanics (classic whodunit often operates without explicit time pressure). Named failure: **The Locked Room Lecture** — puzzle mechanics on display but the reader was never playing; the solution is impressive but inferentially inaccessible. False positive risk: penalizing reader difficulty (puzzle *should* be hard — flag only when impossible, not challenging).

### 4.2 Cozy
Promise: puzzle embedded in community charm. Tighten on: solution fairness, investigation legibility (amateur sleuth must be followable). Loosen on: clock mechanics, procedural realism, red herring sophistication. Named failure: **Tea-Shop Stall Loop** — charming community scenes that advance atmosphere but not the puzzle; the mystery is a pretext for the setting. False positive risk: penalizing atmospheric pacing that is genre-appropriate.

### 4.3 Procedural
Promise: process credibility driving cumulative case logic. Tighten on: investigation legibility, information economy (each step must advance). Loosen on: pacing speed (procedure is naturally slower), solution surprise (process > twist). Named failure: **Clipboard Carousel** — process on display without progress; investigation demonstrates methodology without advancing understanding. False positive risk: flagging realistic process pacing as stalling.

### 4.4 Hardboiled/noir
Promise: investigation under moral corrosion. Tighten on: thematic integration, character investigation, atmosphere-as-evidence. Loosen on: strict fair-play (noir may deliberately frustrate clean solutions), clock mechanics. Named failure: **Smoke Without Signal** — atmosphere and voice substituting for actual investigation; style masking structural emptiness. False positive risk: demanding classic fair-play from a mode that deliberately rejects it.

### 4.5 Psychological thriller
Promise: mind-level manipulation and epistemic pressure. Tighten on: uncertainty design, investigation legibility, reveal choreography (midpoint reorganization). Loosen on: physical evidence, procedural realism, strict fair-play. Named failure: **Unreliable Escape Hatch** — unreliable narration without inferential structure; the reader can't form hypotheses because the rules keep changing without pattern. False positive risk: penalizing controlled disorientation that builds toward productive reorganization.

### 4.6 Conspiracy/political thriller
Promise: institutional threat, paranoia, narrowing options. Tighten on: information economy (multiple streams), clock mechanics (institutional threat closing), red herring integrity (paranoia grounded in actual misdirection). Loosen on: single-culprit solution, full resolution. Named failure: **The Bulletin Board** — connections proliferating without narrowing; more threads don't increase tension because none resolve. False positive risk: flagging deliberate complexity as incoherence.

### 4.7 Domestic thriller
Promise: intimate threat, trust erosion, familiar spaces made dangerous. Tighten on: information economy (behavioral clues, relationship evidence), clock mechanics (escalating domestic danger), investigation legibility. Loosen on: external investigation apparatus, procedural elements. Named failure: **Kitchen Twist Roulette** — twist dependency with weak clue plumbing; proximity and intimacy without information architecture. False positive risk: penalizing emotional pacing that carries architectural weight.

### 4.8 Inverted mystery
Promise: known culprit, pleasure in watching investigation approach truth. Tighten on: investigation legibility (detective's method must compel), reveal choreography (progressive tightening), clock mechanics. Loosen on: fair-play (reader already knows), identity-level surprise. Named failure: **The Transparent Trap** — reader can see the detective's path immediately; no pleasure in the chase because the method is obvious. False positive risk: demanding identity-level surprise from a mode that operates on method-level interest.

### 4.9 Literary hybrid
Promise: mystery architecture carrying interpretive depth. Tighten on: thematic integration, information economy, ending coherence (ambiguity designed not accidental). Loosen on: genre-standard pacing, clean resolution, strict fair-play. Named failure: **The Beautiful Fog** — literary ambiguity substituting for mystery architecture; opacity is aesthetic choice rather than puzzle design. False positive risk: penalizing deliberate anti-closure that is architecturally sophisticated (Auster, Bolaño).

## 5) Distinguish Problem: Intentional Rule-Bending vs Accidental Cheating

### 5.1 Valid intentional modes

1. **Deliberate unfairness in noir** — The solution may be unsatisfying because the world is unsatisfying. The "cheat" is thematic rather than lazy.
2. **Open endings in literary mystery** — Resolution is refused because the refusal IS the point. The investigation reveals the limits of knowing.
3. **Unreliable POV design** — Withholding information the POV would naturally have, done as a structural choice that the text signals and eventually reveals. Unreliability must be an organic result of character psychology, not an arbitrary device.
4. **Slow-burn pacing** — Investigation moving at atmospheric rather than procedural speed, where slowness carries meaning or pressure.
5. **Atmosphere-first passages** — Scenes that seem to stall investigation but deposit evidential or thematic material the reader doesn't yet recognize.
6. **Deliberate puzzle secondary** — Noir, domestic thriller, and literary hybrid modes where factual puzzle is less important than emotional/moral investigation.

### 5.2 Defective look-alikes

1. Unfairness that's arbitrary rather than thematic.
2. Open endings from unfinished architecture rather than deliberate refusal.
3. Unreliable narration that withholds without ever paying off the withholding.
4. Slow pacing that stalls investigation without depositing anything.
5. Atmospheric scenes that genuinely contain nothing evidential or thematic.
6. Puzzle secondary because the author couldn't construct a satisfying one.

### 5.3 Operational tests

1. **Accessibility Test:** Was core solution evidence available to the reader before the reveal? If yes → fair. If no → check whether withholding is structural design or a cheat.
2. **Inference Test:** Could a skilled reader form plausible true-model hypotheses at the midpoint? If yes → tractable. If no → starvation (unless mode deliberately refuses tractability).
3. **Clock Conversion Test:** Does the deadline change character choices, not just narration? If yes → enacted. If no → cosmetic (unless mode doesn't claim clock burden).
4. **Retroactive Coherence Test:** Do prior scenes improve under the final explanation? Does the solution reorganize earlier events? If yes → deep surprise. If no → arbitrary surprise.
5. **Misdirection Foundation Test:** Do red herrings have evidentiary basis a careful reader could evaluate? If yes → fair misdirection. If no → noise.
6. **Architecture-Ambiguity Test:** Does unresolved material produce interpretive richness or just unanswered questions? If the former → designed ambiguity. If the latter → unfinished architecture.
7. **POV Fairness Test:** If unreliable POV, was withholding character-credible and bounded by psychology? Does re-reading reveal a consistently flawed perspective, or sheer authorial concealment?

### 5.4 Outcome classes

1. **Intentional and Effective** (Pass)
2. **Intentional but Unstable** (Pass with note — intention legible, execution wobbles)
3. **Ambiguous / Developmental** (Conditional — could be intentional, could be accidental)
4. **Execution Failure** (Fail)

### 5.5 False-positive controls

Before calling systemic failure, confirm:
1. You didn't impose fair-play whodunit standards on noir or literary mystery. Check contract.
2. You didn't penalize deliberate anti-closure that produces interpretive richness.
3. You didn't flag unreliable narration as a cheat when the unreliability IS the information system.
4. You didn't demand explicit clock from modes (cozy, literary, inverted) where urgency operates differently.
5. You didn't mistake a deliberately loose puzzle (noir, literary) for a broken one. Check thematic purpose.
6. You assessed the reader's inference *capacity* at key points, not just the factual fairness of the ending. Retroactive fairness alone is not sufficient.
