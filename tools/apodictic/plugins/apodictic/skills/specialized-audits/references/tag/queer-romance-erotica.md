# Specialized Audit: Queer Romance and Erotica
## Version 0.4.13
*Last Updated: February 2026*

---

## Purpose

This audit evaluates craft challenges specific to queer romance and erotica—managing pronoun clarity, navigating tropes and stereotypes, balancing joy and struggle, and achieving authenticity in representing queer lives, relationships, and communities. Applies to any work where queer relationships are central to the narrative.

**When to activate:**
- Romance or erotica with same-gender pairings (M/M, F/F, etc.)
- Romance featuring bi, pan, or otherwise non-straight protagonists
- Romance or erotica featuring trans or nonbinary characters
- Work where queer identity is thematically central
- Polyamorous or relationship-anarchist narratives
- Work explicitly marketed to queer readers
- Work by non-queer authors writing queer protagonists (additional scrutiny)

**This audit does not police identity or gatekeep who can write what. It evaluates craft—whether the work achieves authentic, resonant representation and avoids common pitfalls.**

---

## Core Questions

The audit addresses these fundamental questions:

1. **Is the prose technically clear, especially in intimate scenes?**
2. **Does the work engage with or reinforce tired tropes and stereotypes?**
3. **Is queerness incidental, thematic, or traumatic—and is that calibrated to intent?**
4. **Does the narrative feel like it's written for queer readers or explaining queerness to straight ones?**
5. **Are queer characters full humans or representatives of their identity category?**

## Technical Craft: Pronoun Clarity

### The Same-Pronoun Challenge

When both (or all) partners in a scene share pronouns, clarity becomes a craft challenge. This is especially acute in intimate/erotic scenes where bodies are in close proximity.

**Clarity strategies to track:**

**Name anchoring**
- Regular use of character names to re-establish reference
- Risk: Can feel mechanical if overdone

**Action attribution**
- "Marcus pulled back" rather than "He pulled back"
- Ties pronouns to specific actions

**Position/role anchoring**
- Establishing spatial relationships (the one on top, the one against the wall)
- Using temporary role-based references

**Characteristic anchoring**
- Physical distinguishing features (the taller one, dark hair against the pillow)
- Risk: Can feel reductive if overused

**POV discipline**
- Strict POV where "he/she/they" always means the non-POV character
- Requires consistency

### Clarity Assessment

For intimate scenes, assess:
- Can reader always track who is doing what to whom?
- Are there moments of genuine confusion?
- Is clarity achieved gracefully or mechanically?
- Does the solution fit the scene's tone?

**Flag:** Passages where referent is genuinely ambiguous; passages where clarity solutions feel clunky or intrusive.

## Trope and Stereotype Navigation

### Harmful Tropes to Flag

**"Bury Your Gays"**
- Queer characters die at higher rates than straight characters
- Death feels punitive or convenient
- Queer happiness is narratively unstable

**Predatory Queer**
- Queer characters coded as threatening
- Sexuality linked to villainy
- Predation on straight characters

**Tragic Queer**
- Queerness = suffering
- No joy, only struggle
- Suicide, addiction, isolation as queer destiny

**Deceptive Trans**
- Trans characters who "trick" others
- Reveal scenes played for horror or betrayal
- Trans identity as plot twist

**Gay Best Friend / Token**
- Queer character exists to serve straight protagonist
- No interiority or arc of their own
- Defined entirely by identity

**Magical Queer**
- Queer character exists to help straight character grow
- Wisdom-dispenser without own needs
- Sacrifices for straight protagonist's development

### Tired (Not Harmful) Tropes

These aren't offensive, just exhausted. Flag if the work doesn't refresh them:

- Enemies to lovers (ubiquitous in M/M)
- Only gay in the village
- Gaybies ever after (adoption/surrogacy as mandatory HEA element)
- Coming out as entire plot
- Internalized homophobia as primary conflict
- "Who's the man/woman?" relationship mapping
- First queer relationship = true love
- Assimilation narrative (queerness disappears into normativity)

### Stereotype Assessment

For each significant queer character, assess:
- Do they exist beyond their identity category?
- Are they a full person or a representative?
- Would removing their queerness remove their personality?
- Do they have desires, flaws, and arcs unrelated to identity?

## The Joy/Struggle Calibration

### Spectrum Identification

Where does the work sit?

**Pure Joy**
- Queerness is incidental or celebratory
- No identity-based conflict
- Queer characters just live their lives
- *Works for: Escapist romance, wish-fulfillment, "no homophobia AU"*

**Joy-Centered with Context**
- Queerness shapes experience but doesn't dominate
- External challenges exist but don't overwhelm
- Happiness is achievable and stable
- *Works for: Most contemporary romance*

**Struggle-Centered with Hope**
- Identity-based conflict is significant
- Coming out, discrimination, family rejection central
- But hope/resolution is genuine
- *Works for: Coming-of-age, issue-focused literary*

**Trauma-Centered**
- Suffering is primary experience
- May not resolve happily
- Queerness as site of pain
- *Works for: Literary fiction exploring specific historical/social realities*

### Calibration Questions

- Where does the author intend this work to sit?
- Does the execution match the intent?
- Is the ratio appropriate for the target audience?
- If struggle-heavy, is it earned or gratuitous?

### Flag Patterns

**Unearned suffering:**
- Trauma included for "realism" without purpose
- Suffering that doesn't serve character or theme
- Pain as identity tax

**Unearned joy:**
- Homophobia/transphobia magically absent without worldbuilding reason
- Conflicts resolved too easily
- "Everyone was fine with it" in contexts where they wouldn't be

**Neither is automatically wrong—but both should be intentional.**

## Audience Orientation: For Whom?

### The "Explaining Queerness" Problem

A key authenticity marker: Is this written for queer readers or explaining queerness to straight ones?

**Signs of writing FOR queer readers:**
- Assumed familiarity with community dynamics
- In-jokes and references that don't need explanation
- Queerness as texture, not lesson
- Characters don't explain their identities to each other
- Focus on intra-community dynamics

**Signs of writing TO straight readers:**
- Over-explanation of basic concepts
- Characters who explain their identity to other queer characters
- Queerness as educational content
- "Ambassador" characters who translate queer experience
- External (straight) gaze on queer relationships

**Assessment:** Neither orientation is wrong, but:
- Work marketed to queer readers should feel like insider writing
- Work positioned as crossover should handle explanation gracefully
- "Explaining" energy can feel condescending or othering to queer readers

### The Voyeurism Check

Especially in erotica—but relevant for any intimate content:

**Gaze Assessment:**
- Does the erotic content feel celebratory or exhibitionistic?
- Is queer sex written for queer readers' pleasure or straight readers' curiosity?
- Are bodies and acts treated with dignity?
- Does the gaze feel internal or external?

**Specific Red Flags:**
- Excessive focus on "how it works" mechanically (treating queer sex as exotic)
- Straight POV character observing queer intimacy
- Eroticization of trauma, coming out, or first times in exploitative ways
- M/M written as performance for presumed female/straight gaze
- F/F written as performance for presumed male gaze
- Trans bodies described with clinical or objectifying detail
- "Teaching the reader" energy during intimate scenes

**Diagnostic Questions:**
1. Who is the implied reader of this scene?
2. Whose pleasure is centered?
3. Would a queer reader of this identity feel seen or observed?
4. Is there anything here that feels like it's for someone else's titillation?
5. Read by a queer person of this identity: cringe or recognition?

## Queer Genre Fiction Beyond Romance

This audit focuses on romance/erotica but many principles apply to queer characters in other genres. Additional considerations:

### Queer Horror

- The "queer = monstrous" equation: subverting or reinforcing?
- Body horror and trans/queer bodies: tread carefully
- Queer survival: who lives, who dies, and why?
- Found family in horror: queer survival through community
- Conversion, possession, transformation: metaphor minefields
- *Can be powerful when done well; harmful when thoughtless*

### Queer Thriller/Mystery

- Queer victim, queer villain, queer investigator: implications differ
- "Gay panic" defense as plot point: handled how?
- Queer community as setting vs. backdrop
- Closeted characters: whose secret, what stakes?
- Historical queer crime (sodomy laws, etc.): accuracy and sensitivity

### Queer Science Fiction

- Utopia/dystopia: how is queerness positioned?
- Alien/posthuman sexuality: is it actually queer rep or avoidance?
- Found family in space: queer dynamics
- Technology and transition: wish fulfillment, worldbuilding, or handwave?
- Reproduction and family in speculative contexts

### Queer Fantasy

- Magical transition: respectful or dismissive of real trans experience?
- Worldbuilding queerness: ubiquitous, hidden, or nonexistent in-world?
- "No homophobia" secondary worlds: implications and consistency
- Queer-coded villains: reinforcing or subverting?
- Chosen one narratives: queerness as destiny vs. identity

### Assessment for Genre Work

- Does queerness serve the genre or is it incidental?
- Are queer characters protected by plot armor or sacrificed to it?
- Do genre conventions work with or against queer representation?
- Is this queer horror or horror with a queer character? (Both valid, different expectations)

## Authenticity Markers

### Community Texture

Queer lives often include:

**Found family / chosen family**
- Friend networks that function as family
- Not explained, just present
- Specific dynamics (the group chat, the friend who always hosts)

**Queer spaces**
- Bars, bookstores, community centers, apps
- Rendered with specificity, not generically
- Evolution of spaces over time acknowledged

**Intra-community dynamics**
- Generational differences
- Identity-group tensions
- Politics and in-fighting
- Shared references and cultural touchstones

**Flag:** Work that positions queer characters as isolated, with no community connections, unless this is explicitly thematized.

### Relationship Dynamics

**Heteronormative mapping problems:**
- "Who's the man?" assumptions
- Butch/femme as husband/wife analog
- Top/bottom as personality determinant
- Division of emotional/practical labor along gendered lines

**Authentic queer relationship markers:**
- Negotiated rather than assumed roles
- Flexibility and fluidity
- Different models than straight relationships
- Chosen structures (or resistance to structure)

### Identity-Specific Considerations

**Bisexual/Pansexual characters:**
- Bi erasure: Character described as "gay" when in same-gender relationship
- The "picking a side" narrative
- Promiscuity stereotypes
- Opposite-gender exes treated as "before they knew"
- Attraction to multiple genders actually shown, not just stated

**Trans characters:**
- Deadnaming (even in backstory—is it necessary?)
- Over-focus on medical transition
- Reducing trans characters to their transness
- The "reveal" scene problem
- Trans joy, not just trans struggle
- Trans characters in roles unrelated to being trans

**Nonbinary characters:**
- Pronoun consistency (or intentional variation if that's the character)
- Avoiding "third gender" exoticization
- Specific nonbinary experiences, not just "neither"
- Multiple nonbinary characters (so one isn't representative)

**Asexual/Aromantic characters:**
- Asexuality ≠ celibacy ≠ lack of romantic attraction
- Aromanticism ≠ asexuality ≠ lack of relationships
- The spectrum: gray-ace, demisexual, etc.—handled with specificity?
- Ace characters in romance: how does the genre accommodate?
- Avoiding "fixed by the right person" narratives
- Sensual, aesthetic, and other forms of attraction acknowledged?
- Ace joy, not just ace explanation
- QPRs (queerplatonic relationships) as valid relationship structures

**Polyamorous/Relationship-Anarchist characters:**
- Polyamory ≠ cheating, swinging, or commitment-phobia
- Different poly structures (hierarchical, non-hierarchical, solo poly, relationship anarchy)
- Communication and negotiation as ongoing, not one-time setup
- Jealousy as real and navigable, not absent or shameful
- Avoiding "throuple solves love triangle" as easy fix
- Time and logistics: poly takes work the narrative should acknowledge
- Existing relationships when new partner joins: how are they depicted?
- Metamour dynamics (partners' partners)
- The "one true threesome" problem: is this poly or just fantasy?

## Intersectionality

Queer identity doesn't exist in isolation. When queer characters hold other marginalized identities:

### Queer + Race/Ethnicity

- How does racial/ethnic background shape queer experience?
- Cultural-specific attitudes toward queerness (neither monolithic nor stereotyped)
- Family dynamics that reflect specific cultural contexts
- Intra-community racism in queer spaces
- Fetishization concerns (especially in erotica)
- Whose stories get told? Whose perspectives centered?

### Queer + Disability

- Disabled queer characters as full romantic/sexual beings
- Accessibility in queer spaces and dating
- Ableism within queer communities
- Chronic illness and intimacy
- Mental health: neither romanticized nor reduced to obstacle
- Avoiding "inspiring" narratives that center able-bodied gaze

### Queer + Class/Economic Status

- Economic realities of queer life (job discrimination, family rejection's financial impact)
- Class differences within queer communities
- Gentrification of queer spaces
- Who can afford to be visibly out?
- The "affluent gay" stereotype vs. economic diversity

### Assessment Questions

- Do intersecting identities feel integrated or additive?
- Is the character's experience shaped by intersection, not just sum of parts?
- Are cultural specificities rendered with nuance?
- Is the author equipped to write this intersection? (Sensitivity reader question)

## Historical Queer Fiction

When queer romance is set in the past:

### Historical Framing Questions

- What was the actual landscape for queer people in this time/place?
- What language/concepts existed? (Gay, homosexual, invert, etc.)
- How did people find each other?
- What were the risks? Consequences?
- What queer communities/subcultures existed?

### Common Problems

**Presentism:**
- Modern queer identity frameworks in historical settings
- Characters who think about themselves in anachronistic terms
- "Coming out" as concept before it existed

**Misery porn:**
- Historical queerness as only suffering
- Ignoring evidence of queer joy in the historical record
- Assuming more oppression than actually existed in specific contexts

**Wish fulfillment without acknowledgment:**
- Ignoring historical realities entirely
- Happy endings that history wouldn't have allowed
- *This can be valid in romance if acknowledged—see Calibration*

### Historical Calibration

During intake, establish:
- How historically rigorous does this work intend to be?
- Is this researched history or historical fantasy?
- What compromises is the author consciously making?
- Does the work acknowledge its relationship to historical reality?

**Pair with Historical Fiction audit for full period assessment.**

## Coming Out Narratives

### When Coming Out Is Central

If the work includes significant coming out arc:

**Freshness check:**
- Does this add something to the well-worn narrative?
- Is it specific to this character and situation?
- Or is it generic "coming out story #4,127"?

**Complexity check:**
- Is coming out a single event or ongoing process?
- Different comings-out to different audiences?
- Complications, backsliding, context-dependence?

**Resolution check:**
- Is resolution earned or convenient?
- Are negative reactions realistic for context?
- Are positive reactions realistic for context?

### When Coming Out Is Absent

**Validity check:**
- Not every queer story needs coming out
- Characters can simply be out
- "Already out" is increasingly realistic for contemporary settings

**Worldbuilding check:**
- If no one comes out, why? Is homophobia absent from this world?
- If so, is that intentional and consistent?
- Or is it an unconsidered convenience?

## False Positive Warnings

**What looks like a problem but isn't:**

### Trope usage isn't automatically bad
Tropes become tropes because they resonate. The question is whether they're refreshed, subverted, or just replicated.

### Struggle isn't automatically trauma porn
Queer people face real challenges. Depicting them isn't harmful if done with purpose and care.

### Writing outside identity
Authors can write across identity with research, sensitivity readers, and humility. The audit assesses craft, not credentials.

### Historical "inaccuracy" as choice
Historical queer romance often makes deliberate compromises for HEA. This is genre-appropriate, not failure.

### Not every trope needs subversion
Sometimes readers want the familiar done well, not deconstructed.

### Single-identity focus
A work can center one queer identity without representing all. Not every M/M novel needs trans or nonbinary characters.

## Calibration Questions

During intake, establish:

1. **What is the queer identity configuration?** (M/M, F/F, bi protagonist, trans characters, etc.)
2. **Where on the joy/struggle spectrum is this intended to sit?**
3. **Who is the target audience?** (Queer readers, crossover, general market)
4. **Is this own-voices or across-identity writing?**
5. **What is the relationship to coming out?** (Central, present, absent)
6. **If historical, what is the accuracy intention?**
7. **Are there specific tropes being engaged, subverted, or avoided?**
8. **Has the author worked with sensitivity readers?**

- Tired tropes: present/refreshed/replicated
- Character-by-character stereotype check

### Joy/Struggle Calibration
- Spectrum position identification
- Intent vs. execution assessment
- Earned vs. unearned suffering/joy

### Audience Orientation Assessment
- For whom is this written?
- "Explaining queerness" instances
- Voyeurism check (for erotica)

### Authenticity Assessment
- Community texture evaluation
- Relationship dynamics check
- Identity-specific considerations (as applicable)

### Historical Assessment (if applicable)
- Period-appropriate language and concepts
- Historical accuracy vs. genre conventions
- Wishful vs. researched elements

### Coming Out Assessment (if applicable)
- Freshness and complexity
- Resolution quality
- Integration with overall narrative

### Specific Flags
For each flag:
- Location
- Issue type
- Severity (craft issue vs. potential harm)
- Suggested recalibration

### Recommendations
Abstract structural terms:
- "Pronoun clarity breaks down at [location]; consider name anchoring"
- "[Trope] appears without refresh; consider subversion or deeper engagement"
- "Joy/struggle balance tips toward [x] in Act II; recalibrate if unintentional"
- "Narrative explains queerness at [location] in ways that may alienate target audience"
- "Character [X] lacks interiority beyond identity; develop non-identity-related arc"

**NOT:** What queer identity to depict, whether author should write this, specific identity-based content.

## The Fundamental Question

The audit ultimately asks: **Does this work render queer lives, loves, and experiences with the fullness, specificity, and dignity they deserve?**

Queer romance and erotica at its best celebrates the varieties of queer love and desire. It gives readers mirrors and windows—recognition for queer readers, understanding for others. It avoids reducing characters to their identities while honoring how identity shapes experience.

The goal is **authentic resonance**: work that feels true to queer experience, whether joyful, painful, or complex. Work that treats queer characters as humans first, humans whose queerness matters without being all that matters.
