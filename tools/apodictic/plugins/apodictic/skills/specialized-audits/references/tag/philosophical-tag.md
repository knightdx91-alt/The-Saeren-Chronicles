## Tag Audit Family Context

This is a **tag audit** — it evaluates an experience-layer promise, not a structural contract. Philosophical fiction promises that the narrative is doing epistemological, ethical, or metaphysical work: ideas are load-bearing elements of the story, not decoration, and the reader is invited into the thinking process.

The philosophical tag can sit on any parent genre: philosophical horror, philosophical historical fiction, philosophical SF, philosophical thriller, philosophical literary fiction. The parent genre's structural obligations remain; the tag adds a layer of intellectual inquiry that shapes how the reader experiences those structures.

For the tag audit family architecture, see the Cozy Tag audit (`references/tag/cozy-tag.md`), which established the structural pattern: experience model with named axes, named diagnostic flags, intensity spectrum, cross-genre calibration, anchor + interacting lenses method, and an inline **Level-Setting Notes** section grounding the axes in external evidence. (Cozy additionally has a standalone companion brief at `references/tag/cozy-tag-level-setting.md` because its external evidence base is unusually deep — that's the exception, not the standard.)

---

## Purpose

Diagnose whether a manuscript credibly delivers a "philosophical" reading experience as a cross-genre tag.

**Core claim:** Philosophical is not a parent genre with fixed plot beats. It is an inquiry layer: the text stages meaningful questions, pressures rival positions through character and consequence, and produces conceptual movement. The reader doesn't just *learn what the author thinks* — the reader *thinks alongside the text*.

**What this audit is:**
- A contract-to-delivery diagnostic for philosophical intent
- A question-architecture and embodiment diagnostic
- A mismatch detector between "philosophical" labeling and on-page execution

**What this audit is not:**
- A correctness test of the author's worldview
- A substitute for argument-spine analysis in non-narrative prose (use Dialectical Clarity)
- A demand that all fiction become discursive or essayistic
- A requirement that the text reference named philosophers

## Level-Setting Notes (Market + Form)

### Metadata Reality

- Thema includes `FDV` ("Metaphysical / philosophical fiction"), so the philosophical tag is a recognized classification path in Thema markets.
- BISAC fiction currently has no explicit "Philosophical Fiction" heading; philosophical-positioned fiction is typically shelved via adjacent codes (often Literary and/or Visionary & Metaphysical), plus supporting secondary signals.

**Operational implication:** treat "philosophical" as a delivery claim that may or may not map cleanly to shelf metadata depending on market standard.

### Craft Reality

Narrative-persuasion research repeatedly shows that story impact depends on transportation (immersion), and that overt argumentative pressure can trigger reader resistance. For philosophical fiction, this creates a core constraint:

- If ideas are detached from dramatic embodiment, reader transport drops.
- If thesis-pressure becomes coercive, resistance rises.

**Operational implication:** philosophical force comes from staged conflict, not lecture density. The most powerful philosophical fiction doesn't argue — it *demonstrates*, and the argument emerges from the demonstration.

---

## Trigger Conditions

Activate when any of the following are true:

1. Author, pitch, comps, or positioning language includes "philosophical," "novel of ideas," "metaphysical," "idea-driven," or "thought experiment."
2. The manuscript repeatedly foregrounds claims about knowledge, ethics, meaning, identity, freedom, truth, justice, consciousness, or value.
3. Beta/early readers report either:
   - "Interesting ideas, but didactic / lecture-heavy," or
   - "Feels deep, but I cannot state what it is actually asking."
4. Pass 11 needs a sharper read on philosophical market claim and readiness.

Do not auto-run when philosophical intent is absent.

## Relationship to Existing Audits

This audit complements existing infrastructure; it does not replace it.

- **Banister (Epistemic Humility):** evaluates rhetorical fairness across contested positions. Banister asks "is the argument *fair*?" This audit asks "is the argument *alive*?"
- **Dialectical Clarity:** evaluates claim/support structure in argument-dominant nonfiction. If dominant mode is argument/support (non-narrative), prioritize Dialectical Clarity. If dominant mode is narrative inquiry (fiction/hybrid), use this Philosophical Tag audit.
- **Character Architecture + Scene Turn:** evaluates whether ideas change choices and scene outcomes.
- **Emotional Craft:** evaluates whether conceptual material still transmits feeling.

## Philosophical Intensity Spectrum

Not all philosophical promises are equal. A manuscript's declared or inferred philosophical intensity determines how strictly these lenses apply.

**High Intensity** — The philosophical inquiry *is* the primary reader promise. The narrative exists to stage a question and pressure it through character, consequence, and event. Ideas are load-bearing structural elements; remove them and the story collapses. The reader comes to *think*, and the thinking is the experience. *Think: Dostoevsky, Iris Murdoch, Borges, Carmen Maria Machado, Ursula Le Guin's The Dispossessed.*

**Medium Intensity** — Philosophical concerns are a major experience layer alongside genre obligations. The story works as a story, but its distinctive quality comes from the ideas running through it. Characters face dilemmas that have philosophical weight. The reader thinks *while being entertained*, and the thinking enriches the entertainment. *Think: Ishiguro's Never Let Me Go, Atwood's The Handmaid's Tale, Ted Chiang's stories.*

**Low Intensity** — Philosophical questions provide depth and texture to an otherwise genre-driven narrative. The ideas are present and real but they don't dominate the experience. A reader might finish the book and say "that raised interesting questions" rather than "that made me think differently." *Think: a thriller with genuine ethical dilemmas, a mystery that explores epistemological uncertainty, a fantasy with a functioning political philosophy.*

**Why this matters:** A PH-4 (Idea-Stakes Disconnect) that's Must-Fix at High intensity might be Should-Fix at Medium and Could-Fix at Low. The spectrum prevents both over-diagnosis (flagging a genre thriller for insufficient philosophical rigor) and under-diagnosis (letting a self-described "novel of ideas" off the hook for undramatized thinking).

## Philosophical Delivery Model (Seven Axes)

Evaluate each axis as `Strong / Mixed / Weak`, then assign named flags where needed.

### Axis A: Question Architecture

Does the manuscript have a legible, consequential philosophical question — not just a topic?

A topic is "identity." A question is "can you remain yourself if every memory that makes you who you are was implanted?" The difference is that a question has stakes: it can be answered well or badly, it can be pressured by events, and characters can be broken by it.

Track:
- Is the central question legible by early-to-mid manuscript?
- Does the question survive pressure, or dissolve into topic drift?
- Are stakes attached to the question (personal, social, moral, epistemic)?
- Does the question sharpen or deepen over the course of the narrative, or does it just repeat?

**Named failure patterns:**

- **Topic Fog:** The manuscript orbits a philosophical territory (identity, freedom, justice) without crystallizing a specific question that can be pressured by events. The reader senses that something important is being gestured at but can't articulate what the book is *asking*. This is the most common philosophical failure in literary fiction.
- **Question Collapse:** A strong early question dissolves as the plot takes over. The manuscript asks something genuinely interesting in the first third, then forgets about it in favor of narrative momentum. The philosophical layer is abandoned rather than resolved.
- **Question Multiplication:** The manuscript raises too many philosophical questions without giving any of them sufficient pressure. Each chapter introduces a new idea. The result is breadth without depth — the reader gets a survey course rather than an inquiry.

### Axis B: Dramatic Embodiment

Do ideas alter decisions, risks, and outcomes — or do they sit in exposition while the plot does something else?

This is the axis that separates philosophical fiction from fiction that contains philosophy. In philosophical fiction, what a character *believes* determines what they *do*, and what they do generates consequences that pressure what they believe. The ideas are in the plot machinery, not alongside it.

Track:
- Are key claims instantiated in scene-level choices?
- Do characters pay costs for positions they hold?
- Can major conceptual turns be located at scene/arc turns, not only in exposition?
- Does the plot *test* the ideas, or merely *illustrate* them?

**Named failure patterns:**

- **Seminar Scene:** Two or more characters sit down and discuss ideas at length in a way that sounds like a philosophy classroom rather than a conversation between people with stakes. The ideas are present but they're not dramatized — they're reported. The tell: the scene could be replaced by an essay and nothing narrative would be lost.
- **Parallel Tracks:** The philosophical content and the plot run side by side without interacting. Characters have interesting thoughts, and they also have adventures, but the thoughts don't drive the adventures and the adventures don't pressure the thoughts. Two stories sharing a manuscript.
- **Illustration Mode:** The narrative exists to demonstrate a pre-decided conclusion. Events are arranged to prove a thesis. Characters are moved into positions that confirm the author's argument. The ideas are present and embodied, but they're not *tested* — the outcome is never in doubt. This is distinct from having a stance (which is fine); it's the absence of genuine intellectual risk.

### Axis C: Counterposition Strength

Are rival positions represented in their strongest viable form?

The philosophical reader's experience depends on feeling that the question is genuinely open — that the text is *thinking*, not *preaching*. This requires that opposing positions be given their best case, not their weakest. The strongest philosophical fiction makes the reader genuinely uncertain which side is right, at least for stretches.

Track:
- Does each serious position get credible articulation?
- Are oppositions reduced to weak caricatures or bad-faith avatars?
- Does the text engage hard objections rather than bypassing them?
- Are there moments where the "wrong" position seems compelling?

**Named failure patterns:**

- **Straw Army:** Opposition exists but it's systematically weakened. Characters who hold the "wrong" view are less intelligent, less sympathetic, or less articulate than those who hold the "right" view. The argument appears balanced but the deck is stacked. (Overlaps with Banister audit's territory — if Banister is also running, this axis defers to Banister for severity.)
- **Mouthpiece Asymmetry:** The protagonist gets eloquent, nuanced articulation of their position. The antagonist or foil gets clumsy, reductive versions of theirs. The reader can feel the author's thumb on the scale not through argument but through prose quality.
- **Vanishing Objection:** The text raises a strong objection to its own thesis — then drops it without engagement. The hard question is acknowledged (which feels intellectually honest) but never answered (which isn't). This often happens when the author genuinely doesn't have a good answer and hopes the reader won't notice.

### Axis D: Conceptual Progression

Does the manuscript's thinking evolve, or does it repeat its opening position at increasing volume?

Philosophical fiction needs a conceptual arc — the ideas should move. Early formulations should be complicated by later events. The question should look different at the end than at the beginning, even if (especially if) the "answer" is more complex rather than more certain.

Track:
- Are early formulations revised by later evidence/events?
- Does the work move from premise → complication → refined understanding?
- Is there a philosophical turn — a moment where the question itself changes shape?
- Can you track a conceptual delta between beginning and end?

**Named failure patterns:**

- **Broken Record:** The manuscript states its philosophical position early, then restates it throughout without development. Each "philosophical moment" is a variation on the same insight. There's no conceptual movement — the reader is told the same thing five times in different scenes.
- **Volume Escalation:** The ideas don't develop, but they get *louder*. More characters state the thesis. More events illustrate it. More dramatic moments underline it. The manuscript mistakes intensity for progression. The philosophical content is a single note played fortissimo rather than a melody.
- **Late Pivot Without Foundation:** The manuscript introduces a significant philosophical shift in the final act that hasn't been prepared by earlier complications. The conceptual turn feels arbitrary rather than earned — the ideas jump rather than develop.

### Axis E: Philosophical Feel

Does the reader *think alongside* the text, or observe thinking from a distance?

This is the experience-layer axis — the one that distinguishes philosophical fiction as a *tag* (an experience promise) from philosophical content as an ingredient. The best philosophical fiction creates the sensation of active thought in the reader: the vertigo of an undermined certainty, the weight of a genuine dilemma, the pleasure of arriving at an insight a half-beat before (or after) the character does. The reader is a participant in the inquiry, not an audience for it.

Track:
- Does the text create space for the reader to think, or does it deliver conclusions?
- Are there moments of productive uncertainty — where the reader genuinely doesn't know what the "right" answer is?
- Does the prose invite inference and implication, or does it explain?
- Is there a gap between what the text *shows* and what it *means* that the reader must bridge?

**Named failure patterns:**

- **Closed Loop:** The text does all the thinking and presents the results. Every philosophical moment is fully articulated, fully resolved, fully explained. The reader is never invited to bridge a gap or hold a tension. The experience is *receiving* ideas rather than *having* them. Intellectually complete but experientially inert.
- **Explanatory Reflex:** The text shows something philosophically resonant — a scene, an image, a choice — and then immediately explains what it means. The explanation kills the reader's opportunity to think. The show is good; the tell that follows it is the problem.
- **Opacity Posture:** The inverse of Closed Loop. The text withholds so aggressively that the reader can't engage with the ideas at all. Difficulty is mistaken for depth. The philosophical content is *present* but inaccessible — not because the ideas are genuinely complex but because the text refuses to give the reader enough to work with. This is the "emperor's new clothes" failure mode.

### Axis F: Legibility Under Complexity

Is the philosophical content readable without flattening?

Philosophical fiction faces a permanent tension: the ideas must be complex enough to be worth thinking about, and clear enough to be thinkable. The audit isn't asking for simplification — it's asking whether the complexity is *navigable*.

Track:
- Terminology density vs. narrative clarity
- Ratio of scene-anchored thinking to free-floating abstraction
- Reader can state "what is at issue here" after major sections
- Jargon is earned (introduced through context and consequence) rather than assumed

**Named failure patterns:**

- **Jargon Fog:** Specialized philosophical vocabulary is deployed without grounding. The text reads like a translated monograph rather than a novel. Characters speak in concepts that haven't been earned through dramatic context. The reader who doesn't already know the terminology is locked out.
- **Abstraction Drift:** The text floats away from scene and character into extended passages of pure abstraction. The ideas may be interesting, but they've detached from the dramatic embodiment that makes them *fiction* rather than *essay*. Related to Seminar Scene (Axis B) but operates at the prose level rather than the scene level.
- **Decoration Philosophy:** References to named philosophers, philosophical traditions, or conceptual frameworks are sprinkled in to signal seriousness but don't interact with the plot, characters, or decisions. Remove the references and nothing changes. This is the philosophical equivalent of Comfort Prop in the Cozy audit — signifiers of depth without functional depth.

### Axis G: Resolution Ethics

Does the ending honor the philosophical promise — whether that promise is resolution, ambiguity, or transformed understanding?

Track:
- If closed ending: is closure earned by pressure-tested reasoning and consequence, or imposed by plot convenience?
- If open ending: is ambiguity substantive (the question is genuinely hard and the text has shown why) or evasive (the text didn't do the work to earn a position)?
- Does the final movement honor the declared ambiguity band?
- Does the ending feel like the *result* of the thinking the text has done?

**Named failure patterns:**

- **Resolution Collapse:** The manuscript raises genuinely hard philosophical questions, then resolves them too neatly. The story is braver in its questions than in its answers. The ending simplifies what the middle complicated. Often happens when the plot needs to land and the author sacrifices philosophical complexity for narrative closure.
- **Ambiguity Dodge:** The text ends ambiguously not because the question is genuinely hard but because the author doesn't want to commit. The ambiguity doesn't feel earned — it feels like avoidance. The tell: the text hasn't done the work to make *any* answer seem compelling, so the lack of an answer reads as evasion rather than honesty.
- **Thesis Snap:** After a narrative that genuinely explored multiple positions, the final pages suddenly lock into a single, clear moral. The philosophical complexity collapses into a lesson. Everything that came before is retroactively framed as buildup to this conclusion. The reader feels cheated — not because the text took a position, but because it pretended to be exploring when it was always heading to a predetermined destination.

## Cross-Genre Calibration Matrix

Use parent genre as calibration, not override.

| Parent Genre | Philosophical-Compatible Profile | Typical Breach |
|--------------|----------------------------------|----------------|
| Literary | Ideas woven into interiority, ambiguity, thematic density | Ideas detach into essay; or ideas are merely atmospheric ("deep vibes") |
| SF | Thought experiment as premise, extrapolation tests ideas through worldbuilding | Ideas front-loaded in premise, then abandoned for adventure plot |
| Horror | Epistemological/existential questions dramatized through dread and bodily experience | Horror machinery overwhelms the inquiry; or ideas reduce to allegory |
| Fantasy | Moral/political philosophy embodied in world systems and power structures | Worldbuilding carries ideas at the macro level but characters don't engage them |
| Thriller | Ethical dilemmas under time pressure, moral compromise with real stakes | Ideas raised in quiet moments, ignored during action sequences |
| Historical | Ideas tested against actual historical conditions and consequences | History as setting for ideas rather than testing ground; anachronistic framing |
| Mystery | Epistemological inquiry (what can be known? how?) mapped onto investigation structure | Philosophical content decorates a conventional whodunit |
| Romance | Questions about desire, autonomy, identity, and obligation within intimate relationships | Ideas raised in dialogue, abandoned during emotional/intimate scenes |

Rule: parent-genre obligations still apply. Philosophical modifies reader-experience calibration; it does not erase genre contract.

## Analysis Method (Anchor + Interacting Lenses)

This audit is not a sequential checklist. After one required anchor, evaluate the manuscript through interacting lenses and synthesize patterns across them.

### Required Anchor: Philosophy Claim Lock

Before analysis, state the implied philosophical promise:

```
Governing Question: [one sentence — a question, not a topic]
Provisional Stance: [if any]
Anti-Stance: [what the manuscript is arguing against, or what it refuses]
Ambiguity Target: [Low / Medium / High]
Philosophical Intensity: [High / Medium / Low]
Reader Promise: [the thinking experience offered]
```

If governing question cannot be stated from the text, flag immediately (`PH-1` likely).

### Core Lenses (Run Iteratively, Not Sequentially)

Use all lenses on the same scene set and update judgments as cross-evidence appears.

#### Lens 1: Question Architecture
Focus: Is there a stable, consequential philosophical question?
Primary axis: A. Connects to: D (does the question develop?), G (does the ending answer or honor it?).
Failure tendency: Topic Fog, Question Collapse, Question Multiplication (`PH-1`).

#### Lens 2: Embodiment and Stakes
Focus: Do ideas alter decisions, risks, and outcomes?
Primary axis: B. Connects to: A (is the question driving choices?), E (does embodiment create the feel of thinking?).
Failure tendency: Seminar Scene, Parallel Tracks, Illustration Mode (`PH-4`).

#### Lens 3: Counterposition Pressure
Focus: Are rival positions represented in their strongest form?
Primary axis: C. Connects to: D (does encountering the counterposition change the trajectory?), G (is the ending's stance earned by having engaged the opposition?).
Failure tendency: Straw Army, Mouthpiece Asymmetry, Vanishing Objection (`PH-3`).

#### Lens 4: Conceptual Arc
Focus: Does the manuscript's thinking evolve?
Primary axis: D. Connects to: A (does the question sharpen?), B (do events drive the evolution?), G (does the ending reflect the evolved thinking?).
Failure tendency: Broken Record, Volume Escalation, Late Pivot Without Foundation (`PH-6`).

#### Lens 5: Reader Participation
Focus: Does the reader *think alongside* the text?
Primary axis: E. Connects to: F (is the thinking accessible enough to participate in?), B (does embodiment create thinking-space?).
Failure tendency: Closed Loop, Explanatory Reflex, Opacity Posture (`PH-7`).

#### Lens 6: Navigable Complexity
Focus: Is the philosophical content readable without flattening?
Primary axis: F. Connects to: E (does legibility enable participation?), B (are ideas grounded in scenes?).
Failure tendency: Jargon Fog, Abstraction Drift, Decoration Philosophy (`PH-5`).

#### Lens 7: Resolution Integrity
Focus: Does the ending honor the philosophical promise?
Primary axis: G. Connects to: A (does it answer or honor the governing question?), C (has it earned its stance by engaging opposition?), D (does it reflect conceptual progression?).
Failure tendency: Resolution Collapse, Ambiguity Dodge, Thesis Snap (`PH-8`).

### Interaction Patterns (Where Diagnosis Becomes Accurate)

Prioritize cross-lens patterns over any single metric:

- **Strong ideas + weak embodiment** = reads essayistic, low narrative force. The philosophy is real but it's not fiction — it's an essay wearing a novel's clothes (`PH-4`).
- **Strong embodiment + weak counterposition** = reads propagandistic. The ideas drive choices, but the choices are rigged. The reader feels manipulated rather than invited to think (`PH-3`).
- **High abstraction + emotional flatness** = perceived as "important but inert." The ideas may be interesting but they don't *feel* like anything. Cross-reference Emotional Craft audit (`PH-5`).
- **High ambiguity target + no clarified stakes** = perceived as vagueness, not complexity. The text isn't asking a hard question — it's avoiding asking any question (`PH-1`, `PH-8`).
- **High thesis clarity + no conceptual revision** = didactic certainty risk. The text knows what it thinks from page one and never wavers. The reader is lectured, not engaged (`PH-2`, `PH-6`).
- **Strong question + strong embodiment + strong counterposition + genuine conceptual arc** = the signature of excellent philosophical fiction. Protect this pattern. It's rare.
- **Decoration Philosophy + Seminar Scenes + Broken Record** = the "philosophy-flavored" manuscript. Philosophical signifiers everywhere, philosophical *work* nowhere. The most common failure mode in manuscripts that aspire to philosophical depth.
- **Strong feel + weak legibility** = the reader has the *experience* of thinking but can't articulate what they've thought. This can work at Low intensity (atmospheric philosophical texture) but fails at High intensity where the reader expects to arrive somewhere.

### Minimum Evidence Packet

For a full run, sample at least:
- Opening question-establishment corridor
- One midpoint pressure corridor
- One corridor where a rival position has its best case
- Ending movement

Minimum reference density: 2-4 scene/page references per severe flag.

## Philosophical Flag Family (PH Codes)

| Code | Flag | Named Patterns | Severity Guidance |
|------|------|----------------|-------------------|
| `PH-1` | Question Failure | Topic Fog, Question Collapse, Question Multiplication | Should-Fix; Must-Fix if central promise depends on inquiry |
| `PH-2` | Thesis Preachiness | Illustration Mode, Closed Loop | Should-Fix or Must-Fix based on intensity |
| `PH-3` | Counterposition Weakness | Straw Army, Mouthpiece Asymmetry, Vanishing Objection | Must-Fix when systemic |
| `PH-4` | Idea-Stakes Disconnect | Seminar Scene, Parallel Tracks | Must-Fix when ideas never alter choices |
| `PH-5` | Abstraction Overload | Jargon Fog, Abstraction Drift, Decoration Philosophy | Should-Fix |
| `PH-6` | No Conceptual Arc | Broken Record, Volume Escalation, Late Pivot Without Foundation | Should-Fix; Must-Fix for explicit "novel of ideas" claim |
| `PH-7` | Participation Failure | Closed Loop, Explanatory Reflex, Opacity Posture | Should-Fix; Must-Fix at High intensity |
| `PH-8` | Resolution Failure | Resolution Collapse, Ambiguity Dodge, Thesis Snap | Must-Fix when ending promise is inquiry-driven |
| `PH-9` | Philosophical Label Mismatch | (market-level flag, no named pattern) | Must-Fix for positioning use |

For each PH flag include:
- Named pattern (if applicable)
- Evidence (2-4 scene/page references)
- Confidence (High/Medium/Low)
- Reader impact if unfixed

Severity calibrates to declared philosophical intensity: a PH-4 that's Must-Fix at High may be Should-Fix at Low.

## Common False Positives and Misreads

### Not a Philosophical Failure

- Not naming philosophers explicitly (philosophical fiction doesn't require name-dropping)
- Keeping prose clear and concrete (clarity is not anti-philosophical; it is often the hardest philosophical achievement)
- Having a stance (inquiry is compatible with commitment; the question is whether the commitment was earned or assumed)
- Maintaining plot momentum (philosophical work need not be slow)
- Ending with uncertainty when uncertainty is earned and bounded
- Using genre machinery (a philosophical thriller is not less philosophical for having a ticking clock)
- Humor and lightness (philosophical inquiry can be playful; solemnity is not depth)

### High-Clarity Philosophy (Common Misread)

There is a persistent critical bias that equates difficulty with depth. A manuscript that is philosophically rigorous *and* highly readable will sometimes be dismissed as "not really philosophical" by readers calibrated to expect difficulty. This audit explicitly refuses that equation.

Philosophical depth is demonstrated by:
- Pressure-tested ideas in consequential action
- Genuine intellectual risk (the possibility of being wrong)
- Conceptual progression earned through dramatic means
- The reader's active participation in the thinking

Philosophical depth is *not* demonstrated by:
- Dense jargon
- Extended monologues
- Suspension of dramatic movement
- Prose that requires a graduate degree to parse

A manuscript that achieves philosophical depth through clarity and accessibility should be diagnosed as *successful*, not as "not philosophical enough."

### True Risks Often Missed

- **Straw Army disguised as balance:** Opposition characters exist and speak, but their best arguments are never given the prose quality or dramatic weight that the thesis-aligned characters receive.
- **Question drift mistaken for complexity:** The manuscript touches many philosophical topics, creating an impression of depth, but never pressures any single question hard enough to produce genuine insight.
- **Conceptual language as character trait:** A character "speaks philosophically" (uses big words, references ideas) but their philosophical talk never connects to their choices. The philosophy is characterization, not inquiry.
- **Ending claims complexity while dodging the hardest implication:** The text raises a genuinely difficult question, explores it seriously, then resolves it in a way that avoids the most uncomfortable possible answer. The philosophical courage runs out at the last moment.
- **Decoration Philosophy at scale:** The text is *about* philosophy (characters are philosophers, scenes take place in academic settings, ideas are discussed) but isn't *doing* philosophy (no question is pressured, no position is tested, no thinking moves).

## Lightweight Scoring (Optional)

Use only if needed for triage clarity.

Score each axis A-G:
- `2 = Strong`
- `1 = Mixed`
- `0 = Weak`

Total (0-14):
- `12-14`: Strong Philosophical Delivery
- `8-11`: Partial Philosophical Delivery
- `0-7`: Philosophical Mismatch

Hard gates override total score:
- Systemic `PH-3` or `PH-4` caps result at `Partial` or lower.
- `PH-9` with explicit philosophical positioning prevents "Strong."

Severity of all flags calibrates to declared philosophical intensity.

## Firewall Compliance

Allowed:
- Diagnose question architecture and philosophical delivery
- Flag mismatch between tag claim and manuscript behavior
- Prioritize vulnerabilities by reader impact

Forbidden:
- Inventing arguments, scenes, or doctrinal positions
- Writing replacement philosophical passages
- Adjudicating which worldview is "correct"
- Prescribing what the author should *think*

This audit diagnoses whether the philosophical promise is structurally and narratively delivered. The author chooses the content-level solution.
