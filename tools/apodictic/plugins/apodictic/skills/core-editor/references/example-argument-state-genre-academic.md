## 1. Context and Classification

Form: academic-article
Goal: establish a measurable peer-signaling diffusion mechanism the literature has not isolated
Argument type: AT2 — evaluative/explanatory (with the contribution claim as C0)
  Burden level: HIGH
Audience:
  Expertise: EXPERT (peer reviewers)
  Receptivity: HOSTILE
Evaluator: peer-reviewer (strictest calibration; CL0 is disqualifying)

## 2. Claim Architecture

C0 (main claim): curb-cut policy diffuses between cities through a measurable peer-signaling mechanism distinct from state mandate

Subclaims:
  C1: the existing diffusion literature attributes spread to mandate, leaving a gap
  C2: a peer-signaling mechanism predicts the observed adoption timing better
  C3: the mechanism is identifiable in the panel data within stated scope

Stakes: the field keeps mis-attributing diffusion and mis-targets the policy lever
  Stakes type: EPISTEMIC

### Contribution Claim

_seeded by C0 + the claim ladder — this paper shows a peer-signaling diffusion mechanism the mandate-centric literature does not isolate._

### Related-Work Positioning

_seeded by subclaim C1 — locates the contribution against the mandate-attribution consensus; names the gap it fills. A contribution with no related-work positioning would be a bare novelty assertion (the academic signature W4 risk)._

### Method and Evidence

_seeded by the support plan (§3, pending) — the panel-data identification strategy that distinguishes signaling from mandate timing._

### Limitations and Scope

_seeded by none (the writer states scope directly) — the mechanism is claimed within the panel's city set and period; no claim beyond it._

## 6. Objection and Dialectical Integrity Map

Objection 1 (the anti-thesis the argument must defeat): the observed pattern is fully explained by state mandate timing, so there is no distinct mechanism to claim
  Engaged: _pending — to be argued in Related-Work + the identification strategy once a draft exists_

Reviewer-anticipation (the writer's pre-list; the genre layer never authors these):
  - this is already known
  - the gap isn't real
  - the method doesn't support the claim
