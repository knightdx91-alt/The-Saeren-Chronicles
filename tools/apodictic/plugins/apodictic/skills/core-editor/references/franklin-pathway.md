# The Franklin Pathway: Pre-Spine Viability Gate

---

## Purpose

Handle inputs where the material **is not a story yet**—topic, situation, argument, reporting pile—and therefore cannot be meaningfully audited by Plot Architecture (50 spines) or Character Architecture (AQ/CQ).

This is not coaching scenes. It is structural triage + structural requirements, producing either:
- a viable candidate spine the Editor can then audit, or
- an honest classification that the piece is not story-shaped (or is story-used-as-evidence).

**Named for:** Jon Franklin's *Writing for Story* (complication → development → resolution), adapted as a pre-spine diagnostic and scaffolding tool.

**Applies to:**
- Drafts built from reporting, research, or lived experience that haven't found narrative shape
- Manuscripts where essay, argument, or situation has overwhelmed story
- Material at the notes/premise stage that needs structural testing before drafting
- Nonfiction forms the Editor's fiction-weighted passes don't fully cover: reported features, profiles, personal essays, narrative journalism

---

## Firewall Compliance

The Franklin Pathway operates within the Editor's firewall by producing structural requirements and mapping, not content.

### Allowed in Franklin Pathway

- Structural classification and gating ("this isn't a story yet")
- Candidate spine hypotheses **extracted from provided material**
- Structural functions needed for a story (turn-types), with "covered/missing" mapping
- Backward causality trace identifying the true entry point
- Context smother diagnostics + move/cut recommendations

### Not Automatic (Optional Separate Coaching Document)

- Inventing specific reversals, scenes, or content
- Writing new scene plans with invented settings/characters
- Suggesting what the protagonist "should do" as plot invention

### The Distinction in Practice

- ❌ NOT ALLOWED: "Make the defender discover the records in a dramatic confrontation with the agency director."
- ✅ ALLOWED: "The function chain requires a turn where first attempt fails or backfires, raising stakes. Existing material that could serve this function: [specific reference]. If none exists: structural gap."

The architect says where the load-bearing wall goes. The writer decides what's in the room.

---

## Trigger Conditions

Activate the Franklin Pathway when **≥2** of the following are true:

### Signal 1: Plot Architecture Fit Failure

Cannot map material to any of the 50 spine types without distortion. No spine type produces a PASS on its primary logic gate, or the best-fit spine produces FLAGS on more than 50% of its gates.

### Signal 2: Reverse Outline Causality Failure (Pass 0 / Pass 2)

Beat chain is dominated by "and then" rather than "therefore/but." More than 40% of scene transitions logged as arbitrary or unclear causal mechanism. Key turns don't produce state-change.

### Signal 3: Structural Mapping Failure (Pass 2)

No identifiable inciting incident, midpoint, or climax because the text never commits to a single causal line. Proportional analysis shows no discernible act structure.

### Signal 4: Protagonist Identification Failure (Character Architecture)

Character Architecture cannot compute AQ because "who is the story about" is underdetermined, or agency is distributed across a system without a focal actor.

### Signal 5: Reader Experience Signals (Pass 1)

No identifiable question driving the reader forward by page 20. "I would stop reading here" flags in the first 20% without compensating hook. Promise tracking unable to identify what ride the reader thinks they're on.

### Trigger Logic

| Signals Firing | Action |
|---------------|--------|
| 0–1 | Stay in normal pipeline; treat as weak execution of an existing spine, not absence of spine |
| 2–3 | Enter Franklin Pathway |
| 4–5 | Enter Franklin Pathway with priority flag: material likely needs fundamental reconception |

**Important:** The triggers are diagnostic signals, not judgments. Material that trips these signals may contain excellent writing, important reporting, or powerful experience.

## The Four-Way Gate (Step 0)

Before building anything, classify the material. This is the most important output the pathway produces.

### Classification 1: Story-Shaped (Proceed)

A viable spine exists in the material, even if poorly executed. The draft can state a complication event and a resolution event anchored to the same causal line.

→ Exit Franklin. Resume normal Editor passes. If input is nonfiction, optionally invoke the Narrative Nonfiction Craft audit on 1–2 scenes + lead/ending to improve craft execution (not spine).

### Classification 2: Storyable Material (Construct Spine, then Proceed)

The material contains events, characters, and stakes that could form a narrative, but it has not chosen a spine yet. There exists at least one:
- discrete destabilizing event,
- actor with constrained choices,
- outcome that would constitute resolution,

but the draft hasn't committed to them as a throughline.

→ Run Franklin Steps 1–4. If a viable candidate spine emerges, resume normal passes using that spine as the working hypothesis. If input is nonfiction, invoke the Narrative Nonfiction Craft audit to diagnose craft execution against the candidate spine.

### Classification 3: Argument With Embedded Narrative (Reroute)

The piece's dominant function is claim/support, and narrative is used strategically as evidence or illustration.

**Test:** The draft's paragraphs primarily answer "What do you think?" not "What happens next?" Narrative segments can be removed without destroying the logic of the argument—they enhance persuasion, not causality.

→ Do not force a story spine on the whole piece. Run Franklin Steps 1–4 on the embedded narrative segments only, to ensure they have internal spines. Invoke the **Dialectical Clarity Audit** on the argument structure (AT/CL/SM/BP/OB/NE codes). Invoke the Narrative Nonfiction Craft audit on the whole piece for reader contract and argument/narrative integration (F-codes, CQ, LC/E codes).

### Classification 4: Not Storyable (Redirect)

Material is primarily theme, meditation, exposition, or analysis without an event-problem/resolution pair that can carry narrative. No event-pair can be stated without inventing missing causal steps.

→ Output a clean redirect: what it is, what a story would require (what's missing), and which forms fit it better (thematic essay, argument-driven piece, analytical report, lyric meditation, policy brief). Optionally invoke the Narrative Nonfiction Craft audit Steps 1 (Form Classification), 2 (CQ), and 9 (Lead Contract) to recommend the best non-story form.

This is not a failure. It's a classification. Forcing story shape onto non-story material distorts both the story and the truth.

### Gate Diagnostics

When classifying, specify which structural element is present or absent:

| Element | Present? | Evidence |
|---------|----------|----------|
| Destabilizing event (complication) | | |
| Actor with constrained choices (protagonist) | | |
| Outcome that resolves the complication | | |
| Causal chain connecting complication → resolution | | |

| Missing Element | What This Usually Means | Common in |
|----------------|------------------------|-----------|
| No event-problem | Topic without tension; situation without disruption | Policy writing, feature articles, "about" pieces |
| No outcome | Open situation; ongoing condition; unresolved process | In-progress reporting, therapy memoir, advocacy |
| No protagonist | System-level analysis; distributed agency; institutional focus | Investigative journalism, policy analysis, sociology |
| No causal chain | Material exists but hasn't been organized by causation | Reporting piles, interview transcripts, research notes |

**Output:** Classification (1–4) + 3–5 bullet reasons + gate diagnostic table. If nonfiction: add Form Engine classification (F-code from Narrative Nonfiction Craft audit Step 1) + contract risk note.

## Vocabulary

Shared terms for the Franklin Pathway. These supplement, not replace, the Editor's existing terminology.

### Story Spine

The minimal structural statement: **Complication** (the destabilizing event that forces action) + **Resolution** (the outcome that answers the complication and changes the situation).

**Rule:** Complication and resolution must be events—things that happen—not themes ("racism," "grief," "innovation"), states ("she was unhappy"), or topics ("the foster system").

### Focus

A unit of narrative that ends in a **change**: a decision, reversal, discovery, failure, escalation, or commitment. The focus is a mid-level unit between "scene" and "act." It may contain multiple scenes. It is defined by its ending, not its beginning.

### Focus Statement

A one-sentence description of what the focus accomplishes—its ending state. Focus statements are endings, not beginnings.

- ❌ "The defender starts investigating." (beginning)
- ✅ "The defender discovers the agency is stonewalling, and realizes no one will fix it voluntarily." (ending = change)

### Function Chain

The ordered sequence of required structural turn-types that bridge complication → resolution. Expressed as structural functions with placeholders, not as specific plot events. Distinguished from a "focus chain" (which maps specific content to each function).

### Scene vs. Summary

- **Scene:** Moment-by-moment action with concrete setting, time, and causal steps.
- **Summary:** Compressed narration used to bridge, contextualize, or accelerate.

**Franklin bias:** Dramatize the structural turns (focus endings); summarize the rest. Most early drafts do the opposite—they summarize the turns and dramatize the bridges.

## The Franklin Diagnostic (Steps 1–4)

These steps run when the Gate returns **Storyable Material** (Classification 2) or **Argument With Embedded Narrative** (Classification 3, applied to the embedded narrative segments). They produce structural requirements and mapping, not content.

### Step 1: Candidate Spine Drafting

Produce **3 candidate complication/resolution pairs extracted from the material as written.**

**Format:**
- Complication (event): [Actor] [verb] [destabilizing event/problem].
- Resolution (event): [Actor] [verb] [outcome/change].

**Constraints:**
- Every noun and verb must be **traceable to supplied material** (notes, draft, reporting). If it's not traceable, mark it as "requires invention" and do not use it in the primary candidate.
- Use concrete verbs. No abstractions as the main verb ("explores," "examines," "highlights," "navigates").
- Avoid "about" language. The spine describes what happens, not what the material is about.
- Both sentences must describe events, not conditions.

**Quality test:**

| Type | Example | Verdict |
|------|---------|---------|
| Topic | "This is about the foster system." | ❌ Not a spine |
| State | "She struggles with grief." | ❌ Condition, not event |
| Commentary | "It explores injustice." | ❌ About-language |
| Event | "A public defender discovers her client is being held past his legal release date." | ✅ Spine |
| Decision | "A father decides to testify against his son's wishes." | ✅ Spine |
| Reversal | "A student's experiment fails days before the competition." | ✅ Spine |

**Output:** Best candidate pair + why it best matches what's already in the material. Two alternate pairs. Material sources cited for each.

### Step 2: Backward Causality Trace

Given the best resolution candidate, work backward:

- "For this resolution to occur, the preceding necessary turn is X."
- "For X to occur, the preceding necessary turn is Y."
- Repeat 3–5 times until reaching the true complication.

**This is diagnostic because it identifies:**
- Missing prerequisites (structural gaps)
- Where the draft should enter the action (the true beginning)
- Which "beginning" paragraphs are preamble that belongs after the hook or not at all

**Cross-reference with Pass 0:** If a reverse outline exists, the backward trace can identify which scenes from Pass 0 are pre-complication material that should be cut, compressed, or repositioned.

**Output:** Backward chain list (resolution → ... → true complication) + one paragraph on where the true entry point is and what the current intro should do.

### Step 3: Function Chain + Coverage Map

Instead of writing turns as specific plot events, write them as **required structural functions** (turn-types).

**Standard function chain:**

1. **Complication function:** Destabilizing event forces protagonist into a constrained choice.
2. **Development Turn 1:** First attempt fails or backfires, raising stakes.
3. **Development Turn 2:** New information or reversal changes the plan.
4. **Development Turn 3:** Irreversible commitment / risk point.
5. **Resolution function:** Outcome that answers the complication, with a clear state-change.

Then map: **"Which existing material could serve each function?"**

| Function | Turn-Type | Existing Material | Coverage |
|----------|-----------|-------------------|----------|
| Complication | Destabilizing event | [specific reference or "none"] | Covered / Partial / Missing |
| Dev Turn 1 | Attempt + failure | [specific reference or "none"] | Covered / Partial / Missing |
| Dev Turn 2 | Reversal / new info | [specific reference or "none"] | Covered / Partial / Missing |
| Dev Turn 3 | Irreversible commitment | [specific reference or "none"] | Covered / Partial / Missing |
| Resolution | Outcome + state-change | [specific reference or "none"] | Covered / Partial / Missing |

If none exists for a function: flag as **"structural gap"** (not "you should invent X").

**The Escalation Requirement:**

Each development turn must escalate relative to the previous turn on at least one dimension:

| Escalation Type | What Changes |
|----------------|-------------|
| **Stakes rise** | What's at risk increases (personal → professional → institutional → existential) |
| **Options narrow** | Available choices decrease (many paths → fewer paths → forced choice) |
| **Knowledge shifts** | Understanding transforms (wrong theory → partial truth → devastating truth) |
| **Alliances break** | Support structure erodes (allies → doubts → isolation → betrayal) |
| **Cost increases** | Price of action goes up (inconvenience → sacrifice → irreversible loss) |
| **Terrain changes** | The problem itself transforms (what seemed like X is actually Y) |

If consecutive development turns don't escalate on any dimension, flag as **"plateau risk."**

**Output:** Function chain + coverage map + escalation tags + structural gaps flagged.

### Step 4: Agency Identification

**If protagonist is identifiable** from the spine and function chain → defer to AQ/CQ calculation in the Character Architecture audit. Do not duplicate.

**If protagonist is NOT identifiable** → output protagonist candidates with structural consequences:

| Candidate | What This Choice Makes Possible | What It Costs |
|-----------|--------------------------------|---------------|
| [Person A] | [structural consequence] | [what gets lost] |
| [Person B] | [structural consequence] | [what gets lost] |
| [Reporter/narrator] | [structural consequence] | [what gets lost] |

**Common nonfiction options:**
- The person most affected by the outcome
- The person whose actions most directly connect complication to resolution
- The reporter/narrator, if their investigation provides the causal chain
- A proxy figure who embodies the systemic issue in a single case

**Output:** Either "protagonist identified → defer to Character Architecture" or "protagonist candidates + structural consequences of each choice."

## Context Smother Diagnostic (Nonfiction)

A diagnostic tool for nonfiction and research-heavy material. Stays within the firewall by diagnosing and recommending structural moves, not inventing rewrites.

### Context Load Index

Calculate: **percentage of paragraphs before the first destabilizing event that are explanatory vs. event-action.**

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Total paragraphs before complication | |
| Explanatory/background paragraphs | |
| Event/action paragraphs | |
| Context Load Index (explanatory / total) | |

**Thresholds:**
- CLI < 0.50: Acceptable (story enters early)
- CLI 0.50–0.70: Elevated (context may be smothering the hook)
- CLI > 0.70: Critical (reader encounters essay before story)

### Context Slab Classification

Identify each block of consecutive explanatory paragraphs ("context slab") and label:

| Slab | Location | Type | Classification |
|------|----------|------|----------------|
| | | **Necessary for comprehension** — reader literally can't follow the story without it | |
| | | **Optional enrichment** — adds depth but story works without it | |
| | | **Defensive/credentialing** — establishes writer's authority, not reader's need | |
| | | **Argumentation** — belongs in a different structural track, not the narrative | |

### Recommended Moves

For each slab, recommend (not rewrite):
- "Move after complication" (reader needs this, but not yet)
- "Convert to one line of context inside a scene" (compress)
- "Cut if the piece is story-first" (not needed for narrative)
- "Reroute to Dialectical Clarity Audit" (this is claim/evidence, not story context)

### Information Timing Principle

Context arrives when curiosity demands it — not before.

The Context Smother Diagnostic identifies *where* context slabs occur and whether they're necessary. This principle provides the *when*: information should be delivered at the moment the reader's question makes it welcome.

**The Timing Test:**

For each context slab or expository passage:

| Question | If YES → | If NO → |
|----------|----------|---------|
| Has the narrative raised a question that this information answers? | WELL-TIMED — reader wants this | PREMATURE — reader hasn't asked yet |
| Would the reader be confused without this information at this point? | NECESSARY — deliver it | DEFERRABLE — hold until curiosity peaks |
| Does this information arrive during a moment of narrative momentum? | DISRUPTIVE — find a lower-momentum placement | ACCEPTABLE — reader has room for it |

**The Demand Principle:** Information delivered before the reader wants it is exposition. Information delivered after the reader wants it is revelation. The writer controls the gap.

**Cross-reference:** This principle applies to all nonfiction forms but is especially critical for narrative journalism and reported features, where the writer has large amounts of context to place. See also Question Management Diagnostic below. The Narrative Nonfiction Craft audit extends this with formal I-codes (I1–I5 slab types, I0/I6/I7/I8 placement failures) and adds a "question served" field per slab — converting Context Smother from "too much context" to "context mistimed."

**Output:** Context Load Index + slab classification table + recommended moves + timing assessment for each slab.

## Scene Selection Diagnostic (Nonfiction)

**Based on:** Jack Hart's *Storycraft*. Extends the Franklin Pathway's nonfiction coverage with scene-level selection tools.

For narrative nonfiction, the writer has a pile of material — interviews, documents, observations, memories — and must decide which moments to dramatize as full scenes, which to summarize, and which to cut. This diagnostic provides the selection criteria.

### The Dramatization Test

For each event or moment in the material, apply:

| Criterion | Question | If YES → |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| **Structural turn** | Does this event change the story's direction? | DRAMATIZE — this is a focus ending |
| **Character reveal** | Does this moment show the protagonist making a choice under pressure? | DRAMATIZE — agency visible only in scene |
| **Emotional transmission** | Does this moment need sensory detail and real-time pacing to land? | DRAMATIZE — summary would kill the emotion |
| **Causal pivot** | Does what happens here directly cause what happens next? | DRAMATIZE — the chain needs to be visible |
| **Information delivery** | Does this moment primarily exist to convey facts to the reader? | SUMMARIZE — unless facts emerge through action |
| **Transition** | Does this moment bridge two structural events? | SUMMARIZE or CUT |
| **Atmospheric** | Does this moment establish setting or mood without event? | Use sparingly; convert to scene context |

### Scene Reconstruction Standards

In nonfiction, scenes must be reconstructable from evidence:

| Source Type | Reconstruction Confidence | Note |
|-------------|--------------------------|------|
| Firsthand observation by writer | High | Strongest basis for scene construction |
| Participant interview (multiple sources agree) | High | Cross-corroborated detail |
| Single-source interview | Medium | Flag if sole basis for a dramatic scene |
| Documents/records that imply action | Medium | Scene built from inference — acknowledge method |
| Memory reconstruction (memoir) | Variable | Cross-reference with Memoir/Creative Nonfiction audit |

**Firewall note:** This diagnostic identifies which events should be dramatized and assesses reconstruction confidence. It does not invent scenes or embellish beyond source material.

### The Proportion Rule

For narrative nonfiction, track:

| Section | Dramatized Scenes | Summary/Exposition | Ratio |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|-------|
| Act I | [word count] | [word count] | |
| Act II | [word count] | [word count] | |
| Act III | [word count] | [word count] | |

**Thresholds:**
- Scene ratio > 60%: Narrative-driven (standard for features, profiles)
- Scene ratio 40–60%: Balanced (common for long-form journalism, memoir)
- Scene ratio < 40%: Exposition-heavy — cross-reference with Context Smother Diagnostic

### Status-Complication-Resolution at Scene Level

In nonfiction, individual scenes often follow the same pattern as the overall story, in miniature:

1. **Status quo at scene opening:** What's the situation when we enter?
2. **Complication within scene:** What disrupts or pressures the situation?
3. **Resolution at scene end:** How has the situation changed?

This is the scene-level application of Franklin's spine. For each dramatized nonfiction scene, verify:

```
Scene [X]: [Description]
  Status: [situation at opening]
  Complication: [what disrupts]
  Resolution: [how situation changes]
  Missing element: [if any — which part of the pattern is absent?]
```

**Cross-reference:** The Scene Turn Diagnostics audit provides the parallel tool for fiction using goal-conflict-outcome. The nonfiction version uses status-complication-resolution because nonfiction POV characters may not have conscious goals in every scene — things happen to them, around them, or because of systems larger than any individual. The Narrative Nonfiction Craft audit extends SCR with ST-codes (Status Pressure), adding "who has authority/credibility/face" as the specific status dimension and testing whether status shifts advance the central question.

**Output:** Dramatization test results + reconstruction confidence + proportion table + scene-level SCR analysis.

## Narrative Stance Diagnostic (Nonfiction)

**Based on:** Jack Hart's *Storycraft* and Vivian Gornick's *The Situation and the Story*. The narrator's stance — their relationship to the material — is the engine that converts situation into story.

### The Core Diagnostic Question

**What is the narrator's relationship to the events?**

In fiction, POV and voice are calibrated through Pass 7. In nonfiction, there's an additional question: the narrator exists in a real relationship to real events, and that relationship is itself a craft decision.

### Stance Classification

| Stance | Description | Common In | Risk |
|--------|-------------|-----------|------|
| **Witness** | "I was there; here's what I saw" | Memoir, immersive journalism | Passivity — narrator observes but doesn't act or interpret |
| **Investigator** | "I pursued the story; here's what I found" | Investigative journalism, true crime | Narcissism — investigation becomes the story, displacing the subject |
| **Advocate** | "This matters; here's why you should care" | Policy writing, advocacy journalism | Argument displaces narrative; context smothers story |
| **Participant** | "I was part of this; here's what it meant" | Personal essay, memoir | Self-absorption — events serve self-exploration without reader payoff |
| **Invisible** | Narrator absent; events presented without apparent mediation | Literary journalism, some reported features | Pseudo-objectivity — narrator's choices hidden but still operating |

### Stance Consistency Check

```
CHECK: Does the narrator maintain a consistent stance throughout?

IF stance consistent → PASS
IF stance shifts with clear reason (e.g., investigation → participation) → PASS (note transition)
IF stance drifts without acknowledgment → FLAG: "Stance Drift" (→ SC0)
IF stance claimed (e.g., "objective observer") but actual stance differs → FLAG: "Stance Mismatch" (→ SC1)
```

**Cross-reference:** The Memoir/Creative Nonfiction audit's Gornick Layer provides formal SC-codes (SC0–SC3) for stance coherence, adding SC2 (contempt problem) and SC3 (self-exculpation) to the informal flags above.

### The Situation/Story Distinction (Gornick)

For memoir and personal essay specifically:

- **The situation** is what happened — the external events, the circumstances, the facts.
- **The story** is the narrator's evolving understanding of what happened — the meaning the narrator makes from the situation.

**Logic Gate:**
```
CHECK: Does this memoir/essay have a story beneath the situation?

IF narrator's understanding of events changes over the course of the piece → PASS
IF narrator reports events but never reprocesses them → FLAG: "Situation Without Story" (→ GS0)
IF narrator's stance at the end is identical to their stance at the beginning → FLAG: "Static Narrator" (→ EP2)
```

**Cross-reference:** The Memoir/Creative Nonfiction audit handles truth/memory ethics and scene reconstruction. Its **Gornick Layer** (GS/NI/SC/TD/BR/SEL/EP codes) provides the full formal diagnostic for situation/story separation, narrating intelligence, stance coherence, temporal distance, braid integrity, selection, and ending stance proof. When the flags above fire, invoke the Gornick Layer for detailed diagnosis.

**Routing for Classification 4 + personal narrative:** When Franklin returns Classification 4 (Not Storyable) but the material is personal narrative (memoir, personal essay, autobiographical nonfiction), run the Gornick Layer before issuing redirect. The piece may have a story at the stance level — the narrator's revaluation — even if it lacks a story at the event level.

**Output:** Stance classification + consistency check + situation/story diagnosis (for memoir/essay). When Gornick Layer is invoked: full 7-step diagnostic with codes.

## Question Management Diagnostic (Nonfiction)

**Based on:** Jack Hart's *Storycraft*. The story is propelled by what the reader wants answered. Managing reader questions — raising them, sharpening them, deferring answers, delivering payoffs — is the primary engine of narrative nonfiction.

### The Core Mechanism

In fiction, scene-level momentum comes from character goals (Bickham's goal → conflict → outcome). In nonfiction, the parallel engine is the reader's question: **What does the reader want to know, and when do they get the answer?**

This isn't the same as character goal. The reader's question may be about the character ("Will she win the case?"), the system ("How does this happen?"), or the meaning ("Why does this matter?"). It's the reader's curiosity that drives forward momentum, and the writer's control over question placement and answer timing that creates narrative drive.

### Question Inventory

For each major section or act, identify:

```
Section [X]:
  Active questions: [what the reader wants to know at this point]
  Questions raised: [new questions introduced in this section]
  Questions answered: [questions resolved in this section]
  Questions deferred: [questions explicitly withheld — answer comes later]
```

### Question Flow Logic Gates

**Gate 1: Opening Question**
```
CHECK: Does the narrative raise a compelling question within the first 10% of text?

IF clear question established → PASS
IF question arrives but is vague ("what happened?") → SOFT FLAG: "Weak Opening Question"
IF no identifiable question in first 10% → FLAG: "No Driving Question"
```

**Gate 2: Question Continuity**
```
CHECK: Is at least one unanswered question active at every point in the narrative?

IF always at least one active question → PASS
IF question gap exists (all questions answered, no new ones raised) → FLAG: "Question Vacuum"
   This is often where readers stop reading.
```

**Gate 3: Question Escalation**
```
CHECK: Do questions deepen or sharpen over the course of the narrative?

IF opening question evolves ("Will she win?" → "Can she win without becoming what she fights?") → PASS
IF same question repeats without deepening → FLAG: "Static Question"
IF questions multiply without hierarchy → FLAG: "Question Scatter"
```

### Integration with Existing Diagnostics

**Franklin Signal 5** (Pass 1): "No identifiable question driving the reader forward by page 20." The Question Management Diagnostic provides the mechanism for this signal — it's not just about hooks, but about the question engine.

**Context Smother Diagnostic:** The Information Timing Principle answers the "when" question for context placement — deliver context when the reader's question makes it welcome.

**Scene Selection Diagnostic:** The Dramatization Test's first criterion (structural turn) often coincides with a question-answering moment. Dramatize the answers to the biggest questions; summarize the rest.

**Narrative Nonfiction Craft Audit:** The audit extends this diagnostic with formal QS-codes (QS0–QS4) for per-question failure tracking, a question state timeline format (Raised → Held → Complicated → Answered → Replaced), and a CQ + Promise Map that tests whether the central question has a promise type and is reinforced at section turns.

**Output:** Question inventory + flow gate results + timing assessment.

## Failure Modes

Named diagnostic patterns the Franklin Pathway catches. These extend the Editor's existing diagnostic vocabulary.

### Failure Mode A: Topic Disguised as Story

**Symptoms:** Lots of explanation; minimal action; no decisive change; protagonist unclear or absent; scenes organized by topic rather than causation.

**Franklin Diagnosis:** Gate returns Not Storyable (Classification 4) or Argument With Embedded Narrative (Classification 3). No event-problem forcing action.

**Guidance:** Choose a single representative case, incident, or moment and build the spine around it. The topic becomes context that explains the story; the story provides the engine the topic lacks. Or: acknowledge it's an essay and structure as argument — invoke the Dialectical Clarity Audit for the argument-shaped material.

### Failure Mode B: Protagonist Disappears at Climax

**Symptoms:** Resolution happens to the protagonist, not by them; systems or other actors resolve the conflict; protagonist is present at ending but passive.

**Franklin Diagnosis:** Step 4 flags the resolution function as structurally dependent on a non-protagonist actor, or the agency identification finds the character who actually resolves the complication is different from the proposed protagonist.

**Guidance:** Either reframe the protagonist (who actually acts at the climax?) or reframe the resolution (the protagonist's decisive action is their response to events, even if they can't control the outcome—a decision to testify, leave, refuse, speak).

### Failure Mode C: Chronology Swamp

**Symptoms:** Timeline feels dutiful ("and then... and then..."); hook arrives late; causal links obscured by chronological obligation.

**Franklin Diagnosis:** Step 2 (Backward Causality Trace) reveals the true complication is buried under pre-complication chronology. The backward chain ends at a point well into the current draft.

**Guidance:** Start at the true complication. Use controlled flashback or context placement to deliver necessary prior information only when the reader's questions demand it. Chronology serves story, not the reverse.

### Failure Mode D: Plateau in the Middle

**Symptoms:** Development turns feel sequential but not escalating; "and then" connectors between turns; reader engagement drops in the middle third; repeating beats (same type of obstacle, same type of response).

**Franklin Diagnosis:** Step 3 (Function Chain) reveals development turns that fail the Escalation Requirement—no change in stakes, options, knowledge, alliances, cost, or terrain between consecutive turns.

**Guidance:** Each development turn must change at least one dimension. If two turns change the same dimension in the same direction (stakes rise, then stakes rise again), the second needs a different escalation type to avoid monotony. Merge redundant turns. Inject a genuine reversal (something the protagonist believed or relied on turns out to be wrong/gone). Or shift the problem itself (what seemed like a legal battle becomes a political one).

### Failure Mode E: Ensemble Without Throughline

**Symptoms:** Multiple compelling figures, no single protagonist; each section works but the whole doesn't build; material organized by source/interview rather than narrative logic.

**Franklin Diagnosis:** Step 0 and Step 4 reveal distributed agency with no convergence. Multiple protagonist candidates but none carries the full development chain.

**Guidance:** Choose a throughline figure—the person whose decisions and stakes carry the reader from complication to resolution. Other figures become supporting characters whose stories illuminate the throughline. The key question: whose problem is this, and whose actions determine the outcome?

## Nonfiction Form Coverage

The Franklin Pathway fills a genre gap. The core framework and genre modules are fiction-weighted. The Memoir/Creative Nonfiction audit handles personal narrative but not all nonfiction forms.

### Reported Feature

**Typical material:** Interviews, documents, research, observation, institutional records.
**Franklin approach:** Identify the event-problem (what happened or is happening that demands attention); identify the protagonist (who is most affected, or who is trying to fix it); build the spine from their perspective.
**Common trap:** Organizing by topic instead of by story. The reporting is thorough but the structure is encyclopedic.

### Profile

**Typical material:** Access to a subject, biographical research, observation.
**Franklin approach:** Profiles need a throughline event—a day something fails, a decisive performance, a deadline, a conflict. Without a throughline event, you have a character sketch, not a story. Build the spine around the event; weave biography as context that explains present action.
**Common trap:** "Interesting person" without narrative tension. The subject is compelling but nothing is at stake.

### Personal Essay

**Typical material:** Memory, reflection, lived experience.
**Franklin approach:** The complication must be a specific event (not "I was unhappy"), and the resolution must involve a decision or irreversible change (not "I felt better"). Memoir needs action and cost, not just feeling.
**Common trap:** Therapeutic narration without dramatic structure.
**Cross-reference:** Memoir/Creative Nonfiction audit for truth/memory/ethics concerns.

### Narrative Journalism

**Typical material:** Documents, sources, court records, policy analysis, field reporting.
**Franklin approach:** The spine often follows an individual case that exemplifies a systemic issue. The development chain alternates between the individual story and the systemic context. Context Smother Diagnostic is critical: system-level analysis should answer questions raised by the individual story, not precede it.
**Common trap:** Leading with statistics and policy, burying the human story.

## Pipeline Integration

### If Story-Shaped (Classification 1)

Resume normal Editor pipeline immediately. Franklin exit.

### If Storyable Material (Classification 2)

1. Run Franklin Steps 1–4.
2. If a candidate spine emerges:
   - Re-run Plot Architecture fit using that spine hypothesis.
   - Resume full Editor passes (reverse outline, structural mapping, etc.) against the hypothesized spine.
   - Use the function chain's coverage map to focus the Editor's attention on structural gaps.
3. If no spine emerges despite storyable elements:
   - Reclassify as Not Storyable (Classification 4) or Argument With Embedded Narrative (Classification 3).

### If Argument With Embedded Narrative (Classification 3)

Invoke the **Dialectical Clarity Audit** on the argument structure. The audit produces:
- **Argument Type & Promise** (AT-codes: what kind of argument, what the reader expects)
- **Claim Ladder** (CL-codes: main claim C0, subclaims C1–C3, stakes claim)
- **Support Map** (SM-codes: per-subclaim evidence inventory and link validity)
- **Burden of Proof & Scope** (BP-codes: claim scope vs. evidence scope)
- **Objection Handling** (OB-codes: live objections engaged or avoided)
- **Narrative-as-Evidence Inventory** (NE-codes: per-vignette function — illustration, evidence, emotional anchor, or unattached)

Run Franklin Steps 1–4 on each embedded narrative segment separately. This prevents false-positive triggers from producing nonsense—the argument structure gets the Dialectical Clarity Audit while embedded narratives get spine checks.

Also invoke the Narrative Nonfiction Craft audit on the whole piece for reader contract (LC/E codes) and question management (QS-codes).

### If Not Storyable (Classification 4)

Output redirect:
- What the material is (essay, meditation, report, topic exploration).
- What a story would require (what's missing).
- Which forms fit the material as it exists.
- If the Memoir/Creative Nonfiction audit is loaded, hand off to "Structure in Memoir" for non-narrative organization options.

## Integration with Core Framework

### Operating Tier

The Franklin Pathway adds a third tier:

| Tier | When | Function |
|------|------|----------|
| **Core DE** | Default | 80/20 diagnostic for most manuscripts |
| **Full DE** | Complexity triggers | Deep analysis for complex manuscripts |
| **Franklin Pathway** | Absence-of-story triggers | Pre-spine viability gate for pre-narrative material |

Franklin is not an escalation from Core DE. It's a **redirect**: when the Editor detects the material doesn't have a story spine, it exits the normal pass sequence and enters the Franklin workflow.

### Pass Modifications

**Pass 0 (Reverse Outline):** Add causal chain assessment. After documenting scene transitions, calculate percentage with clear causal mechanisms vs. arbitrary/unclear. If arbitrary/unclear exceeds 40%, flag as Franklin Signal 2.

**Pass 1 (Reader Experience):** Add "forward drive" check. If no reader question identifiable by page 20 and no promise tracking produces results, flag as Franklin Signal 5.

**Pass 2 (Structural Mapping):** Add "beat identification failure" protocol. If standard beat identification fails to produce results, flag as Franklin Signal 3 rather than forcing the analysis.

**Plot Architecture Audit:** Add "no-fit" output. If no spine type produces a PASS on its primary logic gate, output "NO VIABLE SPINE IDENTIFIED" and flag as Franklin Signal 1.

**Character Architecture Audit:** Add "protagonist identification failure" output. If AQ cannot identify a protagonist, flag as Franklin Signal 4.

### Module Interactions

**With Memoir/Creative Nonfiction Audit (including Gornick Layer):** Franklin handles structural story-finding; Memoir handles truth/memory/ethics; the Gornick Layer (GS/NI/SC/TD/BR/SEL/EP codes) handles the meaning engine. For personal narrative, all three may be active. Franklin's Narrative Stance Diagnostic provides the informal logic gates; the Gornick Layer provides the formal code taxonomies. When Franklin returns Classification 4 for personal narrative, run the Gornick Layer before issuing redirect — the piece may have a story at the stance level even without an event-spine. Franklin's Classification 4 output can also hand off to Memoir's structure section.

**With Plot Architecture Audit:** Plot Architecture diagnoses existing spines; Franklin builds absent ones. A manuscript that starts in Franklin and emerges with a spine can then be analyzed by Plot Architecture for spine-specific logic gates. The 50 spine types provide vocabulary for classifying the spine Franklin produces.

**With Short Fiction Audit:** Short nonfiction (personal essay, flash) may trigger both. Franklin's function chain may compress to 2–3 turns for very short work. Short Fiction's "single-effect" principle applies.

**With Narrative Nonfiction Craft Audit:** Franklin is the gate; the Narrative Nonfiction Craft audit is the craft layer. Franklin diagnoses whether the material has a viable spine. The Narrative Nonfiction Craft audit diagnoses whether the nonfiction executes its craft well — reader contract (CQ + Promise, LC/E codes), question management (QS-codes extending the Question Management Diagnostic), information timing (I-codes extending Context Smother), scene pressure (ST-codes extending SCR), meaning line (SW-codes), anchor/system balance (AS-codes for F4 form), and attribution discipline (A-codes extending Reconstruction Standards). Franklin invokes the Narrative Nonfiction Craft audit at each classification; the craft audit can also run independently on story-shaped nonfiction that doesn't need Franklin.

**With Dialectical Clarity Audit:** Franklin is the gate; the Dialectical Clarity Audit handles what the gate's story-spine tools can't — argument-shaped material. Classification 3 (Argument With Embedded Narrative) invokes the Dialectical Clarity Audit on the argument structure and Franklin Steps 1–4 on the embedded narrative segments. Classification 4 (Not Storyable) can invoke it when the redirect identifies argument-shaped material. The Dialectical Clarity Audit diagnoses argumentative structure (AT/CL/SM/BP/OB/NE codes) while the Narrative Nonfiction Craft audit handles reader engagement for the same material. The two complement each other: one asks "is the argument valid?" and the other asks "does the reader stay engaged?"

## Output Format

### Diagnostic Phase (Steps 0–4)

1. **Gate Classification** (1–4): Which classification + 3–5 bullet reasons + gate diagnostic table
2. **Candidate Spine** (if Classification 2 or 3): Best pair + alternates, with material sources cited
3. **Backward Chain** (if Classification 2 or 3): Resolution → ... → true complication + entry point analysis
4. **Function Chain + Coverage Map** (if Classification 2 or 3): Turn-types + existing material mapping + structural gaps + escalation tags
5. **Agency Identification** (if Classification 2 or 3): Protagonist confirmed → defer to Character Architecture, OR protagonist candidates + structural consequences
6. **Context Smother Diagnostic** (if nonfiction): Context Load Index + slab classification + recommended moves

### For Classification 3 (Argument With Embedded Narrative)

- Dialectical Clarity Audit output (AT/CL/SM/BP/OB/NE codes — see Specialized Audit: Dialectical Clarity)
- Franklin diagnostic (Steps 1–4) applied to each embedded narrative segment
- Narrative Nonfiction Craft audit output for reader contract (LC/E/QS codes)

### For Classification 4 (Not Storyable)

- Classification statement with specific missing elements
- Redirect to appropriate non-narrative form
- What a story version would require (if story potential exists)

### Coaching in the Editorial Letter

The diagnostic outputs above identify structural requirements—what the material needs to become story-shaped, or why it isn't storyable. When the editorial letter is written, it may include coaching guidance: explaining *why* a function chain gap matters, walking through the logic of backward causality, offering the reasoning behind a classification decision, or providing examples of how the Context Smother Diagnostic's recommended moves work in practice. This coaching belongs in the deliverable (the editorial letter), not in the diagnostic specification. The pathway stays structural; the letter can teach.

---

*The Franklin Pathway is the Editor's pre-spine viability gate. It handles material that can't yet be audited as story by classifying it, and—where story potential exists—producing structural requirements the writer can build against. The system identifies what the structure needs; the writer invents what fills it.*
