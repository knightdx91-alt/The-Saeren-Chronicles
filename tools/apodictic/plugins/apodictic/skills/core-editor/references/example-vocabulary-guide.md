*For the facilitator: this guide names the structural concepts behind the diagnosis and grounds
each one in a specific place in the manuscript, then offers questions to open the discussion.
The aim is to help the group **see** the architecture and talk about it — not to hand anyone a
fix. Severity and the revision record live in the editorial letter.*

## Glossary

- **Controlling idea** — the single argument the whole book makes through its events. Here it
  lives in the sister-relationship arc and comes into focus by the close, but blurs in the
  middle third (§Theme; Ch. 9).
- **Reveal economy** — the rate at which a story spends what it knows. This draft holds its
  central reveal until late, so the middle third runs on little new information (Ch. 9, line 220).
- **Causal gap** — a place where one event should cause the next but the link is left off the
  page. The Chapter 9 turn has one: the choice and its consequence aren't connected on-page
  (Ch. 9, line 220).
- **Character agency** — whether a character drives events or is driven by them. Maya's choices
  steer Part I, then recede where the aftermath is summarized (Ch. 7, lines 142-160).
- **Dramatic irony** — the gap between what the reader knows and what a character knows. This
  novel runs on almost none, which is a structural characteristic, not a flaw (§Reader Knowledge).

## Discussion Prompts

- Where does the controlling idea first come into focus, and where does it blur?
- What does the reader know at the Chapter 9 turn that Maya does not — or is the gap the other way?
- Whose choice drives the middle third, and is its consequence shown on-page or summarized?
- If the book runs on little dramatic irony, what holds the reader's attention across the middle?
- Which scene shows the book at its highest capacity, and what specifically makes it work?
